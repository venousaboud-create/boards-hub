01

Overview & Classification

Definition

Vasculitis is a clinicopathologic process characterized by inflammation of and damage to blood vessels. The vessel lumen is usually compromised, resulting in ischemia of tissues supplied by the involved vessel. Any type, size, and location of blood vessel may be involved. Vasculitis may be the primary manifestation of a disease OR a secondary component of another disease.

Core Concept
Vasculitis may be confined to a single organ (e.g., skin) or simultaneously involve multiple organ systems. The vasculitic syndromes have a great deal of heterogeneity while also possessing a considerable degree of overlap.

Classification

Primary Vasculitis
  • ANCA-associated: GPA (Wegener’s), MPA, EGPA (Churg-Strauss)
  • IgA Vasculitis (Henoch-Schönlein)
  • Cryoglobulinemic Vasculitis
  • Polyarteritis Nodosa (PAN)
  • Kawasaki Disease
  • Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA)
  • Takayasu Arteritis
  • Behçet’s Disease
  • Cogan’s Syndrome
  • Single-Organ Vasculitis (cutaneous, CNS, aortitis)
Secondary Vasculitis
  • Drug-induced vasculitis
  • HCV-associated cryoglobulinemic vasculitis
  • HBV-associated vasculitis
  • Cancer-associated vasculitis
  • Lupus vasculitis
  • Rheumatoid vasculitis
  • Sarcoid vasculitis

Vessel Size Classification

Vessel SizeKey DiseasesOrgans Targeted
Large (aorta + main branches)GCA, Takayasu ArteritisAorta, subclavian, carotid, temporal artery
Medium (muscular arteries)PAN, Kawasaki DiseaseRenal, visceral, coronary arteries
Small (capillaries, venules, arterioles)GPA, MPA, EGPA, IgA vasculitis, CryoglobulinemicKidney, skin, lung, peripheral nerve

Conditions That Mimic Vasculitis — Must Exclude First

⚠ Must Rule Out Before Diagnosing Vasculitis
  • Infections: bacterial endocarditis, disseminated gonococcal infection, histoplasmosis, coccidioidomycosis, syphilis, Lyme, Rocky Mountain spotted fever, Whipple’s disease
  • Coagulopathies/Thrombotic Microangiopathies: antiphospholipid syndrome, TTP
  • Neoplasms: atrial myxoma, lymphoma, carcinomatosis
  • Drug Toxicity: cocaine, levamisole, amphetamines, ergot alkaloids, methysergide, arsenic
  • Other: sarcoidosis, atheroembolic disease, Goodpasture’s (anti-GBM), amyloidosis, migraine, fibromuscular dysplasia, reversible cerebral vasoconstrictive syndrome (RCVS), segmental arterial mediolysis

Pathophysiology & Pathogenesis

Three Major Mechanisms

Mechanism 1

Immune Complex Deposition

IgA vasculitis, Lupus vasculitis, Serum sickness, HCV cryoglobulinemic vasculitis, HBV-associated vasculitis

Mechanism 2

ANCA Production

GPA (Wegener’s), MPA, EGPA (Churg-Strauss)

Mechanism 3

Pathogenic T Lymphocyte Responses + Granuloma Formation

Giant Cell Arteritis, Takayasu Arteritis, GPA, EGPA

Mechanism 1: Immune Complex Formation & Deposition

Antigen-antibody complexes form in antigen excess and deposit in vessel walls. Increased vascular permeability is caused by vasoactive amines (histamine, bradykinin, leukotrienes) released from platelets and mast cells (via IgE-triggered mechanisms).

  • Complement activation: C5a is strongly chemotactic for neutrophils → infiltrate vessel wall → phagocytose immune complexes → release intracytoplasmic enzymes → vessel wall damage
  • Subacute/Chronic: mononuclear cell infiltration → vessel lumen compromise → ischemic changes in supplied tissues
  • HBV association: hepatitis B antigen identified in both circulating AND deposited immune complexes in PAN-like vasculitis
  • HCV association: hepatitis C virions and HCV antigen-antibody complexes identified in cryoprecipitates of cryoglobulinemic vasculitis patients
Clinical Pearl
Circulating immune complexes do NOT always lead to vascular deposition. Many patients with active vasculitis have NO demonstrable immune complexes. The causal role of immune complexes remains unproven in most vasculitic syndromes.

Mechanism 2: ANCA — The Two Types

cANCA (Cytoplasmic)
  • Pattern: diffuse, granular cytoplasmic staining by immunofluorescence
  • Target antigen: Proteinase-3 (PR3) — 29-kDa neutral serine proteinase in neutrophil azurophilic granules
  • >90% of patients with active GPA are cANCA/PR3 positive
pANCA (Perinuclear)
  • Pattern: perinuclear or nuclear staining by immunofluorescence
  • Main antigen: Myeloperoxidase (MPO)
  • Other targets producing pANCA pattern: elastase, cathepsin G, lactoferrin, lysozyme — NOT associated with vasculitis
  • Diseases: MPA, EGPA, some GPA, isolated necrotizing crescentic GN

How ANCA Causes Vessel Damage — The Pathogenic Mechanism:

  • At rest: PR3 and MPO are inaccessible inside azurophilic granules and lysosomes of neutrophils/monocytes
  • Priming stimuli: TNF-α or IL-1, OR activation of the alternative complement cascade → PR3 and MPO translocate to the cell membrane
  • Extracellular ANCA then binds the translocated antigens → neutrophil degranulation + reactive oxygen species production → tissue damage
  • ANCA-activated neutrophils adhere to and kill endothelial cells in vitro
  • ANCA activation also induces release of proinflammatory cytokines IL-1 and IL-8
  • Adoptive transfer experiments in genetically engineered mice provide further evidence for direct pathogenic role in vivo
⚠ Arguments AGAINST ANCA as Primary Pathogen
Active GPA can exist WITHOUT ANCA. Antibody titers do NOT correlate well with disease activity. Patients in remission may maintain high ANCA levels for years. A rising ANCA alone does NOT predict relapse (only 43% relapse within 1 year of ANCA rise — large prospective study).
pANCA Without MPO — NOT Vasculitis
A pANCA pattern NOT due to anti-MPO antibodies is associated with NON-vasculitic conditions: rheumatic and non-rheumatic autoimmune diseases, IBD, certain drugs, and infections (endocarditis, bacterial airway infections in cystic fibrosis). Only anti-MPO has been convincingly associated with vasculitis.

Mechanism 3: T Lymphocyte Responses & Granuloma Formation

  • Vascular endothelial cells express HLA class II molecules after IFN-γ activation → interact with CD4+ T cells like antigen-presenting macrophages
  • Endothelial cells secrete IL-1 → activates T lymphocytes → initiates in-situ immunologic processes
  • IL-1 and TNF-α are potent inducers of ELAM-1 and VCAM-1 → enhanced leukocyte adhesion to endothelium → vessel wall inflammation
  • In GCA: sequence analysis shows restricted clonal T-cell expansion → antigen resides in arterial wall. Two CD4+ lineages participate: TH1 (IFN-γ-producing) and TH17 (IL-17-producing) — TH17 cells may be less responsive to glucocorticoids

Diagnosis & Treatment Principles

General Diagnostic Approach

Consider vasculitis in any patient with an unexplained systemic illness. Key clinical clues that should suggest vasculitis:

  • Palpable purpura
  • Pulmonary infiltrates + microscopic hematuria
  • Chronic sinusitis
  • Mononeuritis multiplex
  • Unexplained ischemic events
  • Glomerulonephritis with evidence of multisystem disease
Diagnostic Algorithm
1. Exclude mimics first → 2. Establish diagnosis (biopsy ± angiogram) → 3. Properly categorize the specific vasculitis syndrome → 4. Determine pattern and extent of disease → 5. Look for offending antigen or underlying disease → 6. Treat accordingly
  • Definitive diagnosis: biopsy of involved tissue. "Blind" biopsies (organs with no subjective or objective evidence of involvement) have very low yield — avoid.
  • For PAN, Takayasu arteritis, or primary CNS vasculitis — arteriogram of organs with suspected involvement should be performed

General Treatment Principles

  • Remove offending antigen if identified
  • Treat underlying disease if vasculitis is secondary to infection, neoplasm, or connective tissue disease
  • Aggressive immunosuppression (glucocorticoids + other agents) for diseases with irreversible organ dysfunction and high morbidity/mortality (GPA is the prototype)
  • Avoid aggressive therapy for vasculitic manifestations that rarely cause irreversible damage (e.g., isolated idiopathic cutaneous vasculitis)
  • Start glucocorticoids for systemic vasculitides that cannot be specifically categorized; add other immunosuppressives if inadequate response or unacceptably toxic steroid regimen needed
  • Continually attempt to taper glucocorticoids and discontinue when possible
  • Most forms of vasculitis have potential for relapse — include assessment of relapse risk in treatment planning
  • Choice of maintenance agent based on published data, organ involvement, toxicity profile, and individual patient factors
Toxicity Monitoring — Essential for All Agents
  • Cyclophosphamide: WBC >3000/μL, neutrophils >1500/μL; CBC q1-2 weeks; morning dosing with large fluid intake (bladder protection); monitor for bladder cancer indefinitely
  • Methotrexate: give with folic acid 1 mg/day OR folinic acid 5-10 mg once weekly (24h after MTX); contraindicated in renal insufficiency (renally eliminated)
  • Azathioprine: check TPMT level before starting (inadequate levels → severe cytopenia)
  • Rituximab: screen for HBV before use; monitor immunoglobulin levels; give in skilled infusion center (infusion reactions); premedications reduce reaction risk
  • Tocilizumab/Sarilumab: check LFTs, CBC, lipids q4-8 weeks then q3 months; CRP/ESR normalize and CANNOT be used to monitor disease activity or infection
Infection Prophylaxis
P. jirovecii and fungi can infect even with normal WBC, especially with glucocorticoids. GPA/MPA patients on glucocorticoids + another immunosuppressive agent → TMP-SMX prophylaxis (or alternative if intolerant). Continue Pneumocystis prophylaxis during maintenance therapy, especially with rituximab or persistent lymphopenia.

Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (GPA / Wegener's)

Epidemiology & Defining Features

Prevalence & Demographics
Prevalence: 3/100,000; M:F = 1:1; mean onset 40–60 yrs; primarily whites; rare in Blacks
Classic Triad
Upper airway disease (sinusitis, otitis, saddle-nose) + Lower airway (nodules, infiltrates, cavitation) + Glomerulonephritis (RPGN, pauci-immune, crescentic)
Limited GPA
Same histology as classic GPA but without renal involvement. Still requires treatment. Watch for progression to full GPA.
  • Upper airway: chronic sinusitis, nasopharyngeal ulcers, saddle-nose deformity (cartilage destruction), subglottic stenosis (up to 16%), serous otitis media (Eustachian tube obstruction), hearing loss, mastoiditis
  • Lower airway: pulmonary nodules (may cavitate — "Swiss cheese" on CT), infiltrates, hemoptysis, diffuse alveolar hemorrhage (life-threatening)
  • Renal: focal/segmental necrotizing GN → rapidly progressive GN (RPGN); pauci-immune (no immune complex deposits); crescentic on biopsy
  • Eyes: scleritis, episcleritis, orbital pseudotumor (proptosis), dacryocystitis, retinal artery occlusion
  • Musculoskeletal: arthralgias/arthritis in 2/3 of patients
  • Skin: palpable purpura, ulcers, nodules, papules, vesicles; Leukocytoclastic vasculitis on biopsy
  • Nervous system: mononeuritis multiplex, cranial neuropathies, CNS vasculitis
  • Cardiac: pericarditis, myocarditis (rare)

ANCA in GPA

  • cANCA (PR3-ANCA): positive in >90% of active generalized GPA; sensitivity 34–92% (varies with disease extent and activity)
  • Active limited GPA: cANCA positive ~67%; active renal GPA: ~80–90%
  • Specificity for GPA: ~98% when combined with positive PR3 ELISA
  • ANCA titers: useful for monitoring, but rising ANCA alone (without symptoms) is NOT sufficient to retrear — only 43% of patients with rising titers actually flare within 1 year
  • ANCA-negative GPA does occur (especially limited disease) — do not exclude diagnosis based on ANCA alone
ANCA Rising ≠ Mandate to Treat
Only 43% of patients with rising ANCA actually relapse within 1 year. Rising ANCA is a risk factor, not an indication for treatment change. Treat based on clinical symptoms + objective findings, not ANCA titer alone.

Diagnosis

  • Biopsy is gold standard: involved tissue (upper airway, lung, or kidney preferred)
  • Histology: necrotizing granulomatous inflammation; granulomas in upper/lower airways; pauci-immune crescentic GN in kidney (usually NO granulomas in renal biopsy)
  • "Blind" biopsies (no clinical evidence of involvement) have low yield — avoid
  • ANCA + classic clinical picture can suffice for diagnosis when biopsy is not feasible or risky
  • Urinalysis is essential: RBC casts → nephritis; must check in all patients
  • CT chest: cavitating nodules most specific finding; nodules can be bilateral

Treatment — Severe/Generalized Disease (Organ-Threatening)

Induction of Remission — Severe GPA
Rituximab 375 mg/m² IV weekly ×4 (or 1g IV ×2, 2 weeks apart) + high-dose glucocorticoids (methylprednisolone 1g IV ×3 days → prednisone 1 mg/kg/day, max 80 mg/day)

OR Cyclophosphamide 2 mg/kg/day PO (max 200 mg/day) or IV pulse 15 mg/kg q2–3 wks + high-dose glucocorticoids

Rituximab preferred over cyclophosphamide for induction based on RAVE and RITUXVAS trials (non-inferior, fewer adverse effects, especially for relapsing disease)
Avacopan (Tavneos) — Novel Adjunct
Avacopan (C5a receptor inhibitor) 30 mg PO BID — approved as an adjunct to reduce glucocorticoid exposure in ANCA-associated vasculitis (GPA/MPA). In ADVOCATE trial: non-inferior for remission at 26 wks; superior for sustained remission at 52 wks versus prednisone taper. Does NOT completely eliminate the need for glucocorticoids in severe disease but allows substantial reduction.
  • Plasmapheresis/PLEX: historically used for severe renal disease or DAH; PEXIVAS trial showed NO benefit for renal outcomes or survival — no longer routinely recommended
  • Goal of induction: achieve remission (typically 3–6 months), then transition to maintenance

Treatment — Non-Severe/Limited Disease

  • Non-organ-threatening: Methotrexate (20–25 mg/week, with folic acid 1 mg/day) + glucocorticoids
  • Mycophenolate mofetil is an alternative for patients intolerant of methotrexate
  • Still requires careful monitoring and follow-up

Maintenance of Remission

Maintenance Options (after induction remission)
  • Rituximab: 500 mg IV at month 6, then q6 months ×18 months (MAINRITSAN trial — superior to azathioprine)
  • Azathioprine: 2 mg/kg/day — traditional standard; less effective than rituximab but widely used; switch from cyclophosphamide after remission achieved
  • Methotrexate: 20–25 mg/week — alternative for renal-limited or non-renal disease; contraindicated in significant renal insufficiency
  • Mycophenolate mofetil: 1.5 g BID — less effective than azathioprine in some trials but used in practice
  • Duration of maintenance: typically at least 18–24 months after remission; some experts advocate longer especially for GPA
  • Relapse rate: 50–70% of remissions eventually relapse; relapse risk greatest in cANCA-positive and nasal/sinus disease

Refractory/Relapsing Disease

  • Switch induction agent (cyclophosphamide → rituximab, or vice versa)
  • IV pulse cyclophosphamide if not used in initial induction
  • Failed biologics / refractory: Complement inhibition (avacopan), IVIg, mycophenolate mofetil combinations, or clinical trials

Prognosis & Monitoring

  • 5-year survival >80% with treatment (vs. median survival 5 months untreated historically)
  • Without treatment: 82% die within 1 year from renal failure
  • Monitor: UA (casts), CBC, creatinine, ESR/CRP, ANCA titers, chest imaging as indicated
  • TMP-SMX prophylaxis: reduces Pneumocystis AND may reduce relapse rate (possible benefit for upper airway disease — some evidence)
  • TMP-SMX also protects nasal passages from Staphylococcus — reduces nasal carriage which can trigger flares

Microscopic Polyangiitis (MPA)

Defining Features

What Defines MPA vs GPA
MPA = systemic vasculitis with pauci-immune GN and/or pulmonary capillaritis — but WITHOUT granulomatous inflammation and WITHOUT significant upper airway disease. No saddle-nose, no sinus granulomas.
  • Necrotizing vasculitis of small vessels (capillaries, venules, arterioles) — NO immune complex deposits on immunofluorescence = "pauci-immune"
  • No granuloma formation — this is the key histological distinction from GPA
  • No upper airway disease (sinusitis, subglottic stenosis, saddle-nose not seen)
  • Like GPA, affects kidneys (necrotizing/crescentic GN) and lungs (pulmonary capillaritis → alveolar hemorrhage)

Clinical Features

SystemMPA FeaturesNotes
KidneyPauci-immune crescentic GN (RPGN)Most common manifestation; most cause of morbidity
LungPulmonary capillaritis, alveolar hemorrhageHemoptysis, ↓ PaO₂; less common than renal
SkinPalpable purpura, mononeuritis multiplexLivedo reticularis also seen
ConstitutionalFever, weight loss, fatigue, arthralgiasCommon prodrome
GIAbdominal pain, bleedingLess common than in IgA vasculitis

ANCA in MPA

  • pANCA (MPO-ANCA): positive in ~60–70% of MPA cases (vs. PR3-ANCA in GPA)
  • Some MPA cases are PR3-ANCA positive (~30%)
  • ANCA-negative MPA also occurs (~10–25%)
  • MPO-ANCA titers tend to fall with effective treatment; rising titers may predict relapse

Treatment

MPA Treatment = Same Approach as GPA
Induction: Rituximab (375 mg/m² ×4 or 1g ×2) OR cyclophosphamide (2 mg/kg/day PO or IV pulse) + high-dose glucocorticoids
Avacopan (30 mg BID) can be added to reduce steroid burden (FDA-approved for ANCA-associated vasculitis including MPA)
Maintenance: Rituximab q6 months, OR azathioprine 2 mg/kg/day, OR methotrexate 20–25 mg/week
  • RAVE trial included both GPA and MPA — rituximab non-inferior to cyclophosphamide for both
  • MPA may have slightly lower relapse rate compared to GPA
  • Pulmonary-renal syndrome (alveolar hemorrhage + GN) → life-threatening; aggressive induction mandatory
  • Prognosis: 5-yr survival ~74–80% with treatment; renal outcomes dependent on severity at presentation

Eosinophilic Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (EGPA / Churg-Strauss)

Defining Features — The Triad

EGPA Classic Triad (Required for Diagnosis)
1. Asthma (adult-onset, often severe, preceding vasculitis by years) +
2. Peripheral eosinophilia (>1500 cells/μL; often dramatically elevated, typically >10% of WBC) +
3. Systemic vasculitis (small-to-medium vessel; granulomatous; eosinophilic tissue infiltration)
  • Annual incidence: 1–3/million; mean onset 4th–5th decade; M:F ~1:1
  • Eosinophilia >1000 cells/μL in >80%; often markedly elevated (5,000–20,000+ cells/μL)
  • Three phases: Prodromal (asthma + allergic rhinitis, years) → Eosinophilic (tissue eosinophilia, PF, GI) → Vasculitic (systemic vasculitis)
  • Paradox: as vasculitis phase develops, asthma and eosinophilia may actually improve

Clinical Features by System

SystemManifestationKey Points
RespiratoryAsthma (universal), sinusitis, nasal polypsAsthma often precedes vasculitis by 3–9 yrs; do NOT interpret worsening asthma as asthma flare — could be EGPA
CardiacEosinophilic myocarditis, pericarditis, cardiomyopathy, valvular disease, CHFCardiac involvement accounts for 39% of EGPA mortality — get echo at diagnosis in ALL patients
Nervous systemMononeuritis multiplex (most common; 75%), cranial neuropathiesFoot/wrist drop; often asymmetric
SkinPalpable purpura, subcutaneous nodules, livedo reticularisCutaneous nodules = eosinophilic granulomas

RenalLess common than GPA/MPA; eosinophilic interstitial nephritis or pauci-immune GNANCA-positive subset has more renal involvement

ANCA in EGPA

  • pANCA (MPO-ANCA) in approximately 48% of patients
  • ANCA-positive subset: more renal and nerve involvement, more glomerulonephritis
  • ANCA-negative subset: more cardiac, pulmonary, constitutional symptoms
  • ANCA negativity does NOT exclude EGPA — clinical triad is the basis for diagnosis

Diagnosis

  • ACR 1990 criteria: ≥4 of 6 criteria (asthma, eosinophilia >10%, mono/polyneuropathy, pulmonary infiltrates not fixed, paranasal sinus disease, extravascular eosinophils on biopsy)
  • Biopsy: necrotizing vasculitis + eosinophilic granulomas + tissue eosinophilia
  • Echocardiogram at diagnosis: mandatory to evaluate for cardiac eosinophilic involvement
  • Troponin, BNP, ECG: screen for cardiac involvement

Treatment

Induction — Standard
High-dose glucocorticoids: prednisone 1 mg/kg/day for severe organ-threatening disease; alone may suffice for mild disease
Add cyclophosphamide for severe manifestations: cardiac involvement, renal disease, CNS involvement, extensive GI disease, severe neuropathy
Mepolizumab (Nucala) — Anti-IL-5
Mepolizumab 300 mg SQ monthly (FDA-approved for EGPA in 2017, MIRRA trial). Effective for:
• Reducing relapse rate and maintaining remission
• Glucocorticoid-sparing in non-severe disease
• Achieving remission in relapsing/refractory disease

Note: Standard mepolizumab dose for eosinophilic asthma is 100 mg SQ monthly — EGPA dose is HIGHER at 300 mg SQ monthly
  • Benralizumab (anti-IL-5Rα): emerging data for EGPA; not yet FDA-approved for this indication as of early 2026 but being studied
  • Maintenance: glucocorticoid taper; rituximab has been used for refractory cases
  • 5-year survival: 25% untreated → 72% with treatment (major improvement with GC-based therapy)
  • Five-factor score (FFS) predicts poor prognosis: cardiac, renal, GI, CNS involvement, no ENT disease

Polyarteritis Nodosa (PAN)

The Four NOTs of PAN (Classic Exam Distinguishers)

What PAN Is NOT (Classic Board Distinctions)
1. NOT small vessel — PAN affects medium vessels (muscular arteries), NOT capillaries/venules
2. NOT associated with GN — No glomerulonephritis (affects renal artery, not glomerular capillaries)
3. NOT associated with lung disease — No pulmonary vasculitis (bronchial arteries not involved in primary PAN)
4. NOT ANCA-associated — ANCA is negative in classic PAN; if ANCA positive → consider MPA instead
  • PAN = necrotizing vasculitis of medium-sized muscular arteries; focal, segmental; may form aneurysms
  • Incidence: 2–9/million; M:F ~2:1; mean onset 45–65 years
  • Classic PAN does NOT involve capillaries, venules, or arterioles → NO glomerulonephritis, NO DAH

HBV-Associated PAN

Hepatitis B — Screen ALL Patients
HBV-associated PAN is a distinct entity:
• Occurs within first 6 months of HBV infection
• Treatment: antiviral therapy (entecavir or tenofovir) + short-course glucocorticoids → plasma exchange
• Do NOT treat with long-term immunosuppression (worsens HBV replication)
• Screen ALL patients with PAN for HBV surface antigen — mandatory
• HBV-associated PAN has declined dramatically with HBV vaccination programs

Clinical Features

  • Constitutional: fever, weight loss, fatigue, malaise (most common presenting symptoms)
  • Musculoskeletal: arthralgias, myalgias, myopathy
  • Neuropathy: mononeuritis multiplex (most common neurologic manifestation; ischemic peripheral nerve injury)
  • Renal: renal artery involvement → renovascular hypertension, renal infarcts; NOT GN; renal dysfunction from ischemia, not inflammation of glomeruli
  • GI: mesenteric vasculitis → abdominal pain (postprandial, "intestinal angina"), GI ischemia, infarction, bleeding; one of most serious manifestations
  • Testes: orchitis (testicular pain) — pathognomonic of PAN when present
  • Skin: livedo reticularis, cutaneous nodules along arteries, ulcers
  • Aneurysms: up to 1 cm along medium arteries (celiac, hepatic, renal, mesenteric); may rupture

Diagnosis

Arteriogram is the Diagnostic Procedure of Choice
When biopsy of involved organ is not feasible or non-diagnostic, visceral arteriography (celiac, mesenteric, renal) is indicated. Classic finding: multiple microaneurysms (up to 1 cm), irregular stenoses, "beading" of arteries. Aneurysms can be seen on CT angiography or MR angiography as well.
  • Biopsy: involved tissue (muscle, sural nerve, skin, kidney) shows necrotizing arteritis of muscular arteries
  • Labs: elevated ESR/CRP, leukocytosis, anemia; no specific serologic marker
  • ANCA: negative (if positive, reconsider MPA diagnosis)
  • HBsAg, anti-HCV: mandatory workup

Treatment & Special Situations

DADA2 — Genetic PAN Mimic
Deficiency of Adenosine Deaminase 2 (DADA2): autosomal recessive; presents like PAN (medium vessel vasculitis); frequently pediatric or young adult; recurrent strokes common. Testing: plasma ADA2 enzyme activity (low); CECR1 gene mutation testing. Treatment: TNF inhibitors (NOT standard GC/cytotoxic therapy). Must be excluded in early-onset PAN, especially with CNS involvement.
  • Classic PAN (non-HBV): glucocorticoids (prednisone 1 mg/kg/day) + cyclophosphamide for severe disease
  • HBV-PAN: short-course GC + antiviral + plasmapheresis (not long-term IS)
  • 5-year survival: 10–20% untreated → 80% with glucocorticoids ± cyclophosphamide
  • Poor prognostic factors (Five-Factor Score): renal insufficiency (Cr >1.58), proteinuria >1 g/day, GI involvement, CNS involvement, cardiac cardiomyopathy
  • Surgical intervention for bowel infarction or ruptured aneurysm may be necessary

Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA) & Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)

GCA — Overview

Epidemiology
Annual incidence: 6.9–32.8/100,000 (age ≥50); highest in Scandinavian descent; F:M ~3:1; virtually never under age 50; peak incidence 70–79 yrs
Critical Risk
Untreated: blindness (anterior ischemic optic neuropathy — AION); 18× increased risk of thoracic aortic aneurysm; aortic dissection; limb ischemia

GCA Clinical Phenotypes (4 Types)

PhenotypeKey FeaturesPoints
Classic cranialNew headache (temporal), scalp tenderness, jaw claudication, visual symptoms (amaurosis fugax, diplopia), PMR symptomsTemporal artery tender, nodular, pulseless
Large-vessel GCALimb claudication (arm > leg), Raynaud's, bruits over subclavian/axillary, BP differential between armsOften ANCA-negative; PET-CT useful; higher aortic risk
Occult GCAFUO, weight loss, anemia, markedly elevated ESR — NO cranial symptomsDiagnosis by temporal artery biopsy or imaging
PMR-associatedShoulder/hip girdle pain + stiffness; 40–50% of GCA patients have PMR featuresDramatic steroid response is characteristic
  • Jaw claudication = pain with chewing (masseter ischemia) — highly specific for GCA (~sensitivity 34%, specificity 90%)
  • Amaurosis fugax = transient monocular vision loss — ophthalmologic emergency; start prednisone IMMEDIATELY
  • Diplopia: cranial nerve ischemia (CN III, IV, VI)
  • Scalp tenderness: unable to brush hair, wear hat, lie on pillow

Temporal Artery Biopsy

Biopsy Protocol
Length: at least 3–5 cm with serial sectioning (skip lesions are common)
Timing: perform ASAP, but do NOT delay glucocorticoids for biopsy. Biopsy remains positive for up to 14 days after GC initiation
Contralateral biopsy: if first biopsy negative but high clinical suspicion → biopsy other side (increases yield ~5–10%)
Histology: granulomatous arteritis with giant cells (Langerhans + foreign-body type), intimal hyperplasia, fragmented internal elastic lamina, T-lymphocyte and macrophage infiltration
  • Sensitivity of temporal artery biopsy: 70–90% in suspected GCA
  • Negative biopsy does NOT exclude GCA — up to 25% of GCA patients have negative biopsy
  • Ultrasound of temporal/axillary arteries: "halo sign" (hypoechoic thickening); can guide diagnosis when biopsy not feasible
  • PET-CT: best for large-vessel GCA (increased FDG uptake in vessel walls)

GCA Treatment

Glucocorticoids — Start Immediately (Don't Wait)
Vision loss risk → treat on clinical suspicion BEFORE confirmation
Prednisone 1 mg/kg/day (40–60 mg) — for cranial GCA without vision symptoms
Methylprednisolone 1 g IV/day × 3 days then prednisone — for vision loss or at-risk patients
Taper very slowly: initial rapid taper over first 3 months, then very gradual; most patients need 1–2 years of treatment
Relapse rate during taper: 60–85%
GC toxicity occurs in 35–65% of patients (fracture, diabetes, cataract, infection)
Tocilizumab (Actemra) — IL-6 Receptor Inhibitor
FDA-approved for GCA (GiACTA trial 2017)
• 162 mg SQ weekly OR 162 mg SQ every 2 weeks
• Both superior to placebo + 26-week prednisone taper for sustained remission at 52 weeks
• Weekly dosing was most effective
• Used as steroid-sparing agent; allows faster GC taper
• CRP/ESR normalize on tocilizumab — CANNOT use to monitor disease activity
Sarilumab (Kevzara) — IL-6 Receptor Inhibitor
Sarilumab 200 mg SQ every 2 weeks — FDA-approved for GCA (SEMAPHORE trial 2023)
• Alternative to tocilizumab for GCA
• Used in combination with prednisone taper for sustained remission
• Also normalizes CRP/ESR — not useful for monitoring
  • Aspirin 81 mg/day: reduces risk of vision loss and stroke in GCA — give to all patients without contraindication
  • Methotrexate: modest steroid-sparing effect; used when IL-6 inhibitors not available or tolerated
  • PPI: for GC-related GI protection; calcium + vitamin D for bone protection

Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)

PMR Key Facts
Annual incidence (≥50 yrs, Olmsted County): 58.7/100,000
• 40–50% of GCA patients have concurrent PMR
• 10–20% of isolated PMR patients develop GCA — counsel and monitor all PMR patients for GCA symptoms
• PMR = proximal muscle pain and stiffness (>1 hr morning stiffness; bilateral; shoulder and hip girdle)
• Typically NO muscle weakness (distinguishes from myositis/myopathy)
• Elevated ESR/CRP; often mild anemia; may have mildly elevated CK
• Dramatic and rapid response to low-dose prednisone (12.5–25 mg/day) is characteristic — failure to respond should prompt reconsideration of diagnosis

Takayasu Arteritis

Overview

Demographics
Large-vessel granulomatous vasculitis; predominantly young women (F:M = 8:1); onset typically age 10–40 years; most common in Asia and Latin America; also called "pulseless disease" or "aortoarteritis"
Vessels Involved
Aorta and its major branches (subclavian, carotid, renal, pulmonary arteries). Segmental, patchy. Results in stenoses, occlusions, and aneurysms.
  • Pathology: granulomatous inflammation of adventitia and media; giant cells; intimal hyperplasia → stenosis; wall destruction → aneurysm
  • Chronic inflammation of aorta and major branches → limb claudication, renovascular HTN, stroke, visual disturbances, cardiac involvement

Clinical Features by Phase

PhaseFeatures
Early systemic phaseConstitutional symptoms: fever, malaise, weight loss, arthralgias, myalgias, elevated ESR/CRP; "prepulseless" phase
Occlusive/late phaseVascular manifestations: diminished or absent pulses ("pulseless disease"), BP differential >10 mmHg between arms, bruits over aorta/subclavian, limb claudication, renovascular HTN, retinopathy (Takayasu retinopathy), heart failure
  • Bruits: subclavian, carotid, aortic — highly characteristic; listen for in ALL young women with unexplained constitutional symptoms
  • BP differential: >10 mmHg between arms is significant; measure in both arms always
  • Renovascular HTN: renal artery stenosis → hypertension in a young woman without other risk factors → suspect Takayasu
  • Retinopathy: "Takayasu retinopathy" — arteriovenous anastomoses, microaneurysms, vitreous hemorrhage; from carotid/ophthalmic artery involvement

Diagnosis

Arteriographic Classification
Arteriogram (conventional or CT/MR angiography) is the gold standard for diagnosis and classification:
Type I: branches of aortic arch only
Type IIa: ascending aorta, aortic arch, and branches
Type IIb: IIa + thoracic descending aorta
Type III: thoracic + abdominal descending aorta (±renal arteries)
Type IV: abdominal aorta and/or renal arteries only
Type V: combined features of Types IIb + IV (most common in some series)
  • MR angiography: preferred (no radiation); can show vessel wall edema/thickening indicating active inflammation
  • PET-CT: FDG uptake in vessel walls indicates active disease
  • ESR/CRP: often elevated but may be normal in late "burnt-out" phase
  • ACR criteria: age <40, limb claudication, decreased brachial pulse, BP differential >10 mmHg, bruit, arteriographic evidence of narrowing/occlusion

Treatment

Surgery / Revascularization Timing
Surgical/endovascular procedures (bypass, angioplasty, stenting) should be performed during quiescent disease, NOT during active inflammation. Operating during active disease → poor outcomes, anastomotic failure, restenosis. Achieve remission first, then consider revascularization.
  • Glucocorticoids: prednisone 1 mg/kg/day; initial treatment; 50% improve but 50% relapse during taper
  • Methotrexate or azathioprine: added for GC-refractory or steroid-sparing
  • TNF inhibitors (infliximab, etanercept): effective for refractory Takayasu; significant evidence base
  • Tocilizumab: IL-6 blockade; promising for refractory disease
  • Aspirin: for antithrombotic protection (prevents thrombotic events in stenosed vessels)
  • Control cardiovascular risk factors: HTN management (critical — renovascular HTN can be severe), lipids, smoking cessation

IgA Vasculitis (Henoch-Schönlein Purpura)

Overview & Classic Tetrad

IgA Vasculitis Tetrad (All 4 = Textbook; Diagnosis Requires ≥2)
1. Palpable purpura (non-thrombocytopenic; lower extremities and buttocks; gravity-dependent)
2. Arthritis/Arthralgias (knees and ankles; non-deforming)
3. Gastrointestinal involvement (abdominal pain, intussusception, GI bleeding)
4. Renal involvement (IgA nephropathy pattern; hematuria ± proteinuria ± nephrotic/nephritic)
  • Most common systemic vasculitis in children (peak age 2–11 years); also occurs in adults
  • Annual incidence: 6–20/100,000 in children; 1–2/100,000 in adults
  • Pathology: IgA1 immune complex deposition in small vessel walls (capillaries, venules, arterioles); identical to IgA nephropathy in the kidney
  • Often follows upper respiratory infection (especially Group A Streptococcus in children)

Clinical Features in Detail

FeatureFrequencyKey Points
Purpura100%Non-thrombocytopenic (platelets normal); gravity-dependent (legs, buttocks); may be painful; may precede other features by days-weeks
Arthritis60–84%Knees and ankles; periarticular swelling; transient; no joint destruction
GI involvement~70% in children; less in adultsColicky abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, melena, intussusception (rare but serious — ileoileal in children); lead-point

Diagnosis

  • Predominantly clinical; biopsy confirms when needed
  • Skin biopsy: leukocytoclastic vasculitis with IgA deposits on immunofluorescence — gold standard finding
  • Renal biopsy: mesangial IgA deposits; may show crescents in severe disease
  • Labs: serum IgA elevated in ~50% of patients; no specific test
  • Platelet count: normal (distinguishes from thrombocytopenic purpura)
  • UA: hematuria, RBC casts if renal involvement

Treatment & Prognosis

Glucocorticoids Do NOT Prevent Renal Disease
GC are used for severe GI and joint disease, but there is NO evidence that glucocorticoids prevent or treat renal involvement in IgA vasculitis. Renal outcomes are not improved by GC — manage renal disease with ACE inhibitors, careful BP control, and nephrology involvement.
  • Most cases are self-limited in children — supportive care (hydration, NSAIDs for arthritis)
  • Recurrence: 10–40% of cases; usually within 4 months of initial episode; recurrence does not predict worse outcome
  • Renal prognosis in children: 1–5% develop ESRD; most recover completely
  • Adult disease is more severe: higher rate of persistent renal disease; more nephritis; worse prognosis than pediatric
  • Severe renal disease (crescentic): high-dose glucocorticoids ± cyclophosphamide (based on limited evidence)
  • Intussusception: urgent surgical/radiologic consultation; air enema reduction

Cryoglobulinemic Vasculitis

Cryoglobulin Types & Associations

TypeCompositionDisease Associations
Type I (monoclonal)Single monoclonal Ig (IgG or IgM)Multiple myeloma, Waldenström's macroglobulinemia, B-cell lymphoma; hyperviscosity syndrome; less vasculitis
Type II (mixed)Monoclonal IgM RF + polyclonal IgGHCV infection most common; also SLE, Sjögren's; most commonly causes vasculitis
Type III (mixed)Polyclonal IgM RF + polyclonal IgGCTDs (SLE, Sjögren's), chronic infections; mild vasculitis
HCV is the #1 Cause — Screen Every Patient
HCV infection is responsible for up to 90% of essential mixed cryoglobulinemia (types II/III). Approximately 5% of patients with chronic HCV infection develop symptomatic cryoglobulinemic vasculitis. Screen ALL patients with cryoglobulinemic vasculitis for HCV first.

Clinical Features — Meltzer's Triad & Beyond

Classic Meltzer's Triad (Type II Essential Mixed Cryoglobulinemia)
1. Purpura (palpable, lower extremities; from leukocytoclastic vasculitis)
2. Weakness/fatigue
3. Arthralgias
  • Purpura: classic presentation; cold-triggered; non-thrombocytopenic; lower extremities
  • Renal: MPGN (membranoproliferative GN) in 80% of renal lesions; hematuria, proteinuria, nephrotic syndrome, renal failure
  • Hypocomplementemia: present in ~90% — especially low C4 (cryoprecipitate activates classical complement pathway consuming C4); C3 variably low; C4 low > C3 low is characteristic
  • Peripheral neuropathy: sensorimotor; mononeuritis multiplex; common and often disabling
  • Raynaud's phenomenon: from cold-induced protein precipitation
  • Sicca symptoms: Sjögren's-like (especially Type II with Sjögren's overlap)
  • Rheumatoid factor positive: IgM RF activity of the monoclonal component in Type II

Diagnosis

  • Serum cryoglobulins: blood must be drawn and transported at 37°C (keep warm) — cold causes precipitation, artifactually eliminating the sample
  • Cryocrit: percentage of cryoprecipitate; quantitative measure
  • Complement: low C4 (most sensitive), low C3 (variable), low CH50
  • HCV antibody + HCV RNA: mandatory; also test for HBV, HIV, other infections
  • Immunofixation: identify monoclonal protein in Type I/II
  • Skin biopsy: leukocytoclastic vasculitis; eosinophilic intravascular deposits (cryoglobulins)
  • Renal biopsy: MPGN pattern; intraluminal thrombi (cryoglobulin deposits); subendothelial deposits on EM

Treatment

HCV-Associated Cryoglobulinemia — Treat the Virus First
Direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) for HCV → viral eradication → resolution of cryoglobulinemia in 60–80% of cases. This is first-line treatment for mild-moderate HCV-associated cryoglobulinemic vasculitis. Response may take months after viral clearance.
Rituximab for Severe/Refractory Disease
Rituximab (375 mg/m² weekly ×4 or 1g ×2) — anti-CD20; effective for severe cryoglobulinemic vasculitis; especially useful for:
• Renal involvement (MPGN)
• Severe neuropathy
• Diffuse vasculitis
• Refractory to antivirals
Can be combined with antiviral therapy. In HCV+ patients, protect from HBV reactivation screen before use.
  • Glucocorticoids: complete response only ~7% — poor overall for cryoglobulinemia; use for severe acute manifestations or as bridge
  • Plasmapheresis: for acute severe flares (removes circulating cryoglobulins rapidly); used as bridge to immunotherapy
  • Cyclophosphamide: reserved for severe organ-threatening disease not responsive to rituximab
  • Cold avoidance, warm clothing

Other Vasculitis Syndromes

Kawasaki Disease

Kawasaki Disease — Pediatric Vasculitis Prototype
Acute self-limited vasculitis of medium vessels; predominantly coronary arteries. Most common in children under 5 (80% of cases). Ethnicity: highest in Japanese children; also common in Korean, Asian descent. Affects boys > girls (1.5:1). Seasonal peaks (winter-spring).
Classic Diagnostic Criteria (≥5 of 6, OR fever + coronary aneurysm = incomplete Kawasaki)
Fever ≥5 days + ≥4 of:
• Bilateral non-purulent conjunctivitis (bulbar; spares limbus)
• Oral changes: strawberry tongue, red cracked lips, diffuse erythema of oropharynx
• Polymorphous rash (truncal; non-vesicular)
• Extremity changes: erythema/edema of palms and soles (acute); desquamation (subacute — periungual)
• Cervical lymphadenopathy ≥1.5 cm (usually unilateral)
  • Coronary artery aneurysms: occur in ~25% without treatment; risk reduced to <5% with IVIG
  • Fatality rate: 0.5–2.8%; mostly from cardiac causes
  • Aneurysm risk factors: younger age, prolonged fever, male sex, elevated WBC/ESR/CRP, low albumin, low platelets (early)
Treatment — Time-Sensitive
IVIG 2 g/kg IV over 10–12 hours (single dose) + Aspirin 80–100 mg/kg/day in 4 divided doses (high-dose anti-inflammatory phase; until fever resolves for 48–72 hrs) → then Aspirin 3–5 mg/kg/day (low-dose antiplatelet; continue 6–8 weeks or longer if coronary abnormalities persist)

Give within first 10 days of fever (ideally 5–9 days) for maximum coronary protection
IVIG-resistant Kawasaki: repeat IVIG or add infliximab or methylprednisolone pulse
MIS-C (Multisystem Inflammatory Syndrome in Children) — COVID-19 Related
Post-COVID inflammatory condition in children; shares features with Kawasaki disease; occurs 4–6 weeks after SARS-CoV-2 infection; predominantly affects 5–15 year olds; cardiac involvement common; treat similarly to Kawasaki (IVIG + aspirin ± glucocorticoids). Distinguish from typical Kawasaki by history of COVID-19 and predominant GI/cardiac/rash features.

Primary CNS Vasculitis

  • Rare; granulomatous or necrotizing vasculitis limited to CNS (no systemic involvement)
  • Clinical: headaches, encephalopathy, focal neurological deficits, stroke, cognitive impairment
  • Diagnosis: brain MRI (T2 hyperintensities, infarcts); cerebral angiogram (beading, segmental narrowing); brain/leptomeningeal biopsy (gold standard)
  • Exclude: infections (HSV, VZV, HIV, CMV, bacterial), malignancy (CNS lymphoma), RCVS (reversible cerebral vasoconstriction syndrome)
  • RCVS mimics CNS vasculitis on angiography but resolves spontaneously; recurrent thunderclap headaches; avoid IS therapy in RCVS
  • Treatment: glucocorticoids + cyclophosphamide (GPA-type regimen); prognosis variable

Behçet's Syndrome

Behçet's — Classic Features
Recurrent oral ulcers (aphthous; multiple; painful; heals without scars) + Genital ulcers (painful; scar) + Ocular disease (posterior uveitis, panuveitis, retinal vasculitis → blindness risk) + Skin lesions (erythema nodosum, pseudofolliculitis, acneiform nodules, positive pathergy test)

Geographic distribution: "Silk Road" pattern — Turkey, Middle East, Central/East Asia, Japan
HLA-B51 association
  • Pathergy test: hyperreactivity of skin to minor trauma (prick with needle → papule/pustule at 24–48h)
  • Vascular Behçet's: thrombophilia; venous thrombosis (deep and superficial); large vessel arterial aneurysms; pulmonary artery aneurysms (hemoptysis = diagnostic clue)
  • Neurological Behçet's (neuro-Behçet's): meningoencephalitis, CNS venous thrombosis, dural sinus thrombosis
  • Treatment: colchicine (mucocutaneous), azathioprine (eye/joints/major organ), glucocorticoids (acute flares), anti-TNF (infliximab/adalimumab — refractory ocular/GI/neurological), apremilast (mucocutaneous disease)

Cogan's Syndrome

  • Rare; interstitial keratitis + audiovestibular dysfunction (Menière's-like: tinnitus, vertigo, sensorineural hearing loss)
  • Can have systemic vasculitis (aortitis, coronary, mesenteric); also aortic insufficiency
  • Treatment: glucocorticoids (topical and systemic), cochlear implant if deafness ensues

Idiopathic Cutaneous Vasculitis

  • Most common form of vasculitis overall
  • Skin manifestations: palpable purpura, urticaria, vesicles, ulcers (small vessel leukocytoclastic vasculitis)
  • Usually self-limited (<4 weeks); treat underlying cause
  • Single-organ limited → rarely causes irreversible organ damage → avoid aggressive immunosuppression
  • Workup: exclude secondary causes (infection, drug, CTD, malignancy)
  • Treatment: NSAIDs, antihistamines, colchicine, dapsone, short-course GC if persistent/severe

Secondary Vasculitis

Drug-Induced Vasculitis

Drug-Induced ANCA-Associated Vasculitis
Several drugs can cause ANCA-positive vasculitis (usually MPO/pANCA) with renal involvement (GN):
Classic offenders: PTU (propylthiouracil), hydralazine, minocycline, allopurinol, phenytoin, D-penicillamine, cocaine (levamisole-adulterated)
Key distinguishing feature: Drug-induced ANCA vasculitis often has multiple ANCA specificities (positive for both PR3 and MPO, or anti-elastase, anti-lactoferrin) — more antibodies than primary ANCA vasculitis
Management: Discontinue offending drug; immunosuppression if needed; usually improves after drug removal
  • Any drug can cause hypersensitivity vasculitis (cutaneous, small vessel)
  • Diagnosis of exclusion: temporal relationship between drug initiation and vasculitis; resolution after drug discontinuation
  • Classic culprits for cutaneous vasculitis: allopurinol, penicillin, sulfonamides, NSAIDs, thiazides, hydantoins

Serum Sickness & Immune Complex-Mediated

  • Classic serum sickness: 7–14 days after foreign protein (heterologous antiserum, biologics) — fever, rash (urticaria), arthralgias, lymphadenopathy, ± GN
  • Serum sickness-like reaction: no immune complexes; similar clinical picture; after certain drugs
  • Mechanism: immune complex deposition in vessel walls → complement activation → vasculitis
  • Self-limited; treat with antihistamines, NSAIDs, GC if severe

CRITICAL: Always Rule Out Infection Before Immunosuppression
Infection can mimic vasculitis AND cause vasculitis. Immunosuppression for what appears to be vasculitis could be catastrophic if the underlying cause is infection. Always screen for HBV, HCV, HIV, endocarditis, and TB before starting aggressive immunosuppression.

CTD-Associated & Malignancy-Associated Vasculitis

  • SLE: small vessel vasculitis (cutaneous ± visceral); hypocomplementemia; ANA/dsDNA positive; treat underlying SLE
  • Rheumatoid arthritis: rheumatoid vasculitis — rare; severe long-standing RA with high RF titer; digital ischemia, mononeuritis multiplex, cutaneous ulcers; treat with rituximab
  • Sjögren's syndrome: cryoglobulinemic vasculitis; cutaneous vasculitis; type II/III cryoglobulins common
  • Malignancy-associated: hairy cell leukemia (classic association with PAN-like); lymphoma/leukemia; paraneoplastic vasculitis; treat malignancy first
  • Sarcoidosis: can cause large-vessel vasculitis; granulomatous inflammation; distinguish from GCA/Takayasu by other features

Exam Traps & High-Yield Boards Pearls

Classic traps that distinguish top scorers from average performers. Know these cold.

⚠ Trap 1: Rising ANCA = Automatic Treatment Change
WRONG. Rising ANCA alone without clinical symptoms/signs is not an indication to start or change treatment. Only 43% of patients with rising ANCA actually relapse within 1 year. Always treat based on clinical evidence, not labs alone.
⚠ Trap 2: PAN Causes Glomerulonephritis or Pulmonary Disease
WRONG. Classic PAN affects medium muscular arteries — NOT capillaries/venules. PAN does NOT cause GN (it causes renal infarcts/renovascular HTN) and does NOT cause pulmonary vasculitis/DAH. If there's GN or lung disease with ANCA negativity, reconsider MPA or other diagnoses.
⚠ Trap 3: Treating HBV-Associated PAN with Long-Term Immunosuppression
WRONG. HBV-PAN requires antivirals (entecavir/tenofovir) + short-course GC + plasmapheresis. Long-term immunosuppression promotes HBV replication and worsens outcomes. Treat the virus, not just the vasculitis.
⚠ Trap 4: Waiting for Biopsy Before Starting Steroids in GCA with Visual Symptoms
WRONG. In GCA with visual symptoms (amaurosis fugax, vision loss), start high-dose glucocorticoids immediately. Vision loss can be irreversible. Biopsy remains positive for up to 14 days after GC initiation — never delay steroids.
⚠ Trap 5: Using ESR/CRP to Monitor Disease Activity in GCA on Tocilizumab or Sarilumab
WRONG. IL-6 receptor inhibitors (tocilizumab, sarilumab) normalize CRP and ESR pharmacologically. These acute phase reactants cannot be used to monitor disease activity or infection in patients on these drugs. Rely on symptoms and imaging.
⚠ Trap 6: Glucocorticoids Prevent Renal Disease in IgA Vasculitis
WRONG. GC have not been shown to prevent or treat renal involvement in IgA vasculitis. They are used for severe GI disease and arthralgias, but the renal course is independent. Nephrology follow-up and ACE inhibitors are key for renal management.
⚠ Trap 7: ANCA-Negative = Cannot Have ANCA Vasculitis
WRONG. 10–25% of GPA and MPA patients are ANCA-negative. Limited GPA (no renal involvement) is ANCA-negative in ~33% of cases. ANCA negativity does not exclude the diagnosis — clinical features, biopsy, and imaging matter most.
⚠ Trap 8: EGPA Mepolizumab Dose = Eosinophilic Asthma Dose
WRONG. Mepolizumab for eosinophilic asthma = 100 mg SQ monthly. Mepolizumab for EGPA = 300 mg SQ monthly (3× higher dose). This commonly appears on boards. Know both doses.
⚠ Trap 9: Negative Temporal Artery Biopsy Excludes GCA
WRONG. Sensitivity of temporal artery biopsy is ~70–90%. Up to 25% of GCA patients have negative biopsies due to skip lesions (hence need 3–5 cm with serial sectioning). Bilateral biopsy increases yield. Treat based on strong clinical suspicion even with negative biopsy.
⚠ Trap 10: Plasmapheresis (PLEX) Improves Renal Outcomes in ANCA Vasculitis
WRONG. The PEXIVAS trial (2020) showed that PLEX did NOT improve renal survival or overall survival in severe ANCA vasculitis. PLEX is no longer routinely recommended for ANCA-associated vasculitis with renal involvement or DAH (though some centers still use it for life-threatening DAH).
⚠ Trap 11: Worsening Asthma in a Patient with History of Sinus Disease = Asthma Exacerbation
THINK EGPA. Adult with long-standing asthma + worsening asthma + new sinusitis + peripheral eosinophilia + neuropathy (foot drop) → EGPA vasculitic phase. Do not treat as simple asthma flare. Check eosinophil count, ANCA, sural nerve biopsy.
⚠ Trap 12: PMR Can Be Treated with Low-Dose Prednisone Alone Indefinitely
CAUTION. 10–20% of isolated PMR patients will develop GCA over time. Counsel all PMR patients about GCA symptoms (new headache, jaw claudication, visual symptoms, scalp tenderness) and instruct them to report immediately. GCA in a PMR patient requires urgent high-dose GC, not the low-dose PMR regimen.
⚠ Trap 13: Drug-Induced Vasculitis Has the Same ANCA Pattern as Primary Vasculitis
NOT ALWAYS. Drug-induced ANCA vasculitis (PTU, hydralazine, minocycline) often has multiple ANCA specificities simultaneously (PR3 + MPO + anti-elastase + anti-lactoferrin). Primary GPA = predominantly PR3; Primary MPA = predominantly MPO. Multiple ANCA types → think drug-induced.
⚠ Trap 14: Testicular Pain in a Middle-Aged Man = Epididymo-Orchitis or Torsion
ALSO THINK PAN. Orchitis in the right clinical setting (constitutional symptoms, mononeuritis multiplex, livedo, renovascular HTN, ANCA-negative) is a classic and relatively specific manifestation of PAN. Don't forget to image the testes and consider angiography.
⚠ Trap 15: Methotrexate Can Be Used in Renal Insufficiency
WRONG. Methotrexate is renally eliminated. It is contraindicated in renal insufficiency — accumulation causes severe toxicity (bone marrow suppression, mucositis, hepatotoxicity). In GPA/MPA patients with renal involvement, use azathioprine or mycophenolate for maintenance instead.
