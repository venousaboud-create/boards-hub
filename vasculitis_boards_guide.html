§ 01

Definition & Classification

Core Definition
Vasculitis is a clinicopathologic process characterized by inflammation of and damage to blood vessels. The vessel lumen is usually compromised → ischemia of supplied tissues. Any type, size, and location of blood vessel may be involved. May be primary or secondary; confined to one organ or multisystem.

Primary vs. Secondary Vasculitis Syndromes

Primary Vasculitis Syndromes

  • Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (GPA) — Wegener's
  • Microscopic Polyangiitis (MPA)
  • Eosinophilic GPA (EGPA) — Churg-Strauss
  • IgA Vasculitis — Henoch-Schönlein
  • Cryoglobulinemic Vasculitis
  • Polyarteritis Nodosa (PAN)
  • Kawasaki Disease
  • Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA)
  • Takayasu Arteritis
  • Behçet's Disease
  • Cogan's Syndrome
  • Single-organ vasculitis (cutaneous leukocytoclastic angiitis, cutaneous arteritis, primary CNS vasculitis, isolated aortitis)

Secondary Vasculitis Syndromes

  • Drug-induced vasculitis
  • HCV-associated cryoglobulinemic vasculitis
  • HBV-associated vasculitis
  • Cancer-associated vasculitis
  • Lupus vasculitis
  • Rheumatoid vasculitis
  • Sarcoid vasculitis
  • Others
Key Point
Despite heterogeneity, these syndromes also share a considerable degree of overlap — classification guides treatment but individual patient factors always guide final decisions.

Vessel Size Classification (Concept Framework)

Small Vessel

§ 02

Pathophysiology & Pathogenesis

Most vasculitic syndromes are assumed to be mediated at least in part by immunopathogenic mechanisms in response to antigenic stimuli — but evidence is largely indirect. Genetic predisposition, environmental exposures, and regulatory immune mechanisms all contribute. Three major hypothesized mechanisms:

Mechanism 1: Pathogenic Immune-Complex Formation

Pathophysiology
Antigen–antibody complexes form in antigen excess → deposited in vessel walls (permeability increased by vasoactive amines: histamine, bradykinin, leukotrienes from platelets/mast cells via IgE) → activate complement (C5a strongly chemotactic for neutrophils) → neutrophils infiltrate vessel wall, phagocytose complexes, release intracytoplasmic enzymes → vessel wall damage → lumen compromise → ischemia.

Associated Diseases

  • IgA vasculitis (Henoch-Schönlein)
  • Lupus vasculitis
  • Serum sickness and cutaneous vasculitis syndromes
  • HCV-associated cryoglobulinemic vasculitis
  • HBV-associated vasculitis

Evidence & Caveats

  • First and most widely accepted mechanism
  • Causal role NOT clearly established in most syndromes
  • Many patients with active vasculitis do NOT have demonstrable circulating or deposited immune complexes
  • HBV antigen identified in complexes in PAN-like vasculitis
  • HCV virions + antigen-antibody complexes identified in cryoglobulinemic vasculitis

Mechanism 2: Antineutrophil Cytoplasmic Antibodies (ANCA)

ANCA Types
cANCA (cytoplasmic): diffuse granular cytoplasmic staining by immunofluorescence → antigen = Proteinase-3 (PR3), a 29-kDa neutral serine proteinase in neutrophil azurophilic granules. >90% of active GPA patients have anti-PR3.

pANCA (perinuclear): perinuclear/nuclear staining → major antigen = myeloperoxidase (MPO). Only anti-MPO convincingly associated with vasculitis. Other targets (elastase, cathepsin G, lactoferrin, lysozyme, BPI) can produce pANCA pattern but NOT vasculitis.
Mechanism of Pathogenesis
At rest: PR3 and MPO reside in azurophilic granules — inaccessible to serum antibodies. When neutrophils/monocytes are primed by TNF-α, IL-1, or alternative complement cascade → PR3/MPO translocate to cell membrane → interact with extracellular ANCA → neutrophils degranulate + produce reactive oxygen species → tissue damage. ANCA-activated neutrophils can adhere to and kill endothelial cells in vitro. Monocyte activation → releases pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1, IL-8).

ANCA-Associated Diseases

  • GPA — mainly anti-PR3 (cANCA)
  • MPA — mainly anti-MPO (pANCA)
  • EGPA (Churg-Strauss) — anti-MPO in ~48%
  • Isolated necrotizing crescentic GN

Arguments AGAINST Primary ANCA Role

  • Active GPA can occur in the absence of ANCA
  • Absolute antibody titers do NOT correlate well with disease activity
  • Patients in remission may have high ANCA levels for years
  • pANCA NOT due to anti-MPO → associated with non-vasculitic entities (IBD, CTD, endocarditis, cystic fibrosis)
Important Limitation of ANCA
These three diseases share ANCA positivity and are called "ANCA-associated vasculitis" — but they possess unique clinical phenotypes and must continue to be viewed as separate entities.

Mechanism 3: Pathogenic T Lymphocyte Responses & Granuloma Formation

Pathophysiology
Granulomatous vasculitis evidence for cell-mediated immune injury. Vascular endothelial cells express HLA class II molecules after cytokine activation (IFN-γ) → interact with CD4+ T lymphocytes (like antigen-presenting macrophages). Endothelial cells secrete IL-1 → activates T lymphocytes → in situ immunologic processes. IL-1 and TNF-α induce ELAM-1 and VCAM-1 → enhances leukocyte-endothelial adhesion.

Associated Diseases

  • Giant cell arteritis
  • Takayasu arteritis
  • GPA
  • EGPA (Churg-Strauss)

GPA Cytokine Pattern

  • Peripheral blood mononuclear cells: ↑IFN-γ, NOT IL-4/IL-5/IL-10
  • ↑TNF-α from PBMCs and CD4+ T cells
  • ↑IL-12 from monocytes
  • Result: unbalanced TH1-type T-cell cytokine pattern — pathogenic and therapeutic implications
§ 03

Approach to Diagnosis & General Treatment Principles

When to Suspect Vasculitis

Consider vasculitis in any patient with an unexplained systemic illness. Clinical features that should specifically raise suspicion:

  • Palpable purpura
  • Pulmonary infiltrates + microscopic hematuria
  • Chronic sinusitis
  • Mononeuritis multiplex
  • Unexplained ischemic events
  • Glomerulonephritis with evidence of multisystem disease

Step 1: Rule Out Mimics First

Critical First Step
Especially important to exclude infectious diseases with overlapping features, particularly if patient is deteriorating rapidly and empirical immunosuppression is being considered.

Biopsy Rule
Definitive diagnosis = biopsy of involved tissue. "Blind" biopsies of organs with no subjective/objective evidence of involvement have very low yield — avoid. When PAN, Takayasu, or CNS vasculitis are suspected, arteriogram of organs with suspected involvement should be performed.

General Treatment Principles

  • Remove offending antigen if identified
  • Treat underlying disease if secondary vasculitis
  • Decisions based on published literature supporting efficacy for that specific vasculitic disease
  • Weigh risk-versus-benefit carefully — treatment toxicity can be substantial
  • Immediately institute glucocorticoids ± immunosuppression in diseases with irreversible organ system dysfunction and high morbidity/mortality
  • Avoid aggressive therapy for manifestations that rarely cause irreversible damage (e.g., isolated idiopathic cutaneous vasculitis)
  • Continually attempt to taper glucocorticoids and discontinue when possible
  • Most vasculitides carry relapse potential → treatment planning must include relapse risk assessment
Cyclophosphamide Safety

Take all at once in the morning with large fluid intake throughout day → reduces bladder injury risk. Monitor for bladder cancer indefinitely.

Blood Count Monitoring

Maintain WBC >3000/μL and neutrophils >1500/μL. CBC every 1–2 weeks while on cyclophosphamide. MTX/AZA/MMF: CBC q1–2 weeks × first 1–2 months then monthly.

Methotrexate Pearls

Add folic acid 1 mg/day or folinic acid 5–10 mg/week (24h after MTX). Contraindicated in renal insufficiency. Check TPMT before AZA.

Rituximab Precautions

Screen for HBV reactivation before use. Administer in skilled infusion center (infusion reactions). Premedications can lessen reactions. Monitor immunoglobulins.

Infection Prevention

Pneumocystis jirovecii and fungi can occur even with normal WBC on GC. GPA/MPA on induction (GC + another immunosuppressive) → TMP-SMX prophylaxis. Continue through maintenance, especially with rituximab or persistent lymphopenia.

§ 05

Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (GPA)

Core Definition
Distinct clinicopathologic entity characterized by granulomatous vasculitis of the upper AND lower respiratory tracts together with glomerulonephritis. Variable degrees of disseminated vasculitis involving small arteries and veins may also occur.

Epidemiology

Prevalence

3 per 100,000

Sex Ratio

M:F = 1:1

Age of Onset

Mean 40–60 years; 15% are <19 years; extremely rare before adolescence

Race

Extremely rare in Blacks compared to Whites

Pathology & Pathogenesis

  • Histopathologic hallmarks: necrotizing vasculitis of small arteries and veins + granuloma formation (intravascular or extravascular)
  • Lung: multiple bilateral nodular or cavitary infiltrates → biopsy reveals necrotizing granulomatous vasculitis
  • Upper airway: inflammation, necrosis, granuloma formation ± vasculitis
  • Renal: earliest = focal segmental glomerulitis → can evolve to rapidly progressive crescentic GN; granuloma rarely seen on renal biopsy
  • Pauci-immune glomerulonephritis — NO immune complex deposition in the renal lesion (distinguishes GPA from immune complex-mediated GN)
  • Immunopathogenesis: unclear; suggests aberrant cell-mediated immune response to exogenous or endogenous antigen entering through or residing in upper airway
  • Staphylococcus aureus nasal carriage → associated with higher relapse rate (but no evidence of direct pathogenic role)
  • TH1-type cytokine pattern: ↑IFN-γ, ↑TNF-α, ↑IL-12; NOT IL-4/IL-5/IL-10

Clinical Manifestations (Table 375-5, NIH data: n=158)

Renal Disease Is the Key Prognostic Factor
Renal disease dominates the clinical picture and, if untreated, accounts directly or indirectly for most mortality. Even when initially mild (proteinuria, hematuria, RBC casts), once clinically detectable renal functional impairment occurs, rapidly progressive renal failure usually ensues.
Increased VTE Risk
Patients with GPA have an increased incidence of venous thrombotic events. While routine anticoagulation is not recommended, maintain heightened awareness for DVT/PE clinical features.

Laboratory Findings

  • ↑ESR and/or CRP, anemia, leukocytosis, mild hypergammaglobulinemia, thrombocytosis (acute-phase reactant)
  • Anti-PR3 ANCA (cANCA): positive in ~90% of active GPA
  • Sensitivity drops to ~60–70% in the absence of active disease (remission)
  • Small percentage have anti-MPO instead of anti-PR3; up to 20% may lack ANCA entirely
ANCA Predictive Value
Active glomerulonephritis: positive ANCA predictive value = 90%
Sinus/lung disease alone (no renal): predictive value drops to 30–60% → tissue biopsy necessary to rule out infection or malignancy. Also biopsy if clinically inconsistent features or ANCA absent.

Diagnosis & Differential Diagnosis

  • Established by demonstration of necrotizing granulomatous vasculitis on biopsy in patient with compatible clinical features
  • Pulmonary tissue offers highest diagnostic yield — almost invariably reveals granulomatous vasculitis
  • Upper airway biopsy: acute/chronic inflammation; can show granulomas ± vasculitis
  • Renal biopsy: confirms pauci-immune GN
Critical Differentials
Midline destructive diseases: Extreme tissue destruction localized to midline upper airway; erosion through skin of face common (extremely rare in GPA); primary vasculitis NOT seen → most important: extranodal NK/T-cell lymphoma (nasal type) — EBV+, polymorphous atypical lymphoid cells with NK cell immunophenotype → treat with irradiation (NEVER irradiate GPA lesions).

Cocaine-induced midline destructive disease: ANCA that target human neutrophil elastase (HNE) can mimic GPA; levamisole adulteration → cutaneous infarction + serologic changes mimicking vasculitis; granulocytopenia common in levamisole-induced disease (NOT seen in GPA).

Lymphomatoid granulomatosis: EBV+ B-cell proliferation + exuberant T-cell reaction; lung/skin/CNS/kidney; angioinvasive perivascular infiltration of atypical mononuclear cells (NOT inflammatory vasculitis); up to 50% develop true malignant lymphoma.

Treatment — Historical Context

Impact of Cyclophosphamide
Pre-therapy: universally fatal within months. GC alone: some symptomatic improvement, little effect on course. Addition of cyclophosphamide: improvement in >90%, complete remission in 75%, 5-year patient survival in >80%. Despite this, 50–70% of remissions later relapse.

Treatment — Two Phases: Induction + Maintenance

Relapse Determination Rule
Relapse must be based on objective evidence of disease activity — rule out infection, medication toxicity, chronic disease sequelae. Many patients in remission remain ANCA positive for years. A rise in ANCA alone should NOT lead to reinstitution of immunosuppressive therapy (only 43% relapsed within 1 year of ↑ANCA).

Remission Induction of Severe Disease

Severe disease = life- or organ-threatening manifestations. Active GN is always severe even when renal function is normal. Alveolar hemorrhage = severe; nodular disease without respiratory compromise = nonsevere.

Cyclophosphamide Induction
  • Standard: 2 mg/kg/day PO; dose-reduce in renal insufficiency
  • IV option: 15 mg/kg, 3 infusions q2 weeks then q3 weeks — similar remission rate but higher relapse rate
  • Switch after 3–6 months to maintenance agent
  • Daily preferred in rapidly progressive GN (Cr >4 mg/dL) or alveolar hemorrhage with mechanical ventilation
Rituximab Induction
  • 375 mg/m² IV once weekly × 4 weeks + GC
  • As effective as CYC for inducing remission in two RCTs
  • Superior to CYC in relapsing disease
  • No bladder toxicity or infertility risk
  • Rate of adverse events similar to CYC
  • Do NOT combine CYC + RTX for induction — no added efficacy; additive toxicity (especially infection)
Glucocorticoid Strategy
  • Historically: prednisone 1 mg/kg/day × 1 month then taper
  • Reduced-dose GC RCT: noninferior to standard-dose + lower rate of serious infection
  • Life-threatening disease: methylprednisolone 1000 mg/day × 3 days
Avacopan (C5a Receptor Inhibitor)
  • RCT: sustained remission at 52 weeks higher with avacopan vs. prednisone
  • Allows rapid glucocorticoid withdrawal in severe active disease
  • GC exposure markedly less in avacopan group
  • No data yet in nonsevere disease
Plasma Exchange in GPA/MPA
RCT finding: Plasma exchange provides NO added benefit in reducing composite outcome of ESRD or death.
Alveolar hemorrhage: Retrospective study also showed no benefit.
Exception — USE plasma exchange: GPA/MPA patients who are also anti-GBM antibody positive.
High Cr >5.7 mg/dL + active GN: Meta-analysis shows improved renal function but increased risk of serious infection.
If plasma exchange + rituximab: must be separated by ≥48 hours to avoid RTX removal.

Remission Induction + Maintenance of Nonsevere Disease

  • Methotrexate or mycophenolate mofetil + glucocorticoids (often lower dose) → induce then maintain remission
  • Rituximab can be used (weigh risks/benefits)
  • Cyclophosphamide is rarely, if ever, justified for nonsevere GPA
  • No data for avacopan in nonsevere disease

Failed Biologic Agents (Do Not Use)

  • Etanercept (TNF inhibitor): did NOT sustain remission — do not use in GPA
  • Belimumab (anti-B lymphocyte stimulator): no added benefit in reducing relapse as adjunctive to AZA
  • Abatacept (CTLA4-Ig): RCT in nonsevere relapsing GPA — did NOT reduce relapse, worsening, or failure to achieve remission

Organ-Specific Treatment Pearls

Sinonasal Disease

TMP-SMX may benefit isolated sinonasal GPA. NEVER use TMP-SMX alone to treat active GPA involving other organs. Local care: moisturization + humidification.

Subglottic Stenosis

Often scars. Responds best to nonmedical intervention: dilation + glucocorticoid injection. Does NOT require systemic immunosuppression.

§ 06

Microscopic Polyangiitis (MPA)

Core Definition (Chapel Hill Consensus 1992)
Necrotizing vasculitis with few or no immune complexes affecting small vessels (capillaries, venules, arterioles). Glomerulonephritis very common. Pulmonary capillaritis often occurs. Absence of granulomatous inflammation differentiates from GPA.

Epidemiology & Pathology

Incidence

3–5 per 100,000

Age of Onset

Mean ~57 years; males slightly more affected

Historical Note

First described by Davson in 1948 in GN patients with PAN

  • Predilection to involve capillaries and venules (also small- and medium-sized arteries)
  • Pauci-immune — immunohistochemical staining reveals paucity of immunoglobulin deposition → immune complex formation does NOT play a pathogenic role
  • Renal lesion histologically identical to GPA
  • Highly associated with ANCA (may play role in pathogenesis)

Clinical, Labs & Diagnosis

Key Clinical Features

  • Onset may be gradual (fever, weight loss, musculoskeletal pain) or acute
  • Glomerulonephritis ≥79% of patients → can be rapidly progressive → renal failure
  • Alveolar hemorrhage: 12% (hemoptysis first symptom)
  • Mononeuritis multiplex; GI and cutaneous vasculitis
  • Upper airway disease and pulmonary nodules NOT typical → if present, suggests GPA
  • ↑ESR/CRP, anemia, leukocytosis, thrombocytosis
  • ANCA in 75%, predominantly anti-MPO (pANCA)

Diagnosis & Treatment

  • Diagnosis: histologic evidence of vasculitis or pauci-immune GN + compatible clinical features of multisystem disease
  • Tissue biopsy should be pursued even in strongly ANCA-associated cases if clinical picture is not fully compatible
  • Treatment: identical to GPA
  • 5-year survival with treatment: 74%
  • Disease-related mortality: alveolar hemorrhage, GI/cardiac/renal disease
  • Relapse observed in at least 34% of patients
§ 07

Eosinophilic Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (EGPA / Churg-Strauss)

Core Definition (Churg & Strauss, 1951)
Characterized by asthma + peripheral and tissue eosinophilia + extravascular granuloma formation + vasculitis of multiple organ systems.

Epidemiology

Incidence

1–3 per million/year

Age of Onset

Mean 48 years; any age (except possibly infants)

Sex

F:M = 1.2:1

ANCA

~48% ANCA positive (usually anti-MPO)

Pathology & Pathogenesis

  • Necrotizing vasculitis: small- and medium-sized muscular arteries, capillaries, veins, venules
  • Characteristic histopathologic feature: granuloma (in tissues or vessel walls) + eosinophilic infiltration
  • Any organ can be involved; lung predominant; also skin, cardiovascular, kidney, PNS, GI
  • Strong association with asthma + eosinophilia + granuloma + vasculitis → aberrant immunologic phenomena

Clinical & Laboratory Manifestations

Key Clinical Features

  • Asthmatic attacks + pulmonary infiltrates — dominate the clinical picture
  • Mononeuritis multiplex: 2nd most common — up to 72%
  • Allergic rhinitis + sinusitis: up to 61% (often early in course)
  • Cardiac disease: ~14% (myocarditis, pericarditis, endocarditis, coronary vasculitis) → most common cause of death (39% of mortality)
  • Skin lesions: ~51% (purpura + cutaneous/subcutaneous nodules)
  • Renal disease: less common than GPA/MPA
  • Nonspecific: fever, malaise, anorexia, weight loss

Laboratory Findings

  • Striking eosinophilia — virtually all patients; >1000 cells/μL in >80%
  • ↑ESR and/or CRP, fibrinogen, α₂-globulins in 81%
  • ANCA (usually anti-MPO): ~48%
  • Other labs reflect organ system involved
Diagnosis Criteria
Patient must have: asthma + peripheral blood eosinophilia + clinical features consistent with vasculitis. Histologic confirmation can be challenging because pathognomonic features often do NOT occur simultaneously.

Treatment

Prognosis Without Treatment

5-year survival: 25%. With treatment: 78-month actuarial survival 72%. Myocardial involvement most frequent cause of death.

First Critical Step

Echocardiography in ALL newly diagnosed patients — cardiac involvement may influence therapeutic decisions.

§ 08

Polyarteritis Nodosa (PAN)

Core Definition (Kussmaul & Maier, 1866)
Multisystem, necrotizing vasculitis of small- and medium-sized muscular arteries characteristically involving renal and visceral arteries.
Does NOT involve pulmonary arteries (bronchial vessels may be involved).
No glomerulonephritis, no granuloma, no significant eosinophilia, no venule involvement (if present → MPA).

Pathology

  • Vascular lesion: segmental necrotizing inflammation of small- and medium-sized muscular arteries; tends to involve bifurcations and branchings
  • May spread circumferentially to involve adjacent veins — but NOT venules
  • Acute: PMNs infiltrate all vessel wall layers → intimal proliferation + degeneration
  • Subacute/chronic: mononuclear cell infiltration → fibrinoid necrosis → thrombosis → infarction
  • Healing: collagen deposition → further lumen occlusion
  • Aneurysmal dilations up to 1 cm in size along involved arteries are characteristic
  • Kidney: arteritis without glomerulonephritis; glomerulosclerosis if significant HTN
Special Associations
HBV-associated PAN: circulating immune complexes (HBV Ag + IgM + complement) in vessel walls → suggests immunologic pathogenesis. First-line = antiviral therapy + concurrent immunosuppression for severe vasculitis, then withdraw immunosuppression.
HCV-associated PAN: similar approach.
Hairy cell leukemia: can be associated with PAN (mechanism unclear).
DADA2 (adenosine deaminase type 2 deficiency): PAN-like vasculitis → TNF inhibitors (not standard PAN treatment); usually childhood onset, variable vascular pathology.

Laboratory Findings
No diagnostic serologic tests for PAN. Leukocytosis >75% (neutrophil predominance). Eosinophilia RARE — if present at high levels → suggests EGPA instead. ↑ESR/CRP (almost always). Anemia of chronic disease. Hypergammaglobulinemia. Screen all for HBV and HCV. ANCA rarely found.

Diagnosis

  • Biopsy of symptomatic organs (nodular skin lesions, painful testes, nerve/muscle) → highest diagnostic yield
  • If no accessible tissue: catheter-directed dye arteriogram (MR/CT arteriography does NOT have sufficient resolution) → aneurysms of small- and medium-sized arteries in renal, hepatic, visceral vasculature is sufficient for diagnosis
  • Aneurysms are NOT pathognomonic; findings may also be limited to stenotic segments and obliteration of vessels

Treatment

Untreated Prognosis

5-year survival: 10–20%. Death: GI complications (bowel infarction/perforation) and cardiovascular causes; hypertension compounds dysfunction in kidneys/heart/CNS.

Treatment Response

Prednisone + cyclophosphamide → significantly improves survival. Less severe cases: glucocorticoids alone. Treat hypertension to lessen vascular complications.

Relapse

Estimated 10–20% relapse after remission.

§ 09

Giant Cell Arteritis & Polymyalgia Rheumatica

Key Relationship
GCA and PMR represent differing clinical spectrums of a single disease process. PMR is seen in 40–50% of GCA patients. 10–20% of isolated PMR patients later develop GCA. Always perform careful history/exam at initial and subsequent visits in PMR patients to look for GCA features.

Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA)

Definition
Inflammation of medium- and large-sized arteries. Characteristically involves one or more branches of the carotid artery, particularly the temporal artery. Systemic disease — can involve arteries in multiple locations, particularly the aorta and its main branches.
Age

Almost exclusively >50 years

Sex/Race

More common in women; rare in Blacks

Incidence

6.9–32.8/100,000 in ≥50 years; highest in Scandinavia and regions with large Scandinavian populations

Genetics

HLA-DR4 association; HLA-DRB1*04 variants; familial aggregation

PMR Incidence

58.7/100,000 in ≥50 years (Olmsted County, MN)

Pathology & Pathogenesis

  • Panarteritis with inflammatory mononuclear cell infiltrates + frequent giant cell formation
  • Proliferation of intima + fragmentation of internal elastic lamina
  • Antigen-driven disease: activated T lymphocytes, macrophages, and dendritic cells play critical roles
  • T-cell receptor analysis: restricted clonal expansion → antigen residing in arterial wall
  • CD4+ T cell subsets in lesions: IFN-γ–producing TH1 cells + IL-17–producing TH17 cells; TH17 may have differing (less) responsiveness to glucocorticoids
  • CD4+ T cells enter through vasa vasorum → activate → orchestrate macrophage differentiation

Clinical — 4 Phenotypic Manifestations

1. Cranial Arteritis
  • Headache (predominant) + tender/thickened/nodular temporal artery (pulsating early, may become occluded)
  • Scalp pain, jaw claudication, tongue claudication
  • Ischemic optic neuropathy → sudden blindness — most dreaded complication (particularly in untreated patients)
  • Most patients have head/eye complaints BEFORE visual loss → early treatment lessens risk
  • Other cranial ischemic complications: strokes, scalp/tongue infarction
2. Polymyalgia Rheumatica
  • Stiffness, aching, pain in neck/shoulder/lower back/hip/thigh muscles
3. Large-Vessel Disease (up to 1/3)
  • Can be primary presentation or emerge later
  • Subclavian artery stenosis → arm claudication
  • Aortic aneurysms (thoracic > abdominal) → risk of rupture/dissection
  • GCA patients are 18× more likely to develop thoracic aortic aneurysms than general population
4. Systemic Inflammation
  • Malaise, fatigue, anorexia, weight loss, sweats
  • ↑ESR/CRP (most patients), normochromic/slightly hypochromic anemia
  • ↑Alkaline phosphatase, ↑IgG and complement
IL-6 Inhibitor Monitoring Caveat
Therapeutic IL-6 blockade (tocilizumab) prevents hepatic CRP production and normalizes ESR. These values will normalize in treated patients, eliminating the ability to use CRP and ESR to monitor disease activity or infection in these patients.

Diagnosis of GCA

  • Clinical: fever + anemia + high ESR/CRP ± PMR in patient >50 years
  • Temporal artery biopsy (3–5 cm segment, serial sectioning) — not positive in all patients due to patchy findings
  • Do NOT delay therapy pending biopsy — temporal artery biopsies may still show vasculitis after ~14 days of GC therapy
  • Ultrasound (temporal ± axillary arteries): noncompressible "halo sign" supports diagnosis
  • Large-vessel disease: MRI or CT arteriography (PET increasingly investigated but role in diagnosis/monitoring unclear)
  • Dramatic clinical response to glucocorticoid trial further supports diagnosis

Treatment of GCA

Treatment Goals
Reduce symptoms and, most importantly, prevent visual loss. Same approach for cranial and large-vessel disease.

ESR and/or CRP serves as indicator of inflammatory disease activity → guide tapering schedule. Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)

Diagnosis

Clinical diagnosis: typical symptoms (stiffness/aching/pain in hip and shoulder girdle muscles) + ↑ESR/CRP + absence of GCA features + prompt response to low-dose prednisone. RF and anti-CCP: must be negative. CK levels: not elevated (distinguishes from myositis). Can mimic RA with peripheral arthritis — if worsening arthritis pattern, consider seronegative RA or other inflammatory arthropathy.

Treatment

  • Prednisone 10–20 mg/day → most patients respond promptly
  • Majority recur during prednisone taper
  • Sarilumab (anti-IL-6R) 200 mg SC q2wk + 14-week GC taper: higher sustained remission vs. GC alone in GC-resistant PMR (relapsed on ≥7.5 mg/day) — restrict to those with inadequate GC response or GC-taper relapse
  • MTX: reduced prednisone dose by only ~1 mg on average; did NOT decrease GC side effects
  • Infliximab: no benefit in relapse or GC requirements

Takayasu Arteritis

Core Definition
Inflammatory disease of large-sized arteries with a strong predilection for the aortic arch and its branches. Pulmonary artery may also be involved.

Epidemiology & Pathology

Incidence

1.2–2.6 cases per million/year

Demographics

Most prevalent in adolescent girls and young women; more common in Asia but NOT racially or geographically restricted

Disease Course

Often chronic and relapsing; spontaneous remissions possible

Pathology

  • Panarteritis with inflammatory mononuclear cell infiltrates ± giant cells
  • Marked intimal proliferation + fibrosis, scarring + vascularization of media, disruption of elastic lamina
  • Vasa vasorum frequently involved
  • Narrowing of lumen ± thrombosis; aneurysms from vessel wall weakening
  • Involvement more marked at origin of arteries than distally

Clinical & Diagnosis

  • Generalized symptoms first: malaise, fever, night sweats, arthralgias, anorexia, weight loss (may precede vessel involvement by months)
  • Then vascular compromise: absent pulses (especially subclavian), BP discrepancy between arms, arterial bruits
  • HTN in 32–93% → contributes to renal, cardiac, and cerebral injury
  • Labs: ↑ESR/CRP, mild anemia, ↑immunoglobulin levels
Diagnostic Clue
Suspect strongly in a young woman with decreased/absent peripheral pulses, BP discrepancy between arms, or arterial bruits. Confirmed by characteristic arteriographic pattern: irregular vessel walls, stenosis, post-stenotic dilation, aneurysm formation, occlusion, ↑collateral circulation. Complete MRI or CT arteriography of aorta and major branches.
IgG4-Related Disease Mimic
IgG4-related disease can cause aortitis and periaortitis. Histologically differentiated by: dense lymphoplasmacytic infiltrate rich in IgG4+ plasma cells + storiform pattern of fibrosis + obliterative phlebitis.

Treatment

Outcomes

North American reports: overall survival ≥94%. Other studies: 5-year mortality 0–35%. Death from CHF, CVA, MI, aneurysm rupture, renal failure.

Best Outcomes

Combination of GC for acute symptoms + aggressive surgical/arterioplastic approach to stenosed vessels: ↓stroke risk, corrects HTN from renal artery stenosis, ↑blood flow. Surgical correction only when vascular inflammation is well-controlled.

IgA Vasculitis (Henoch-Schönlein)

Core Definition
Small-vessel vasculitis characterized by: palpable purpura (buttocks + lower extremities) + arthralgias + GI signs/symptoms + glomerulonephritis.

Epidemiology & Pathogenesis

Age

Usually children 4–7 years; can occur in infants and adults

Epidemiology

M:F = 1.5:1; spring peak incidence; not rare (5–24 admissions/year at pediatric hospital)

Pathogenesis

Immune complex deposition; IgA = antibody class most common in complexes and renal biopsies. Inciting antigens: URI, drugs, foods, insect bites, immunizations

Clinical Manifestations

Pediatric Presentation

  • Palpable purpura: virtually all patients
  • Polyarthralgias (without frank arthritis): most
  • GI involvement 70%: colicky abdominal pain + N/V/D/constipation + blood/mucus per rectum; bowel intussusception may occur
  • Renal 10–50%: mild GN (proteinuria + hematuria + RBC casts) → usually resolves spontaneously; progressive GN rare

Adult Presentation (Differences)

  • Presenting symptoms most frequently: skin + joints
  • Initial GI complaints less common
  • Renal disease: may be more frequent and severe (not consistent finding) but more insidious → requires close follow-up
  • Myocardial involvement: rare in children, can occur in adults
  • Labs: mild leukocytosis, normal platelets, occasional eosinophilia, normal complement, IgA elevated in ~50%

Diagnosis

  • Clinical diagnosis
  • Skin biopsy: leukocytoclastic vasculitis with IgA + C3 deposition by immunofluorescence
  • Renal biopsy: rarely needed for diagnosis but may provide prognostic information

Treatment & Prognosis

Prognosis: Excellent
Mortality exceedingly rare. 1–5% of children progress to ESRD (may be higher in adults). Most recover completely; some do not require therapy. Recurrences in 10–40%.
Prednisone Limitations in IgA Vasculitis
Prednisone 1 mg/kg/day tapered useful for: tissue edema, arthralgias, abdominal pain.
NOT proved beneficial for skin or renal disease. Does NOT shorten duration of active disease or lessen recurrence.
Rapidly progressive GN: anecdotal reports of benefit from GC + additional immunosuppressive agent.
§ 12

Cryoglobulinemic Vasculitis

Core Definition
Cryoglobulins = cold-precipitable mono/polyclonal immunoglobulins. Cryoglobulinemia → systemic vasculitis: palpable purpura + arthralgias + weakness + neuropathy + glomerulonephritis. Most common association: hepatitis C virus (HCV).

Epidemiology, Pathology & Pathogenesis

  • Incidence not established; ~5% of chronic HCV → cryoglobulinemic vasculitis
  • Also associated with: multiple myeloma, lymphoproliferative disorders, CTD, infection, liver disease; can be idiopathic
  • Skin biopsy: inflammatory infiltrate around vessel walls + fibrinoid necrosis + endothelial hyperplasia + hemorrhage; Ig + complement deposition
  • Membranoproliferative GN: 80% of all renal lesions
  • Abnormalities of uninvolved skin: basement membrane alterations + deposits in vessel walls
HCV Pathogenic Mechanism
Aberrant immune response to HCV → immune complexes consisting of: HCV antigens + polyclonal HCV-specific IgG + monoclonal IgM rheumatoid factor → deposited in vessel walls → inflammatory cascade → cryoglobulinemic vasculitis. Supported by: high frequency of HCV infection, HCV RNA + anti-HCV Ab in cryoprecipitates, HCV antigens in vasculitic skin lesions, effectiveness of antiviral therapy.

Clinical, Labs & Treatment

Clinical & Lab

  • Cutaneous vasculitis + arthritis + peripheral neuropathy + GN (most common)
  • Renal disease: 10–30%
  • Life-threatening RPGN/CNS/GI/cardiac: infrequent
  • Circulating cryoprecipitates (fundamental finding)
  • Rheumatoid factor almost always present — useful clue when cryoglobulins not detected
  • Hypocomplementemia: 90%
  • ↑ESR/CRP, anemia; screen all for HCV (antibodies + RNA)

Treatment & Prognosis

  • Acute mortality uncommon; GN poor prognosis: 15% → ESRD; 40% → fatal CV disease/infection/liver failure
  • HCV-associated: antiviral therapy = first-line; viral clearance → objective vasculitis improvement + ↓cryoglobulins, IgM, RF
  • GC: transient improvement only; complete response in only 7%
  • Rituximab: RCT evidence of benefit → use with antiviral therapy or alone if relapsed through/intolerant to/contraindicated to antivirals
  • Plasma exchange + cytotoxic agents: anecdotal only; significant risks; not confirmed
§ 13

Other Vasculitis Syndromes

Kawasaki Disease

Key Features
Acute, febrile, multisystem disease of children. 80% <5 years; peak ≤2 years. Nonsuppurative cervical adenitis + skin/mucous membrane changes (edema, conjunctival congestion, erythema oral cavity/lips/palms, fingertip desquamation).

Cardiac Complications

  • Coronary artery aneurysms: ~25%
  • Case-fatality rate: 0.5–2.8%
  • Complications typically in 3rd–4th week (convalescent stage)
  • Path: intimal proliferation + mononuclear infiltration; beadlike aneurysms + thromboses
  • Also: pericarditis, myocarditis, MI, cardiomegaly
  • Vasculitis of coronary arteries in almost all fatal autopsied cases

Treatment

  • IV γ-globulin 2 g/kg as single infusion over 10h + aspirin 100 mg/kg/day × 14 days, then 3–5 mg/kg/day × several weeks → reduces coronary artery abnormalities
  • Surgery for giant coronary artery aneurysms: thromboendarterectomy, thrombus clearing, aneurysmal reconstruction, CABG
  • Test for SARS-CoV-2 in any patient with Kawasaki-like presentation (MIS-C: also GI symptoms, myocarditis, neurocognitive symptoms, shock)

Idiopathic Cutaneous Vasculitis

Most commonly encountered vasculitis in clinical practice. >70% = part of primary systemic vasculitis or secondary vasculitis (inciting agent/underlying disease). Only 30% = truly idiopathic.

Clinical Features

  • Hallmark: palpable purpura (also macules, papules, vesicles, bullae, subcutaneous nodules, ulcers, recurrent urticaria)
  • Pruritic or painful (burning/stinging sensation)
  • Lower extremities (ambulatory) or sacral (bedridden) — hydrostatic forces on postcapillary venules
  • Edema; hyperpigmentation with recurrent lesions
  • Path: postcapillary venules most common; leukocytoclasis; RBC extravasation → purpura; cutaneous arteritis (slightly larger vessels) can also occur

Treatment

  • Remove antigenic stimulus; treat underlying disease
  • Self-limited: no therapy needed
  • Persistent, no systemic cause: dapsone, colchicine, hydroxychloroquine, NSAIDs (anecdotal)
  • GC often used (lowest effective prednisone, rapid taper)
  • Chronic cutaneous venule vasculitis: rarely responds dramatically; immunosuppressives only as last resort; MTX/AZA anecdotally
  • Cyclophosphamide: almost NEVER use (potential toxicity)

Primary CNS Vasculitis

  • Vasculitis restricted to CNS vessels; no systemic vasculitis; usually mononuclear infiltrates ± granuloma formation
  • Presents: headaches + altered mental function + focal neurologic defects; systemic symptoms generally absent
  • Diagnosis: abnormal MRI brain + abnormal LP + arteriographic abnormalities (beading) → confirmed by brain parenchyma + leptomeningeal biopsy
  • Key DDx: Reversible Cerebral Vasoconstrictive Syndrome (RCVS) — thunderclap headache + reversible arteriographic changes; also infection, atherosclerosis, emboli, CTD, sarcoidosis, malignancy, drugs
  • Prognosis: poor. Some reports: GC ± cyclophosphamide → clinical remissions
  • After remission: switch CYC to AZA or mycophenolate mofetil (good CNS penetration)

Single-Organ Vasculitis

Vasculitis in arteries or veins of any size in a single organ with no features of systemic vasculitis. Examples: isolated aortitis, testicular vasculitis, vasculitis of the breast, isolated cutaneous vasculitis, primary CNS vasculitis. Some patients may later develop more systemic disease. If affected organ entirely removed and no systemic evidence: close follow-up without immunosuppression.

Behçet's Disease & Cogan's Syndrome

Behçet's Disease

Recurrent episodes of oral and genital ulcers + iritis + cutaneous lesions. Underlying pathologic process = leukocytoclastic venulitis. Vessels of any size and any organ can be involved. Treated with apremilast (among others). Detailed coverage in Chap. 376.

Cogan's Syndrome

Interstitial keratitis + vestibuloauditory symptoms. May be associated with systemic vasculitis, particularly aortitis with involvement of the aortic valve. Initiation of treatment as early as possible after hearing loss onset → improves outcome. Glucocorticoids are the mainstay of treatment.

§ 14

Secondary Vasculitis

Drug-Induced Vasculitis

  • Usually presents as palpable purpura (generalized or lower extremities/dependent areas); also urticarial lesions, ulcers, hemorrhagic blisters
  • Signs limited to skin OR systemic (fever, malaise, polyarthralgias)
  • Classic drugs: allopurinol, thiazides, gold, sulfonamides, phenytoin, penicillin
ANCA-Associated Drug Vasculitis
Best evidence of causality: hydralazine and propylthiouracil (PTU). Clinical manifestations range from cutaneous lesions to GN and pulmonary hemorrhage. Outside of drug discontinuation → treat based on severity. Life-threatening small-vessel vasculitis → GC + cyclophosphamide (as in GPA); may taper more rapidly than primary vasculitis.

Serum Sickness & Serum Sickness–Like Reactions

  • Fever + urticaria + polyarthralgias + lymphadenopathy; onset 7–10 days after primary exposure or 2–4 days after secondary exposure to heterologous protein (classic) or nonprotein drug such as penicillin/sulfa (serum sickness-like)
  • Most manifestations NOT due to vasculitis; occasional patients have cutaneous venulitis → rarely progresses to systemic vasculitis

Vasculitis Associated with Other Underlying Diseases

Infectious
  • Rickettsias: invade endothelial cells of small blood vessels → vasculitis
  • Systemic fungi (histoplasmosis): inflammatory response around vessels may mimic primary vasculitis
  • Also: SBE, EBV, HIV and other infections
Malignancies
  • Leukemic, lymphoid, or reticuloendothelial neoplasms
  • Most common: leukocytoclastic venulitis confined to skin
  • Systemic vasculitis may occur
Connective Tissue Diseases
  • SLE, RA, inflammatory myositis, relapsing polychondritis, Sjögren's syndrome
  • Most common: small-vessel venulitis isolated to skin
  • Some → fulminant systemic necrotizing vasculitis
Other Associations
Secondary vasculitis also observed with: ulcerative colitis, congenital complement component deficiencies, sarcoidosis, primary biliary cirrhosis, α₁-antitrypsin deficiency, intestinal bypass surgery.

Polyangiitis Overlap Syndromes

Patients with clinicopathologic characteristics that do NOT fit a specific disease but have overlapping features of different vasculitides. Diagnostic/therapeutic considerations and prognosis depend on sites and severity of active vasculitis. If potential for irreversible damage to a major organ → treat as per GPA protocol.

§ 15

Exam Traps

Trap 01ANCA Rise Alone = Reinstitute Therapy?
A patient with GPA in clinical remission is found to have a rising anti-PR3 titer. The vignette implies you should increase immunosuppressive therapy. What is the correct action?
CORRECT: Do NOT reinstitute or increase immunosuppressive therapy based on ANCA rise alone. A large prospective study showed only 43% of patients relapsed within 1 year of an increase in ANCA levels. Relapse determination must be based on objective evidence of disease activity, after ruling out infection, medication toxicity, or chronic disease sequelae.
Trap 02GPA Renal Biopsy = Immune Complex Deposition?
A patient with GPA has a renal biopsy. The question asks what immune complex pattern is expected. The answer choices include various immune complex deposits. Which is correct?
CORRECT: GPA produces PAUCI-IMMUNE glomerulonephritis — immune complex deposition is NOT found in the renal lesion of GPA. This distinguishes it from immune complex-mediated GN (IgA nephropathy, lupus nephritis, post-infectious GN, etc.).
Trap 03Subglottic Stenosis in GPA — Systemic Immunosuppression?
A patient with GPA has developed subglottic stenosis causing significant airway obstruction. The question asks for optimal management. Systemic immunosuppression is one of the options.
CORRECT: Subglottic stenosis in GPA often scars and responds optimally to nonmedical intervention: dilation + glucocorticoid injection. Systemic immunosuppressive therapy is NOT the correct treatment for subglottic stenosis.
Trap 04PAN = Pulmonary Artery Involvement?
A patient with confirmed PAN develops new dyspnea. You diagnose pulmonary artery vasculitis. Is this consistent with PAN?
CORRECT: PAN does NOT involve pulmonary arteries (bronchial vessels may be involved, but this is uncommon). Pulmonary artery involvement should make you think of another diagnosis. Additionally, PAN does NOT cause GN, granulomas, or significant eosinophilia, and ANCA is rarely found.
Trap 05Temporal Artery Biopsy After Starting GC?
An elderly patient with fever, headache, jaw claudication, and markedly elevated ESR has suspected GCA. You want to perform temporal artery biopsy before starting treatment to avoid "masking" the diagnosis. Is this the correct approach?
CORRECT: Do NOT delay therapy pending biopsy. Temporal artery biopsies may still show vasculitis even after approximately 14 days of glucocorticoid therapy. The risk of visual loss in untreated GCA far outweighs the diagnostic benefit of delaying treatment.
Trap 06Monitoring GCA on Tocilizumab with ESR/CRP?
A GCA patient on tocilizumab has a normal ESR and CRP. Does this mean their disease is in remission?
CORRECT: You CANNOT use CRP or ESR to monitor disease activity or assess for infection in patients on tocilizumab (or sarilumab). These agents block IL-6 signaling, which prevents hepatic CRP production and normalizes ESR. These values will be normalized regardless of disease activity.
Trap 07EGPA — Echocardiography Optional?
A newly diagnosed EGPA patient presents primarily with asthma and mononeuritis multiplex. Is cardiac evaluation necessary?
CORRECT: Echocardiography must be performed in ALL newly diagnosed EGPA patients. Clinically recognizable heart disease occurs in ~14% and is the most important cause of mortality (responsible for 39% of patient deaths). Cardiac involvement influences therapeutic decisions.
Trap 08Mepolizumab for Life-Threatening EGPA?
A patient with EGPA presents with fulminant cardiac involvement. Should mepolizumab be initiated?
CORRECT: Mepolizumab is NOT for life-threatening EGPA. Patients with life-threatening EGPA were excluded from the mepolizumab RCT. Life-threatening/fulminant disease (especially cardiac involvement) requires daily cyclophosphamide + prednisone.
Trap 09Plasma Exchange in GPA/MPA for ESRD Prevention?
A patient with severe ANCA-associated vasculitis is being treated. Should plasma exchange be added to reduce risk of end-stage renal disease?
CORRECT: A large RCT showed plasma exchange provides NO added benefit in reducing the composite outcome of ESRD or death. Do NOT routinely add plasma exchange to the induction regimen. Exception: use plasma exchange in GPA/MPA patients who are also anti-GBM antibody positive.
Trap 10Cyclophosphamide + Rituximab Together for Induction?
For severe GPA induction, would combining cyclophosphamide AND rituximab together be more effective?
CORRECT: There is no evidence that using cyclophosphamide and rituximab together for induction is more efficacious. Combined use raises concern for additive toxicity, particularly infection. Choose one or the other based on individual patient factors.
Trap 11PMR — CK Elevated, RF Positive?
A patient with suspected PMR has elevated creatine kinase and positive rheumatoid factor. Is this consistent with PMR?
CORRECT: NO — this is NOT consistent with PMR. In PMR: CK levels are NOT elevated (distinguishes from inflammatory myositis) and RF and anti-CCP must be negative (distinguishes from RA). Positive RF/anti-CCP or elevated CK should prompt reconsideration of the diagnosis.
Trap 12Cryoglobulinemic Vasculitis — GC Complete Response?
A patient with HCV-associated cryoglobulinemic vasculitis is started on high-dose glucocorticoids as monotherapy. What is the expected complete response rate?
CORRECT: GC produces a complete response in only 7% of patients with cryoglobulinemic vasculitis. The correct first-line treatment for HCV-associated cryoglobulinemic vasculitis is antiviral therapy. Rituximab has RCT evidence of benefit and should be considered for active vasculitis.
Trap 13Midline Destructive Disease with ANCA = GPA?
A patient has destructive midline lesions with skin erosion through the face and an ANCA targeting human neutrophil elastase. Is this GPA, and should it be irradiated?
CORRECT: Erosion through the skin of the face is extremely rare in GPA — it's characteristic of other midline destructive diseases (e.g., extranodal NK/T-cell lymphoma). ANCA targeting HNE (not PR3/MPO) suggests cocaine-induced disease. Extranodal NK/T-cell lymphoma → irradiation is appropriate. Upper airway lesions should NEVER be irradiated in GPA.
Trap 14PAN-Like Vasculitis in a Child — Standard PAN Treatment?
A child presents with early-onset PAN-like vasculitis. Should the treatment approach be the same as for adult PAN?
CORRECT: In early-onset PAN-like vasculitis, consider DADA2 (adenosine deaminase type 2 deficiency). These patients have a different treatment: TNF inhibitors (not standard cyclophosphamide/prednisone used in PAN). This is a critical distinction with major therapeutic implications.
Trap 15Mycophenolate vs. Azathioprine for GPA Maintenance?
After inducing remission in GPA with cyclophosphamide, a patient is switched to mycophenolate mofetil for maintenance. Is this the best choice?
CORRECT: Mycophenolate mofetil can sustain remission, but it is associated with a higher relapse rate compared to azathioprine. The preferred agents for maintenance after cyclophosphamide induction are rituximab (lowest relapse rate in RCT) or azathioprine. Mycophenolate is used when patients are unable to receive MTX or azathioprine or have relapsed on such treatment.
