01

Definition

Core Definition
Diabetes mellitus (DM) is a group of common metabolic disorders that all share the phenotype of hyperglycemia. It is caused by a complex interaction of genetics and environmental factors.

Three Core Mechanisms Leading to Hyperglycemia

  • Reduced insulin secretion — beta cell failure or destruction
  • Decreased glucose utilization — insulin resistance in peripheral tissues (muscle, fat)
  • Increased glucose production — failure of insulin to suppress hepatic gluconeogenesis
Why It Matters
The mechanism drives the treatment: secretion defect → insulin or secretagogues; resistance → metformin, thiazolidinediones, GLP-1 RA; excess production → metformin, SGLT-2i.

End-Organ Consequences (The Big 3 US Statistics)

  • DM is the leading cause of ESRD (end-stage renal disease) in the US
  • DM is the leading cause of nontraumatic lower-extremity amputations
  • DM is the leading cause of adult blindness (ages 20–74)
  • Persons with DM have increased risk of cardiovascular disease — the main cause of morbidity and mortality

Global Burden

  • Worldwide: 30 million in 1985 → 537 million in 2021 (prevalence 10.5%)
  • Projected: 643 million by 2030, 783 million by 2045
  • US (2021): 11.6% of population have DM; 38% have prediabetes
  • 22.8% of US adults with DM are undiagnosed
  • ~50% of those with DM in middle/low-income countries are undiagnosed
  • 2021: DM caused nearly 6.7 million deaths worldwide (12.2% of global all-cause mortality)
02

Classification

Framework
DM is classified by its pathogenic process, not by age of onset or insulin use. Getting the classification right matters for personalized therapy. Some patients cannot be neatly classified at diagnosis.
Type 1 DM

Mechanism: Immune-mediated destruction of pancreatic beta cells → absolute insulin deficiency

Key feature: Autoantibodies against islet cell antigens (GAD, IA-2, ZnT8, insulin)

Genetics: HLA region on chromosome 6 (~50% of genetic risk); concordance 30–70% in identical twins

Presentation: Often dramatic — marked hyperglycemia; 25–50% present with DKA

Age: Any age; classically adolescent but 40% onset after age 30

Type 2 DM

Mechanism: Variable degrees of insulin resistance + impaired insulin secretion + increased hepatic glucose production

Key feature: Insulin resistance precedes secretory defect, but DM only develops when secretion becomes inadequate

Genetics: Polygenic; concordance 70–90% in identical twins; >600 genetic loci identified

Presentation: Often insidious; ≥80% are obese; may present with chronic complications already present

T2DM may be present >10 years before diagnosis

Type 3 — Other Specific Types

A. Genetic Defects of Beta Cell — MODY

Autosomal dominant; onset usually <25 years; impaired insulin secretion without autoimmunity

  • HNF-4α (MODY 1) — progressive glycemic worsening; responds to sulfonylureas
  • Glucokinase (MODY 2) — mild, stable fasting hyperglycemia; does NOT respond to oral agents; no treatment needed (except possibly in pregnancy)
  • HNF-1α (MODY 3) — most common; progressive decline; responds well to sulfonylureas; may be initially misdiagnosed as T1DM
  • HNF-1β (MODY 5) — insulin resistance + secretion defect; requires insulin; associated with renal cysts, pancreatic exocrine insufficiency, abnormal LFTs
  • Others: insulin promoter factor-1, NeuroD1, KLF11, PAX4, GATA4, GATA6, mitochondrial DNA
B–J. Other Causes
  • Neonatal DM — onset <6 months; mutations in ATP-sensitive K⁺ channel (Kir6.2, ABCC8) → may respond to sulfonylureas; GATA6 = most common cause of pancreatic agenesis
  • Exocrine pancreas — pancreatitis, pancreatectomy, cystic fibrosis, hemochromatosis, cancer (“Type 3c” or “pancreatic DM”) → insulin preferred
  • Endocrinopathies — acromegaly, Cushing’s, glucagonoma, pheochromocytoma, hyperthyroidism → hormones antagonize insulin
  • Drug-induced — glucocorticoids, calcineurin/mTOR inhibitors, thiazides, antipsychotics (2nd-gen), PCSK9 inhibitors, statins, HIV antiretrovirals, pentamidine, diazoxide
  • Infections — congenital rubella, CMV, coxsackievirus (rare)
  • Immune checkpoint inhibitors — rapid-onset insulin-deficient DM with DKA; rare (<1% of treated)
  • Genetic syndromes — Wolfram, Down’s, Turner’s, Klinefelter’s, Prader-Willi

Type 4 — Gestational DM (GDM)

  • Glucose intolerance developing in the 2nd or 3rd trimester of pregnancy
  • Insulin resistance is driven by metabolic and hormonal changes of pregnancy
  • DM diagnosed in the 1st trimester = pre-existing diabetes, NOT GDM (ADA 2021)
  • Most women revert to normal glucose tolerance postpartum
  • Risk of developing T2DM within 10–20 years: 35–60%
  • Children born to GDM mothers have increased risk of metabolic syndrome and T2DM later in life

Atypical Diabetes

  • Features of both T1 and T2 DM; not linked to single-gene defects
  • T2 DM phenotype before puberty, T2 DM in very lean individuals, T1 phenotype without autoantibodies
  • Ketosis-prone DM — present with DKA but do NOT require long-term insulin; often African American or Asian; later treated with oral agents
  • Autoimmune diabetes of adults (LADA) — phenotypic T2 DM but GAD/ICA positive; 5–10% of apparent T2 DM; more likely to need insulin within 5 years
  • Fulminant DM — acute-onset T1 DM noted in Japan; may be viral
Classification Pitfall
Age of onset, obesity, and need for insulin are not reliable for classification in adults. Many T1DM adults are now overweight/obese. Some T2DM patients present with DKA. Always check autoantibodies when unsure.
03

Insulin Physiology

Why This Matters for Boards
Glucokinase is the rate-limiting step for glucose-stimulated insulin secretion. Mutations here cause MODY 2. The K-ATP channel is the target of sulfonylureas. Understanding this mechanism explains which drugs work and why.

Insulin Biosynthesis

  • Produced by beta cells of pancreatic islets
  • Synthesized as: Preproinsulin → Proinsulin → Insulin + C-peptide
  • C-peptide is co-secreted and co-stored with insulin; cleared more slowly → useful marker of endogenous insulin secretion
  • Elevated proinsulin = indicator of beta cell dysfunction (seen in both T1 and T2 DM)
  • Beta cells also co-secrete IAPP (amylin) — a 37-aa peptide; forms amyloid deposits in islets of T2DM patients

Glucose-Stimulated Insulin Secretion — Step by Step

Glucose enters beta cell via GLUT transporter (GLUT1/2 in humans)
↓
Glucokinase phosphorylates glucose → Glucose-6-phosphate
(RATE-LIMITING STEP — altered in MODY 2)
↓
Glycolysis → ATP production increases
↓
ATP inhibits ATP-sensitive K⁺ channel (K-ATP channel)
SUR subunit = sulfonylurea binding site | Kir6.2 = inward rectifier
↓
K⁺ channel closes → membrane depolarization
↓
Voltage-dependent Ca²⁺ channels open → Ca²⁺ influx
↓
Insulin secretion from secretory granules
Sulfonylurea Mechanism
Sulfonylureas bind the SUR subunit of the K-ATP channel and close it directly (bypassing glucose/ATP). This stimulates insulin secretion independent of blood glucose level — which is why they cause hypoglycemia even when glucose is normal.

Two Phases of Insulin Secretion

  • First phase — rapid, immediate response to glucose spike; often the earliest defect lost in T1 and T2 DM
  • Second phase — prolonged, sustained release

Incretin Hormones

  • GLP-1 (glucagon-like peptide 1) and GIP (glucose-dependent insulinotropic peptide)
  • Released from gut L-cells after food ingestion
  • Stimulate insulin secretion via cAMP — only when blood glucose is above fasting level (glucose-dependent) → therefore do NOT cause hypoglycemia alone
  • Also suppress glucagon production and secretion
  • Targeted by: GLP-1 receptor agonists (e.g., semaglutide, liraglutide) and DPP-4 inhibitors (prolong endogenous GLP-1 activity)

Glucose Homeostasis: Organ Roles

Fasting State

Low insulin + modest glucagon increase → hepatic gluconeogenesis + glycogenolysis → glucose output

Insulin-sensitive tissues: reduced glucose uptake; increased lipolysis and amino acid mobilization

Postprandial State

Glucose load → insulin rises + glucagon falls → optimized glucose disposal

Majority of postprandial glucose utilized by skeletal muscle (insulin-stimulated)

Brain utilizes glucose in insulin-independent fashion

Insulin Actions

Anabolic: promotes carbohydrate, fat, and protein storage

50% of secreted insulin cleared by liver (first pass) → portal:peripheral ratio ~2:1

GLUT4 translocation to cell surface in muscle/fat → crucial for glucose uptake

04

Diagnosis

Fundamental Principle
DM is defined as the level of glycemia at which a diabetes-specific complication (e.g., retinopathy) occurs — not merely a deviation from a population mean. The threshold of 126 mg/dL (FPG) and 6.5% (HbA1c) were chosen because below these levels, retinopathy is essentially absent.

Diagnostic Criteria — Any ONE of the Following Confirms DM

TestNormalPrediabetesDiabetes
HbA1c <5.6% (<38 mmol/mol) 5.7–6.4% (39–47 mmol/mol) ≥6.5% (≥48 mmol/mol)
Fasting Plasma Glucose (FPG) <5.6 mmol/L (<100 mg/dL) 5.6–6.9 mmol/L (100–125 mg/dL) ≥7.0 mmol/L (≥126 mg/dL)
2-hour PG (OGTT) <7.8 mmol/L (<140 mg/dL) 7.8–11.0 mmol/L (140–199 mg/dL) ≥11.1 mmol/L (≥200 mg/dL)
Random Plasma Glucose + Symptoms — — ≥11.1 mmol/L (≥200 mg/dL) + polyuria / polydipsia / weight loss
Critical Rule — Confirmation Required
In the absence of unequivocal hyperglycemia and acute metabolic decompensation, any abnormal screening test must be confirmed by repeat testing on a different day. A single HbA1c or FPG finding is not enough to diagnose DM definitively. Exception: if two different tests are both abnormal at the same visit → diagnosis confirmed.

Why is the FPG Threshold 126 mg/dL? (Not 110 or 140?)

  • Derived from population studies (Pima Indians and others) showing that retinopathy begins to appear when FPG consistently exceeds 116–126 mg/dL
  • Below this threshold: essentially no diabetes-specific retinopathy
  • This is the key concept — DM is defined by complication threshold, not arbitrary statistical deviation
  • The threshold reflects where microvascular damage begins in the retina — a highly glucose-sensitive tissue
Board Application
"Why is FPG ≥126 used?" → Answer: because this is the glucose level at which diabetes-specific complications (particularly retinopathy) begin to occur. It is a complication-based threshold, not a statistical one.

HbA1c — Details

  • Must be performed in a laboratory using a NGSP-approved method (National Glycohemoglobin Standardization Program) traceable to the DCCT reference assay
  • Some point-of-care HbA1c assays meet these criteria — can be used diagnostically
  • Fasting is defined as no caloric intake for at least 8 hours (for FPG)
  • Certain conditions affect HbA1c accuracy: hemoglobinopathies (sickle cell, thalassemia), certain ancestries → may falsely alter HbA1c; use glucose-based tests instead

OGTT (Oral Glucose Tolerance Test)

  • 75g of anhydrous glucose dissolved in water; 2-hour plasma glucose measured
  • Not recommended for routine clinical use in non-pregnant adults
  • Preferred (over HbA1c) in:
    • Pregnancy / gestational diabetes screening
    • Cystic fibrosis-related diabetes (beginning age 10)
    • Post-organ transplantation diabetes
  • The OGTT detects IGT (impaired glucose tolerance) more sensitively than FPG alone

Symptoms of Hyperglycemia

  • Polyuria — osmotic diuresis from glucosuria
  • Polydipsia — secondary to osmotic fluid loss
  • Weight loss — urinary loss of glucose + calories; muscle breakdown from catabolism
  • Fatigue, weakness, blurry vision (lens water content changes — resolves with glycemic control)
  • Frequent superficial infections (vaginitis, fungal skin infections)
  • Slow healing of skin lesions

Stress Hyperglycemia — Important Distinction

Stress Hyperglycemia vs. True DM
A patient hospitalized with acute illness (pneumonia, MI, surgery) may have elevated blood glucose due to stress hormones (cortisol, catecholamines, glucagon). This is NOT necessarily DM. Key features of stress hyperglycemia:
  • Blood glucose elevated during acute illness but patient lacks classic DM symptoms (no polyuria, polydipsia)
  • No DKA, no prior DM history, no other corroborating evidence
  • The correct next step is to recheck glucose after recovery from the acute illness, NOT to diagnose DM or start long-term hypoglycemic therapy immediately
  • Glucose 140–180 mg/dL in ICU patients is very common and should be managed with insulin drip — but does not confirm DM diagnosis
  • Repeat HbA1c or FPG 4–8 weeks after discharge to confirm or exclude true DM
Board Answer
Hospitalized patient with high glucose, no symptoms, no prior history → first manage acutely, then recheck glucose after recovery before diagnosing DM.
05

Prediabetes

Definition
Prediabetes = impaired glucose homeostasis that does not yet meet criteria for DM. Three overlapping but non-identical categories — they identify different individuals via different biological mechanisms.

Three Categories of Prediabetes

IFG — Impaired Fasting Glucose

FPG: 5.6–6.9 mmol/L (100–125 mg/dL)

ADA definition. WHO uses >6.1 mmol/L (110 mg/dL) as the lower cutoff for IFG.

Primarily reflects hepatic insulin resistance and increased basal hepatic glucose output

IGT — Impaired Glucose Tolerance

2-h PG: 7.8–11.0 mmol/L (140–199 mg/dL)

Reflects primarily peripheral (muscle) insulin resistance and delayed insulin secretion in response to postprandial glucose

More predictive of cardiovascular risk than IFG alone

HbA1c 5.7–6.4%

ADA definition of prediabetes by HbA1c

Individuals with HbA1c 6.0–6.5% have a 25.5% 5-year risk of progressing to T2DM

IFG, IGT, and HbA1c 5.7–6.4% do NOT identify the same individuals

Clinical Consequences of Prediabetes

  • All three categories: greater risk of progressing to T2DM
  • All three categories: increased cardiovascular disease risk
  • All three: should be counseled about lifestyle modification
  • Risk is continuous, not discrete — the higher the HbA1c/glucose within the prediabetes range, the higher the risk

Management of Prediabetes / Glucose Intolerance

First-Line Approach
Lifestyle modification is the cornerstone: structured program to reduce body weight and increase physical activity. The correct approach for glucose intolerance with elevated HbA1c is to address lifestyle AND consider pharmacologic therapy — not simply to observe.
  • Lifestyle intervention (DPP data): diet + 30 min/day exercise, 5 days/week → 58% reduction in T2DM onset; patients lost 5–7% body weight; effect persisted ≥15 years
  • Metformin: 31% reduction in T2DM onset vs placebo (DPP)
  • Other agents shown to delay T2DM: α-glucosidase inhibitors, thiazolidinediones, GLP-1 RA, SGLT-2i, orlistat
  • GLP-1 receptor agonists and tirzepatide (GLP-1 + GIP dual agonist) currently transforming pharmacologic prevention due to greater weight loss
  • Individuals with prediabetes also need cardiovascular disease screening
Boards Distinction
A patient with HbA1c of 6.0% and glucose intolerance — the answer is NOT just "diet and exercise alone" and NOT "start metformin without lifestyle." The correct approach is: lifestyle modification + pharmacologic consideration (diet is not sufficient as the sole intervention when there is documented glucose intolerance with clinical risk factors). The pharmacologic choice depends on individual factors (obesity = GLP-1 RA preferred; BMI-independent = metformin is standard first option for pharmacotherapy in prediabetes).

Screening for Type 2 DM

Why Screen?
(1) Large number of DM patients are asymptomatic and unaware. (2) T2DM may be present for up to a decade before diagnosis. (3) Some have complications at diagnosis. (4) Early treatment may alter natural history. (5) Prediabetes diagnosis spurs prevention efforts.

ADA Screening Criteria for T2DM in Adults (Table 415-2)

Group 1 — Any Age, Overweight/Obese + ≥1 Risk Factor

BMI ≥25 kg/m² (or ≥23 in Asian Americans) + at least ONE of:

  • Family history: parent or sibling with T2DM
  • Race/ethnicity: African American, Latino, Native American, Asian American, Pacific Islander
  • Hypertension (BP ≥130/80 mmHg)
  • HDL <35 mg/dL and/or triglycerides >250 mg/dL
  • Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) or acanthosis nigricans
  • History of cardiovascular disease
  • Physical inactivity
  • Severe obesity or other condition associated with insulin resistance
  • Group 2: Previously identified IFG, IGT, or HbA1c 5.7–6.4% → screen annually
  • Group 3: Women with history of GDM → screen at least every 3 years
  • Group 4: All others → initiate testing at age 35, repeat every 3 years
  • Group 5: HIV medications or medications associated with DM (2nd-gen antipsychotics, glucocorticoids)
  • Group 6: History of pancreatitis → screen within 3–6 months of episode
  • Group 7: Cystic fibrosis → beginning at age 10; use OGTT (not HbA1c)
  • Group 8: After organ transplantation → use OGTT

Key Screening Facts

Missing Criterion — No "Beta Cell Function"

A woman with BMI 31, family Hx of T2DM, presenting for screening — the finding that SHOULD prompt screening is the combination of overweight + family history (both present).

The answer that says "no beta cell function" is WRONG for screening — that is a diagnostic or therapeutic concept, not a screening criterion. Screening is about risk factors, not beta cell status.

Preferred Screening Tests

HbA1c or FPG are preferred (most reliable and convenient for asymptomatic individuals)

OGTT is NOT recommended for routine screening (reserved for pregnancy, CF, post-transplant)

Continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) is being studied as a diagnostic tool but not yet standard

Asian Americans — Special Consideration

  • Screening threshold: BMI ≥23 kg/m² (lower than general population ≥25)
  • Reason: East and South Asians develop T2DM at lower BMI and younger age
  • Greater visceral adiposity at lower absolute BMI
  • Reduced insulin secretory capacity compared to other groups

Type 1 DM — Pathogenesis

Core Concept
T1DM = immune-mediated destruction of beta cells → absolute insulin deficiency. The process is staged, occurring over years before clinical presentation. Beta cell destruction is primarily CD8+ T cell-mediated (not antibody-mediated, despite ICA being the marker).

Three-Stage Model of T1DM Development

Genetic predisposition (HLA alleles + >60 non-HLA loci) + Environmental trigger
↓
Stage 1: ≥2 islet cell autoantibodies (ICAs) present; normoglycemia maintained
No symptoms — this IS the onset of T1DM by current staging
↓
Stage 2: Continued autoimmunity + development of dysglycemia (IFG or IGT)
Still no symptoms; teplizumab approved for this stage
↓
Stage 3: Overt hyperglycemia meeting diagnostic criteria for DM
Symptomatic; 25–50% present with DKA
  • The time course between Stage 1 and Stage 3 varies widely — from months to years
  • A “honeymoon phase” may occur in the first 1–2 years after Stage 3 onset — reduced insulin requirements due to residual beta cell function
  • Many with long-standing T1DM still produce small amounts of insulin (detectable C-peptide); beta cells can persist in pancreas decades after diagnosis

Islet Cell Autoantibodies (ICAs)

  • Target antigens: insulin, GAD (glutamic acid decarboxylase), IA-2, ZnT8
  • Present in >85% of new-onset T1DM
  • Two or more ICAs → nearly 70% risk of T1DM at 10 years; >80% at 15 years in high-risk children
  • ICAs are markers, not causative — beta cell destruction is primarily CD8+ T cell cytotoxicity
  • Measurement recommended when: younger age at diagnosis, ketoacidosis, rapid need for insulin, personal/family history of autoimmune disease

Genetics of T1DM

  • Concordance in identical twins: 30–70% (environmental factors also required)
  • HLA region on chromosome 6 accounts for ~50% of genetic risk
  • High-risk haplotypes: DRB1*0301-DQB1*0201 (DR3-DQ2) and DRB1*0401-DQB1*0302 (DR4-DQ8)
  • Protective haplotype: DRB1*1501
  • >60 additional non-HLA loci contribute (CTLA-4, IL-2 receptor, PTPN22, insulin gene promoter)
  • Family risk: 1–9% if parent has T1DM; 6–7% if sibling; >80% of T1DM patients have NO affected relative

Disease-Modifying Therapy — Teplizumab

Teplizumab
A single 14-day course of teplizumab (Fc receptor-nonbinding anti-CD3 monoclonal antibody) delayed onset of Stage 3 T1DM by a median of 32.5 months in high-risk individuals with Stage 2 T1DM (multiple autoantibodies + dysglycemia). FDA approved for Stage 2 T1DM. Used at selected centers.

Alpha Cell Dysfunction in T1DM

  • Alpha cells (glucagon-producing) are spared from autoimmune destruction
  • However, they lose normal regulation: fasting and postprandial hyperglucagonemia
  • Crucially: impaired glucagon response to hypoglycemia — this is why T1DM patients have defective counter-regulation and severe hypoglycemia unawareness over time

Type 2 DM — Pathogenesis

Core Concept
T2DM = insulin resistance (primary, precedes secretory defect) + progressive beta cell failure. Glucose tolerance remains normal initially because beta cells compensate. DM manifests only when compensation fails.

Sequence of Events in T2DM Development

Genetic susceptibility + Obesity + Metabolic inflammation
↓
Insulin resistance in muscle, liver, fat
(glucose utilization 30–60% lower than normal)
↓
Beta cells compensate → hyperinsulinemia → normal glucose tolerance (Point A→B on insulin secretion/sensitivity curve)
↓
Compensation begins to fail → IGT (elevated postprandial glucose, Point C)
↓
Frank beta cell failure → overt T2DM (Point D)
Beta cell function reduced ~50% at T2DM onset

Mechanisms of Insulin Resistance

  • Insulin resistance is relative — supranormal insulin levels CAN normalize glucose (key: beta cells simply cannot sustain that level)
  • Lipid intermediates accumulate in skeletal myocytes → impair mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation → reduce insulin-stimulated ATP production → impair GLUT4 translocation
  • Impaired fatty acid oxidation → reactive oxygen species generation → metabolic inflammation → feeds back to worsen insulin resistance
  • Insulin receptor levels and tyrosine kinase activity are reduced but this is secondary to hyperinsulinemia, not the primary defect
  • Primary defect: postreceptor signaling — defects in PI3-kinase pathway that mediates GLUT4 translocation

Role of Adipose Tissue (Visceral Fat)

  • ≥80% of T2DM patients are obese; visceral/central adiposity is the dangerous pattern
  • Adipocytes secrete: free fatty acids, retinol-binding protein 4, leptin, TNF-α, resistin, IL-6 (pro-inflammatory), and adiponectin (insulin-sensitizing — reduced in obesity)
  • Adipose resident macrophages: important source of metabolic inflammation
  • Visceral fat drains into portal circulation → hepatic dysfunction, hepatic insulin resistance
  • Free fatty acids: impair glucose utilization in muscle, promote hepatic glucose production, impair beta cell function (“lipotoxicity”)

Hepatic Contribution

  • Increased FPG = primarily driven by increased hepatic glucose output (failure to suppress gluconeogenesis)
  • Postprandial hyperglycemia = primarily driven by decreased peripheral glucose utilization (muscle)
  • Dyslipidemia of T2DM: elevated TG, reduced HDL, increased small dense LDL — all from increased VLDL-TG synthesis due to insulin resistance in adipose and liver
  • If hepatic lipid is retained → MASLD (metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease)

Impaired Insulin Secretion in T2DM

  • Initially mild; selectively involves glucose-stimulated secretion (first-phase response is greatly reduced)
  • Response to non-glucose secretagogues (e.g., arginine) is initially preserved
  • Overall beta cell function reduced by ~50% at onset of T2DM
  • Eventually progressive beta cell failure ensues
  • Mechanisms of decline: glucose toxicity, lipotoxicity, islet amyloid deposits (IAPP), proinflammatory cytokines from islet-associated macrophages

Ethnic and Population Differences in T2DM

  • Latinos: greater insulin resistance
  • East Asians and South Asians: more beta cell dysfunction; develop T2DM at lower BMI and younger age
  • African Americans: more prone to nonketotic hyperosmolar presentation
  • Social determinants of health play a major role in T2DM rates globally

MODY — Monogenic Diabetes

Core Concept
MODY = Maturity-Onset Diabetes of the Young. Autosomal dominant; onset <25 years (sometimes neonatal); impaired insulin secretion without autoimmunity. Each subtype has distinct characteristics and treatment implications that boards love to test.

When to Suspect MODY

  • Diabetes in a young person (<25 years, sometimes in neonatal period)
  • Autosomal dominant family history (parent and grandparent with DM)
  • Absence of typical T1 or T2 DM features (no autoantibodies, not obese)
  • Stable mild fasting hyperglycemia without progression
  • Onset within first 6 months of life (consider neonatal DM)

Key MODY Types — Board-Tested Distinctions

MODY TypeGene MutationClinical FeaturesTreatment
MODY 1 HNF-4α Progressive glycemic worsening; severe hypoglycemia with sulfonylureas Sulfonylureas (responsive)
MODY 2 Glucokinase Mild, STABLE fasting hyperglycemia; does NOT progress; retinopathy rare; diagnosis often incidental NO treatment needed (except possibly in pregnancy). Does NOT respond to oral agents or insulin for long-term glycemic control
MODY 3 HNF-1α Most common MODY; progressive decline in glycemic control; renal glucosuria (low glucose threshold); may initially be diagnosed as T1DM Sulfonylureas — highly responsive; some were on insulin and successfully switched to sulfonylurea
MODY 5 HNF-1β Insulin resistance + secretion defect; renal cysts; pancreatic exocrine insufficiency; abnormal LFTs Requires insulin; minimal response to sulfonylureas

Glucokinase MODY (MODY 2) — The Classic Board Scenario

Mechanism
Glucokinase is the rate-limiting enzyme that converts glucose to glucose-6-phosphate in the beta cell — this is the “glucose sensor.” Mutations raise the threshold for insulin secretion. A higher glucose level is required to trigger insulin release → the set point for blood glucose is shifted upward. This produces stable, mild fasting hyperglycemia (116–126 mg/dL) that does not progress to microvascular complications and does NOT respond to oral hypoglycemic agents.
Board Answer — MODY 2 Patient
Young patient (19–25y), lean, family Hx of mild DM, FPG consistently 116–130 mg/dL, no autoantibodies, no T1/T2 features → Glucokinase MODY (MODY 2) → correct answer = dietary advice only / no pharmacologic treatment. Oral agents and insulin will NOT correct the underlying set-point defect and are not indicated for long-term management. Exception: may treat during pregnancy.

Neonatal Diabetes (onset <6 months)

  • Any DM onset before 6 months of age → must do genetic screening
  • Most common mutations: ATP-sensitive K⁺ channel subunits (Kir6.2 / ABCC8) → impair glucose-stimulated insulin secretion
  • Key board point: these patients may respond to sulfonylureas and can discontinue insulin
  • GATA6 mutations = most common cause of pancreatic agenesis
  • Homozygous glucokinase mutations → severe neonatal DM
  • Mitochondrial DNA mutations → associated with diabetes AND deafness
10

Gestational DM (GDM)

Core Concept
GDM = glucose intolerance developing in the 2nd or 3rd trimester. Most women revert to normal glucose tolerance after delivery, but carry a 35–60% lifetime risk of developing T2DM in the next 10–20 years. This number is a classic board fact.

Mechanism

  • Pregnancy creates physiological insulin resistance via metabolic and hormonal changes (human placental lactogen, cortisol, progesterone)
  • Increased insulin demands during 2nd and 3rd trimester may exceed beta cell capacity
  • Women who develop GDM have underlying beta cell susceptibility that is unmasked by pregnancy
  • IDF 2021: 16% of pregnancies worldwide affected by GDM or pre-existing DM

Classification Detail

  • DM diagnosed in the 1st trimester = pre-existing diabetes (NOT GDM) — per ADA 2021 recommendation
  • GDM = specifically 2nd or 3rd trimester onset
  • Screening for GDM is NOT discussed in Chapter 415 (covered separately)

Long-Term Consequences

For the Mother

35–60% risk of T2DM in the next 10–20 years after delivery

ADA recommends lifelong screening for DM/prediabetes at least every 3 years

Increased cardiovascular risk

For the Child

Increased risk of metabolic syndrome later in life

Increased risk of T2DM later in life

The in utero hyperglycemic environment programs metabolic dysfunction

The Board Scenario — GDM Postpartum

Classic Board Question
A 50-year-old woman, 3rd trimester of her current pregnancy, glucose 130 mg/dL before pregnancy. Her 3rd trimester HbA1c was 91 mg/dL. What is the next best step after delivery?

The answer is: Arrange follow-up screening for T2DM. She has a 35–60% chance of developing T2DM in the next 10–20 years. The ADA mandates at least every-3-year screening for DM/prediabetes postpartum — for life. Additionally, monitor for metabolic syndrome in offspring.
  • Women with GDM should be screened for T2DM at 4–12 weeks postpartum with OGTT (not HbA1c — as HbA1c may be inaccurate in the postpartum period due to increased RBC turnover)
  • Then lifelong screening every 3 years
11

Other Forms of DM

Pancreatic (Type 3c) DM

  • Caused by damage/destruction of exocrine pancreas → islet damage → insulin AND glucagon deficiency
  • Causes: pancreatitis, pancreatectomy, cystic fibrosis, hemochromatosis, pancreatic cancer, fibrocalculous pancreatopathy
  • An episode of pancreatitis should prompt screening for DM
  • Pancreatic cancer + new-onset T2DM have an association (new-onset DM may be a harbinger)
  • Cystic fibrosis-related DM: prevalence ~50% (rising as CF survival improves); use OGTT beginning age 10
  • Insulin is the preferred therapy for most pancreatic DM (both insulin and glucagon deficient)

Immune Checkpoint Inhibitor DM

  • Anti-PD-1, anti-PD-L1, anti-CTLA-4 therapies → rare (<1% of treated patients)
  • Rapid-onset insulin-deficient DM, often with DKA
  • Occurs at variable time after starting immunotherapy
  • Some similarities to T1DM (HLA associations, occasional ICA) but likely different pathogenesis
  • Requires insulin therapy

COVID-19 and DM

  • Hyperglycemia more common in those with SARS-CoV-2 infection
  • Pre-existing DM worsens COVID-19 outcomes
  • Most likely: SARS-CoV-2 does NOT directly cause T1DM or T2DM but increases DM ascertainment and creates conditions that worsen hyperglycemia (as in other serious illnesses)

Insulin Resistance Syndromes

  • Type A insulin resistance: young women; severe hyperinsulinemia + obesity + hyperandrogenism; undefined insulin-signaling defect
  • Type B insulin resistance: middle-aged women; anti-insulin receptor antibodies → may block or stimulate the receptor (causing hypoglycemia)
  • PCOS: very common; chronic anovulation + hyperandrogenism; significant subset have insulin resistance; substantially increases T2DM risk independent of obesity
  • Lipodystrophies: selective loss of adipose tissue → severe insulin resistance + hypertriglyceridemia; can be inherited or acquired
12

Prevention of T2DM

Diabetes Prevention Program (DPP) — The Key Trial
Landmark randomized trial in individuals with IGT demonstrating that T2DM can be prevented or delayed.

DPP Results — Numbers to Know

Intensive Lifestyle

58% reduction in T2DM incidence vs placebo

Diet + exercise 30 min/day, 5 days/week

Lost 5–7% body weight over 3 years

Effect seen regardless of age, sex, or ethnicity

Effects persisted for ≥15 years

Metformin

31% reduction in T2DM incidence vs placebo

Less effective than lifestyle in the trial overall

Most effective in: younger patients, higher BMI, higher baseline glucose

Remains standard first-line pharmacotherapy for prediabetes when pharmacologic treatment is indicated

Emerging Pharmacotherapy

GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide, liraglutide) and tirzepatide (GLP-1 + GIP dual agonist) are transforming prevention due to superior weight loss

Also shown to prevent DM: α-glucosidase inhibitors, thiazolidinediones, SGLT-2 inhibitors, orlistat

Bariatric Surgery — The Most Effective Intervention for T2DM Prevention/Remission

  • Swedish Obese Subjects study: 78% reduction in incidence of T2DM development
  • 68.2% of patients with existing T2DM achieved complete remission within 5 years of surgery
  • However, one-third redeveloped T2DM within 5 years of remission
  • Better outcomes with: earlier-stage T2DM, not on insulin, shorter disease duration, lower HbA1c
  • Mechanism: caloric restriction + reduced insulin resistance + altered gut hormones (GLP-1, GIP)
  • Mortality of bariatric surgery: generally <1%
  • Most common RYGB complication: stomal stenosis/marginal ulcers (5–15%) → treated with endoscopic dilation + acid suppression
  • Restrictive-malabsorptive procedures (RYGB) → lifelong supplementation needed: vitamin B12, iron, folate, calcium, vitamin D

Exam Traps

Trap 01MODY 2 — Don't Treat What Doesn't Need Treatment
Young lean patient, family Hx of “mild” DM in parent and grandparent, FPG consistently 116–130 mg/dL, no autoantibodies, no DKA, no obesity. Question: “What is the most appropriate management?”
Correct Answer: Dietary advice only — no pharmacologic treatment. This is MODY 2 (glucokinase mutation). The hyperglycemia is mild, stable, and does NOT progress to microvascular complications. Sulfonylureas and insulin will not correct the set-point defect. Treating this patient with oral agents is wrong and causes unnecessary hypoglycemia. Exception: may consider treatment during pregnancy only.
Trap 02Sulfonylureas in MODY 3 — Better Than Insulin
Young patient initially diagnosed as T1DM, on insulin for years, now reclassified as having MODY (HNF-1α mutation). What is the preferred treatment?
Correct Answer: Switch to sulfonylureas. HNF-1α (MODY 3) patients are exquisitely sensitive to sulfonylureas and can achieve excellent glycemic control. Many patients were placed on insulin unnecessarily due to misclassification as T1DM. The key is: sulfonylureas → MODY 3, NOT insulin.
Trap 03Stress Hyperglycemia — Don't Label It DM Yet
A 50-year-old with BMI 29.5 admitted for community-acquired pneumonia (CURB65 score good). Blood glucose found to be 220 mg/dL. No polyuria, no polydipsia, no prior DM history. What is the next step?
Correct Answer: Treat the acute hyperglycemia, then recheck glucose after recovery before diagnosing DM. Stress hyperglycemia from acute illness is common. Blood glucose 140–180 mg/dL (even higher) occurs in ICU patients due to counter-regulatory hormones. This is NOT diagnostic of DM without confirmation after the acute illness resolves. Diagnosing DM during acute illness (without prior evidence) and starting long-term oral hypoglycemics is incorrect.
Trap 04GDM — The 35–60% Number
A woman had gestational diabetes during her last pregnancy. What is her long-term risk of developing T2DM?
Correct Answer: 35–60% risk of T2DM in the next 10–20 years. This is a high-yield number. The ADA recommends lifelong screening for DM/prediabetes at least every 3 years postpartum. Most women revert to normal glucose tolerance after delivery — but this false reassurance masks a very high long-term risk. Also screen at 4–12 weeks postpartum with OGTT (preferred over HbA1c postpartum).
Trap 05The FPG 126 mg/dL Threshold — Why This Number?
Question asks why the diagnostic threshold for DM was set at FPG ≥126 mg/dL and not at another value.
Correct Answer: Because 126 mg/dL is the level at which diabetes-specific complications (specifically retinopathy) begin to appear. It is NOT a statistical cutoff from a normal distribution. The threshold is complication-based. The Pima Indian population studies showed retinopathy starts when FPG exceeds ~116–126 mg/dL. Below this: retinopathy is essentially absent. This is the fundamental principle of DM diagnosis.
Trap 06Screening Criterion — BMI Threshold for Asians
An Asian American patient with BMI 24 kg/m² comes for routine screening. No other risk factors mentioned. Should DM screening be recommended?
Correct Answer: Yes — for Asian Americans, the BMI screening threshold is ≥23 kg/m² (not ≥25). With BMI 24, this patient meets the threshold. Reason: Asian populations develop T2DM at a lower BMI due to greater visceral adiposity and reduced insulin secretory capacity at lower body weights. Using the standard 25 kg/m² threshold would miss many at-risk Asian Americans.
Trap 07Glucokinase — The “Rate-Limiting Step”
Question: “What is the primary regulator/signal for glucose-stimulated insulin secretion from the beta cell?”
Correct Answer: Glucokinase-mediated phosphorylation of glucose to glucose-6-phosphate is the rate-limiting step that controls glucose-regulated insulin secretion. It is the glucose sensor. ATP generated from this metabolism closes the K-ATP channel → depolarization → Ca²⁺ influx → insulin secretion. Mutations in glucokinase = MODY 2 (higher glucose level required to trigger secretion → elevated set point). The SUR subunit (K-ATP channel) is the target of sulfonylureas.
Trap 08Glucose Intolerance + HbA1c 6% — What's the Right Management?
40-year-old with HbA1c of 6.0%, FPG 108 mg/dL, family history of T2DM, BMI elevated. What is the best management?
Correct Answer: Lifestyle modification (diet + exercise) AND consider pharmacologic therapy — specifically evaluate individual factors to choose agent. The trap is selecting “diet alone” (insufficient for this level of risk/glucose intolerance with multiple risk factors) or “start metformin without lifestyle” (incomplete). The DPP showed both lifestyle and metformin reduce progression to DM — lifestyle being more effective. The correct approach is structured lifestyle program + pharmacologic consideration based on BMI, cardiovascular risk, and patient preference. Pharmacological options: metformin (31% reduction), GLP-1 RA (greater weight loss benefit).
Trap 09Neonatal DM — Don't Default to Insulin
An infant develops DM at 3 months of age. Genetic testing shows an activating mutation in the Kir6.2 subunit of the ATP-sensitive K⁺ channel. Current treatment: insulin. What should be considered?
Correct Answer: Switch to sulfonylureas. Activating mutations in K-ATP channel subunits (Kir6.2/ABCC8) cause neonatal DM by keeping the channel open (so ATP cannot close it). Sulfonylureas close the K-ATP channel independently of ATP → can restore insulin secretion. Many of these children can completely discontinue insulin. This is one of the best examples of precision/personalized medicine in diabetes.
Trap 10Single Abnormal Test — Not Enough
A patient has a single fasting glucose of 130 mg/dL on routine labs. They are asymptomatic. The question asks: “What is the diagnosis?”
Correct Answer: You cannot diagnose DM from a single asymptomatic abnormal test. In the absence of unequivocal hyperglycemia and acute metabolic decompensation, the blood glucose criteria must be confirmed by repeat testing on a different day (or a second different diagnostic test on the same day). A single FPG ≥126 in an asymptomatic patient requires CONFIRMATION before diagnosis. Exception: two different abnormal tests at the same visit = diagnosis confirmed.
