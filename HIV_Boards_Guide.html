1. AIDS-defining conditions & staging

The two ways to diagnose AIDS (CDC stage 3)

  • CD4 < 200 cells/μL in anyone >6 years old — regardless of symptoms
  • Any AIDS-defining illness (Table 208-1) — regardless of CD4 count

✅ AIDS-defining — must memorize

  • PCP (Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia)
  • Toxoplasmosis of brain
  • Cryptococcosis (extrapulmonary)
  • CMV retinitis / disease
  • Kaposi's sarcoma
  • Non-Hodgkin's lymphoma (Burkitt's, immunoblastic, CNS primary)
  • Invasive cervical carcinoma
  • MAC disseminated
  • Progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (PML)
  • Esophageal candidiasis
  • Wasting syndrome
  • HIV encephalopathy
  • TB (any site)

❌ NOT AIDS-defining (non-AIDS malignancies)

  • Hodgkin's lymphoma — increased risk but NOT defining
  • Melanoma
  • Lung, liver, anal cancer
  • Multiple myeloma
  • Leukemia
  • Zoster in a single dermatome — NOT AIDS-defining (but signals early immune decline)
  • Recurrent bacterial pneumonia — AIDS-defining only in adults ≥6 years when recurrent
  • Meningococcal meningitis — NOT AIDS-defining
KS occurrence is NOT strictly tied to CD4 count — it can appear even with normal CD4. All other OIs are linked to CD4 thresholds. For boards: KS = AIDS-defining; Hodgkin's lymphoma = NOT AIDS-defining.

2. Acute HIV infection

Key fact for boards

  • ~50% of patients experience an acute syndrome 2–4 weeks after primary infection
  • The other ~50% are asymptomatic — so "most will be asymptomatic" is also defensible depending on how the question is framed
  • Resembles infectious mononucleosis: fever, pharyngitis, lymphadenopathy, rash, myalgias

What happens during acute HIV

  • Massive burst of plasma viremia (millions of copies/mL)
  • CD4 initially drops → then partially recovers
  • CD8 cells expand → inversion of CD4/CD8 ratio
  • p24 antigen detectable before antibodies appear
  • Antibodies appear 3–6 weeks (almost always within 12 weeks)

What the peak viral load does NOT predict

  • Peak viremia during acute infection does NOT predict rate of disease progression
  • What DOES predict progression: the viral set point at ~1 year after infection
  • Higher set point → faster progression to AIDS

Thrush (oropharyngeal candidiasis) in HIV — clinical significance

  • Occurs typically when CD4 < 300/μL
  • 59% of patients with oral candidiasis developed AIDS within the next year
  • Important: positive throat culture for Candida in HIV patients has NO diagnostic value (colonization is common)
  • Oral candidiasis = indication to start PCP prophylaxis regardless of CD4

What is NOT true about thrush in HIV

  • "15% positive blood culture" — FALSE. Fungemia from thrush is not standard
  • "Most HIV patients worldwide have thrush" — FALSE
  • "CD4 must be <150 to get thrush" — FALSE (threshold is ~300)

The answer to "risk of HIV from unprotected anal intercourse" is approximately 1.4% per act (~138/10,000). On boards this often appears as a "per act" question — not the lifetime risk.

Why anal intercourse is highest sexual risk

  • Rectal mucosa is thin and fragile → microtrauma
  • Deposited semen sits against susceptible CD4+ cells
  • Langerhans cells directly below surface = easy viral entry
  • Vaginal mucosa = several layers thicker → more protection

Factors that INCREASE transmission risk

  • High viral load in source patient
  • Genital ulcer disease (HSV, syphilis, chancroid)
  • Non-ulcerative STIs (gonorrhea, chlamydia)
  • Lack of circumcision (insertive partner)
  • Acute HIV infection (viral load very high)

U = U (Undetectable = Untransmittable)

  • If viral load suppressed to <20 copies/mL by ART → essentially zero sexual transmission
  • Demonstrated for both heterosexual and MSM couples
  • >125,000 sex acts studied without transmission when partner virally suppressed

4. Mother-to-child transmission (MTCT) prevention

Without any intervention — transmission rates

  • Industrialized countries: 15–25% overall
  • Developing countries: 25–35%
  • With full ART + cesarean: rate falls to <1%

When does transmission occur?

  • 50–65% — during delivery (most common)
  • 23–30% — before birth (in utero)
  • 12–20% — via breastfeeding

Most effective MTCT prevention strategy (boards answer)

ART for mother throughout pregnancy AND during delivery Suppresses viral load → reduces exposure of infant
Antiretroviral prophylaxis for the newborn after birth Typically zidovudine ± other agents for 4–6 weeks
Cesarean section delivery Reduces exposure to maternal blood/secretions during birth canal passage
Avoid breastfeeding In developed countries — formula available as safe alternative
The boards answer for "most effective single intervention to prevent MTCT" = antiretroviral therapy for the mother during pregnancy AND delivery. Cesarean alone is insufficient. Immunoglobulin to newborn is NOT recommended as primary prevention.

5. Initial workup & when to start ART

Initial evaluation — required tests

  • CD4+ T cell count
  • Plasma HIV RNA level
  • HIV resistance testing (genotype)
  • HLA-B5701 — before starting abacavir
  • PPD / IFN-γ release assay (TB screening)
  • Anti-Toxoplasma IgG antibody
  • RPR or VDRL (syphilis)
  • Hepatitis A, B, C serologies
  • Mini-Mental Status Exam (baseline)
  • Urinalysis, routine chemistries, lipids

Which test is NOT routinely needed?

  • Serology for toxoplasmosis for Toxoplasma is done (to know baseline)
  • VDRL/RPR — yes, required
  • PPD — yes, required
  • HLA-B5701 — yes, required (abacavir hypersensitivity)
  • If the option is "complete blood count" or "routine culture" — not specifically listed as required in initial eval protocol

HLA-B5701 — why it matters

  • Screens for hypersensitivity to abacavir
  • If positive → abacavir absolutely contraindicated
  • Also associated with slower HIV progression

When to start ART — current guidelines

  • EVERYONE with HIV should start ART — regardless of CD4 count or viral load
  • Start as soon as possible after diagnosis
  • Exception: delay 2–4 weeks if active cryptococcal meningitis or TB (to reduce IRIS risk)
  • For TB + HIV with CD4 ≥50: delay ART 2–8 weeks after starting TB treatment
  • For TB + HIV with CD4 <50: start ART as soon as possible
Old teaching was CD4 <350 or <500. Current answer: ALL HIV-positive patients need ART regardless of CD4. The only delay scenario is cryptococcal meningitis or active TB (IRIS concern).

PCP prophylaxis — who ACTUALLY needs it (boards scenarios)

  • ✅ HIV with CD4 = 90 (asymptomatic) — YES, start TMP-SMX (CD4 <200)
  • ✅ HIV with prior PCP episode even if CD4 now >200 — YES (prior episode = indication)
  • ✅ HIV with oropharyngeal candidiasis — YES (regardless of CD4)

TMP-SMX is the wonder drug of HIV — it simultaneously prevents PCP, Toxoplasma encephalitis, AND some bacterial respiratory pathogens. One pill, three protections.

7. PCP (Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia)

Classic presentation

  • Gradual onset fever, dry cough, dyspnea on exertion
  • Characteristic retrosternal chest pain, worse on inspiration
  • Can be indolent — weeks of symptoms before diagnosis
  • 95% have CD4 <200; 79% have CD4 <100
  • Can occur in patients unaware of HIV status

Investigations

  • CXR: bilateral interstitial infiltrates (often faint) OR normal early
  • Classic dense perihilar infiltrate = UNUSUAL in AIDS
  • ABG: PaO₂ reduced; A-a gradient elevated
  • LDH elevated (common)
  • Definitive: induced sputum, BAL, or transbronchial biopsy showing organisms

Treatment decision — the critical branch point (boards favorite)

SeverityCriteriaTreatmentAdd steroids?Bronchoscopy?
Mild-moderate PaO₂ >70 mmHg AND A-a gradient <35 mmHg TMP-SMX oral No If diagnosis uncertain
Severe PaO₂ <70 mmHg OR A-a gradient >35 mmHg TMP-SMX IV (or IV pentamidine if intolerant) YES — adjunct prednisone Yes (confirm diagnosis)

The question scenario (Image 11): CD4=96, PaO₂=60, RR=34 — answer

  • PaO₂ = 60 mmHg → severe (below 70 threshold)
  • Bilateral infiltrates on CXR, no specific organism found yet
  • Correct approach: Start TMP-SMX + prednisone (steroids) empirically → then bronchoscopy to confirm
  • Do NOT wait for bronchoscopy before starting treatment in severe disease
  • Do NOT use pentamidine as first-line when TMP-SMX can be used

TMP-SMX — drug details

  • Dose: 15–20 mg/kg/day (based on trimethoprim component) divided q6–8h for 21 days
  • Side effects: rash (20–85%), bone marrow suppression, hyperkalemia (blocks tubular potassium secretion), elevated creatinine (competes with tubular secretion), hepatotoxicity
  • ~33% of patients can continue despite cutaneous reaction
  • Alternative: dapsone/trimethoprim, clindamycin/primaquine, atovaquone (mild-moderate); IV pentamidine (severe, TMP-SMX intolerant)
PCP treatment = 21 days total. Severe disease = add prednisone 40 mg bid × 5 days → 40 mg qday × 5 days → 20 mg qday × 11 days. Start steroids before or with antibiotics — reduces inflammation from dying organisms.

8. Cryptococcal meningitis

Epidemiology

  • CD4 typically <100/μL; initial AIDS-defining illness in ~2% of patients
  • Organism: Cryptococcus neoformans (up to 12% = C. gattii)
  • Especially common in untreated AIDS in Africa (~5% of patients)

Classic presentation

  • Subacute meningoencephalitis: fever, headache, malaise
  • Seizures and focal deficits = uncommon
  • CSF opening pressure usually elevated
  • Neck stiffness may be absent
  • ~1/3 also have pulmonary disease
  • Can cause cryptococcomas, cranial nerve involvement

CSF findings

  • May be normal or near-normal (WBC, protein)
  • Glucose may be low or normal
  • India ink = sees organisms directly
  • Cryptococcal antigen = highly sensitive diagnostic test
  • Blood cultures for fungus often positive
  • Do NOT rely on cell count alone — immunosuppressed patients mount poor inflammatory response

The question scenario (Images 1 & 20): CD4=103, India ink positive, 88% mononuclear CSF — treatment answer

  • Diagnosis: Cryptococcal meningitis
  • Correct treatment: Amphotericin B + flucytosine (induction phase)
  • NOT fluconazole alone as initial therapy for acute meningitis
  • NOT acyclovir (that's for HSV)

Treatment — three phases

Induction (≥2 weeks) Amphotericin B 0.7 mg/kg/day IV or liposomal amphotericin 3–4 mg/kg/day IV PLUS flucytosine 25 mg/kg q6h PO
Consolidation (8 weeks) Fluconazole 800 mg/day PO (after CSF culture turns negative)
Maintenance (until CD4 >200 for 6 months on ART) Fluconazole 200 mg/day PO

When to start ART after cryptococcal meningitis?

  • Delay ART 4–6 weeks after starting antifungal therapy
  • Reason: Immune reconstitution (IRIS) can cause dangerous worsening of meningitis
  • This is an exception to "start ART immediately"
  • Repeat lumbar puncture may be needed to manage elevated intracranial pressure

The question scenario (Image 6): CD4=87, India ink positive, glucose=73 — why not just fluconazole?

  • Fluconazole is used for maintenance/secondary prophylaxis — NOT for acute meningitis induction
  • Acute meningitis requires amphotericin B + flucytosine for induction
  • Starting HAART immediately = risk of IRIS worsening the meningitis
  • TMP-SMX covers PCP/Toxo but does NOT treat Cryptococcus
India ink in CSF = Cryptococcus until proven otherwise. Treatment = AmB + flucytosine (not fluconazole alone). Hold ART for 4–6 weeks to prevent IRIS.

9. Mycobacterium avium complex (MAC)

Key facts

  • CD4 at diagnosis: average 10/μL — this is a very late complication
  • Prophylaxis starts at CD4 <50/μL
  • Most common presentation: disseminated disease with fever, weight loss, night sweats
  • ≥85% are mycobacteremic
  • Common in US patients; rare in Africa
  • Source: environmental (soil, water) — not person-to-person

Treatment

  • Macrolide (clarithromycin) + ethambutol = backbone
  • Consider adding rifabutin, ciprofloxacin, or amikacin for extensive disease
  • Continue until: symptoms resolve + cultures negative + CD4 >100 for 3–6 months on ART
  • Prophylaxis: azithromycin 1200 mg/week or clarithromycin 500 mg bid
MAC prophylaxis threshold = CD4 <50. MAC disease average CD4 = 10. If the question says CD4 = 50 and asks when to start MAC prophylaxis — the threshold is strictly <50, so at exactly 50 you haven't crossed it yet. The answer for CD4 at which MAC becomes a major risk = 50/μL.

10. JC virus — Progressive Multifocal Leukoencephalopathy (PML)

The question scenario (Image 2): 50yo HIV, refuses treatment, right hand weakness + speech difficulty, white matter lesions on MRI, CSF: elevated cells, normal glucose, elevated protein

  • Long-standing HIV + untreated = very low CD4
  • White matter lesions in frontal area = subcortical demyelination
  • CSF showing mononuclear cells, elevated protein, normal glucose
  • Non-focal MRI (multiple non-enhancing white matter lesions) → JC virus / PML

PML presentation

  • Late AIDS complication: 1–4% of AIDS patients
  • Multifocal neurologic deficits: hemiparesis, aphasia, visual field defects, ataxia
  • Headache, fever, vomiting = RARE → suggests alternate diagnosis
  • ~20% experience seizures
  • Progressive over weeks-months

Differentiating PML from other CNS causes

PML key facts

  • Caused by JC virus (human polyomavirus)
  • ~80% of adults have antibodies (prior exposure) — most never develop disease
  • Reactivation of latent virus when immune system collapses
  • Lesions: demyelination in subcortical white matter, occipital and parietal predilection
  • MRI: non-enhancing white matter lesions on T2, no mass effect
  • No specific antiviral treatment — only ART improves prognosis
  • Pre-ART: median survival 3–6 months. With ART: median survival ~2 years
  • IRIS paradox: PML may worsen transiently after starting ART
PML = non-enhancing white matter lesions + progressive neuro deficits + JC virus PCR in CSF. No fever. No ring enhancement (that's Toxo). No mass effect. Diagnosis confirmed by JC PCR in CSF (76% sensitivity, ~100% specificity).

11. EBV manifestation in HIV — Oral Hairy Leukoplakia

The question (Image 8): What is the clinical manifestation of EBV in HIV patients?

  • Answer: Oral Hairy Leukoplakia
  • NOT polyarthritis (that's HBV or reactive arthritis)
  • NOT pemphigus (autoimmune)
  • NOT autoimmune parotitis

Oral Hairy Leukoplakia (OHL)

  • Caused by florid EBV replication
  • Location: lateral borders of tongue (and adjacent buccal mucosa)
  • Appearance: white, frond-like, cannot be scraped off (vs. thrush which can)
  • Usually asymptomatic
  • NOT premalignant
  • CD4 typically <300/μL
  • Signals significant immunosuppression

Other EBV-related conditions in HIV

  • EBV-related B cell lymphomas (especially CNS primary lymphoma)
  • CNS lymphoma: ~100% EBV positive
  • Burkitt's lymphoma: ~50% EBV positive in HIV setting
  • Immunoblastic lymphoma: often EBV positive
  • Lymphocytic interstitial pneumonitis (LIP) — associated with EBV + HIV (common in children)
EBV in HIV = Oral Hairy Leukoplakia (lateral tongue, white fronds, cannot scrape off) OR EBV-associated CNS lymphoma. Thrush = Candida (can scrape off). Distinguish on appearance and location.

12. Immune Reconstitution Inflammatory Syndrome (IRIS)

The question scenario (Image 11): Patient from Nigeria, CD4=43, 200K viral load, started antibiotics → 3 weeks later returns with fever + confusion, CT = no focal lesion. What is cause?

  • The timeline: started antiretroviral therapy → immune system begins recovering → mounts inflammatory response against previously undetected/under-treated infection
  • Answer: IRIS
  • Most likely underlying infection: TB (or MAC, Cryptococcus) that was sub-clinical

IRIS — definition and mechanism

  • Paradoxical worsening of clinical condition after starting ART
  • Occurs as immune system recovers → mounts exaggerated response to residual antigens
  • Mechanism: type IV hypersensitivity-like reaction to microbial antigens
  • Two types:
    • Paradoxical IRIS — worsening of known, previously treated infection
    • Unmasking IRIS — reveals previously undiagnosed infection

IRIS characteristics

  • Occurs 2 weeks to 2 years after starting ART
  • Most common in patients with CD4 <50 who experience rapid drop in viral load
  • Affects 10–30% of patients starting ART
  • Most common pathogens: TB, MAC, Cryptococcus, CMV, HHV
  • Signs: fever, lymphadenitis, pulmonary infiltrates, hepatitis, increased ICP
  • Can be fatal in severe cases

Management

  • Continue ART (do not stop)
  • Continue antimicrobials for the underlying infection
  • Severe cases: glucocorticoids to blunt inflammation
  • Prevention: treat active OIs before starting ART, delay ART 2–8 weeks after TB therapy initiation (if CD4 ≥50)
  • For Cryptococcal meningitis: delay ART 4–6 weeks
IRIS clue: patient starts ART (or antibiotic) → appears to worsen → no new focal lesion on CT → fever + confusion. Think IRIS especially if CD4 was very low (<50) and viral load drops rapidly. Most dangerous IRIS = CNS IRIS from TB or Cryptococcus.

13. Non-AIDS chronic complications — why do they occur?

The question (Image 17): HIV patients develop cardiovascular, renal, cognitive diseases — why?

  • Answer: Persistent immune activation and chronic inflammation
  • NOT due to protease inhibitor side effects alone
  • NOT just low CD4 without treatment
  • These complications occur EVEN in patients with suppressed viral loads on ART
ComplicationMechanismDetails
Cardiovascular disease Chronic immune activation → coagulation, inflammation; also lipid effects of some ART drugs 3.5/1000 patient-years MI rate; CD4 <500 = risk factor comparable to smoking
HIV-associated nephropathy (HIVAN) Direct HIV infection of kidney epithelial cells; APOL1 gene variants (African descent) Focal segmental glomerulosclerosis; proteinuria, no hypertension, no edema
Neurocognitive disorders (HAND) Viral proteins (gp120, Tat) → neurotoxic; macrophage activation in CNS; low CD4 nadir ~50% mild-moderate impairment even on ART; spectrum from ANI to dementia
Lipodystrophy Primarily thymidine analogues (zidovudine, stavudine) + protease inhibitors Fat redistribution: truncal obesity + peripheral wasting + metabolic syndrome
Bone fragility / osteoporosis Chronic immune activation; tenofovir effects on bone 2–6% decrease in bone mineral density in first 2 years of ART
Non-AIDS complications now account for ~75% of deaths in HIV-positive patients on ART. Cardiovascular and cancer (non-AIDS) are leading causes. This is the "accelerated aging" syndrome driven by chronic immune activation, NOT just the virus or drug toxicity alone.

14. Drug side effects — TMP-SMX & others

The question (Image 15): AIDS patient CD4=100, receiving TMP-SMX for PCP. Which metabolic side effect is expected?

  • Answer: Hyperkalemia
  • Mechanism: TMP-SMX blocks sodium channels in renal collecting tubule → reduces potassium secretion → hyperkalemia
  • Also: competes with creatinine for tubular secretion → raises serum creatinine (not true kidney injury)
DrugKey Side Effects for BoardsMechanism
TMP-SMX Rash (20–85%), bone marrow suppression, hyperkalemia, elevated creatinine Blocks tubular K⁺ secretion; competes with creatinine at tubule
Abacavir Hypersensitivity reaction (can be fatal on rechallenge) HLA-B5701 associated; screen before use
Tenofovir disoproxil (TDF) Nephrotoxicity, bone loss (osteomalacia) Mitochondrial toxicity in renal tubules; Fanconi syndrome
Protease inhibitors Lipodystrophy, hyperlipidemia, hyperglycemia, hepatotoxicity Inhibit CYP3A4 → multiple drug interactions
Efavirenz CNS effects (nightmares, depression), rash, teratogenic (avoid in pregnancy) NNRTI; CYP2B6 polymorphisms affect metabolism
Zidovudine Anemia (macrocytic, elevated MCV), myopathy, bone marrow suppression Nucleoside analogue; mitochondrial DNA polymerase inhibition
Dapsone (alternative to TMP-SMX) Hemolytic anemia (G6PD deficiency), methemoglobinemia Check G6PD before use
TMP-SMX hyperkalemia mechanism: trimethoprim acts like amiloride — blocks epithelial sodium channels (ENaC) in collecting duct → reduces potassium excretion → hyperkalemia. This is especially important at high doses used for PCP treatment.
