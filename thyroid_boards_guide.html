Anatomy & Development

Gross Anatomy

  • Two lobes connected by an isthmus; anterior to trachea between cricoid cartilage and suprasternal notch
  • Normal weight: 12–20 g; highly vascular, soft consistency; each lobe ~4 cm long
  • Four parathyroid glands on posterior poles — must be identified and preserved during thyroid surgery
  • Recurrent laryngeal nerves traverse lateral borders — injury causes unilateral or bilateral vocal cord paralysis
  • Goiter on clinical exam: lateral lobe volume greater than the patient’s thumb; ultrasound total volume >30 mL = abnormal

Embryology & Migration

  • Develops from the floor of the primitive pharynx at the foramen cecum (junction of anterior 2/3 and posterior 1/3 of tongue) during the 3rd week of gestation
  • Migrates caudally along the thyroglossal duct to final position in the neck; duct normally obliterates
  • Ectopic thyroid tissue: lingual thyroid (base of tongue, most common) or anywhere along thyroglossal tract
  • Thyroglossal duct cysts: midline neck mass that moves upward with tongue protrusion — distinguishing feature
  • Thyroid hormone synthesis begins at ~11 weeks’ gestation; fetal brain depends on maternal T4 until then
  • Transcription factors for thyroid development: TTF1 (NKX2-1), TTF2 (FOXE1), PAX8, NKX2-5 — mutations cause agenesis or ectopic thyroid with congenital hypothyroidism

C Cells (Parafollicular Cells)

  • Derived from neural crest cells via ultimobranchial body; located at junction of upper 1/3 and lower 2/3 of each lobe
  • Produce calcitonin — lowers serum calcium but plays minimal physiologic role in calcium homeostasis in humans
  • Clinically critical: origin of medullary thyroid carcinoma (MTC); calcitonin is the tumor marker

Follicle Architecture

  • Functional unit: spherical follicle — single layer of polarized follicular epithelial cells surrounding a central lumen of colloid
  • Colloid: viscous fluid containing large amounts of thyroglobulin (Tg), the scaffold for thyroid hormone synthesis
  • Follicular cells are polarized: basolateral surface (bloodstream-facing) has TSH-R and NIS; apical surface (lumen-facing) has pendrin and TPO
  • Iodide enters basolaterally via NIS, is oxidized and attached to Tg apically, stored as colloid, then endocytosed and cleaved to release T4/T3 into bloodstream
🔔 Congenital Hypothyroidism — Neonatal Screening
Congenital hypothyroidism: ~1 in 2000–4000 newborns. Most common cause of preventable intellectual disability. Neonatal screening (heel-prick TSH or T4) is universal in industrialized countries. LT4 10–15 μg/kg/day started within days of birth prevents irreversible neurologic damage. Early treatment = normal IQ.

Thyroid Hormone Synthesis

Step-by-Step Synthesis Pathway

Step 1 — Iodide Uptake (NIS)

NIS (Na⁺/I⁻ symporter) on basolateral membrane; electrogenic (2 Na⁺ per I⁻); concentrates iodide 20–40× above plasma. Driven by Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase gradient. Upregulated by TSH and iodine deficiency; downregulated by excess iodide and certain drugs. NIS is the target of radioiodine (¹³¹I) therapy — concentrated exclusively in functional thyroid tissue.

Step 2 — Apical Transport (Pendrin)

Pendrin (SLC26A4) on the apical membrane transports iodide into the follicular lumen. Mutation = Pendred syndrome: defective organification + goiter + sensorineural deafness (autosomal recessive). Also expressed in cochlea where it regulates endolymph composition.

Step 3 — Organification

TPO (thyroid peroxidase) oxidizes iodide using H₂O₂ generated by DUOX1/DUOX2 + DUOXA. Reactive iodine covalently attaches to tyrosyl residues of Tg — forming MIT (monoiodotyrosine) and DIT (diiodotyrosine). TPO mutations → dyshormonogenesis + goiter.

Step 4 — Coupling

TPO also catalyzes the phenoxy ether bond coupling reaction within the Tg molecule: MIT + DIT → T3; DIT + DIT → T4. Tg is a 660 kDa homodimeric glycoprotein (2769 amino acids, 134 tyrosines of which ~40 are iodinated, ~4–8 form T4/T3). Thyroid makes predominantly T4.

Step 5 — Storage & Secretion

Tg-bound T4/T3 is stored in follicular lumen as colloid. TSH stimulus → Tg endocytosed → lysosomal proteolysis → T4 and T3 released into bloodstream. Uncoupled MIT and DIT recycled by iodotyrosine deiodinase (DEHAL1) to recover iodide.

Wolff-Chaikoff Effect

Acute large iodide load transiently inhibits organification — an autoregulatory response. Normal gland escapes within 1–2 weeks by downregulating NIS (reducing intracellular iodide). In Hashimoto’s or post-RAI thyroid: cannot escape → sustained hypothyroidism from iodine overload (e.g., amiodarone, contrast agents).

TSH Action on the Thyroid

  • TSH-R: 7-transmembrane GPCR coupled to Gsα → activates adenylyl cyclase → cAMP → PKA → promotes thyroid cell growth, iodide uptake, organification, coupling, secretion
  • Also couples to Gq/phospholipase C pathway at high TSH levels → IP3/DAG → PKC activation (less dominant)
  • Loss-of-function TSH-R mutations: thyroid hypoplasia + congenital hypothyroidism (TSH levels very high, gland unresponsive)
  • Gain-of-function TSH-R mutations: constitutive activation → sporadic/familial hyperthyroidism with thyroid autonomy; somatic mutations found in >90% of toxic adenomas
  • TSI (thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulins) in Graves’ disease mimic TSH → bind TSH-R → constitutive hormone excess

Iodine Physiology

  • RDA: 150 μg/day adults; 220 μg/day pregnancy; 290 μg/day breastfeeding (WHO recommends 250 μg for pregnancy/lactation)
  • Normal 24-hour RAI uptake: 10–25% in iodine-replete state; reflects proportion of plasma iodide trapped by the gland
  • Urinary iodine >100 μg/L = population iodine sufficient
  • Iodine deficiency → goiter (upregulated NIS + TSH-driven growth), cretinism (severe deficiency in early life → irreversible intellectual disability + growth retardation), hypothyroidism
  • Iodine excess → Wolff-Chaikoff effect → ↑ risk of autoimmune thyroid disease; Jod-Basedow effect (hyperthyroidism in pre-existing MNG)
⚠ Congenital Hormone Synthesis Defects (Dyshormonogenesis)
Mutations in: TPO or Tg (most common), DUOX2/DUOXA2, DEHAL1, pendrin, NIS, TSH-R, thyroid transcription factors. All cause congenital goitrous hypothyroidism with elevated TSH. Tg goiter = most radio-dense on scan (TSH-driven follicular hyperplasia + Tg accumulation).

Thyroid Axis & Hormone Action

HPT Axis (Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Thyroid)

  • TRH (thyrotropin-releasing hormone, tripeptide pyroglutamyl-histidyl-proline): synthesized in paraventricular nucleus of hypothalamus → released into portal circulation → binds GPCR on pituitary thyrotropes → ↑ TSH synthesis and glycosylation + ↑ TSH secretion
  • TSH (31 kDa glycoprotein) → binds TSH-R on thyroid follicular cells → ↑ T4 and T3 synthesis + secretion
  • Negative feedback: T3/T4 (especially via TRβ2) directly suppress TRH gene transcription in hypothalamus and TSH β-subunit gene in pituitary; also suppresses TRH-R expression on pituitary
  • TSH is secreted in a pulsatile, diurnal pattern: highest at night (2–4 AM), lowest in late afternoon; blunted in illness
  • TSH half-life: ~50 min; carbohydrate modifications regulated by TRH affect bioactivity
  • Pharmacologic TSH suppressors: dopamine, glucocorticoids (high dose), somatostatin, bexarotene
Thyroid hormone is the DOMINANT regulator of TSH
The log-linear inverse relationship between TSH and free T4 means small changes in free T4 cause large proportional changes in TSH. This is why TSH is the most sensitive test — it amplifies subtle thyroid dysfunction.

Resistance to Thyroid Hormone (RTH)

RTH-β (Classic RTH)

Autosomal dominant mutations in TRβ ligand-binding domain → mutant receptor retains co-repressor binding even with T3 — dominant negative effect (mutant inhibits wild-type TRβ function). Labs: ↑↑ free T4/T3 + inappropriately normal or elevated TSH. Clinical: goiter, ADHD, mild IQ reduction, delayed bone maturation, tachycardia. Most patients are euthyroid in tissues expressing TRα1 (not affected by TRβ mutation). Tx: usually none — avoid misdiagnosing as hyperthyroid.

RTH-α

Mutations in TRα. Features of selective tissue hypothyroidism in TRα-expressing organs. Clinical: growth retardation, skeletal dysplasia (short limbs, epiphyseal dysgenesis), severe constipation, bradycardia, delayed neurocognitive development. Labs: normal TSH + low-normal T4 + elevated T3 (T4→T3 conversion enhanced to compensate). Thyroxine treatment may improve manifestations.

⚠ RTH-β vs TSH-Secreting Pituitary Adenoma
Both: elevated free T4/T3 + non-suppressed TSH. Distinguish by: TSH-secreting adenoma = elevated α-subunit of TSH, pituitary mass on MRI, locally invasive, no family history. RTH-β = normal α-subunit, no pituitary mass, similar labs in family members, TRβ gene mutation on sequencing. Management is completely different.

Binding Protein Disorders

  • TBG deficiency (X-linked): low total T4/T3, normal free T4, normal TSH → patient is euthyroid. Do NOT treat — giving LT4 would cause thyrotoxicosis
  • TBG excess (estrogen, pregnancy, oral estrogens, methadone, heroin): high total T4/T3, normal free T4, normal TSH → euthyroid. Do not treat
  • FDH (Familial Dysalbuminemic Hyperthyroxinemia): autosomal dominant albumin mutations (R218H most common) → albumin binds T4 with abnormally high affinity → high total T4, normal free T4 (by dialysis), normal TSH. Many immunoassays give falsely elevated “free T4” — equilibrium dialysis is the gold standard to distinguish from true hyperthyroidism
  • Salicylates/phenytoin: displace T4/T3 from binding proteins → transient rise in free T4, then TSH-mediated restoration of euthyroid state (new steady state with lower total T4 but normal free T4 and TSH)

Deiodinase Enzymes

Type I (DIO1)

Liver, kidney, thyroid. Low-affinity for T4. Converts T4→T3 (outer ring, 5’-deiodination) — major source of circulating T3. Inhibited by: PTU, propranolol (high dose), amiodarone, glucocorticoids, iodinated contrast, systemic illness, fasting. PTU uniquely blocks this (methimazole does not).

Type II (DIO2)

Pituitary, CNS, brown adipose, thyroid, skeletal muscle. High affinity for T4 → generates local T3 for intracellular use. Critical for LT4 replacement efficacy. Upregulated in hypothyroidism → increased T4→T3 conversion in brain/pituitary (this is why T3 is often normal in hypothyroid patients).

Type III (DIO3)

Inner ring deiodination → produces reverse T3 (rT3, metabolically inactive) from T4; inactivates T3 to T2. Active in placenta (protects fetus), skin. Massively upregulated in sick euthyroid syndrome → ↓T3, ↑rT3. Overexpression in infantile hemangiomas → consumptive hypothyroidism (treatment-resistant).

Cell Entry — MCT8 Transporter

  • T4 and T3 enter cells via passive diffusion (lipophilic) AND specific transporters: MCT8, MCT10, OATP1C1
  • MCT8 mutation (X-linked, Allan-Herndon-Dudley syndrome): T4 cannot enter CNS cells (brain is MCT8-dependent) → T4 shunted to peripheral T4→T3 conversion → paradoxical labs: LOW T4 + HIGH T3 + HIGH TSH
  • Clinical: severe psychomotor retardation, hypotonia, absent speech, peripheral thyrotoxicosis features (tachycardia, hyperthermia, muscle wasting)
  • After cell entry: primarily act via nuclear TRs; also nongenomic effects on plasma membrane, mitochondria (rapid actions)
⚠ MCT8 Labs are Paradoxical — Board Trap
MCT8 mutation → LOW T4 + HIGH T3 + HIGH TSH. This is the OPPOSITE of what you expect in hypothyroidism (where TSH is high but T4 is low and T3 is usually normal). The elevated T3 results from increased peripheral conversion of the excess T4 that cannot enter brain cells.

Thyroid Function in Pregnancy

Five Factors Altering Thyroid Function in Pregnancy

1. hCG (1st Trimester)

hCG shares α-subunit with TSH and weakly stimulates TSH-R. Peaks at 8–10 weeks → mild thyroid stimulation → reciprocal fall in TSH (often to low-normal or suppressed). Rare TSH-R variants with enhanced hCG affinity → gestational thyrotoxicosis + hyperemesis gravidarum. Resolves as hCG falls after 1st trimester.

2. TBG Rise (Sustained)

Estrogen → increased TBG sialylation → slower TBG clearance → total TBG doubles by 2nd trimester. Result: total T4 and T3 rise ~1.5× throughout pregnancy. Free T4 initially normal, then progressively decreases — often below nonpregnant range in 3rd trimester. Do not mistake high total T4 for hyperthyroidism.

3. Immune Modifications

Pregnancy-induced immune tolerance (to prevent fetal rejection): can cause improvement of Graves’ disease in later pregnancy (TRAb/TSI levels often fall). Postpartum period: immune rebound → major relapse risk for Graves’ + onset of postpartum thyroiditis. Graves’ neonatal thyrotoxicosis: maternal TSI crosses placenta → stimulates fetal TSH-R.

4. Placental Type III Deiodinase

Placenta expresses abundant type III deiodinase → converts T4→rT3 and T3→T2 (protects fetus from excess maternal thyroid hormone). Net effect: increased maternal thyroid hormone requirements. Athyreotic women require ~45% more LT4; thyroid-intact women with Hashimoto’s require less.

5. Increased Renal Iodide Clearance

GFR increases in pregnancy → increased urinary iodide excretion. In areas of marginal iodine sufficiency: this plus fetal demand → iodine deficiency → goiter (maternal + fetal). WHO recommends 250 μg/day iodine during pregnancy and lactation. Prenatal vitamins: should contain at least 150 μg/day as potassium iodide.

Lab Ranges in Pregnancy

  • Use trimester-specific TSH reference ranges when available (laboratory-specific, assay-specific)
  • First trimester: TSH lower limit ↓ ~0.4 mIU/L (due to hCG); upper limit ↓ ~4.0 mIU/L (approximate)
  • Second and third trimesters: TSH range approximately same as nonpregnant
  • Total T4/T3: ~1.5× higher throughout; do NOT use as index of thyroid status
  • Free T4: measured by dialysis is best; immunoassay unreliable in late pregnancy (methodologic interference)
⚠ Hyperemesis Gravidarum — Do NOT Give Antithyroid Drugs
Gestational thyrotoxicosis from hCG: TSH suppressed, T4/T3 elevated, severe nausea/vomiting. No Graves’ features (no ophthalmopathy, no TRAb). Treatment: IV fluid + antiemetics. Antithyroid drugs are NOT indicated unless true Graves’ disease is diagnosed concurrently. The condition is self-limiting as hCG falls.

LT4 Management in Hypothyroid Pregnant Women

  • Maternal hypothyroidism associated with: miscarriage, preterm delivery, preeclampsia, gestational hypertension, impaired fetal neurodevelopment
  • As soon as pregnancy confirmed: immediately increase LT4 to 9 doses/week (take a double dose on 2 nonconsecutive days per week — this approximates the necessary ~45% increase)
  • Monitor TSH: every 4 weeks in first half of pregnancy; every 6–8 weeks after 20 weeks
  • Goal TSH: lower half of trimester-specific range, or <2.5 mIU/L (pre-conception target also <2.5)
  • After delivery: return to pre-pregnancy dose; recheck TSH at 6 weeks postpartum
  • Separate LT4 from iron/calcium supplements (impair absorption): take on empty stomach, 30 min before other medications

Laboratory Evaluation

TSH — First-Line Test

  • TSH is the most sensitive and specific initial test for primary thyroid dysfunction; used for both screening and evaluation of symptomatic patients
  • Normal TSH effectively excludes primary thyroid disease (does NOT exclude central/secondary hypothyroidism)
  • Suppressed TSH (<0.01 mIU/L): usually indicates thyrotoxicosis (primary or central excess)
  • Elevated TSH: primary hypothyroidism (most common); also RTH-β, TSH-secreting adenoma (rare), recovery from NTI, assay artifact
  • Subnormal TSH (0.01–0.4 mIU/L): 1st trimester pregnancy (hCG), recovery from treated hyperthyroidism, high-dose glucocorticoids or dopamine, NTI in 10%
  • Third-generation TSH assays: functional sensitivity <0.01 mIU/L; sufficient for clinical use; TRH stimulation test is now obsolete
🚨 Biotin Interference — Critical Pearl
Most TSH immunoassays use biotinylated antibodies with streptavidin solid phase. Biotin (≥1000μg/day, including many hair/nail supplement doses) falsely suppresses TSH + falsely elevates free T4/T3. The free biotin in serum competes with biotinylated antibodies → assay failure. Stop biotin for at least 2 days before testing (effect clears within 18h of last dose). Always ask about biotin use before acting on unexpected thyroid results.

Free Thyroid Hormone Measurement

  • Any abnormal TSH → confirm with free T4 (unbound T4) measurement before initiating treatment
  • Equilibrium dialysis: gold standard — physically separates free from bound fraction; expensive; not routine
  • T4 immunoassay with T4 analogue: most commonly used; affected by protein abnormalities (FDH, pregnancy, severe illness)
  • T3 toxicosis (2–5% of thyrotoxicosis): suppressed TSH + normal free T4 → must measure free T3; more common in iodine-deficient areas, toxic adenomas, early Graves’
  • T3 measurement in hypothyroidism: NOT indicated — often normal due to compensatory type II deiodinase upregulation (does not reflect thyroid status)

Autoimmune Thyroid Markers

  • TPO antibodies (anti-TPO): present in >95% of Hashimoto’s and ~80% of Graves’; also in 5–15% of euthyroid women (↑ risk of future hypothyroidism and postpartum thyroiditis). Clinically most useful autoimmune marker; complement-fixing
  • Tg antibodies (anti-Tg): present in fewer patients; less specific; important because they interfere with Tg measurement — must be checked when monitoring thyroid cancer via Tg levels (falsely low Tg if anti-Tg present)
  • TRAb (TSH receptor antibodies): measured by competition assay (labeled TSH displaced from TSH-R); elevated in Graves’; TRAb disappearance after antithyroid drugs predicts remission; high maternal TRAb (>3× ULN in 3rd trimester) predicts fetal/neonatal thyrotoxicosis
  • Serum thyroglobulin (Tg): elevated in all causes of thyrotoxicosis except thyrotoxicosis factitia (exogenous hormone suppresses endogenous Tg production and release). Post-thyroidectomy + RAI ablation: Tg <0.2 ng/mL = remission marker for differentiated thyroid cancer

Hashimoto’s Thyroiditis — Pathogenesis

  • Tissue destruction primarily by CD8⁺ cytotoxic T lymphocytes; CD4⁺ T cells provide cytokine help
  • Cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1β, IFN-γ) → thyroid cell apoptosis via Fas (CD95) pathway and perforin/granzyme pathway; also induce thyroid expression of HLA class II, adhesion molecules, CD40, iNOS
  • TPO and Tg antibodies: useful markers; activate complement; do NOT directly cause hypothyroidism (T-cell-mediated injury is required); transplacental passage has no effect on fetal thyroid
  • TSH-R blocking antibodies (~20% of patients, especially Asians): cause hypothyroidism + atrophy rather than Hashimoto’s goiter; transplacental passage → transient neonatal hypothyroidism
  • Genetics: HLA-DR3, DR4, DR5 (Caucasians); CTLA-4, PTPN22, CD25, CD40 polymorphisms; Down syndrome (Chr 21); Turner syndrome (X chromosome factors)
  • Environment: high iodine ↑ risk; low selenium ↑ risk; alcohol protective; smoking cessation ↑ transient risk; postpartum immune rebound
Hashimoto’s Associated Autoimmune Diseases
Vitiligo, pernicious anemia (anti-IF antibodies + atrophic gastritis), Addison’s disease (Schmidt syndrome = autoimmune thyroid + Addison’s), alopecia areata, type 1 DM. Autoimmune polyendocrine syndrome type 2 (APS-2): autoimmune thyroid disease 70%, T1DM 40%, Addison’s 40%. Also: celiac disease (10%), rheumatoid arthritis, SLE, Sjögren’s, myasthenia gravis.

Clinical Manifestations of Hypothyroidism

Symptoms (by frequency)
  • Tiredness, weakness, lethargy
  • Dry, coarse skin and hair; hair loss
  • Feeling cold (cold intolerance)
  • Difficulty concentrating, poor memory, psychomotor slowing
  • Constipation; weight gain despite poor appetite
  • Dyspnea on exertion; hoarse voice (myxedematous vocal cords)
  • Menorrhagia (later oligomenorrhea/amenorrhea), infertility
  • Paresthesia (carpal tunnel, tarsal tunnel); impaired hearing
  • Depression; decreased libido
Signs
  • Dry, rough, pale, cool skin; carotenemia (yellow tint)
  • Periorbital puffiness; non-pitting edema face/hands/feet (myxedema — glycosaminoglycan accumulation)
  • Diffuse alopecia; thinning of outer 1/3 eyebrows
  • Bradycardia; hypertension (diastolic); cardiac enlargement (effusion or cardiomyopathy)
  • Delayed tendon reflex relaxation (“hung-up” reflex — most specific sign)
  • Goiter (Hashimoto’s) or small/absent gland (atrophic thyroiditis, post-RAI)
  • Serous effusions: pericardial (~30%), pleural, ascites
  • Proximal muscle weakness; pseudomyotonia; elevated CPK
Hypothyroidism Labs
Elevated TSH; low free T4. Additional: elevated CPK (— myopathy); elevated LDL + TG (— impaired LDL receptor expression); elevated homocysteine; normocytic or macrocytic anemia (B12/folate deficiency with pernicious anemia). T3 measurement NOT indicated — normal in ~25% of hypothyroid patients.

⚠ Causes of Increased LT4 Requirements
Malabsorption: celiac disease, H. pylori gastritis, small bowel bypass, atrophic gastritis. Drugs that impair T4 absorption: cholestyramine/colestipol, ferrous sulfate, calcium carbonate, sevelamer, sucralfate, antacids (Ca/Mg-containing), bile acid sequestrants, PPIs (reduce gastric acid → impair ionization). Drugs that increase T4 clearance: rifampicin, carbamazepine, phenytoin, phenobarbital, sertraline, TKIs, ritonavir. Increased TBG: oral estrogens (not transdermal), SERMs (tamoxifen, raloxifene). Also: pregnancy (immediate need for dose ↑).

Subclinical Hypothyroidism

  • Definition: elevated TSH + normal free T4 + few/no symptoms; prevalence 6–8% in women, 3% in men (higher in elderly)
  • Annual progression to overt hypothyroidism: ~4% (higher when TPO antibodies positive)
  • Treat with LT4 when: (1) TSH >10 mIU/L, (2) pregnant or planning pregnancy, (3) symptomatic young/middle-aged patient with CV risk, (4) TPO antibody positive
  • First confirm: repeat TSH at 3 months (transient causes: recovery from illness, subacute thyroiditis, drug effect)
  • For TSH 4–10 mIU/L without symptoms: annual monitoring without treatment is appropriate in most adults

Myxedema Coma

🚨 Emergency — Mortality 20–40% Even with Treatment
Almost exclusively in elderly patients. Precipitants: sedatives (particularly in hospital), anesthesia, antidepressants, infections, CHF, MI, stroke, GI bleeding, cold exposure. Outcome does NOT correlate with TSH or T4 levels.
Clinical Features
  • Reduced/altered consciousness → coma; seizures
  • Profound hypothermia (can reach 23°C/74°F)
  • Hypoventilation → hypoxia, hypercapnia, respiratory acidosis
  • Hypoglycemia (impaired gluconeogenesis)
  • Dilutional hyponatremia (impaired free water excretion)
  • Bradycardia; hypotension; ileus; urinary retention
  • All classic signs of hypothyroidism
Treatment Protocol
  • IV LT4: 200–400 μg loading dose IV → then 1.6 μg/kg/day IV (75% of usual dose when using IV vs PO)
  • IV T3: 5–20 μg IV loading → 2.5–10 μg q8h (risk: precipitate arrhythmia; use cautiously)
  • Hydrocortisone 50–100 mg IV every 6–8h (assume impaired adrenal reserve until proven otherwise)
  • Warming: ONLY active external warming if temp <30°C; otherwise use space blankets (passive — prevent further heat loss; active warming at >30°C → peripheral vasodilation → cardiovascular collapse)
  • Ventilatory support (hypercapnic respiratory failure in first 24–48h)
  • Correct hypoglycemia; avoid hypotonic fluids (worsen hyponatremia)
  • Empirical broad-spectrum antibiotics (until infection excluded)
  • Avoid CNS depressants (especially sedatives, opioids)

Hyperthyroidism & Thyrotoxicosis

Definitions & Causes

  • Thyrotoxicosis: clinical syndrome of excess thyroid hormone effect regardless of cause
  • Hyperthyroidism: specifically, overproduction of hormone by the thyroid gland itself
  • Major causes: Graves’ disease (~60–80%), toxic MNG, toxic adenoma; rare: TSH-secreting adenoma, hCG-mediated, thyroiditis, struma ovarii, metastatic functioning thyroid cancer

Graves’ Disease — Epidemiology & Pathogenesis

  • Most common cause of hyperthyroidism; affects up to 2% of women, 10× less frequent in men
  • Typical onset: 20–50 years; can occur in elderly (often atypical/apathetic presentation)
  • Genetics: HLA-DR3 in Caucasians; CTLA-4, CD25, CD40, PTPN22, FCRL3, TSH-R gene polymorphisms; concordance ~20–30% in MZ twins vs <5% DZ
  • Environmental triggers: psychosocial stress, postpartum immune rebound, smoking (especially for ophthalmopathy), sudden iodine increase (Jod-Basedow), immune reconstitution (HAART, alemtuzumab), immune checkpoint inhibitors (pembrolizumab, ipilimumab)
  • Mechanism: B cells in thyroid, bone marrow, and lymph nodes synthesize TSI (thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulins) that bind and activate TSH-R → constitutive T4/T3 overproduction + thyroid growth (diffuse goiter)

Clinical Features of Thyrotoxicosis

Symptoms
  • Hyperactivity, nervousness, irritability, anxiety
  • Heat intolerance + excessive sweating
  • Palpitations; fatigue + proximal muscle weakness
  • Weight loss with increased or normal appetite (weight gain in ~5% — paradoxical)
  • Diarrhea, increased bowel frequency
  • Oligomenorrhea; decreased libido; infertility
  • Polyuria, polydipsia
Signs
  • Tachycardia (most common sign); atrial fibrillation in elderly
  • Diffuse goiter (Graves’); bruit over gland (hypervascular)
  • Fine tremor (outstretched fingers); hyperreflexia
  • Warm, smooth, moist skin; diffuse alopecia
  • Lid retraction (Dalrymple’s sign); lid lag (von Graefe’s sign)
  • Proximal myopathy (difficulty rising from chair); wasting
  • Osteopenia with long-standing disease; mild hypercalcemia (~20%)
Apathetic Thyrotoxicosis (Elderly)
Elderly patients may present with only fatigue, weight loss, and atrial fibrillation — NO classic hyperadrenergic features. High index of suspicion required. Cardioversion after restoring euthyroidism: AF converts to sinus rhythm in up to 75% without underlying cardiac disease.

Graves’-Specific Features

Ophthalmopathy (TED)

Present in ~25–30% with significant features; occurs in 10% without hyperthyroidism. Mechanism: T cells infiltrate orbital tissues expressing TSH-R → cytokines activate orbital fibroblasts → glycosaminoglycan (hyaluronate) accumulation → extraocular muscle swelling + proptosis; late: irreversible fibrosis. Aberrant IGF-1R signaling also implicated.

Features: gritty sensation, excess tearing, proptosis (>22 mm), periorbital edema, diplopia, corneal exposure. Sight-threatening: optic nerve compression (emergency) or corneal breakdown.

Smoking: major risk factor for development and severity — counsel all patients to stop.

CAS (Clinical Activity Score)

Score 1 point each for: spontaneous pain, pain on gaze, eyelid erythema, conjunctival injection, chemosis, caruncle edema, eyelid edema. CAS ≥3 = active disease → responds to immunosuppression. CAS <3 = inactive → surgery for stable deficits.

NO SPECS grading (alternative): 0=none, 1=only signs, 2=soft tissue, 3=proptosis, 4=extraocular muscle, 5=cornea, 6=sight.

Dermopathy & Acropachy

Thyroid dermopathy (pretibial myxedema): <5% of Graves’; almost always associated with moderate/severe ophthalmopathy; noninflamed indurated pink-purple “orange peel” plaques over anterior/lateral lower legs (rarely upper arms/face). Same mechanism as ophthalmopathy (TSH-R-expressing fibroblasts).

Thyroid acropachy: rare (<1%); form of clubbing; almost always with dermopathy; no known treatment.

Antithyroid Drug Therapy

Thionamides: inhibit TPO → block iodide organification and coupling. Also reduce thyroid antibody titers and promote immune tolerance → may induce remission in Graves’.

Rate: <1% with MMI, <0.5% with PTU. Onset is abrupt and idiosyncratic — routine WBC monitoring is NOT helpful (agranulocytosis occurs between checks). Vasculitis (rare, PTU > MMI), cholestasis (MMI), neonatal hypothyroidism if mother on high-dose drugs.

Radioiodine (¹³¹I) Therapy

  • Preferred first-line in North America for most Graves’ patients; antithyroid drugs predominate in Europe/Asia
  • Standard dose: 370–555 MBq (10–15 mCi); dose calculated from goiter size, RAIU, and severity
  • Pretreatment: antithyroid drugs for 1 month (euthyroid) in elderly/cardiac patients; stop MMI 2–3 days before ¹³¹I (radioprotective effect clears); resume 3–7 days after if needed
  • PTU: longer radioprotective effect — must stop earlier (at least 1 week) or use higher ¹³¹I dose
  • Hypothyroidism: at least 10–20% by 1 year; ~5%/year thereafter; most favor ablation approach (LT4 replacement straightforward)
  • Hyperthyroidism may persist 2–3 months post-RAI → bridge with beta-blockers or antithyroid drugs
  • Radiation safety: avoid close contact with children/pregnant women for 5–7 days; avoid contact with immunocompromised for 3 days
🚨 RAI Contraindications
Pregnancy: ABSOLUTE CONTRAINDICATION. Fetal thyroid begins functioning at 11 weeks’ gestation — RAI would ablate it, causing permanent congenital hypothyroidism. Breastfeeding: also contraindicated (excreted in milk). Wait 6 months after RAI before conceiving. Active moderate-to-severe ophthalmopathy: RAI worsens TED (especially in smokers) — if RAI chosen, protect with prednisolone 0.2–0.5 mg/kg/day for 6–12 weeks.

Thyroidectomy

  • Preferred in: very large goiters, suspected malignancy, moderate-severe ophthalmopathy (avoids RAI-induced worsening), 1st trimester (control difficult), patient preference
  • Pre-op: render euthyroid with antithyroid drugs → then SSKI (potassium iodide) 5 drops TID for 10 days immediately before surgery → reduces thyroid vascularity and blood loss (Plummer’s effect/Wolff-Chaikoff)
  • Complications: hemorrhage/hematoma (airway emergency), laryngeal edema, temporary hypoparathyroidism (very common, transient), permanent hypoparathyroidism (~1% with expert surgeons), RLN injury (~1%), wound infection
  • Recurrence: <2% with experienced surgeon performing near-total/total thyroidectomy

Thyroiditis

Acute Suppurative Thyroiditis

  • Rare bacterial/fungal infection of thyroid. Bacteria: Staph aureus, Strep pyogenes, Enterobacter, anaerobes. Fungi: Aspergillus, Candida, Coccidioides (in immunocompromised)
  • Most common route in children/young adults: fistula from piriform sinus (4th branchial arch remnant — predominantly left-sided)
  • Presentation: acute unilateral thyroid pain, fever, dysphagia, fluctuance, erythema, cervical lymphadenopathy; thyroid function usually normal
  • Labs: leukocytosis, elevated ESR; FNA = polymorphonuclear leukocytes; culture guides antibiotics
  • Surgery to drain abscess + IV antibiotics; piriform sinus fistula requires elective surgical repair after resolution
  • Complications (if untreated): tracheal obstruction, mediastinitis, jugular venous thrombosis, septicemia

Subacute (de Quervain’s) Thyroiditis

Also: granulomatous thyroiditis, viral thyroiditis. Implicated viruses: mumps, coxsackie B, influenza, adenovirus, echoviruses, SARS-CoV-2 (post-COVID and post-COVID vaccine). Peak incidence: 30–50 years; women 3× > men. HLA-Bw35 associated.

Pathology & Natural History

Viral infection + host immune response → patchy inflammatory infiltrate → disruption of follicles → multinucleated giant cells + granuloma formation → fibrosis → follicular regeneration. Released preformed T4/T3 → transient thyrotoxicosis. Four phases over ~6 months total: (1) thyrotoxic, (2) euthyroid (transition), (3) hypothyroid, (4) recovery. Permanent hypothyroidism in ~15%.

Clinical Features

Painful goiter (exquisitely tender, often radiates to jaw/ear/throat), low-grade fever, malaise, preceding upper respiratory infection. Lab: very high ESR (often >100 mm/h), high CRP, normal/low WBC. During thyrotoxic phase: ↑T4/T3, suppressed TSH, very low RAIU (<5%), negative TPO antibodies, ↑Tg (released from damaged follicles).

Key Diagnostic Features of Subacute Thyroiditis
Tender thyroid + very high ESR + low RAIU + negative TPO antibodies + viral prodrome. T4/T3 ratio is LOWER than in Graves’ (Graves’ preferentially secretes T3). Tg is elevated (pre-formed hormone release from damaged follicles).
Treatment
Thyrotoxic phase: beta-blockers for symptoms; antithyroid drugs play NO role (no new hormone being synthesized). Mild pain: NSAIDs or aspirin 600 mg q4–6h. Severe/not responding: prednisone 15–40 mg/day tapering over 6–8 weeks (dramatic pain relief in 24–48h; discontinuing too early → symptom relapse). Hypothyroid phase: LT4 50–100 μg/day if prolonged (>3 months) to allow TSH-driven gland recovery; withdraw when TSH-stimulated recovery complete.

Silent (Painless) and Postpartum Thyroiditis

  • Underlying predisposition: autoimmune thyroid disease (TPO antibodies); HLA-DR3, DR4, DR5
  • Postpartum thyroiditis: occurs in 5–9% of all deliveries; 25% of TPO antibody–positive women; onset 1–6 months postpartum
  • Classic biphasic: thyrotoxicosis (2–4 weeks) → hypothyroidism (4–12 weeks) → resolution; often only one phase is apparent
  • Thyrotoxic phase: mild symptoms (palpitations, heat intolerance), painless small goiter; low RAIU (<5%); normal ESR; positive TPO antibodies
  • Permanent hypothyroidism in ~30–50% of postpartum thyroiditis (compared to ~15% in subacute); annual follow-up essential
  • Treatment thyrotoxic phase: beta-blockers only (antithyroid drugs ineffective — no synthesis); NO glucocorticoids needed (unlike subacute)
  • Hypothyroid phase: LT4 replacement if symptomatic or TSH >10; withdraw after 6–9 months to assess permanent vs transient
  • Recurrence in subsequent pregnancies: ~70%

Drug-Induced Thyroiditis

  • IFN-α (hepatitis B/C, hematologic malignancies): up to 5% develop thyroid dysfunction; most common in TPO antibody–positive women; can cause painless thyroiditis, hypothyroidism, or de novo Graves’
  • Immune checkpoint inhibitors (pembrolizumab, nivolumab, ipilimumab, durvalumab): thyroiditis in 5–20% of treated cancer patients; often causes hypothyroidism; treat as silent thyroiditis; ASCO recommends routine thyroid function monitoring on these agents
  • Tyrosine kinase inhibitors (sunitinib, sorafenib): hypothyroidism in up to 50% of patients; thyroiditis pattern
  • Amiodarone: destructive AIT type 2 (see Section 10)
  • Lithium: blocks thyroid hormone release (similar to iodide); causes goiter and hypothyroidism in genetically susceptible; rarely causes thyrotoxicosis

Riedel’s Thyroiditis

  • Rare; typically middle-aged women; associated with IgG4-related disease (idiopathic fibrosclerosis — also affects retroperitoneum, mediastinum, biliary tree, orbit, salivary glands)
  • Dense fibrosis replaces normal thyroid parenchyma + extends beyond the thyroid capsule → adherent to and encasing adjacent structures
  • Presentation: insidious painless hard thyroid mass (rock-hard, “woody”) with compressive symptoms: tracheal obstruction, dysphagia, venous obstruction, vocal cord paralysis
  • Despite extensive fibrosis: thyroid dysfunction uncommon (varies)
  • FNA usually inadequate (fibrous tissue); requires open biopsy for diagnosis
  • Histology: dense keloid-like fibrosis + lymphoplasmacytic infiltrate (IgG4⁺ plasma cells)
  • Treatment: glucocorticoids (first-line), rituximab, tamoxifen; surgery for decompression

Sick Euthyroid Syndrome & Amiodarone

Sick Euthyroid Syndrome (SES / Nonthyroidal Illness, NTI)

Any acute, severe illness causes abnormal thyroid hormone levels without intrinsic thyroid disease. Mechanism: cytokines (especially IL-6) reduce TBG levels, impair binding-protein function, reduce deiodinase activity. Do NOT routinely test thyroid function in acutely ill patients unless thyroid disorder is strongly suspected on clinical grounds.

Low T3 Syndrome (most common)

Suppressed DIO1 activity → ↓ T4→T3 conversion → low total and free T3, elevated rT3, normal T4 and TSH. Magnitude of T3 fall correlates with illness severity. Considered a physiologic adaptive response (similar to fasting — reduces metabolic rate).

Low T4 Syndrome (severe/critical)

In severely ill or hypotensive patients: upregulation of type III deiodinase in muscle and liver → accelerated T4 and T3 inactivation → low T4 and T3, very high rT3. Free T4 immunoassays unreliable (binding proteins low → underestimate true free T4 by dialysis). Low T4 in sick euthyroid = very poor prognosis indicator.

TSH in Sick Euthyroid Syndrome
TSH ranges from 0.01 to >20 mIU/L in NTI: suppressed (~10%, especially with dopamine/glucocorticoid treatment), elevated (~5% during recovery phase as TSH rebounds). Undetectable TSH (<0.01 mIU/L) is more likely to indicate intrinsic thyroid disease — evaluate further. Thyroid results normalize with clinical recovery — retest after recovery.
⚠ Treatment of NTI is Controversial
Most authorities: do NOT administer thyroid hormone to NTI patients unless clear historical/clinical evidence of hypothyroidism. No RCT has shown mortality benefit from thyroid hormone in NTI; some suggest harm. Monitor and retest after clinical recovery.

Amiodarone Effects on Thyroid Function

  • Class III antiarrhythmic; structurally related to thyroid hormone; contains 39% iodine by weight
  • 200 mg/day dose → >40-fold increase in plasma iodine; 6 mg/kg/day of iodine released
  • Stored in adipose tissue + liver: plasma half-life 40–55 days; iodine levels remain elevated >6 months after discontinuation
  • Inhibits DIO1 and DIO2 → ↓T4→T3 conversion; metabolites (desethylamiodarone) are weak TR antagonists

Nodular Disease & Thyroid Cancer

Goiter

  • Diffuse nontoxic goiter: symmetric enlargement without nodules, euthyroid; endemic if >5% of population affected; most common worldwide cause = iodine deficiency
  • Goitrogens: cassava root (thiocyanate), cruciferous vegetables (inhibit NIS), lithium, iodide excess, cassava root, millet, flavonoids (soy)
  • Pemberton’s sign: facial flushing + neck vein distension when arms raised above head (superior vena caval obstruction from substernal goiter — increased thoracic inlet pressure)
  • Substernal goiter: compressive symptoms (dysphagia, orthopnea, stridor); confirm with CT chest; surgery if symptomatic
  • MNG (multinodular goiter): up to 12% of adults; polyclonal nodule growth; TSH normal; cancer risk per nodule similar to solitary nodule — evaluate dominant or suspicious nodule

Toxic MNG & Toxic Adenoma

Toxic Multinodular Goiter (TMNG)

Autonomous function in pre-existing MNG; common in elderly; often precipitated by iodine exposure (contrast agents). TSH low; free T4 normal or mildly elevated; T3 disproportionately elevated (T3 toxicosis pattern). Scan: heterogeneous with multiple “hot” and “cold” areas.
Treatment: antithyroid drugs (temporary control for elderly); RAI (preferred — also reduces goiter size by 30–40%); surgery (need euthyroid pre-op).

Toxic Adenoma (Hyperfunctioning Solitary Nodule)

Somatic gain-of-function mutations: TSH-R (transmembrane domain, >90%) or GSsα (similar to McCune-Albright) → constitutive cAMP → ↑cell proliferation + autonomous hormone secretion. Thyrotoxicosis usually when nodule >3 cm; TSH subnormal.
Scan: focal intense uptake + suppressed surrounding gland (normal gland suppressed by autonomous hormone).
Treatment: RAI (concentrated in nodule; low hypothyroidism risk since surrounding gland suppressed and less irradiated) or surgical lobectomy. FNA not needed — hot nodules are almost never malignant.

Approach to Thyroid Nodules

  • Palpable nodules: ~5% of adults; US-detected: up to 50% (most <1 cm — likely over-detected)
  • Step 1: Measure TSH. If suppressed → scintigraphy → if hot nodule = no FNA (benign). If TSH normal/elevated → proceed to ultrasound
  • Step 2: Thyroid US with ACR TI-RADS scoring to determine FNA threshold by pattern + size
  • No guideline recommends FNA for nodules <1 cm (micropapillary cancer) unless metastatic cervical LN or ETX present

  • Iodine-refractory metastatic disease: kinase inhibitors — lenvatinib (first-line), sorafenib; mutation-selective: BRAF V600E inhibitors, RET inhibitors (selpercatinib, pralsetinib), NTRK inhibitors (larotrectinib, entrectinib), immune checkpoint if TMB-high

Thyroid Lymphoma & Risk Factors for Malignancy

⚠ Thyroid Lymphoma — Do NOT Operate First
Thyroid lymphoma (DLBCL most common) arises in Hashimoto’s background. Rapidly enlarging, firm mass. Sensitive to external radiation — treat with RT ± chemotherapy based on staging. Surgical resection as initial therapy should be AVOIDED (may spread localized disease). Diagnose by core biopsy + immunohistochemistry, not FNA alone.
High-Risk History Features
  • Head/neck irradiation before age 18 (Chernobyl, prior cancer RT)
  • Age <20 or >65 years
  • Male gender; rapidly enlarging mass
  • Family history: PTC in 2+ relatives, MEN 2, Cowden’s (PTEN), FAP, Carney complex
High-Risk Exam/Imaging Features
  • Vocal cord paralysis; hoarseness (RLN invasion)
  • Fixed nodule; extrathyroidal extension on imaging
  • Ipsilateral cervical lymphadenopathy
  • ¹⁸FDG-PET avidity or Ga-68 Dotatate avidity
  • TI-RADS 5 features on ultrasound

⚠ Exam Traps — 25 High-Yield Pitfalls

Trap 01Normal TSH Does NOT Rule Out Central Hypothyroidism
A 45-year-old woman with pituitary surgery presents with fatigue and cold intolerance. TSH = 1.8 mIU/L (normal). Can you confidently exclude hypothyroidism?
NO. In secondary (central) hypothyroidism, the pituitary produces bioinactive forms of TSH — TSH can be low, normal, or even mildly elevated. TSH cannot be used to screen for or monitor central hypothyroidism. Always measure free T4 when pituitary/hypothalamic disease is present or suspected. Goal of LT4 replacement in central hypothyroidism: free T4 in the upper half of the reference range.
Trap 02Biotin Supplements Cause Falsely Low TSH + Falsely High T4/T3
A patient on 5000 μg/day biotin (for hair loss) has TSH <0.01 and free T4 of 2.8 (elevated). She has no symptoms of hyperthyroidism. How do you proceed?
Stop biotin for 2 days and retest. Biotin (≥1000 μg) interferes with biotinylated TSH assay antibodies → falsely suppressed TSH + falsely elevated free T4/T3. This can mimic Graves’ disease perfectly. The effect clears within 18 hours of last biotin dose. Always ask about biotin supplements before acting on unexpected thyroid results. This is a common board exam scenario.
Trap 03TBG Deficiency: Low Total T4 — Do NOT Treat
A man has total T4 of 2.5 μg/dL (low) with TSH 1.6 (normal) and no symptoms. Should you start LT4?
Absolutely not. This is X-linked TBG deficiency — low binding protein means low total T4 but normal free T4 and normal TSH → truly euthyroid. Giving LT4 to normalize total T4 would cause thyrotoxicosis (and the extra T4 would be rapidly cleared without TBG to extend its half-life). Never treat thyroid disease based on total T4 without free T4 and TSH confirmation.
Trap 04Graves’ Ophthalmopathy Can Exist WITHOUT Hyperthyroidism
A patient has bilateral proptosis and periorbital edema. TSH and free T4 are normal. Can this still be Graves’ disease?
YES — in ~10% of TED patients. Thyroid eye disease can occur when the patient is euthyroid, hypothyroid (Hashimoto’s), or in the setting of treated Graves’. The pathogenesis involves TSH-R and IGF-1R expressed on orbital fibroblasts — autoimmune attack independent of thyroid hormone levels. TRAb are usually positive even in euthyroid Graves’ ophthalmopathy.
Trap 05Thyroid Storm: SSKI Must Come AFTER PTU — Not Before
You plan to administer SSKI first to rapidly block thyroid hormone release in thyroid storm, then give PTU. Is this the correct sequence?
WRONG order. If iodine (SSKI) is given before an antithyroid drug, the thyroid uses the iodine as substrate to synthesize more thyroid hormone, worsening the storm. The correct sequence: (1) PTU first → blocks TPO (no new synthesis or coupling), (2) wait 1 full hour, (3) THEN give SSKI (Wolff-Chaikoff — iodide now inhibits release safely). Never skip or reverse this order.
Trap 06Hyperemesis Gravidarum: Do NOT Give Antithyroid Drugs
A 9-week pregnant woman has suppressed TSH, mildly elevated free T4, and severe vomiting. No exophthalmos, no TRAb. You diagnose Graves’ and prescribe methimazole. Correct?
Not yet — this is gestational thyrotoxicosis from hCG, not Graves’ disease. hCG peaks at ~10 weeks and weakly stimulates TSH-R → transient TSH suppression → hyperemesis gravidarum in some. Treatment: IV fluids and antiemetics. Antithyroid drugs are NOT indicated unless confirmed true Graves’ disease (positive TRAb, ophthalmopathy, persistent hyperthyroidism past 16 weeks). Drug is self-limiting as hCG falls.
Trap 07Do NOT Measure T3 in the Hypothyroidism Workup
A patient is confirmed hypothyroid (elevated TSH, low free T4). You order free T3 to complete the picture. Is this useful?
No — T3 measurement is NOT indicated in hypothyroidism. Circulating T3 is normal in ~25% of hypothyroid patients because type II deiodinase is upregulated in the pituitary and brain, compensating by converting more T4→T3 locally. A normal T3 does not rule out hypothyroidism and adds no clinical value. T3 is only useful in the workup of thyrotoxicosis (T3 toxicosis, monitoring antithyroid therapy).
Trap 08Myxedema Coma: External Warming ONLY if Temp <30°C
A myxedema coma patient has a temperature of 34°C. You apply a warming blanket to raise her temperature. Is this appropriate?
NO — active external warming is restricted to temperatures <30°C. When temperature is above 30°C, external warming causes rapid peripheral vasodilation without adequate cardiac output to compensate → cardiovascular collapse. Instead, use passive insulation (space blankets) to prevent further heat loss, and allow temperature to rise gradually as thyroid hormone treatment takes effect. Active warming only at <30°C.
Trap 09Silent vs Subacute Thyroiditis: ESR + Antibodies Are the Key
A postpartum woman has thyrotoxicosis with low RAIU and painless small goiter. Could be silent or subacute thyroiditis. How do you distinguish?
Check ESR and TPO antibodies. Silent/postpartum thyroiditis: normal ESR + positive TPO antibodies (underlying autoimmune thyroiditis). No glucocorticoids needed. Subacute thyroiditis: very high ESR (often >100 mm/h) + negative TPO antibodies + exquisitely tender goiter + viral prodrome; often requires glucocorticoids for pain. Both have low RAIU and painless (well, subacute has pain) — the ESR and antibodies are the discriminating tests.
Trap 10Thyrotoxicosis Factitia: Distinguish by Serum Thyroglobulin
A patient with weight loss and palpitations has suppressed TSH, elevated T4, and very low RAI uptake (<5%). Could be thyroiditis or exogenous hormone. How do you differentiate?
Measure serum thyroglobulin (Tg). Thyrotoxicosis factitia (exogenous T4/T3): LOW Tg (exogenous hormone suppresses TSH + endogenous Tg secretion; no gland destruction). Thyroiditis (pre-formed hormone release): ELEVATED Tg (follicle destruction releases Tg into bloodstream). Both have low RAIU. Tg is the critical differentiating test when RAIU is low.
Trap 11PTU Has ONLY Three Indications — Not Default Hyperthyroid Treatment
You automatically prescribe PTU to a newly diagnosed hyperthyroid patient. What’s wrong with this?
PTU should not be used routinely. Due to FDA warnings about serious hepatotoxicity (acute liver failure, death), PTU use is limited to three situations: (1) First trimester of pregnancy (methimazole causes embryopathy weeks 6–10), (2) Thyroid storm (PTU uniquely blocks T4→T3 via DIO1), (3) Methimazole allergy/intolerance. In ALL other situations, methimazole is the preferred thionamide. After the first trimester: switch from PTU → methimazole.
Trap 12Amiodarone-Induced Thyrotoxicosis: Do NOT Stop Amiodarone Reflexively
A patient on amiodarone for VT develops AIT type 2. You immediately discontinue amiodarone to reduce iodine load. Is this the right move?
Not necessarily. Due to amiodarone’s half-life of 40–55 days and adipose storage, stopping it provides no immediate clinical benefit. Studies show AIT type 2 resolves at similar rates whether amiodarone is continued or stopped. The decision should be based on whether the underlying arrhythmia (VT/VF) can be safely managed without amiodarone. If not, continue amiodarone and treat AIT type 2 with glucocorticoids.
Trap 13Hot Nodule = Benign; FNA is NOT Indicated
A patient has a 2.5 cm thyroid nodule with suppressed TSH. RAI scan shows it is hyperfunctioning (hot). Should you biopsy it?
NO — hot nodules are almost never malignant. A functioning (hot) nodule on scintigraphy essentially excludes malignancy. FNA is not indicated and would give confusing cytology results. The appropriate management is to evaluate and treat the hyperthyroidism (RAI ablation or lobectomy based on patient preference and nodule size). This is a critical exception to standard nodule evaluation pathways.
Trap 14RTH-β vs TSH-Secreting Adenoma: Both Have High T4 + Non-Suppressed TSH
A patient has free T4 2.5 (elevated), free T3 elevated, and TSH 3.1 mIU/L (inappropriately non-suppressed). How do you differentiate RTH-β from a TSH-secreting adenoma?
Key tests: (1) α-subunit of TSH: elevated in TSH-secreting adenoma, normal in RTH-β. (2) Pituitary MRI/CT: adenoma present in TSH-secreting adenoma, normal in RTH-β. (3) Family history and TRβ gene sequencing: RTH-β is familial (autosomal dominant); similar labs in relatives; mutation on sequencing. Management differs entirely: RTH-β = usually no treatment; TSH-adenoma = transsphenoidal surgery ± somatostatin analogue.
Trap 15Thyroid Lymphoma: Avoid Surgery as Initial Therapy
A patient with known Hashimoto’s develops a rapidly enlarging hard thyroid mass. You plan surgery for diagnosis and resection. Is this appropriate?
NO — do NOT operate first on thyroid lymphoma. Thyroid lymphoma (usually DLBCL) arises in Hashimoto’s background and is highly radiosensitive. Surgical resection as initial therapy risks spreading disease that might otherwise be localized. The correct approach: core needle biopsy (FNA may be inadequate) + immunohistochemistry → staging → external beam radiation (localized) or CHOP-R chemotherapy (systemic). Surgery is reserved for airway decompression only.
Trap 16Estrogen Raises Total T4 but NOT Free T4 or TSH
A woman on oral contraceptives has total T4 of 14 μg/dL (high). She is asymptomatic. Do you investigate for hyperthyroidism?
No. Estrogen increases TBG sialylation → delayed TBG clearance → more TBG in serum → more T4 bound → high total T4. But free T4 and TSH remain normal → patient is truly euthyroid. This pattern is also seen in pregnancy. Do not order further testing or treat. Simply recognize the medication effect. Note: transdermal estrogen has minimal effect on TBG (no first-pass hepatic effect).
Trap 17RAI is ABSOLUTELY Contraindicated in Pregnancy and Breastfeeding
A pregnant woman at 14 weeks with difficult-to-control Graves’ is discussed for RAI therapy. Why is this not an option?
RAI is an ABSOLUTE contraindication in pregnancy. The fetal thyroid begins concentrating iodine at ~11 weeks’ gestation. RAI crosses the placenta and would completely ablate the fetal thyroid → permanent congenital hypothyroidism → irreversible neurologic damage. Also contraindicated in breastfeeding (excreted in breast milk). Patients must not conceive for 6 months after RAI treatment. Always confirm negative pregnancy test before RAI.
Trap 18Pendred Syndrome: Organification Defect + Goiter + Sensorineural Deafness
A child presents with congenital sensorineural deafness and a goiter. What is the thyroid diagnosis?
Pendred syndrome. Mutation in the pendrin (SLC26A4) gene — pendrin normally transports iodide from cell to follicular lumen on the apical surface. Without it: iodide cannot be organified → impaired T4/T3 synthesis → TSH rises → goiter. Pendrin also regulates cochlear endolymph ion composition → sensorineural deafness. Autosomal recessive. Classic triad: organification defect + goiter + sensorineural deafness. Most common form of syndromic hereditary deafness associated with goiter.
Trap 19Subclinical Hypothyroidism: Treat if TSH >10 OR Pregnant — Not Always
An asymptomatic 60-year-old woman has TSH of 7.5 mIU/L (elevated), normal free T4, and positive TPO antibodies. Her cardiologist wants to know if she needs LT4. What do you advise?
Not yet by strict criteria. LT4 is clearly indicated when TSH >10 mIU/L, or if pregnant/planning pregnancy, or for symptomatic young/middle-aged patients with CV risk. For this asymptomatic elderly woman with TSH 7.5: confirm elevation persists over 3 months, then the decision is individualized. Her positive TPO antibodies predict 4% annual risk of progression. A reasonable approach: shared decision, offering a trial of LT4 with reassessment. In very elderly (>80 years), even TSH 5–10 may not need treatment (age-specific ranges are higher).
Trap 20Subacute Thyroiditis: Antithyroid Drugs Play NO Role
A patient with confirmed subacute thyroiditis is thyrotoxic. You prescribe methimazole to block the excess thyroid hormone. Is this appropriate?
NO — antithyroid drugs are useless in subacute thyroiditis. The thyrotoxicosis results from release of pre-formed, stored hormone from follicles destroyed by inflammation — there is no new hormone synthesis occurring. Thionamides (MMI/PTU) only block new synthesis via TPO inhibition. Treatment: beta-blockers for symptom control; NSAIDs or glucocorticoids for pain/inflammation. The thyrotoxic phase is self-limited.
Trap 21MCT8 Mutation: Low T4 + HIGH T3 + HIGH TSH — Paradoxical Pattern
An X-linked male infant has severe psychomotor retardation and thyroid labs showing: TSH elevated, T4 low, T3 markedly elevated. What is the diagnosis?
MCT8 (Monocarboxylate Transporter 8) mutation — Allan-Herndon-Dudley syndrome. MCT8 is required for T4 to enter brain cells. Without it: T4 accumulates peripherally → shunted to peripheral T4→T3 conversion → very high T3. The brain is T4-starved despite high peripheral T3 (brain also MCT8-dependent for T3 entry). TSH is elevated because pituitary cannot sense adequate intracellular T4. Result: low T4 + high T3 + high TSH — a paradoxical and unique pattern. X-linked recessive.
Trap 22Pregnant Hypothyroid Women: Increase LT4 to 9 Doses/Week IMMEDIATELY
A hypothyroid woman on 100 μg LT4 daily becomes pregnant. When and how do you adjust her dose?
Immediately upon confirmed pregnancy — increase to 9 doses per week. This means taking a double dose on 2 nonconsecutive days per week (approximately the 45% increase needed for athyreotic women). Do not wait for the next scheduled appointment. Then monitor TSH every 4 weeks in the first half of pregnancy with a target TSH <2.5 mIU/L. Maternal hypothyroidism, even subclinical, is associated with miscarriage, preterm birth, and fetal neurodevelopmental impairment. After delivery, return to pre-pregnancy dose and recheck TSH at 6 weeks.
Trap 23T3 Toxicosis: TSH Suppressed + Normal Free T4 = Must Check Free T3
A patient has symptoms of thyrotoxicosis, TSH = 0.02 mIU/L (suppressed), but free T4 is normal. You conclude the patient is euthyroid. What did you miss?
T3 toxicosis. In 2–5% of thyrotoxicosis cases, only T3 is elevated while T4 remains normal. Occurs early in Graves’ disease, with toxic adenomas, and in areas of borderline iodine intake. A suppressed TSH with normal free T4 must always prompt measurement of free T3 before concluding the patient is euthyroid. If free T3 is elevated = T3 toxicosis → treat as hyperthyroidism.
Trap 24Spongiform Nodule = No FNA Regardless of Size
An incidental 3.5 cm spongiform thyroid nodule is found. The patient asks about biopsy. Is FNA indicated?
NO FNA regardless of size. A spongiform nodule (microcystic areas comprising >50% of the volume — honeycomb appearance) has a malignancy risk <3%. All major validated risk stratification systems (ACR TI-RADS, ATA, EU-TIRADS) classify it as benign/not suspicious — observation only, NO biopsy. This is one of the most important rules reducing unnecessary FNAs by up to 45%. A spongiform nodule = ACR TI-RADS TR1 = 0 points regardless of size.
Trap 25NIFTP: No Longer Malignant — But Can ONLY Be Diagnosed After Surgery
FNA shows “follicular neoplasm with papillary-like nuclear features” (Bethesda IV). The pathologist mentions NIFTP. The patient asks why surgery is needed if it’s not cancer.
NIFTP cannot be diagnosed by FNA alone — it requires surgical pathology. NIFTP (Noninvasive Follicular Thyroid Neoplasm with Papillary-like nuclear features) is no longer classified as malignant, but diagnosing it requires demonstrating the absence of capsular and vascular invasion, which requires evaluation of the entire capsule — only possible on surgical pathology. On FNA it appears as an indeterminate follicular-patterned lesion (Bethesda III or IV). Surgery is still needed to determine whether invasion is present. NIFTP inclusion has lowered the apparent malignancy risk across Bethesda categories III–V.
