Mitral Stenosis

A fixed obstruction to left-atrial emptying. Almost the entire clinical picture flows from one number — a mitral orifice that has shrunk from a normal 4–6 cm² down toward 1 cm² — and from one consequence: a chronically high left-atrial pressure that has to be backed up through the lungs.

Etiology & Pathology — why the valve narrows

Rheumatic fever is the leading cause

  • Rheumatic fever causes the great majority of MS. Pure or predominant MS occurs in ~40% of all patients with rheumatic heart disease and a history of rheumatic fever; in the rest, lesser MS accompanies MR and/or aortic valve disease.
  • Less common causes of obstruction to LV inflow: congenital MS (parachute valve, cor triatriatum), mitral annular calcification extending onto the leaflets, SLE, rheumatoid arthritis, left atrial myxoma, and infective endocarditis with large vegetations.
  • Epidemiology: as acute rheumatic fever has declined (temperate climates, middle-/high-income countries), MS has fallen markedly over recent decades — but it remains a major problem in low-income regions: sub-Saharan Africa, India, Southeast Asia, and Oceania.

  • The initial insult is rheumatic, but later narrowing is exacerbated by ongoing inflammation, fibrosis, and trauma from the altered, turbulent flow across the deformed valve — the valve damages itself as it works.
  • Calcification immobilizes the leaflets and narrows the orifice further.
Mechanism → finding · where do clots come from?

Thrombus and systemic emboli can arise from the calcific valve itself, but far more often from the dilated left atrium — specifically the LA appendage — once atrial fibrillation develops. This is why AF + MS is such a feared embolic combination, and why anticoagulation becomes non-negotiable in that setting.

Pathophysiology — the master cascade

Everything downstream is the body trying to push blood through a hole that is too small. Follow the pressure.

The hemodynamic hallmark

  • Normal mitral orifice = 4–6 cm². Significant obstruction begins when area falls to <~2 cm²: now blood crosses only if pushed by an abnormally elevated left atrioventricular pressure gradient — this gradient is the hemodynamic hallmark of MS.
  • "Severe" MS = orifice <1.5 cm². At this point an LA pressure of ~25 mmHg is needed just to keep cardiac output normal.
Why heart rate matters as much as the valve

To grade severity you must know both the transvalvular gradient and the flow rate, and flow depends on heart rate, not just CO. Tachycardia shortens diastole proportionately more than systole, robbing the time available for blood to cross the mitral valve. So at any given output, a faster rate (rapid AF, fever, pregnancy) raises the gradient and drives LA pressure higher — which is precisely why a previously stable patient decompensates the moment they go into rapid AF. (The same logic applies to tricuspid stenosis.)

What stays normal — and why that's a teaching point

  • LV diastolic pressure and ejection fraction are normal in isolated MS. The problem is filling, not pumping — the LV is simply under-filled. Don't expect a depressed EF from pure MS.
  • In sinus rhythm, the LA pressure tracing shows a prominent a wave (atrial contraction) and a gradual decline after the v wave and valve opening (a blunted y descent, because the LA empties slowly through the tight valve).

Cardiac output behavior

  • Severe MS (1–1.5 cm²): CO normal or near-normal at rest, but rises subnormally with exertion.
  • Very severe MS (<1 cm²), especially with high pulmonary vascular resistance: CO subnormal even at rest and may fail to rise or even fall during activity.
Pulmonary hypertension — four sequential mechanisms

The level of pulmonary artery pressure (PAP) drives the clinical picture. PH develops via: (1) passive backward transmission of high LA pressure → (2) reactive pulmonary arteriolar constriction — the "second stenosis," triggered by LA/pulmonary venous hypertension → (3) interstitial edema in small-vessel walls → (4) at end stage, fixed obliterative changes in the pulmonary vascular bed. Severe PH then produces RV enlargement, secondary TR and PR, and right-sided heart failure.

Symptoms — reading the timeline

  • Latent period (temperate climates): ~2 decades from rheumatic carditis to MS symptoms; most patients become disabled in the 4th decade of life.
  • Natural history (pre-surgical era): once seriously symptomatic, the disease progressed inexorably to death within 2–5 years. This is the prognostic baseline against which intervention is judged.
  • Early triggers of dyspnea/cough — anything that suddenly raises flow or rate across the orifice: severe exertion, excitement, fever, severe anemia, paroxysmal AF / other tachycardias, sexual intercourse, pregnancy, thyrotoxicosis. WHY each acutely spikes LA pressure.
  • As MS progresses → dyspnea at lesser stress, daily-life limitation, then orthopnea and paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea.
Persistent AF is a turning point

When AF becomes persistent it usually accelerates the rate of symptom progression — it removes the atrial "kick," shortens diastole, and raises LA pressure. Treat the appearance of AF in MS as a clinical inflection point, not a footnote.

Other classic MS complications

  • Hemoptysis: from rupture of pulmonary–bronchial venous connections due to pulmonary venous hypertension. Occurs most when LA pressure is high but pulmonary vascular resistance is not yet markedly elevated, and is rarely fatal.
  • Recurrent pulmonary emboli (± infarction): important late cause of morbidity/mortality; often from right atrial mural thrombus.
  • Pulmonary infections (bronchitis, bronchopneumonia, lobar pneumonia): commonly complicate untreated MS, especially in winter.
  • Ortner's (cardiovocal) syndrome: hoarseness from compression of the left recurrent laryngeal nerve by a greatly enlarged LA.
  • Pulmonary function changes: fibrous thickening of alveolar/capillary walls; reduced vital capacity, total lung capacity, max breathing capacity, and O₂ uptake/ventilation; compliance falls further as capillary pressure rises in exercise.
Thrombi & emboli — the numbers

Systemic embolization incidence is ~10–20%, and is more frequent with AF, age >65, and reduced CO. Crucially, embolization can be the presenting feature in an otherwise asymptomatic patient with only mild MS — a stroke can be the first sign.

Physical findings — each sign mapped to its mechanism

Inspection & palpation

  • Malar flush (pinched, blue facies) in severe MS — low output/cyanosis.
  • Prominent jugular a waves (sinus rhythm + severe PH or TS) → vigorous right atrial systole.
  • Systemic arterial pressure normal or slightly low (limited forward flow).
  • Parasternal lift = enlarged RV from PH.
  • A diastolic thrill at the apex (left lateral decubitus) — very rarely.

Auscultation — the MS sequence

  • Loud S₁, slightly delayed (early disease) — mobile leaflets held open until forced shut.
  • Accentuated P₂ with closely split S₂ when PAP is high.
  • Opening Snap (OS): best heard in expiration at/just medial to apex; follows A₂ by 0.05–0.12 s.
  • Diastolic rumble: low-pitched, at apex, left lateral decubitus; louder after mild exercise; presystolic accentuation in sinus rhythm (atrial kick).
"Silent MS" — why the murmur can vanish

When CO is markedly reduced, there isn't enough flow to generate the rumble, so the classic findings (including the diastolic murmur) may be undetectable — silent MS. They reappear once compensation is restored. A quiet exam does not equal mild disease.

Associated murmurs to recognize

  • Functional TR (with severe PH): pansystolic at left sternal border, louder on inspiration / softer on forced expiration = Carvallo's sign.
  • Graham Steell murmur (PR): high-pitched, diastolic, decrescendo, blowing, at left sternal border, from dilation of the pulmonary valve ring in mitral disease + severe PH. May be indistinguishable from AR — but it can increase with inspiration and comes with a loud, often palpable P₂.
  • A soft grade I–II/VI systolic murmur at/medial to the apex may signal mixed mitral disease (added regurgitation).

Laboratory & imaging

ECG

  • Sinus rhythm: P wave shows LA enlargement ("P mitrale").
  • With severe PH/TS and RA enlargement: P becomes tall & peaked in lead II, upright in V₁.
  • QRS usually normal; severe PH → right-axis deviation + RV hypertrophy.

Chest X-ray

  • Earliest: straightening of the upper-left cardiac border, prominent main PAs, dilated upper-lobe pulmonary veins, posterior esophageal displacement (enlarged LA).
  • Kerley B lines: fine horizontal lines (lower/mid lung) from edematous interlobular septa — appear when resting mean LA pressure exceeds ~20 mmHg.

Echocardiography — the workhorse

  • TTE (color + spectral Doppler) gives: mitral inflow E/A velocities, peak/mean transvalvular gradients, orifice area, severity of any MR, leaflet calcification & restriction, subvalvular distortion, and anatomic suitability for PMBC. Also: LV/RV function, chamber size, PA systolic pressure from the TR jet velocity, and associated AS/AR.
  • TEE = superior images; use when TTE is inadequate, and routinely to exclude LA thrombus before PMBC.
  • Exercise TTE resolves discrepancies between symptoms and resting hemodynamics (mean gradient, PAP, RV function).
Doppler & the Bernoulli equation (Fig. 274-1)

Continuous-wave Doppler of transmitral velocity is converted to pressure via the Bernoulli equation to derive the mean gradient and valve area — e.g., a mean gradient of ~38 mmHg corresponding to a valve area of ~0.7 cm² in very severe rheumatic MS.

Cardiac catheterization

  • Useful when clinical and noninvasive findings disagree (including after TEE/exercise echo), to assess associated AS/AR, and for worsening symptoms late after intervention.
  • CT coronary angiography is increasingly used to screen for CAD preoperatively before valve surgery / transcatheter treatment.

Treatment of Mitral Stenosis

Three tiers: prevention & medical control, commissurotomy (balloon or surgical) to open the valve, and replacement when the valve can't be salvaged.

Medical & preventive therapy

Penicillin — secondary rheumatic prophylaxis

Prevention
Mechanism / goal
Prevents recurrent group A β-hemolytic streptococcal infection and therefore recurrent rheumatic fever, which would further damage the valve.
Who
At-risk patients with rheumatic MS.
Dose
Not specified in the source text.
Pearl
IE prophylaxis is not routine — it's restricted to those at high risk for complications of infection (e.g., prior endocarditis), as with other valve lesions.

Diuretics + sodium restriction

Symptom relief
Mechanism
Lower LA / pulmonary venous pressure → relieve pulmonary congestion (treats the backup, not the valve).
Drugs
Small doses of oral diuretics; dietary sodium restriction.
Outcome
Symptomatic improvement only — does not change the obstruction or prognosis.
Dose
"Small doses"; specific dose not stated in source.

Rate control: β-blockers · non-DHP CCBs · digoxin

AF / rate
Mechanism
Slow ventricular rate → lengthen diastole → more filling time across the tight valve → lower gradient and LA pressure. This is the central reason rate control helps MS.
Drugs
Beta blockers; non-dihydropyridine CCBs (verapamil, diltiazem); digitalis glycosides.
Use
To slow the ventricular rate in patients with AF.
Dose
Not specified in source.

Vitamin K antagonist (warfarin)

Anticoagulation
Mechanism
Anticoagulation to prevent stroke / systemic embolism from LA / LA-appendage thrombus.
Target
INR 2–3, indefinitely, for MS patients with AF, prior thromboembolism, or demonstrated LA thrombus.
Controversial
Routine VKA in sinus rhythm with LA enlargement (max dimension >5.5 cm), ± spontaneous echo contrast.
Key trial
Rivaroxaban vs VKA (Connolly, NEJM 2022): in rheumatic MS + AF, rivaroxaban caused significantly MORE deaths than VKA. ⇒ use warfarin, not a DOAC.
Contraindication
DOACs should not be used in moderate/severe rheumatic MS, and are not approved for mechanical valves.
Cardioversion strategy in MS + recent AF

If AF is recent and MS isn't severe enough for intervention, restore sinus rhythm (chemical or electrical). Anticoagulate to therapeutic INR for ≥3 weeks first; if more urgent, give IV heparin and do TEE to exclude LA thrombus before cardioverting. Sinus rhythm is rarely sustained in severe MS, a markedly enlarged LA, or AF present >1 year — conditions that favor an LA myopathy.

Mitral commissurotomy — opening the valve

  • Indication: symptomatic (NYHA II–IV) patients with isolated severe MS — valve area <~1 cm²/m² BSA or <1.5 cm² in normal-sized adults. Can be done percutaneously (PMBC) or surgically.
  • PMBC technique (Inoue balloon): transseptal puncture → catheter into LA → balloon across the valve → inflated stepwise in the orifice.

Ideal PMBC candidate — all four must hold

  • Relatively pliable leaflets, little/no commissural calcium
  • Subvalvular structures not significantly scarred/thickened
  • No LA thrombus
  • Associated MR ≤ 2+/4+
  • "Echo score" (leaflet thickening, calcification, mobility, subvalvular thickening): a lower score predicts higher PMBC success.
  • Results: short/long-term outcomes similar to surgical commissurotomy but less morbidity and lower periprocedural mortality. In young (<45 y) patients with pliable valves, event-free survival 80–90% over 3–7 years. PMBC is the procedure of choice for such patients with a skilled operator at a high-volume center.
  • Open surgical commissurotomy (cardiopulmonary bypass) when PMBC isn't possible/unsuccessful or for restenosis: open commissures, loosen subvalvular fusion, debulk calcium, remove atrial thrombi. Perioperative mortality ~2%.
Definition & limits of "successful"

Success = 50% reduction in mean gradient AND doubling of valve area. It relieves symptoms and prolongs survival — but does NOT improve prognosis in patients with little/no functional impairment. So in asymptomatic and/or mild–moderate MS (area >1.5 cm²), commissurotomy is not recommended unless there's recurrent systemic embolization or severe PH (PA systolic >50 mmHg rest, >60 mmHg exercise). Poor improvement afterward suggests an ineffective procedure, induced MR, or other valve/myocardial disease. ~Half of surgical commissurotomy patients need reoperation by 10 years.

Pregnancy

  • Commissurotomy if pulmonary congestion persists despite intensive medical therapy. PMBC is preferred — TEE-guided with no/minimal x-ray exposure.

Mitral valve replacement (MVR)

Definitive surgery
When
Significant associated MR; valve severely distorted by prior manipulation; or commissurotomy can't meaningfully improve the valve.
Technique
Routinely done with preservation of chordal attachments to optimize LV functional recovery.
Mortality
~2–5% overall; lower in the young, up to ~2× higher if >65 y with comorbidities.
Threshold
Because of long-term prosthesis complications, operate only if severe MS (area ≤1.5 cm²) AND NYHA III (symptomatic with ordinary activity despite optimal therapy).
Outcome
10-year survival of surgical survivors ~70%; worse if >65 y, marked disability, or low preop CO. PH + RV dysfunction = additional risk factors for poor outcome.

⚠ Exam Traps — Mitral Stenosis

Coaching a Stage-A resident. Each trap: what the question wants you to think, the truth, why it matters, and how it shows up in a vignette (with the bolded correct answer). All drawn from Chapter 274.

01The A₂–OS interval
Trap"A longer gap between A₂ and the opening snap means a tighter, more severe valve."
TruthIt's the opposite. A higher LA pressure pops the valve open sooner, so a SHORTER A₂–OS interval = MORE severe MS (range 0.05–0.12 s).
WhyThe OS times when LA pressure exceeds LV pressure. More severe stenosis = higher LA pressure = earlier crossover.
In a case
Two MS patients are compared on phonocardiogram; you're asked which has more severe stenosis. The one with the shorter A₂–OS interval (and longer diastolic rumble) has the more severe disease.
02Anticoagulation in rheumatic MS + AF
Trap"Patient has AF — start a DOAC (rivaroxaban/apixaban) per CHA₂DS₂-VASc, like any other AF."
TruthIn rheumatic MS + AF, a randomized trial found rivaroxaban caused significantly MORE deaths than a vitamin K antagonist. Use warfarin to INR 2–3. DOACs are contraindicated in moderate/severe rheumatic MS.
WhyThe CHA₂DS₂-VASc–driven DOAC logic does not transfer to rheumatic mitral disease — the evidence shows net harm.
In a case
A 38-y-old woman with rheumatic MS develops AF and is started on a DOAC. The best next step is to switch to warfarin targeting INR 2–3.
03The decompensation trigger
Trap"The valve area didn't change, so new-onset rapid AF / fever / pregnancy shouldn't suddenly cause pulmonary edema."
TruthTachycardia shortens diastole more than systole, cutting the time for blood to cross the valve. At any output, that raises the transvalvular gradient and LA pressure — flooding the lungs even with an unchanged orifice.
WhySeverity is gradient + flow, and flow depends on heart rate. Rate control is therapeutic, not just symptomatic.
In a case
A previously stable MS patient crashes into pulmonary edema after going into rapid AF. The mechanism is loss of diastolic filling time → higher LA pressure, and first move is rate control.
04The "normal" ejection fraction
Trap"A patient with severe MS and worsening dyspnea must have a depressed EF / LV systolic dysfunction."
TruthIn isolated MS, LV diastolic pressure and EF are NORMAL. The LV is simply under-filled — the problem is inflow obstruction, not pump failure.
WhyAttributing MS dyspnea to LV systolic dysfunction sends you down the wrong therapeutic path (inotropes/ACE-I) instead of relieving obstruction.
In a case
Echo in symptomatic isolated MS shows a tight valve but normal LVEF and normal LV diastolic pressure — exactly as expected; the dyspnea comes from high LA/pulmonary pressures.
05"Silent MS"
Trap"No diastolic rumble on exam → the MS must be mild."
TruthWhen CO is markedly reduced, there isn't enough flow to generate the rumble — the classic findings disappear (silent MS) and reappear as compensation is restored.
WhyA quiet auscultation can mean very low-output severe disease, not absence of disease. Image, don't dismiss.
In a case
A low-output patient has no audible murmur but echo shows a 1.0 cm² valve. The explanation is "silent MS" from markedly reduced cardiac output.
06Austin Flint vs the MS rumble
Trap"Apical low-pitched diastolic murmur = mitral stenosis."
TruthSevere AR produces the Austin Flint apical diastolic murmur. Distinguish it: it has no presystolic accentuation and softens with amyl nitrite / arterial vasodilators — the true MS rumble does neither, and MS has an OS + loud S₁.
WhyThe two have opposite treatments; mislabeling sends a severe-AR patient toward the wrong valve.
In a case
An apical diastolic murmur softens after amyl nitrite and has no OS — the lesion is severe aortic regurgitation (Austin Flint murmur), not MS.
07Graham Steell vs aortic regurgitation
Trap"High-pitched decrescendo diastolic murmur at the left sternal border = aortic regurgitation."
TruthIn MS with severe pulmonary hypertension, the Graham Steell murmur of pulmonic regurgitation looks identical — but it can increase with inspiration and comes with a loud, palpable P₂.
WhyRecognizing it as PR (not AR) reframes the whole picture as advanced MS-driven pulmonary hypertension.
In a case
An MS patient with a loud palpable P₂ has an LSB decrescendo diastolic murmur that intensifies on inspiration — this is the Graham Steell murmur of pulmonic regurgitation.
08ASD masquerading as MS
Trap"RV enlargement + increased pulmonary vascularity on CXR = mitral stenosis."
TruthAtrial septal defect shares those features but shows NO LA enlargement, NO Kerley B lines, plus fixed splitting of S₂, a grade II–III mid-systolic murmur at the mid/upper LSB, and incomplete RBBB.
WhyTwo cardinal MS findings (LA enlargement, Kerley B lines) are absent in ASD — that's the discriminator.
In a case
RV enlargement and pulmonary plethora but a fixed split S₂, no LA enlargement, and no Kerley B lines point to atrial septal defect, not MS.
09Left atrial myxoma mimicking MS
Trap"Dyspnea + diastolic murmur + MS-like hemodynamics = rheumatic MS."
TruthA left atrial myxoma obstructs LA emptying and mimics MS — but look for systemic features (weight loss, fever, anemia, emboli, ↑ IgG & IL-6) and auscultatory findings that change with body position. TTE shows the mass.
WhyConstitutional symptoms + positional murmur is the tell that this isn't valvular MS.
In a case
A patient with "MS" plus weight loss, fever, and a murmur that changes with position has a left atrial myxoma; confirm with echocardiography.
10Operating on the asymptomatic / mild patient
Trap"Commissurotomy improves everyone, so intervene early in asymptomatic or mild–moderate MS."
TruthCommissurotomy does NOT improve prognosis in patients with little/no functional impairment. In asymptomatic and/or mild–moderate MS (area >1.5 cm²) it's not recommended UNLESS there's recurrent systemic embolization or severe PH (PASP >50 rest / >60 exercise).
WhyYou expose a low-risk patient to procedural harm with no prognostic gain — except in those two specific exceptions.
In a case
An asymptomatic patient with a 1.8 cm² valve asks about a balloon procedure. Correct answer: no commissurotomy — continue surveillance (unless embolization or severe PH appears).
11The pregnant patient with MS
Trap"She's pregnant, so any mitral intervention is off the table / go straight to open surgery."
TruthIf pulmonary congestion persists despite intensive medical therapy, do a commissurotomy — and PMBC is the preferred strategy, done TEE-guided with no or minimal x-ray exposure.
WhyPMBC's low radiation + TEE guidance make it the fetus-sparing choice over open surgery.
In a case
A pregnant woman with severe MS stays congested despite diuretics and rate control. Best intervention: percutaneous mitral balloon commissurotomy (PMBC), TEE-guided.
12Who is NOT a PMBC candidate
Trap"Severe symptomatic MS → always do the balloon (PMBC)."
TruthPMBC requires all four: pliable leaflets / little commissural calcium, non-scarred subvalvular apparatus, no LA thrombus, and associated MR ≤2+/4+. Heavy calcification, significant MR, or LA clot push you to surgery. TEE is done routinely to exclude LA thrombus first.
WhyBallooning a calcified valve or one with an LA clot risks worsening MR or causing embolic stroke.
In a case
Severe MS with 3+ MR and heavy commissural calcium → not a balloon candidate; the answer is surgical mitral valve intervention, and pre-procedure TEE to exclude LA thrombus.
13Hemoptysis & the PVR paradox
Trap"Hemoptysis means the worst, end-stage MS with sky-high pulmonary vascular resistance."
TruthMS hemoptysis (ruptured pulmonary–bronchial venous connections) occurs most when LA pressure is high but PVR is NOT yet markedly elevated, and is rarely fatal. As PVR rises later, the "second stenosis" actually protects against it.
WhyIt's a marker of pressure transmission, not necessarily of terminal disease — and is usually benign.
In a case
An MS patient coughs up blood; the source is rupture of pulmonary-bronchial venous connections from pulmonary venous hypertension — high LA pressure with not-yet-elevated PVR, and it is rarely fatal.
14Stroke as the first sign
Trap"This patient has only mild MS and no symptoms, so embolic stroke is very unlikely."
TruthSystemic embolization (incidence 10–20%) can be the PRESENTING feature in an otherwise asymptomatic patient with only mild MS — more so with AF, age >65, or reduced CO.
WhySymptom severity does not predict embolic risk; the LA appendage clot doesn't care how breathless the patient is.
In a case
A young patient presents with an embolic stroke and is incidentally found to have mild MS. The stroke is the presenting feature of MS-related systemic embolization.
15Hoarseness in MS (Ortner's)
Trap"Hoarseness in a cardiac patient is unrelated — refer to ENT for an isolated laryngeal cause."
TruthA greatly enlarged LA can compress the left recurrent laryngeal nerve → hoarseness = Ortner's (cardiovocal) syndrome.
WhyIt's a clue to massive LA enlargement, not a coincidental ENT problem.
In a case
An MS patient with significant LA enlargement develops hoarseness — the diagnosis is Ortner's syndrome from left recurrent laryngeal nerve compression.
