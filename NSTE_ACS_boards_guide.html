Overview & Epidemiology

ACS Classification

Patients with acute coronary syndrome (ACS) are divided into two major groups based on the presenting ECG, which determines the urgency and direction of treatment:

STEMI
  • ST-segment elevation on presenting ECG
  • Incidence is declining due to aspirin, statins, less smoking
  • Complete thrombotic occlusion typical
  • Immediate reperfusion priority
NSTE-ACS
  • No ST elevation on ECG
  • Incidence rising: obesity, diabetes, CKD, aging
  • Includes NSTEMI (troponin +) and UA (troponin −)
  • UA proportion falling as hsTn reclassifies to NSTEMI

NSTEMI vs Unstable Angina — The Key Distinction

Both NSTEMI and UA present without ST elevation, but differ critically on troponin status, which reflects whether myocyte necrosis has occurred:

NSTEMI
  • Evidence of myocyte necrosis present
  • Elevated high-sensitivity cardiac troponin (hsTn)
  • Dynamic troponin rise and partial fall
  • Increasingly common due to sensitive assays
Unstable Angina (UA)
  • No myocyte necrosis
  • Troponin NOT elevated above 99th percentile
  • Proportion is decreasing due to hsTn reclassification
  • Still clinically urgent — high near-term risk
🔑 Board Concept
The same patient who was previously diagnosed as UA may now be reclassified as NSTEMI because highly sensitive troponin assays detect smaller degrees of myocyte injury. The threshold for necrosis has not changed — our ability to detect it has.

Epidemiologic Trends

  • NSTEMI rising: driven by increased obesity, diabetes mellitus, chronic kidney disease in an aging population AND wider adoption of highly sensitive troponin assays
  • STEMI declining: attributed to widespread aspirin use, statin therapy, and smoking cessation campaigns
  • UA proportion falling: hsTn reclassifying previous UA to NSTEMI by detecting previously undetected myocyte necrosis

Pathophysiology

Core Mechanism

NSTE-ACS results from an imbalance between myocardial oxygen supply and demand, most commonly caused by coronary arterial thrombosis superimposed on atherosclerotic disease. There are four pathologic processes:

Process 1

Plaque Fissure WITH Inflammation

Adaptive immunity dysregulation with increased activity of effector T cells. An inflammatory response triggers instability of the fibrous cap, leading to rupture and thrombosis.

Red (fibrin-rich) thrombusThin fibrous capLarge lipid core
Process 2

Plaque Fissure WITHOUT Inflammation

Mechanical disruption of a vulnerable plaque without a dominant inflammatory component. The lipid-rich core is exposed, triggering thrombosis via contact activation.

Non-inflammatoryVulnerable plaque
Process 3

Plaque Erosion

Present in at least one-third of ACS — and recognized with increasing frequency. The plaque surface erodes without cap rupture. Neutrophils, NETs (neutrophil extracellular traps), and smooth muscle cells are prominent.

White (platelet-rich) thrombusProteoglycan-richLittle/no lipid core
Process 4

Without Thrombosis

Epicardial or microvascular spasm, or increased myocardial oxygen demand — typically in the presence of fixed epicardial coronary obstruction. No thrombus formation.

VasospasmO₂ demand mismatch
💜 Pearl — Ruptured vs Eroded Plaque
Ruptured plaques have a thin fibrous cap, large lipid core, macrophages, and form fibrin-rich red thrombi. Eroded plaques have intact caps with no/little lipid core, neutrophils + smooth muscle cells, and form platelet-rich white thrombi. This distinction is increasingly recognized as clinically important for therapeutic targeting.

Coronary Anatomy Findings at Angiography

Among NSTE-ACS patients studied at angiography, the distribution of disease is:

~35%

~25%

⚠ Important
~10% of NSTE-ACS patients have NO apparent critical epicardial coronary artery stenosis on angiography. These patients may have coronary microcirculation obstruction and/or epicardial vessel spasm — don't assume all ACS is due to obstructive disease.

Vulnerable Plaque Morphology

  • Eccentric stenosis with scalloped/overhanging edges and narrow neck on coronary angiography
  • Lipid-rich core with a thin fibrous cap — classic appearance of rupture-prone plaque
  • Patients frequently have multiple vulnerable plaques at risk of disruption simultaneously

Clinical Presentation & Diagnosis

Classic Chest Discomfort — Three Key Features

The diagnosis of NSTE-ACS is based on a combination of history, clinical findings, age, ECG, and circulating troponin. Typically, chest discomfort is severe and has at least one of:

  • Rest occurrence: Occurring at rest or with minimal exertion, lasting >10 minutes
  • Recent onset: Of relatively recent onset — within the prior 2 weeks
  • Crescendo pattern: Distinctly more severe, prolonged, or frequent than previous episodes
    • 🔑 Location & Radiation
    Chest discomfort is typically located in the substernal region and radiates to the left arm, left shoulder, and/or superiorly to the neck and jaw. Recognize these patterns on boards.

    Anginal Equivalents — High-Yield for Boards

    Instead of classic chest discomfort, ACS may present as atypical symptoms — especially in women, the elderly, and diabetics:

    Equivalent 1

    Dyspnea — shortness of breath without classic chest pain

    Equivalent 2

    Epigastric discomfort — may mimic GI pathology

    Equivalent 3

    Nausea or weakness — nonspecific but can be the dominant presentation

    🚨 Board Alert — High-Risk Populations
    Women, elderly patients, and patients with diabetes mellitus are MORE likely to present with anginal equivalents rather than classic chest pain. Failure to recognize this leads to missed ACS diagnoses.

    Physical Examination

    Physical exam may be unremarkable in many NSTE-ACS patients (resembles stable angina). However, with large ischemic territory or large NSTEMI, findings include:

    • Diaphoresis and pale, cool skin
    • Sinus tachycardia
    • S3 and/or S4 heart sound
    • Basilar rales (pulmonary edema)
    • Hypotension and in severe cases, cardiogenic shock
    ⚠ Must Exclude Alternative Life-Threatening Diagnoses
    An important goal of the physical exam is to identify alternative diagnoses that mimic ACS:
    • Pulmonary embolism — sudden dyspnea, pleuritic pain, hypoxia
    • Aortic dissection — tearing/ripping pain radiating to back, pulse differential
    • Cardiac tamponade — Beck's triad: hypotension, JVD, muffled heart sounds
    These require vastly different treatment. Mistaking dissection for ACS and giving thrombolytics = catastrophic.

    NSTEMI Diagnosis — Establishing the Criteria

    NSTEMI is established when all three of the following are present:

  • Clinical features consistent with NSTE-ACS (≥1 of the three chest discomfort characteristics above)
  • NO ST-segment elevation on ECG
  • Abnormally elevated circulating troponin reflecting myocyte necrosis, in the absence of another explanation

    Cardiac Biomarkers & ECG

    Electrocardiogram Findings

    • ST-segment depression occurs in about one-third of NSTE-ACS patients — may be transient or persist for days after NSTEMI
    • T-wave changes are more common but less specific for ischemia
    • Exception: new deep T-wave inversions ≥0.3 mV are highly specific and should raise concern
    🔑 ECG Pearl
    The absence of ST elevation does not mean absence of serious disease. One-third of NSTE-ACS show only ST depression; many show only T-wave changes or a completely normal ECG. A normal ECG does NOT rule out NSTEMI.

    Cardiac Troponin — The Preferred Biomarker

    High-sensitivity cardiac troponins (hsTnI or hsTnT) are the preferred markers of myocardial necrosis — sensitive, relatively specific, and the gold standard for NSTEMI diagnosis.

    Temporal Pattern
    • Characteristic rise begins after myocyte necrosis onset
    • Peaks at 12–24 hours after symptom onset
    • Gradually decreases thereafter
    • Direct relationship: degree of elevation correlates with mortality
    1-Hour Rule-Out Algorithm
    • Recommended by recent practice guidelines
    • No abnormal elevation of hsTn at 0 or 1 hour after presentation = low risk for MI
    • Enables rapid triage and early safe discharge in low-risk patients

    Myocardial Injury vs Myocardial Necrosis — Critical Distinction

    🚨 Key Distinction
    Myocardial Injury: Defined as troponin elevation >99th percentile of the upper reference limit in patients without a clear clinical history or ECG features of acute myocardial ischemia. This is NOT the same as MI!

    Myocardial Necrosis (= MI): Requires the clinical context — symptoms, ECG changes — plus troponin elevation.

    Many non-cardiac and cardiac conditions can elevate troponin without constituting NSTEMI:

    Non-Cardiac & Cardiac Causes of Troponin Elevation

    Supply/Demand Imbalance

    • Coronary artery spasm, microvascular dysfunction
    • Coronary embolism or dissection
    • Sustained brady- or tachyarrhythmia
    • Hypotension or shock
    • Respiratory failure, severe anemia
    • Severe hypertension (demand)

    Cardiac Conditions

    • Heart failure, myocarditis
    • Any cardiomyopathy type
    • Takotsubo syndrome
    • Recent coronary revascularization
    • Catheter ablation, defibrillator shocks
    • Cardiac contusion

    Systemic Conditions

    • Sepsis, critical illness
    • Chronic kidney disease
    • Stroke, subarachnoid hemorrhage
    • Pulmonary embolism
    • Amyloidosis, sarcoidosis (infiltrative)
    • Chemotherapeutic agents
    • Strenuous exercise

    Risk Stratification

    Early Risk Assessment — Why It Matters

    Patients with documented NSTE-ACS exhibit a wide spectrum of early (30-day) risk of death, ranging from 1 to 10%, and a recurrent ACS rate of 5–15% during the first year. Early risk assessment guides the decision to use an invasive versus conservative strategy.

    TIMI Risk Score — 7 Clinical Markers

    Each of the following markers counts as 1 point (max 7 points). Risk = incidence of death, new/recurrent MI, or severe ischemia requiring urgent revascularization at 14 days:

    Mnemonic — TIMI 7 Markers
    A CEASE-3
    • Age ≥65 years
    • Cardiac markers elevated
    • ECG: ST deviation >0.5 mm on presenting ECG
    • Angina prior history (prior angina)
    • Stenosis: Known CAD (≥50% stenosis)
    • Episodes ≥2 anginal episodes in prior 24h
    • 3 or more CAD risk factors
    TIMI ScoreAdverse Event Rate at 14 DaysInterpretation
    0–15%Low risk
    28%Low-moderate risk
    313%Moderate risk
    420%Moderate-high risk
    526%High risk
    6–741%Very high risk

    HEART Score — Emergency Department Pathway

    The HEART score assigns 0, 1, or 2 points for each domain based on degree of abnormality:

    HEART Components
    • History — characteristic of ACS?
    • ECG — findings
    • Age — ≥65 highest risk
    • Risk factors — DM, HTN, hyperlipidemia, obesity, smoking, FH, atherosclerosis
    • Troponin — elevation above reference limit
    HEART Score Triage
    • 0–3: Serial troponins → if <99th percentile → early discharge
    • ≥4: Initial troponin
      • ≥99th %ile → Cardiology consult & admission → stress test/angiography
      • <99th %ile → Observation or admission → stress test/angiography

    Multimarker Strategy

    Multimarker strategies including hsTn AND brain natriuretic peptides (BNP) are gaining favor to both define the pathophysiologic mechanisms and stratify risk further. This is especially useful in identifying patients who would benefit most from early invasive strategy.

    ✓ Clinical Application
    The GRACE score (Global Registry of Acute Coronary Events) is also used and incorporates age, heart rate, systolic BP, creatinine, Killip class, cardiac arrest, ST deviation, and troponin. GRACE score >140 = high-risk, triggers early invasive strategy.

    Anti-Ischemic Treatment

    Initial Management Principles

    Patients who "rule-in" for NSTE-ACS should be admitted to hospital, placed on bed rest with continuous ECG monitoring for ST deviation and arrhythmias — preferably on a specialized cardiac unit. Ambulation is permitted if no recurrence of ischemia and no troponin rise for 24h.

    Initial anti-ischemic treatment (in addition to bed rest):

    • Nitrates — for pain relief and vasodilation
    • Beta-adrenergic blockers — to reduce myocardial oxygen demand
    • Inhaled oxygen — for patients with hypoxemia (O₂ saturation <90%) or heart failure with rales

    Drug-by-Drug Guide

    Nitrates

    Mechanism

    Nitric oxide donor → vascular smooth muscle relaxation → venodilation reduces preload → decreased myocardial wall stress and O₂ demand. Also dilates coronary arteries, improving supply.

    Dosing Protocol

    • Step 1: Sublingual nitroglycerin 0.3–0.6 mg OR buccal spray
    • Step 2: If symptoms persist after 3 doses given 5 min apart → IV nitroglycerin
    • IV: Start at 5–10 μg/min (non-absorbing tubing); increase by 10 μg/min every 3–5 min
    • Stop when: symptoms relieved, SBP <90 mmHg, or dose reaches 200 μg/min
    • Transition: Topical or oral nitrates when pain-free for 12–24h

    Contraindications

    PDE-5 inhibitor within 24h (sildenafil, vardenafil) Tadalafil within 48h Hypotension (SBP <90) Right ventricular infarction Severe aortic stenosis
    🚨 Absolute Contraindication
    PDE-5 inhibitors (sildenafil, vardenafil) + nitrates → profound life-threatening hypotension. This is a board-favorite. Remember: sildenafil/vardenafil = 24h window, tadalafil = 48h window (longer half-life).
    Beta-Adrenergic Blockers

    Mechanism

    Competitive antagonism of β₁ adrenoceptors → reduces heart rate, myocardial contractility, and conduction velocity → decreases myocardial oxygen demand. Also reduces circulating catecholamines' harmful effects on the ischemic myocardium.

    Dosing

    • Oral beta blockade targeted to heart rate of 50–60 beats/min
    • IV route may be used in patients with severe ischemia

    Contraindications — Must Know All of These

    PR interval >0.24s 2nd or 3rd degree AV block HR <50 beats/min SBP <90 mmHg Acute or severe heart failure Cardiogenic shock (Killip III-IV) Low cardiac output Severe reactive airways disease Active bronchospasm High-degree AV block
    💜 Clinical Pearl
    Beta blockers are the other mainstay of anti-ischemic treatment alongside nitrates. They can be started IV in severe ischemia but MUST be avoided in acute/severe heart failure or cardiogenic shock — these scenarios require the heart to compensate by tachycardia, and beta blockade removes that compensatory mechanism.
    Calcium Channel Blockers (CCBs)

    Mechanism

    Non-dihydropyridine CCBs (verapamil, diltiazem) slow AV nodal conduction and reduce heart rate and contractility → reduced O₂ demand. Dihydropyridines (nifedipine) primarily vasodilate.

    When to Use

    • Persistent symptoms or ECG ischemia despite full-dose nitrates AND beta blockers
    • Contraindications to nitrates or beta blockers
    • Vasospastic angina — CCBs are the primary treatment

    Contraindications

    SBP <90 mmHg Pulmonary edema LV dysfunction (for diltiazem/verapamil) Avoid short-acting nifedipine
    Morphine / Narcotic Analgesics

    Indications

    • Continued severe angina despite 3 sublingual nitroglycerin tablets
    • Recurrent ischemia despite adequate anti-ischemic therapy

    Dose: 1–5 mg IV every 5–30 minutes

    Contraindications

    Hypotension Respiratory depression Confusion or obtundation
    🚨 Critical Drug Interaction
    Concomitant morphine administration delays absorption and blunts the antiplatelet effect of oral P2Y₁₂ inhibitors (e.g., ticagrelor, clopidogrel). This is clinically important in patients receiving DAPT — consider this when timing medications.
    Statins — Early High-Intensity Therapy

    Mechanism

    HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors → reduce hepatic cholesterol synthesis → upregulate LDL receptors → lower circulating LDL-C. Also have pleiotropic anti-inflammatory and plaque-stabilizing effects.

    Drugs & Doses

    • Atorvastatin 80 mg/day — initiated early, prior to PCI, continued thereafter
    • Rosuvastatin 40 mg/day — alternative high-intensity option

    Early administration reduces BOTH early and late recurrent ischemic events.

    When Statins Are Insufficient (<50% LDL reduction)

    • Add ezetimibe 10 mg daily — inhibits intestinal cholesterol absorption
    • And/or add PCSK9 inhibitor (alirocumab or evolocumab) early after ACS
    ✓ LDL Goal
    Target LDL-C <55 mg/dL in the long-term management of NSTE-ACS. This is the guideline-recommended goal for very high cardiovascular risk patients.

    Antithrombotic Therapy

    Antiplatelet Drugs — The Foundation

    Antithrombotic therapy (antiplatelet + anticoagulant) represents the second major cornerstone of NSTE-ACS treatment, after anti-ischemic therapy. Initial treatment always begins with aspirin.

    Aspirin (Cyclooxygenase Inhibitor)

    Mechanism

    Irreversibly inhibits COX-1 → prevents thromboxane A₂ synthesis → reduces platelet aggregation for the platelet's lifetime (~10 days).

    Dosing

    • Loading: 150–325 mg oral (non-enteric-coated) OR 75–250 mg IV
    • Maintenance: 75–100 mg/day — lower doses maintain efficacy with less bleeding

    Contraindications

    Severe active bleedingAspirin allergy
    P2Y₁₂ Inhibitors — Dual Antiplatelet Therapy (DAPT)

    In the absence of high bleeding risk, all NSTE-ACS patients (invasive OR conservative strategy) should also receive a P2Y₁₂ receptor blocker to inhibit platelet activation.

    Clopidogrel (Thienopyridine — Prodrug)

    • Mechanism: Inactive prodrug → CYP2C19-dependent conversion → active metabolite → irreversible P2Y₁₂ blockade
    • Loading: 600 mg (if PCI planned) or 300 mg (no PCI planned)
    • Maintenance: 75 mg/day
    • Benefit: DAPT (aspirin + clopidogrel) confers 20% relative reduction in CV death/MI/stroke vs aspirin alone
    • Cost: Moderate increase in major bleeding (~1% absolute)
    • Limitation: Up to 1/3 of patients have inadequate response — many due to CYP2C19 genetic variant (reduced active metabolite conversion)

    Prasugrel (Thienopyridine — Superior to Clopidogrel)

    • Mechanism: Irreversible P2Y₁₂ blockade — more rapid onset and higher level of inhibition than clopidogrel
    • Loading: 60 mg pre-PCI
    • Maintenance: 10 mg/day (reduce to 5 mg/day if body weight <60 kg OR age >75 years)
    • Benefit: Significantly reduced CV death/MI/stroke/stent thrombosis vs clopidogrel (but increased bleeding)
    • Key restriction: Approved for ACS patients following angiography when PCI is planned — administer only after coronary anatomy is known
    • Timing: Unlike ticagrelor, benefit demonstrated only with invasive strategy (scheduled PCI)
    🚨 Prasugrel Absolute Contraindications
    • Prior stroke or TIA — high risk of intracranial bleeding
    • High risk for bleeding
    Also: Do NOT give prasugrel before coronary anatomy is known (risk of giving it to a patient who needs CABG → severe surgical bleeding).

    Ticagrelor (Non-Thienopyridine — Reversible)

    • Mechanism: Direct, reversible P2Y₁₂ inhibitor — does NOT require metabolic activation
    • Loading: 180 mg
    • Maintenance: 90 mg twice daily
    • Key advantage over prasugrel: Demonstrated benefit whether managed conservatively OR with early invasive strategy — broader applicability
    • Side effect: Dyspnea — often transient and infrequently serious; direct P2Y₁₂-related (not bronchospasm)
    • Long-term dose reduction: 1 year after ACS, reduce to 60 mg twice daily — reduces bleeding without increased ischemic events

    Cangrelor (IV P2Y₁₂ Inhibitor)

    • IV, direct, rapidly acting P2Y₁₂ inhibitor used at time of PCI
    • Dose: 30 μg/kg bolus + 4 μg/kg/min infusion for 2h or duration of procedure
    • Benefit vs clopidogrel in PCI patients — but increased major bleeding
    Glycoprotein IIb/IIIa Inhibitors (GPIs)

    Mechanism

    Block the final common pathway of platelet aggregation (fibrinogen cross-linking via GPIIb/IIIa receptor). Highly potent antiplatelet agents.

    Current Role — Limited

    • Historical trials showed only modest benefit with increased major bleeding
    • Most prior studies lacked concomitant P2Y₁₂ inhibitor therapy — when added to P2Y₁₂ inhibitors, routine GPIs show no additional benefit
    • Current use: Reserve for PCI with high coronary thrombus burden on angiography OR thrombotic complications of PCI
    • Triple antiplatelet therapy (aspirin + P2Y₁₂ + GPI) should NOT be routine due to bleeding risk
    AgentDosing
    Eptifibatide180 μg/kg bolus × 2 (10 min apart) + 2.0 μg/kg/min infusion up to 18h
    Tirofiban25 μg/kg bolus over 3 min + 0.15 μg/kg/min up to 18h

    DAPT Duration — Critical for Long-Term Management

    Standard: 12 Months
    • Preferred for most NSTE-ACS patients
    • Aspirin (75–100 mg/d) + P2Y₁₂ inhibitor (prasugrel or ticagrelor preferred over clopidogrel)
    • After 12 months → antiplatelet monotherapy
    Shorter: 3–6 Months
    • High bleeding risk + low ischemic risk
    • After 3–6 months: stop aspirin, continue P2Y₁₂ inhibitor alone
    • OR de-escalate: switch from prasugrel/ticagrelor to clopidogrel at 3 months while continuing aspirin
    🚨 DAPT De-escalation NOT in First 30 Days
    De-escalation of DAPT is not recommended in the first 30 days following ACS as this has been associated with increased risk of ischemic events. Wait at least 30 days before any modification.
    🔑 Longer DAPT (>12 months)
    Favored in patients with high atherothrombotic risk:
    • Stenting of left main coronary artery or proximal LAD
    • Proximal bifurcating coronary arteries
    • Recurrent MI or stent thrombosis

    Anticoagulants — Four Parenteral Options

    AgentMechanismKey FeaturesDose
    Unfractionated Heparin (UFH) Enhances antithrombin III → inhibits IIa and Xa Long the mainstay; widely studied; requires monitoring 70–100 U/kg bolus (max 5000 U) IV + 12–15 U/kg/h (ACT 250–300s)
    Enoxaparin (LMWH) Primarily anti-Xa; some anti-IIa Superior to UFH in reducing recurrent events (conservative strategy); modest bleeding increase; renally cleared 1 mg/kg SC q12h; 0.5 mg/kg IV at time of PCI. Renal dose: 1 mg/kg once daily if CrCl <30 mL/min
    Bivalirudin Direct thrombin inhibitor Similar efficacy to UFH/LMWH; used peri-PCI only; rarely used in conservative management 0.75 mg/kg IV bolus + 1.75 mg/kg/h infusion
    Fondaparinux Synthetic factor Xa inhibitor Equivalent efficacy to enoxaparin; lower major bleeding; requires supplemental UFH if PCI (prevents catheter thrombosis) 2.5 mg SC daily (only prior to PCI)
    ⚠ DOAC + Parenteral Anticoagulant Interaction
    In patients who develop NSTE-ACS while receiving a direct oral anticoagulant (DOAC), the DOAC should be held when one of the four parenteral anticoagulants is initiated. Do not combine.

    Atrial Fibrillation + NSTE-ACS — Triple Therapy Management

    • Duration of "triple therapy" (aspirin + clopidogrel + oral anticoagulant) should be kept to a minimum
    • Stop aspirin at discharge or at 1 week post-PCI
    • Continue P2Y₁₂ inhibitor + DOAC for 1 year
    • After 1 year → oral anticoagulation monotherapy without antiplatelet treatment

    Invasive vs Conservative Strategy

    The Fundamental Question

    After initiating anti-ischemic and antithrombotic therapy, the next critical decision is when and whether to perform coronary angiography. Risk stratification drives this decision. Patients are classified into three tiers:

    NSTE-ACS Invasive Strategy Selection Algorithm
    Very High Risk
    Immediate Invasive <2 hours
    • Cardiogenic shock or unstable hemodynamics
    • Acute HF due to ongoing ischemia
    • Cardiac arrest post-presentation
    • Life-threatening arrhythmias
    • Mechanical complications of MI
    • Angina refractory to medical treatment
    • Recurrent dynamic ischemic ECG changes
    High Risk
    Early Invasive <24 hours
    • Confirmed NSTEMI
    • GRACE risk score >140
    • Transient ST elevation
    • Dynamic ST or T-wave changes
    Non-High Risk
    Selective Invasive
    • None of the above criteria
    • Clinical and non-invasive assessments guide management
    • Coronary angiography if: rest pain/ST changes recur, troponin becomes positive, evidence of severe ischemia on stress test, or CT angiography shows epicardial stenosis

    Evidence for Invasive Strategy

    • Multiple clinical trials demonstrated benefit of early invasive strategy in high-risk patients (multiple risk factors, ST deviation, positive biomarkers)
    • Immediate invasive (median 1.4–4.7h) reduced death or new MI vs delayed invasive (median 61–62h) — greater benefit in those with high-risk scores
    • In low-risk patients, outcomes from invasive and conservative strategies are similar
    • Radial arterial access is recommended (vs femoral) to reduce bleeding when early invasive strategy is used

    After Angiography — Revascularization Decision

    Single-Stage PCI

    In patients with multivessel disease, consider complete coronary revascularization during the index procedure — in the absence of:

    • Cardiogenic shock
    • Severe comorbidities (e.g., acute kidney injury)
    • Coronary anatomy unfavorable or complex
    Staged Revascularization

    For patients with:

    • High risk (cardiogenic shock)
    • Severe comorbidities
    • Complex/unfavorable anatomy

    → Culprit lesion PCI first, then evaluate non-culprit lesions

    🔑 PCI vs CABG
    Choice between PCI and CABG (coronary artery bypass grafting) depends on coronary anatomy. CABG is preferred for unfavorable anatomy — complex multivessel disease, especially with left main stenosis and reduced LV function. A Heart Team approach guides the decision for complex cases.

    Long-Term Management

    Hospital Discharge — A "Teachable Moment"

    Hospital discharge is described as a "teachable moment" — the caregiver must review and optimize the medical regimen. Risk factor modification is crucial.

    Lifestyle Targets
    • Smoking cessation
    • Appropriate diet
    • Achieve and maintain optimal weight
    • Daily exercise
    • Blood pressure control
    • Hyperglycemia control
    Metabolic Targets
    • LDL-C <55 mg/dL — very high CV risk goal
    • Blood pressure goal per guidelines
    • HbA1c target in diabetic patients

    Long-Term Drug Therapy — Evidence-Based

    Always Indicated
    • Beta blockers — long-term cardioprotection
    • High-intensity statin (atorvastatin 80mg or rosuvastatin 40mg)
    • Add ezetimibe 10mg if LDL not at goal
    • Add PCSK9 inhibitor (alirocumab or evolocumab) or bempedoic acid if still not at goal
    • ACE inhibitor or ARB
    In Selected Patients
    • SGLT2 inhibitors — in type 2 diabetes mellitus with established CVD
    • GLP-1 receptor agonists — in type 2 DM; CV risk reduction
    • Long-term DAPT >12 months — in high atherothrombotic risk

    Health Equity Considerations

    ⚠ Disparities in Care
    Registries have shown that women and racial minorities, as well as high-risk patients including the elderly and those with diabetes or chronic kidney disease, are less likely to receive evidence-based pharmacologic and interventional therapies — resulting in poorer clinical outcomes and quality of life. Special attention must be directed to these groups.

    Vasospastic Angina (Prinzmetal's)

    Definition & Mechanism

    Originally described by Prinzmetal in 1959 as variant angina — now renamed vasospastic angina (VA). Caused by focal spasm of an epicardial coronary artery → transmural ischemia → LV dysfunction.

    • Spasm mechanism: hypercontractility of coronary arterial smooth muscle driven by adrenergic vasoconstrictors, leukotrienes, or serotonin (exact cause not well defined)
    • Can lead to acute MI, ventricular tachycardia/fibrillation, or sudden cardiac death
    • Prevalence declining in recent decades; remains much more frequent in Japan than North America or Western Europe

    Clinical Characteristics — Key Differences from NSTE-ACS

    Vasospastic Angina
    • Patients generally younger
    • Fewer coronary risk factors (except cigarette smoking)
    • Pain at rest — classic nocturnal pattern
    • ECG: ST elevation (transient) with rest pain — NOT depression
    • May have associated migraine and/or Raynaud's phenomenon (generalized vasospastic disorder)
    • Cardiac examination unremarkable in absence of ischemia
    NSTE-ACS
    • Generally older patients
    • Multiple coronary risk factors
    • Pain with exertion or rest
    • ECG: ST depression or T-wave changes
    • No vasospastic associations
    • Troponin elevated in NSTEMI
    🚨 Board Alert — ST Changes
    Vasospastic angina causes ST ELEVATION — not depression — during episodes. This is unlike NSTE-ACS where ST depression is the typical finding. Don't be fooled into thinking all rest pain + ST elevation = STEMI. The transient nature and clinical context distinguish VA.

    Diagnosis

    • Clinical diagnosis: detection of transient ST elevation with rest pain
    • Many patients have episodes of silent ischemia as well
    • Coronary angiography: Transient coronary spasm is the diagnostic hallmark
    • Atherosclerotic plaques in at least one proximal coronary artery occur in about half of patients
    • Provocative testing: Hyperventilation or intracoronary acetylcholine → provokes focal spasm on angiography or rest angina with ST elevation
    • Positive provocative test in patients without obstructive CAD = safe, identifies high-risk subgroup, endorsed by guidelines

    Treatment

    Primary: Calcium Channel Blockers

    CCBs are the main therapeutic agents for vasospastic angina. They prevent coronary smooth muscle contraction by blocking L-type calcium channels.

    Nitrates

    Sublingual or IV nitroglycerin is effective to relieve acute spasm. However, long-term nitrate efficacy is limited by nitrate tolerance — do not rely on them as sole therapy.

    🚨 Aspirin May WORSEN Vasospastic Angina
    Aspirin may increase the severity of ischemic episodes in vasospastic angina — possibly by inhibiting prostacyclin synthesis (prostacyclin normally opposes vasoconstriction, so reducing it worsens spasm). Aspirin should only be used in vasospastic angina patients with significant concurrent atherosclerosis.
    • Statins: Shown to reduce risk of major adverse events in VA (mechanism not fully established)
    • Beta blockers: Variable response in VA — not first-line
    • Cilostazol and nicorandil (not available in US): may prevent recurrent episodes
    • Coronary revascularization: May help if there are discrete, flow-limiting, proximal fixed obstructive lesions
    • Implantable cardioverter-defibrillator (ICD): For patients with ischemia-associated ventricular fibrillation despite maximal medical therapy

    Prognosis

    Active Phase

    First 6 months — frequent episodes of angina and cardiac events. After this, symptoms tend to diminish over time.

    5-Year Survival

    ~90–95% excellent survival — BUT up to 20% of patients experience an MI. Serious arrhythmias = higher risk for sudden cardiac death.

    Predictors

    Patients with NO or mild fixed coronary obstruction have lower incidence of cardiac death/MI than those with severe obstructive lesions.

    High-Yield Distinctions

    NSTEMI vs UA

    Same presentation, same ECG (no ST elevation) — distinguished ONLY by troponin. Elevated troponin = NSTEMI. Normal troponin = UA. This has therapeutic implications.

    Ticagrelor vs Prasugrel

    Both superior to clopidogrel. Key difference: Ticagrelor works in BOTH conservative and invasive strategies. Prasugrel is ONLY for planned PCI (after angiography). Also: prasugrel is CI in prior TIA/stroke.

    Vasospastic vs NSTEMI

    Vasospastic = rest pain + ST ELEVATION (transient). NSTEMI = rest pain + ST depression or T-wave changes + troponin rise. Aspirin may worsen vasospastic angina. CCBs are primary therapy for vasospastic.

    Morphine + P2Y₁₂

    Morphine delays absorption and blunts antiplatelet effect of oral P2Y₁₂ inhibitors. This is a critical drug interaction. Consider timing of administration when both are needed.

    Myocardial Injury vs MI

    Elevated troponin alone ≠ MI. Myocardial INJURY = troponin >99th percentile without clinical context. MI requires clinical features + ECG changes + troponin. Many non-cardiac causes elevate troponin.

    Fondaparinux Limitation

    Fondaparinux has lowest bleeding among anticoagulants but REQUIRES supplemental UFH if PCI is performed — to prevent catheter-related thrombosis at the arterial access site.

    Exam Traps

    Expert board exam coaching — Stage A Internal Medicine Boards. Each trap shows exactly how the question is framed, the seductive wrong answer, and the correct reasoning.

    Trap 01The Silent Troponin Reclassification Trap
    A 58-year-old man with hypertension presents with 20 minutes of substernal chest pressure at rest, now resolved. ECG shows no ST elevation. The first troponin is negative. The question asks: What is the most appropriate next step — discharge home, repeat troponin in 1 hour, or admit for CABG evaluation?
    Correct Answer: Repeat high-sensitivity troponin at 1 hour (and possibly 3 hours)
    The 1-hour rapid rule-out algorithm requires hsTn to be non-elevated at BOTH 0 and 1 hour before low risk can be established. A single negative troponin is insufficient. The patient could still be in the early phase of NSTEMI before troponin rises.
    ⚠ Trap: Students discharge the patient after one negative troponin. The board-exam keyword here is "high-sensitivity troponin" — the rule-out protocol mandates serial sampling. A normal 1st troponin does NOT rule out NSTEMI.
    Trap 02Nitrates + PDE-5 Inhibitor Timing Trap
    A 62-year-old man presents to the ED with chest pain. He used sildenafil 20 hours ago. He is hypertensive (BP 155/90). The question asks: Should IV nitroglycerin be initiated?
    Correct Answer: NO — sildenafil was taken within 24 hours
    Sildenafil and vardenafil require a 24-hour nitrate-free window. This patient used sildenafil 20 hours ago — still within the 24-hour prohibition. Administering nitrates could cause profound, refractory hypotension due to synergistic vasodilation.
    ⚠ Trap: Students see hypertension (BP 155/90) and think nitrates are indicated. They forget to check for recent PDE-5 inhibitor use. Board keyword: tadalafil = 48h window (longer half-life); sildenafil/vardenafil = 24h window. Always ask about recent sexual dysfunction medication use.
    Trap 03Prasugrel Contraindication — The Prior TIA Patient
    A 65-year-old woman with a history of TIA 6 months ago presents with confirmed NSTEMI. She undergoes coronary angiography showing single-vessel LAD disease. PCI with stenting is planned. The question asks: Which P2Y₁₂ inhibitor should be selected — prasugrel or ticagrelor?
    Correct Answer: Ticagrelor — prasugrel is CONTRAINDICATED in prior TIA/stroke
    Prasugrel is absolutely contraindicated in patients with a prior stroke or TIA due to unacceptably high risk of intracranial hemorrhage. Ticagrelor is the appropriate P2Y₁₂ inhibitor in this patient.
    ⚠ Trap: PCI is planned, which might make prasugrel seem like the natural choice (it's the agent approved for PCI). But prior TIA is an absolute contraindication. Board keyword: prasugrel = CI in prior TIA/stroke. Also CI: high bleeding risk.
    Trap 04Beta Blocker in Acute Heart Failure — The Timing Trap
    A 70-year-old woman with NSTEMI is admitted. On exam she has basilar crackles bilaterally, HR 110 bpm, and BP 100/65. She is mildly short of breath. The question asks: Should beta blockade be initiated now?
    Correct Answer: No — beta blockers are contraindicated in the presence of acute heart failure
    This patient has signs of acute heart failure (bilateral rales, tachycardia, low-normal BP). Beta blockers are contraindicated in acute/severe heart failure, low cardiac output, cardiogenic shock, and Killip class III or IV. Giving beta blockers here removes the compensatory tachycardia and can precipitate cardiogenic shock.
    ⚠ Trap: Students know beta blockers are a "cornerstone" of NSTE-ACS treatment and initiate them immediately. The trap is ignoring the acute heart failure signs. Board keyword: Killip class III-IV = avoid beta blockers acutely. Once heart failure is stabilized, beta blockers can be started at low dose.
    Trap 05Vasospastic Angina — Aspirin Worsens Symptoms
    A 44-year-old Japanese woman with no significant cardiac history presents with episodes of chest pain at rest, occurring nightly at 3 AM. ECG during an episode shows transient ST elevation. Coronary angiography shows no significant stenosis, and acetylcholine provocation induces focal spasm. The question asks: What is the most appropriate pharmacotherapy?
    Correct Answer: Calcium channel blocker (not aspirin as monotherapy)
    CCBs are the primary treatment for vasospastic angina. Crucially, aspirin may INCREASE the severity of ischemic episodes in vasospastic angina by reducing prostacyclin synthesis (prostacyclin normally opposes vasoconstriction). Aspirin should only be used if concurrent atherosclerosis is present.
    ⚠ Trap: Students immediately reach for aspirin in any coronary syndrome. In vasospastic angina without significant atherosclerosis, aspirin can paradoxically worsen spasm. Board keyword: vasospastic angina + aspirin = may worsen; CCB = first-line.
    Trap 06Elevated Troponin ≠ Myocardial Infarction
    A 55-year-old woman with septic shock from a UTI is found to have troponin I elevated to 3× the upper reference limit. She has no chest pain. ECG shows sinus tachycardia only. The question asks: Does this patient have NSTEMI?
    Correct Answer: No — this is myocardial INJURY, not MI
    Myocardial injury is defined as elevated troponin >99th percentile WITHOUT a clinical history or ECG features of acute myocardial ischemia. Sepsis is a classic non-cardiac cause of troponin elevation. MI requires the clinical context: symptoms, ECG changes, AND troponin. Here, the elevated troponin reflects supply-demand imbalance from sepsis-induced hypotension and tachycardia.
    ⚠ Trap: Reflex NSTEMI diagnosis whenever troponin is elevated. Board keyword: myocardial injury vs myocardial necrosis (MI). Sepsis, PE, myocarditis, CKD, strenuous exercise — all can elevate troponin without constituting MI. Always contextualize.
    Trap 07Morphine Blunts P2Y₁₂ Effect — Drug Interaction Timing
    A 60-year-old man with NSTEMI is being prepared for PCI. He received IV morphine 30 minutes ago for pain control. He also received a 180 mg loading dose of ticagrelor. The interventional cardiologist is concerned about residual platelet activity at the time of the procedure. Why might platelet inhibition be inadequate?
    Correct Answer: Morphine delays absorption and blunts the antiplatelet effect of oral P2Y₁₂ inhibitors like ticagrelor
    Morphine slows gastric motility → delayed drug absorption → reduced peak plasma concentrations of ticagrelor → inadequate platelet inhibition at the time of PCI. This is an established, clinically important drug interaction.
    ⚠ Trap: Students assume oral medications work within minutes. The interaction is particularly relevant in the peri-procedural period when adequate platelet inhibition is critical. Board keyword: morphine + oral P2Y₁₂ = delayed absorption and blunted effect. Consider IV cangrelor if rapid platelet inhibition is needed.
    Trap 08Ticagrelor Works in Conservative Strategy — Prasugrel Does Not
    A 78-year-old man with NSTEMI has multiple comorbidities making him a poor candidate for PCI. His team decides on a conservative (non-invasive) management strategy. The question asks: Which P2Y₁₂ inhibitor is appropriate — clopidogrel, prasugrel, or ticagrelor?
    Correct Answer: Ticagrelor (or clopidogrel) — NOT prasugrel
    Ticagrelor demonstrated benefit in both conservatively managed patients AND those with early invasive strategy across a broad spectrum of ACS patients. Prasugrel's approval and evidence base is specifically for ACS patients with PLANNED PCI — it is NOT indicated for conservatively managed patients and should not be given before coronary anatomy is known.
    ⚠ Trap: Assuming all three P2Y₁₂ inhibitors are interchangeable. Board keyword: ticagrelor = both strategies; prasugrel = invasive (PCI) only. Also remember: prasugrel is contraindicated in prior TIA/stroke and this 78-year-old may also qualify for the age-based dose reduction (5 mg/day if >75 years) if prasugrel were somehow chosen.
    Trap 09DAPT De-escalation — The 30-Day Rule
    A 65-year-old woman with NSTEMI, treated with ticagrelor and aspirin, is discharged. On day 15, she develops minor GI bleeding. Her physician considers switching her to clopidogrel (de-escalating) to reduce bleeding risk. Is de-escalation appropriate at this time?
    Correct Answer: No — DAPT de-escalation is NOT recommended in the first 30 days following ACS
    De-escalation of DAPT within the first 30 days is associated with increased risk of ischemic events. The first month after ACS represents the highest risk period for recurrent thrombosis, and reducing antiplatelet potency during this window can be dangerous. After 30 days, de-escalation may be considered.
    ⚠ Trap: Minor bleeding seems like a legitimate reason to de-escalate. But the board-correct answer respects the 30-day boundary. Board keyword: no de-escalation in first 30 days post-ACS. For truly life-threatening bleeding, the rules change — but minor GI bleeding does not override this principle in the boards context.
    Trap 10Fondaparinux + PCI — The Missing UFH Trap
    A patient with NSTEMI is treated with fondaparinux and aspirin and is subsequently taken for PCI. The interventional cardiologist asks the team what additional anticoagulation is needed at the time of PCI. What should be given?
    Correct Answer: Supplemental unfractionated heparin (UFH) must be given at the time of PCI
    Fondaparinux is excellent for reducing major bleeding in NSTEMI but has a critical limitation: it CANNOT be used alone during PCI because it increases the risk of catheter-related thrombosis (thrombosis at the arterial access site). Supplemental UFH is required when fondaparinux is the background anticoagulant and PCI is performed.
    ⚠ Trap: Students think fondaparinux provides complete anticoagulation coverage and no additional agent is needed during PCI. Board keyword: fondaparinux + PCI = requires supplemental UFH. This is a classic pharmacology-mechanism question — fondaparinux cannot prevent catheter thrombosis because it doesn't inhibit thrombin bound to the catheter surface.
