01

Pathophysiology of CKD

▾

Two Broad Mechanisms of Damage

CKD results from a disease-specific initiating insult followed by a universal maladaptive response that perpetuates nephron loss regardless of the original cause.

Mechanism 1 — Disease Specific
  • Genetic abnormalities in renal development
  • Immune complex deposition and inflammation (glomerulonephritis)
  • Metabolic injury — hyperglycemia → diabetic nephropathy
  • Microvascular perturbation — chronic hypertension
  • Toxin exposure affecting vascular, glomerular, or tubulointerstitial compartments
Mechanism 2 — Universal Maladaptive Response

Once nephron mass falls, surviving nephrons hyperfiltrate and hypertrophy — initially compensatory, ultimately destructive:

  • ↑ Intraglomerular pressure → distortion of glomerular architecture
  • Abnormal podocyte function → disruption of filtration barrier
  • Glomerulosclerosis and nephron dropout — self-perpetuating cycle
  • Mediated by vasoactive hormones, cytokines, and growth factors

The RAS and Tubuloglomerular Feedback

Increased intrarenal RAS activity + reduced tubuloglomerular (TG) feedback → initial compensatory hyperfiltration AND subsequent maladaptive hypertrophy and sclerosis. This is why nephron loss from an initial injury leads to progressive decline over many years — even after the original insult resolves.

🔵 Core Mechanism
Short-term adaptation (hyperfiltration + hypertrophy) → increased pressure within remaining nephrons → glomerular architectural distortion → sclerosis → further nephron dropout → self-perpetuating downward spiral.

Risk Factors & Staging

▾

Risk Factors for CKD

Noncommunicable Diseases
  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Increased BMI / sedentary lifestyle
  • Autoinflammatory disease (lupus, vasculitis, cancer immunotherapy)
  • Nephrotoxic exposures (antineoplastic agents)
  • Hypertension — risk factor, cause, or consequence
Communicable Diseases
  • Streptococcal infection
  • Mycobacterial infection
  • HIV (HIVAN)
  • SARS-CoV-2
  • HBV, HCV
Genetic
  • Monogenic — high or low/medium penetrance
  • Polygenic risk factors
  • APOL1 variants (sub-Saharan African ancestry) — odds ratio >2
  • Accounts for up to 20% of adult-onset CKD
Childhood-Related
  • Premature / SGA birth
  • Persistent asymptomatic microscopic hematuria
  • Childhood kidney disease (even if resolved)
  • Treated childhood cancer
Other
  • Age, male sex, family history
  • Prior acute kidney injury
  • Preeclampsia
  • Tobacco use
  • Kidney donation / nephrectomy
🟢 Monitoring Rule
Adults with any risk factor → monitor yearly: urinary albumin excretion, eGFR trend, blood pressure.

🟡 Age-Related GFR Decline
Normal GFR decline is ~1 mL/min/year after the 3rd decade (peak ~120 mL/min; mean at age 70 = ~70 mL/min). A "normal" serum creatinine in an elderly woman may reflect GFR <50 — always calculate eGFR, never interpret creatinine alone.

eGFR Estimation

MDRD Equation

eGFR = 1.86 × (SCr)⁻¹·¹⁵⁴ × (age)⁻⁰·²⁰³
× 0.742 if female

Race multiplier (×1.21 for African ancestry) currently under review → race-free equations now recommended

CKD-EPI Equation (preferred)

GFR = 141 × min(SCr/κ, 1)^α × max(SCr/κ, 1)⁻¹·²⁰⁹ × 0.993^Age × 1.018 (female)

κ = 0.7 female, 0.9 male; α = −0.329 female, −0.411 male. Race multiplier under review.

🟡 Validity Condition
eGFR is only valid in steady state — serum creatinine must be neither rising nor falling. Use cystatin-C when precise GFR is needed for clinical decisions (unaffected by muscle mass variables).

Albuminuria (UACR)

Spot first-morning urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR) has replaced 24-hour collections. Persistent UACR above threshold on 2–3 occasions marks glomerular injury even with a negative conventional dipstick.

🟢 Kidney Failure Risk Equation
Available at www.kidneyfailurerisk.com — uses age, sex, region (North American vs. non–NA), GFR, and UACR. Validated globally; risk appears higher in North America, hence the regional adjustment.

Genetics of CKD

Most Common Mendelian Causes
  • ADPKD: ADPKD1, ADPKD2, IFT140, GANAB, DNAJB2, ALG9
  • Type IV collagen nephropathy (Alport syndrome): COL4A3, COL4A4, COL4A5
  • Autosomal dominant tubulointerstitial disease: UMOD, MUC1, HNF1B
  • Nephronophthisis: NPHP genes
APOL1 — High-Risk Non-Mendelian
  • Sub-Saharan African ancestry; odds ratio >2
  • Diseases: FSGS, HIV nephropathy, SARS-CoV-2 nephropathy, CKD with HTN, lupus nephritis
  • Arose as evolutionary protection against tropical pathogens (West Africa)
  • Requires acquired "second hit" (e.g., ↑ IFN-γ) to trigger disease
  • Targeted therapy: Inaxaplin — APOL1 channel inhibitor; reduces proteinuria in FSGS with high-risk genotype
🔵 When to Pursue Genetic Testing
Family history of unexplained kidney failure; parental consanguinity; extrarenal features (hearing/vision loss, dysmorphic features, autism spectrum); steroid-resistant nephrotic syndrome; FSGS; young-onset unexplained progressive CKD; consideration of APOL1-mediated disease; unexplained kidney failure prior to transplant evaluation.

Etiology & Epidemiology

▾

Global Burden

US Prevalence

≥6% of adults have CKD stages 1–2; 4.5% have stages 3–4

Future Projection

Global YLL due to CKD: ~26M (2016) → 52.5M (2040); rises from 16th to 5th in YLL rankings

Mortality

Global CKD deaths: 1.2M (2016) → projected 3.1M (2040)

Drivers

Population aging + rapid ↑ in diabetes, hypertension, and obesity worldwide

Leading Etiologies (>90% of Global CKD Burden)

EtiologyKey Points
Diabetic NephropathyMost common in North America & Europe; usually secondary to type 2 DM
GlomerulonephritisIncludes IgA nephropathy, membranous, FSGS; varies by geography
Hypertension-Associated CKDIncludes vascular/ischemic kidney disease. Caution: some "hypertensive CKD" is actually primary glomerulopathy (FSGS/global glomerulosclerosis) causing the HTN — not caused by it
ADPKDMost common Mendelian CKD; enlarged kidneys with multiple cysts; gene-specific therapy available (tolvaptan)
Other Cystic & TubulointerstitialNephronophthisis, medullary cystic disease, interstitial nephritis
🟡 Hypertension — Cause or Consequence?
When no primary kidney disease process is evident, CKD is often attributed to hypertension. However, some patients have a subclinical primary glomerulopathy (e.g., FSGS) in which elevated blood pressure is actually a consequence of the kidney disease, not its cause. In others, progressive nephrosclerosis and HTN reflect a systemic vascular disease involving heart and brain.

Pathophysiology & Biochemistry of Uremia

▾

What Is the Uremic Syndrome?

Uremia = symptoms, signs, and laboratory disturbances resulting from reduced kidney function. Urea and creatinine rise with reduced excretory capacity but do not themselves cause the uremic syndrome — they are measurable but very incomplete surrogate markers for the hundreds of retained toxins.

🔴 Board Pearl — Do Not Oversimplify
Monitoring urea and creatinine alone "represents a vast oversimplification of the uremic state." Large numbers of water-soluble, hydrophobic, protein-bound, charged and uncharged nitrogen-containing compounds accumulate when GFR declines. PTH itself is considered a uremic toxin.

Three Spheres of Uremic Dysfunction

Sphere 1 — Toxin Accumulation

Retained solutes normally excreted by the kidney: water-soluble, hydrophobic, protein-bound, charged and uncharged nitrogenous metabolites. PTH itself is a uremic toxin at high levels (muscle weakness, cardiac fibrosis).

Sphere 2 — Loss of Kidney Functions

Beyond excretion: loss of erythropoietin production, calcitriol synthesis, insulin degradation, fluid/electrolyte homeostasis, hormone regulation (PTH, FGF-23, sex hormones, prolactin, glucagon).

Sphere 3 — Systemic Inflammation

↑ CRP + acute-phase reactants; ↓ albumin + fetuin (negative acute-phase reactants). Drives the malnutrition–inflammation–atherosclerosis/calcification syndrome → accelerated vascular disease and morbidity.

Fluid, Electrolyte & Acid-Base Disorders

▾

Sodium and Water

Many forms of kidney disease disrupt sodium balance → dietary sodium exceeds excretion → ECFV expansion → hypertension → accelerated nephron hyperfiltration and injury.

  • ECFV expansion is typically isonatremic (water intake does not exceed renal clearance)
  • Hyponatremia is uncommon in CKD; when present → water restriction
  • ECFV depletion (GI losses, overzealous diuretics) → prerenal component → acute-on-chronic renal failure
  • "Sick day rule" — patients should hold diuretics/antihypertensives during vomiting/diarrhea until resuming normal intake
🟢 Treatment of Sodium Excess
  • Salt restriction — first line always
  • Loop diuretics (furosemide, bumetanide, torsemide) — higher doses required due to diuretic resistance in CKD
  • Thiazide (chlorthalidone) — shown to reduce BP even in stage 4 CKD
  • Loop + metolazone combination for resistant cases
  • Intractable edema + hypertension refractory to all → indication to initiate dialysis

Potassium Homeostasis

GFR decline is NOT always paralleled by a parallel decline in urinary potassium excretion (predominantly aldosterone-dependent secretion in the distal nephron). Augmented GI potassium excretion also serves as a backup defense. Despite this, hyperkalemia is common.

Causes of Hyperkalemia in CKD

  • ↑ Dietary potassium; hemolysis; transfusion of stored RBCs
  • Metabolic acidosis (shifts K⁺ extracellularly)
  • Medications: RAS inhibitors, spironolactone, amiloride, eplerenone, triamterene, nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists
  • Hyporeninemic hypoaldosteronism: diabetes mellitus, obstructive uropathy, sickle cell nephropathy — disproportionate hyperkalemia relative to GFR decline
🟢 Treatment of Hyperkalemia
  • Dietary potassium restriction; kaliuretic diuretics
  • Potassium-binding resins: calcium resonium, sodium polystyrene; newer agents — patiromer, calcium zirconium cyclosilicate (promote GI potassium loss)
  • Dose adjustment or avoidance of potassium-retaining medications
  • SGLT2 inhibitors — counterbalancing effects on renal K⁺ handling → net K⁺ homeostasis preservation
  • Intractable hyperkalemia → consider dialysis (uncommon indication)
🟡 RAS Inhibitor Dilemma
Despite hyperkalemia risk, the benefits of RAS inhibitors in slowing CKD progression and reducing cardiovascular events usually favor cautious use with close potassium monitoring. Co-administering potassium-lowering agents (patiromer) often allows continued RAS inhibitor use.

Metabolic Acidosis

Common in CKD. Kidneys can still acidify urine but produce less ammonia → cannot excrete sufficient protons for most diets. Hyperkalemia further depresses ammonia production, worsening acidosis.

↓ GFR → ↓ Ammonia production
↓
Early CKD: Non-anion-gap (hyperchloremic) metabolic acidosis
↓ (with further GFR decline)
Total urinary net acid excretion severely limited (<30–40 mmol/day)
↓
Organic acid anion accumulation → Anion-gap component added
→ Mixed non-anion-gap + anion-gap metabolic acidosis
  • pH rarely <7.32; usually mild
  • Diabetic nephropathy + tubulointerstitial/obstructive disease → non-anion-gap + hyperkalemia at earlier stages
  • Even modest acidosis → protein catabolism + CKD progression
🟢 Treatment
Oral sodium bicarbonate when serum HCO₃⁻ falls below 20–23 mmol/L. Recent studies: bicarbonate supplementation in stages 3–5 CKD → slower progression to dialysis. Account for sodium load when ECFV expansion is present.

Calcium, Phosphate & Bone Disorders

▾

Pathophysiology of Secondary Hyperparathyroidism

Changes begin when GFR falls below 60 mL/min (though FGF-23 may rise even before measurable GFR reduction). A cascade of interrelated mechanisms unfolds:

↓ GFR → ↓ Phosphate excretion → Phosphate retention
↓
↑ FGF-23 (from osteocytes) + ↑ PTH synthesis + Parathyroid gland hypertrophy
↓
FGF-23 → ↓ Calcitriol production + ↑ Calcitriol degradation → Hypocalcemia
↓
↓ Calcitriol → Hypocalcemia → More PTH via Ca-sensing receptor + direct effect on PTH gene transcription
Loss of Klotho-dependent FGF-23 inhibition of PTH production
↓
Secondary Hyperparathyroidism → High-Turnover Bone Disease (Osteitis Fibrosa Cystica)
🔴 FGF-23 Is an Independent CV Risk Factor
Elevated FGF-23 = independent risk factor for left ventricular hypertrophy and increased mortality in CKD, dialysis, and kidney transplant patients — independent of PTH or phosphate levels.

Types of CKD Bone Disease

High-Turnover: Osteitis Fibrosa Cystica

Cause: Secondary hyperparathyroidism (↑ PTH)

Histology: Abnormal osteoid, bone marrow fibrosis, bone cysts ("brown tumors" — hemorrhagic, appearing brown)

Clinical: Bone pain + fragility; brown tumors with compression syndromes; ESA resistance (bone marrow fibrosis); PTH causes muscle weakness + cardiac fibrosis

Low-Turnover: Adynamic Bone Disease

Cause: Excessive PTH suppression, chronic inflammation. ↑ prevalence in diabetics + elderly

Mechanism: ↓ Bone volume + mineralization → inability to repair microfractures from normal activity

Complications: ↑ Fractures; most severe musculoskeletal pain of all bone disease types; ↑ vascular and cardiac calcification; tumoral calcinosis (soft tissue calcium deposits)

Osteomalacia

Cause: ↓ Production and action of 1,25(OH)₂D₃ (calcitriol)

Result: Accumulation of nonmineralized osteoid

Distinct from adynamic disease and osteitis fibrosa — a third, separate entity

Cardiovascular Calcification

  • Strong association between hyperphosphatemia and cardiovascular mortality
  • CKD patients have coronary artery and heart valve calcification orders of magnitude greater than general population (CT and electron-beam CT)
  • Magnitude proportional to age and hyperphosphatemia; associated with low PTH and low bone turnover
  • Mechanism: ingested calcium cannot be incorporated into low-turnover bone → deposits in vascular bed
  • Hyperphosphatemia induces vascular smooth muscle cells to adopt an osteoblast-like phenotype → vascular ossification

Calciphylaxis

Devastating condition almost exclusive to advanced CKD. Painful livedo reticularis → subcutaneous nodules → ischemic necrosis (legs, thighs, abdomen, breasts). Pathology: vascular occlusion + extensive calcification.

🔴 Warfarin Is a Risk Factor for Calciphylaxis
Warfarin inhibits vitamin K–dependent activation of matrix GLA protein (which normally prevents vascular calcification). If calciphylaxis develops → discontinue warfarin immediately and switch to an alternative anticoagulant (apixaban increasingly used). Assess this in every CKD patient requiring anticoagulation.

Treatment

Phosphate-Binding Agents (taken with meals)
  • Calcium acetate / calcium carbonate: Effective but risk hypercalcemia — especially in adynamic bone disease where calcium cannot be incorporated into bone
  • Sevelamer: Non-calcium polymer; no hypercalcemia; may attenuate vascular calcification
  • Lanthanum: Non-calcium polymer; similar benefits to sevelamer
  • Tenapanor: Sodium-proton inhibitor; ↓ GI phosphate absorption
🟢 Pearl
Sevelamer/lanthanum preferred when hypercalcemia is present or in adynamic bone disease.
Vitamin D Analogues & Calcimimetics
  • Calcitriol: Directly suppresses PTH; risk hypercalcemia + hyperphosphatemia via ↑ GI absorption
  • Paricalcitol: Calcitriol analogue; suppresses PTH with less hypercalcemia risk
  • Cinacalcet / Etelcalcetide: Calcimimetics — enhance Ca-sensing receptor sensitivity in parathyroid cells → dose-dependent ↓ PTH + ↓ plasma calcium
🔵 PTH Target
KDOQI: PTH target = 2–9× upper limit of normal. Very low PTH → adynamic bone disease + ectopic calcification. Very high PTH → osteitis fibrosa cystica.

Cardiovascular Abnormalities

▾

Scale of the Problem

Cardiovascular disease is the leading cause of morbidity and mortality at every stage of CKD. The incremental risk vs. age/sex-matched general population is 10- to 200-fold depending on stage. Most CKD patients die of cardiovascular disease before ever reaching stage 5 CKD. Of those who do reach stage 5, 30–45% already have advanced cardiovascular complications.

Vascular Disease Risk Factors

Traditional ("Classic") Risk Factors
  • Hypertension
  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Hypervolemia
  • Dyslipidemia
  • Sympathetic overactivity
  • Hyperhomocysteinemia
CKD-Specific (Nontraditional) Risk Factors
  • Anemia
  • Hyperphosphatemia
  • Hyperparathyroidism
  • ↑ FGF-23 (LV hypertrophy)
  • Sleep apnea
  • Systemic inflammation (↓ fetuin → ↑ vascular calcification)
🟡 Cardiac Troponin in CKD
Frequently elevated in CKD without acute ischemia — complicates MI diagnosis. Use serial measurements over hours; trend matters more than a single level. Consistently elevated levels = independent prognostic factor for adverse CV events.

Heart Failure

  • Myocardial ischemia + LV hypertrophy + diastolic dysfunction + cardiomyopathy + salt/water retention → heart failure or pulmonary edema
  • Can be preserved (diastolic) or reduced (systolic) ejection fraction
  • "Low-pressure" pulmonary edema in advanced CKD: "bat wing" distribution on CXR; responds to dialysis (increased alveolar capillary permeability = uremic state)
  • AV fistula for hemodialysis → high cardiac output → high-output heart failure
  • Advanced malnutrition-inflammation state can reverse classic risk factors (HTN, hyperlipidemia) → reduced LV function + poor prognosis

Hypertension Management

🔵 BP Target
<130/80 mmHg in CKD patients with diabetes OR proteinuria >1 g/24 h, if achievable without prohibitive adverse effects. Salt restriction = first-line therapy always.
ACE Inhibitors / ARBs — First-Line for Proteinuric CKD

Mechanism

Reduce intraglomerular hypertension by disproportionately reducing efferent arteriolar vasoconstriction → ↓ intraglomerular filtration pressure — beyond systemic BP reduction alone.

Key Rules

  • Never combine ACE inhibitor + ARB — demonstrated greater AKI and adverse cardiac events
  • GFR drop up to 30% of baseline is acceptable — reflects effective glomerular unloading; tolerate if stable
  • Can continue even at eGFR <30 mL/min with monitoring — discontinuation associated with ↑ risk of death and CV events
  • Hyperkalemia risk → co-administer potassium-lowering agents (patiromer) as needed
  • Can precipitate AKI in bilateral ischemic renovascular disease
SGLT2 Inhibitors (Gliflozins) — Emerging Cornerstone

Mechanism

Inhibit SGLT2 in proximal tubule → ↓ Na⁺ reabsorption → ↑ Na⁺ delivery to macula densa → afferent arteriolar constriction (restored TGF) → ↓ intraglomerular pressure. Also: direct proximal tubule cytoprotection.

  • Indicated in both diabetic AND nondiabetic kidney disease
  • Effective even in stage 4 CKD
  • Reduces major cardiovascular events including heart failure
  • Net K⁺ preservation → allows RAS inhibitor co-use
🟢 Calcium Channel Blockers
Among CCBs, diltiazem and verapamil may have superior antiproteinuric and renoprotective effects vs. dihydropyridines. All CCBs useful for systemic HTN in conditions where intraglomerular hypertension is less prominent (e.g., ADPKD). GLP-1 receptor agonists also reduce major cardiovascular events and CKD progression in at-risk patients.

Pericardial Disease

  • Pleuritic chest pain + friction rub = uremic pericarditis
  • ECG: PR-interval depression + diffuse ST-segment elevation
  • Now mainly seen in underdialyzed, nonadherent patients (less common with timely dialysis)
  • Pericardial effusion can be asymptomatic; can rarely lead to tamponade
🔴 Uremic Pericarditis = Absolute Indication for Urgent Dialysis
Initiate (or intensify) dialysis urgently. Hemodialysis must be performed without heparin due to propensity for hemorrhage into pericardial fluid. Consider pericardial drainage for recurrent effusion or tamponade signs. Nonuremic causes: viral, malignant, tuberculous, autoimmune, post-MI, minoxidil.

Hematologic Abnormalities

▾

Anemia of CKD

Normocytic, normochromic anemia evident as early as stage 3 CKD; nearly universal by stage 4. Primary cause: insufficient erythropoietin (EPO) production by diseased kidneys, plus reduced erythrocyte lifespan and other factors.

CauseMechanism
EPO deficiencyDiseased kidney cannot produce sufficient EPO — primary cause
Reduced RBC lifespanUremic toxins damage red cell membrane
Bleeding diathesisUremic platelet dysfunction → chronic GI blood loss
Iron deficiencyPoor dietary absorption + GI blood loss
Hyperparathyroidism / BM fibrosisBone marrow fibrosis inhibits erythropoiesis; causes ESA resistance
Chronic inflammationInflammatory cytokines inhibit erythropoiesis; causes ESA resistance
Folate / B12 deficiencyPoor intake or dialysis-related losses
ComorbiditiesHypothyroidism, HIV, autoimmune disease, immunosuppressives

Clinical Consequences of Anemia

  • ↓ Tissue oxygen delivery → ↑ cardiac output → ventricular dilation + hypertrophy
  • Fatigue, reduced exercise tolerance, angina, heart failure
  • Decreased cognition and mental acuity
  • Impaired host defense against infection
  • Growth restriction in children with CKD

Treatment of Anemia

Erythropoiesis-Stimulating Agents (ESAs)

Recombinant EPO has transformed CKD care — eliminated regular transfusions, reducing transfusion infections, iron overload, and allosensitization (critical for kidney transplant candidates).

Iron Supplementation — Essential Prerequisite

  • Adequate bone marrow iron stores must be available before starting ESA
  • Monitor transferrin saturation (immediate availability) + ferritin (iron stores)
  • Oral iron for pre-dialysis or peritoneal dialysis patients (if GI tolerates)
  • IV iron if GI intolerance or poor absorption — note ↑ susceptibility to bacterial infection; free serum iron effects still under investigation
  • Also ensure adequate B12 and folate

ESA Hemoglobin Target

🔴 Do Not Normalize Hemoglobin
Target: Hgb 100–115 g/L (10–11.5 g/dL). Normalization to 12–13 g/dL was associated with ↑ stroke risk (type 2 DM), ↑ thromboembolic events, and possibly faster CKD progression in RCTs. Normalization is NOT beneficial.

ESA Resistance — Think About

  • Acute or chronic inflammation
  • Inadequate dialysis
  • Severe hyperparathyroidism (bone marrow fibrosis)
  • Chronic blood loss or hemolysis
  • Chronic infection or malignancy
HIF Prolyl-Hydroxylase Inhibitors (New Class)

Inhibit endogenous HIF degradation → ↑ endogenous EPO production + ↑ GI iron absorption. Similar effectiveness to ESAs in trials. Some safety concerns remain (cardiovascular, retinal vessel, tumor vasculogenesis) — under investigation.

Abnormal Hemostasis — A Paradoxical Coexistence

Bleeding Tendency
  • Prolonged bleeding time
  • ↓ Activity of platelet factor III
  • Abnormal platelet aggregation and adhesiveness
  • Impaired prothrombin consumption
  • Clinical: easy bruising, prolonged surgical bleeding, menorrhagia, GI bleeding
🟢 Treatment
DDAVP, cryoprecipitate, IV conjugated estrogens, blood transfusions, ESA therapy. Optimal dialysis corrects prolonged bleeding time.
Thrombotic Tendency
  • Greater susceptibility to thromboembolism
  • Especially with nephrotic-range proteinuria → hypoalbuminemia + renal loss of anticoagulant factors → thrombophilic state
🟡 Anticoagulation Nuances in CKD
  • Warfarin for AFib: individualize — ↑ bleeding risk + calciphylaxis risk
  • LMWH: may need dose adjustment; monitor factor Xa
  • Unfractionated heparin: preferred in hospitalized patients needing non-warfarin anticoagulation
  • Apixaban: increasingly used in CKD + dialysis patients
  • Most new oral anticoagulants are renally eliminated

Neuromuscular, GI, Endocrine & Dermatologic Manifestations

▾

Neuromuscular Abnormalities

CNS, peripheral, and autonomic neuropathy plus muscle abnormalities begin as early as stage 3 CKD.

CNS Complications
  • Stage 3 (early): Mild memory disturbances, concentration difficulty, sleep disturbance
  • Later stages: Neuromuscular irritability — hiccups, cramps, twitching
  • Advanced untreated: Asterixis, myoclonus, seizures, coma
Peripheral Neuropathy (evident at stage 4)
  • Sensory > motor
  • Lower extremities > upper
  • Distal > proximal
  • Restless leg syndrome: Debilitating discomfort in legs/feet relieved by movement
  • Peripheral neuropathy without another cause (e.g., diabetes) = indication for RRT

Gastrointestinal & Nutritional

  • Uremic fetor: Urine-like breath from urea breakdown → ammonia in saliva; often with metallic taste (dysgeusia)
  • Gastritis, peptic disease, mucosal ulcerations at any GI level → abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, GI bleeding
  • Constipation (worsened by calcium and iron supplements)
  • Uremic toxin retention → anorexia, nausea, vomiting
  • Protein-energy malnutrition is common in advanced CKD — often triggers initiation of RRT
  • Metabolic acidosis + inflammatory cytokines → protein catabolism
🟡 Protein Restriction
May reduce nausea/vomiting but risks malnutrition. Should only be performed in consultation with a registered dietitian specializing in CKD management.

Endocrine & Metabolic Disturbances

Glucose Metabolism
  • Glucose intolerance is common; fasting glucose usually normal or mildly elevated
  • ↓ Renal insulin degradation → elevated plasma insulin; insulin dose reduction needed as CKD progresses
  • Metformin: Contraindicated when GFR <~30–45 mL/min
  • Sulfonylureas: Contraindicated when GFR < half of normal
  • Gliptins: Require dose reduction in renal failure
  • SGLT2 inhibitors: Stabilize GFR via TGF correction; renoprotective + CV benefit; approved across CKD stages including 4
Reproductive & Hormonal
  • Women: ↓ Estrogen; menstrual abnormalities, infertility. At GFR ~40 → only ~20% of pregnancies lead to live births; pregnancy may hasten CKD progression. Consult nephrologist + high-risk OB before attempting pregnancy.
  • Men: ↓ Testosterone; sexual dysfunction; oligospermia
  • Adolescents: Delayed sexual maturation even on dialysis
  • Many abnormalities improve with intensive dialysis or successful transplantation

Dermatologic Abnormalities

  • Pruritus: One of the most vexing manifestations of uremia. First line: rule out unrelated skin disorders (scabies); treat hyperphosphatemia. Treatments: moisturizers, mild topical glucocorticoids, antihistamines, UV radiation. Kappa opioid receptor agonists reduce pruritus intensity and improve quality of life.
  • Hyperpigmentation: In advanced CKD — deposition of retained pigmented metabolites (urochromes)
  • Nephrogenic fibrosing dermopathy: Progressive subcutaneous induration on arms/legs; seen in CKD patients exposed to gadolinium-based MRI contrast
🔴 Gadolinium Guidelines
  • CKD Stage 3 (GFR 30–59): Minimize gadolinium exposure
  • CKD Stages 4–5 (GFR <30): Avoid gadolinium unless medically necessary
  • No patient should be denied a critical imaging study — if necessary, rapid hemodialysis after gadolinium administration (even in pre-dialysis patients) may mitigate risk
  • Newer gadolinium forms have lower risk but caution still applies

Evaluation & Diagnosis

▾

History — Key Elements

🟡 Symptoms Are Often Absent
CKD is often diagnosed incidentally on lab work — symptoms are subtle or absent until late. Pain in kidney region and decreased urine volume are NOT typical features. Patients are frequently surprised by the diagnosis.
  • History of hypertension, diabetes, abnormal urinalyses, preeclampsia, early pregnancy loss
  • Drug history (nephrotoxins): NSAIDs, COX-2 inhibitors, antimicrobials, chemotherapy, antiretrovirals, proton pump inhibitors, phosphate-containing bowel cathartics, lithium
  • Uremic symptoms: appetite, weight loss, nausea, hiccups, peripheral edema, cramps, pruritus, restless legs
  • Family history + manifestations in auditory/visual/integumentary → heritable CKD (Alport's, Fabry's, cystinosis, ADPKD)
  • Clustering of CKD within families is common — even of different etiologies

Physical Examination

  • Blood pressure + target organ damage
  • Fundoscopy — especially in diabetics (diabetic retinopathy = associated with diabetic nephropathy)
  • Edema, sensory polyneuropathy
  • Asterixis or pericardial friction rub (not attributable to other causes) = uremic syndrome present → act urgently

Laboratory Workup

  • Serum + urine protein electrophoresis — all patients >35 with unexplained CKD + anemia → rule out multiple myeloma
  • If hematuria/proteinuria → ANA/complement (lupus), HBV, HCV, HIV
  • Serial creatinine measurements → confirm chronicity, pace of deterioration
  • Calcium, phosphorus, PTH → metabolic bone disease
  • Hemoglobin, iron stores, B12, folate
  • 24-hour urine or UACR: protein >300 mg/day → indication for ACEi/ARB + SGLT2 inhibitors; associated with ↑ progression risk

Imaging — Renal Ultrasound (Most Useful)

  • Verifies two kidneys, symmetry, size estimate, masses, obstruction
  • Bilaterally small kidneys → CKD of long-standing duration
  • Normal-sized kidneys despite CKD → exceptions: diabetic nephropathy (enlarged at onset), amyloidosis, HIV nephropathy, ADPKD
  • Discrepancy >1 cm → unilateral developmental abnormality or renovascular disease
  • ADPKD: enlarged kidneys with multiple cysts
🔴 Radiographic Contrast — Caution
Avoid IV/intraarterial contrast dye when possible, especially in diabetic nephropathy. When unavoidable: avoid hypovolemia, minimize dye load, use least nephrotoxic preparation. Adjuncts (sodium bicarbonate, N-acetylcysteine) may be used though efficacy data are less robust than previously thought.

Kidney Biopsy

🔴 Contraindicated with Bilaterally Small Kidneys
Technically difficult, high bleeding risk, too much scarring to yield diagnosis, and the window for disease-specific therapy has already passed.

Other contraindications: uncontrolled hypertension, active UTI, bleeding diathesis (including ongoing anticoagulation), severe obesity.

  • Preferred approach: ultrasound-guided percutaneous
  • If bleeding time is prolonged before biopsy → give desmopressin (DDAVP) immediately prior
  • Not always indicated: in type 1 DM of 15–20 years + retinopathy + nephrotic proteinuria + no hematuria → biopsy usually unnecessary; clinical diagnosis of diabetic nephropathy is very likely

Distinguishing CKD from AKI

🔵 Critical First Step
Distinguish newly diagnosed CKD from acute or subacute renal failure — the latter may respond to targeted therapy. Previous creatinine measurements are key: normal creatinine in recent months → consider reversible process; elevated creatinine in the past → chronic process. Even confirmed CKD may have a superimposed acute process (ECFV depletion, obstruction, nephrotoxin, disease flare).

Treatment & Slowing CKD Progression

▾

Framework of Therapy

CKD therapy divides into: (1) etiology-directed interventions, and (2) interventions targeting the common pathway of glomerular hyperfiltration. Crucially, the same interventions that slow CKD progression also reduce cardiovascular complications.

🔵 Emerging Etiology-Directed Therapies
  • ADPKD: Genome-specific therapy (tolvaptan)
  • Type 1 hyperoxaluria: siRNA therapy (lumasiran)
  • APOL1-mediated kidney disease (AMKD): Inaxaplin (APOL1 channel inhibitor) — shown to reduce proteinuria in FSGS with high-risk APOL1 genotype

🟡 Monitor for Reversible Acceleration
Any acceleration in rate of GFR decline → search for superimposed reversible process: ECFV depletion, uncontrolled HTN, UTI, new obstruction, nephrotoxin exposure (NSAIDs, radiocontrast), reactivation of underlying disease (lupus, vasculitis).

Medication Dose Adjustment

  • Loading dose: usually unchanged (renal elimination not used in calculation)
  • Maintenance doses: require reduction or extended intervals for many drugs
  • Absolutely avoid: metformin (GFR <~30–45), meperidine, most sulfonylureas, NSAIDs/COX-2 inhibitors
  • Many antibiotics, antihypertensives, antiarrhythmics need dose adjustment
  • Online resource: www.globalrph.com/index_renal.htm

Preparation for Renal Replacement Therapy

Indications for RRT Initiation

  • Anorexia and nausea not attributable to reversible causes
  • Evidence of malnutrition
  • Fluid/electrolyte abnormalities refractory to other measures (hyperkalemia, ECFV overload)
  • Uremic pericarditis — absolute indication
  • Peripheral neuropathy without another cause
  • Encephalopathy (now a late indication with timely dialysis)
🟡 Timing — Do Not Use Arbitrary Creatinine or Urea Level
Individual variability in uremic symptoms is high. Patients accustomed to chronic uremia may deny symptoms, then feel dramatically better after dialysis initiation. Early preparation avoids fistula/catheter failure crises.

Patient Education — No Later Than Stage 4

  • In-center hemodialysis vs. home hemodialysis
  • Peritoneal dialysis
  • Kidney transplantation — offers best rehabilitation potential; dialysis replaces only a small fraction of kidney function (no endocrine/anti-inflammatory function)
  • Nondialytic conservative care
  • Patients with education more likely to choose home-based therapy (less expensive; improved quality of life)
  • Preemptive kidney transplantation (from living donor) = preferred if feasible

High-Yield Distinctions

ACEi + ARB Combination — Never

ACE inhibitor OR ARB — first-line for proteinuric CKD. Combining both = demonstrated greater AKI + adverse cardiac events with no net benefit. Add SGLT2 inhibitor instead for additional renoprotection.

Calcium Binders — Dangerous in Adynamic Bone Disease

In adynamic bone disease (low PTH, low turnover), ingested calcium cannot be incorporated into bone and deposits in vasculature. Calcium acetate/carbonate → hypercalcemia + accelerated calcification. Use sevelamer or lanthanum instead.

ESA — Never Target Normal Hemoglobin

Target Hgb 100–115 g/L only. Normalization was shown to increase stroke risk (type 2 DM) and thromboembolic events. No cardiovascular benefit from higher targets.

Hypertension — Cause or Consequence of CKD?

A patient labeled "hypertensive CKD" may actually have primary FSGS or glomerulosclerosis where HTN is a consequence, not the cause. This distinction affects prognosis and treatment approach.

SGLT2 Inhibitors — Beyond Diabetes

Gliflozins are now indicated for CKD protection in BOTH diabetic and nondiabetic kidney disease, effective through stage 4 CKD. Counterbalance RAS inhibitor–induced hyperkalemia.

Warfarin in CKD — Calciphylaxis Risk

Warfarin inhibits matrix GLA protein → vascular calcification. In CKD patients needing anticoagulation: individualize, prefer alternatives (apixaban). If calciphylaxis develops → discontinue warfarin immediately.

Exam Traps

▾

Expert board exam coaching — Stage A Internal Medicine. Each trap shows exactly how the question is framed, the most tempting wrong answer, and the correct reasoning.

Trap 01The "Normal Creatinine" Trap in an Elderly Woman
An 82-year-old woman is found to have a serum creatinine of 1.0 mg/dL, listed as "within normal limits" on the lab report. She is being started on a renally cleared antibiotic. The resident says no dose adjustment is needed. Are they correct?
Correct Answer: No — dose adjustment IS needed.
In an elderly woman with low muscle mass, a creatinine of 1.0 mg/dL commonly reflects a GFR <50 mL/min (CKD stage 3). Creatinine must never be interpreted in isolation — always calculate eGFR using age, sex, and weight. A "normal" lab value does not mean normal kidney function.
⚠ Trap: Students see "within normal limits" and stop there. Board keyword: elderly + female + "normal creatinine" = always calculate eGFR before dosing renally eliminated drugs.
Trap 02ACEi + ARB Combination — More Is Not Better
A 55-year-old man with diabetic nephropathy and persistent proteinuria despite maximum-dose lisinopril. His physician considers adding losartan to further reduce proteinuria. What is the most important concern?
Correct Answer: Do NOT combine ACE inhibitor + ARB — demonstrated greater AKI and cardiac events, no net benefit.
For additional renoprotection, add an SGLT2 inhibitor instead of dual RAS blockade. The combination of ACEi + ARB was tested in major trials (ONTARGET) and caused harm.
⚠ Trap: The logic "more RAS blockade = better" seems mechanistically sound. Board keyword: "add losartan to lisinopril" = red flag combination. The correct add-on is an SGLT2 inhibitor.
Trap 03GFR Drop After Starting ACEi — Stop or Continue?
A 60-year-old with CKD stage 3 starts lisinopril. Two weeks later, eGFR falls from 52 to 38 mL/min (a 27% drop). He feels well. What is the correct management?
Correct Answer: Continue the ACE inhibitor with close monitoring — this is an acceptable and expected response.
A nonprogressive eGFR decline up to 30% of baseline reflects effective reduction of intraglomerular hypertension (glomerular unloading) and is tolerable if stable. Clinical studies show discontinuing RAS inhibitors in CKD was associated with increased risk of death and CV events.
⚠ Trap: Any creatinine rise triggers a reflex to stop the ACEi. Board keyword: "27% drop, feels well, stable" = continue. Only stop if >30% drop, progressive rise, or suspicion of renovascular disease.
Trap 04Uremic Pericarditis — "Treat Like Regular Pericarditis"
A 58-year-old with stage 5 CKD not on dialysis presents with pleuritic chest pain, pericardial friction rub, PR-interval depression, and diffuse ST elevation on ECG. The intern orders NSAIDs and colchicine. What should be done instead?
Correct Answer: Urgent hemodialysis — without heparin.
Uremic pericarditis is an absolute indication for urgent initiation of dialysis. NSAIDs could further worsen renal function. Hemodialysis must be performed without heparin due to propensity for hemorrhage into pericardial fluid. This is not standard pericarditis.
⚠ Trap: The typical pericarditis protocol (NSAIDs + colchicine) is applied reflexively. Board keyword: "uremic" + "pericarditis" = dialysis, no NSAIDs, no heparin.
Trap 05Hemoglobin Target — Normalization Is Harmful
A 68-year-old with stage 4 CKD has hemoglobin of 9.2 g/dL. He is started on ESA therapy. The question asks what hemoglobin target to set.
Correct Answer: Target Hgb 100–115 g/L (10–11.5 g/dL) — do NOT target ≥12 g/dL.
Normalization of hemoglobin in CKD has NOT been demonstrated to be beneficial. Targeting normal Hgb was associated with increased stroke risk (type 2 DM) and thromboembolic events, and possibly faster CKD progression in randomized trials.
⚠ Trap: "Higher hemoglobin = less symptoms = better" seems logical. Board keyword: "normalize" + "CKD" + "ESA" = harm. Know the specific range: 100–115 g/L.
Trap 06Phosphate Binder in Adynamic Bone Disease
A dialysis patient with markedly elevated serum phosphate is found on bone biopsy to have adynamic bone disease with very low PTH. Which phosphate binder is most appropriate?
Correct Answer: Sevelamer or Lanthanum (non-calcium-containing binder)
In adynamic bone disease, calcium cannot be incorporated into low-turnover bone and instead deposits in vascular and soft tissues. Calcium-based binders (calcium acetate, calcium carbonate) worsen hypercalcemia and accelerate vascular calcification. Sevelamer/lanthanum do not predispose to hypercalcemia and may attenuate vascular calcium deposition.
⚠ Trap: Calcium-based binders chosen by default. Board keyword: adynamic bone disease + low PTH = avoid calcium-containing binders.
Trap 07Warfarin + CKD/Dialysis = Calciphylaxis Risk
A peritoneal dialysis patient on chronic warfarin for atrial fibrillation develops a painful nodule on the abdomen that progressively develops into ischemic skin necrosis with livedo reticularis pattern. What is the essential intervention?
Correct Answer: Discontinue warfarin and switch to alternative anticoagulation (e.g., apixaban).
Warfarin inhibits vitamin K–dependent activation of matrix GLA protein, which normally prevents vascular calcification. Its loss leads to calciphylaxis. Discontinuation is mandatory. Treatment also included hyperbaric oxygen + IV thiosulfate in the referenced case.
⚠ Trap: Focus on wound care and miss the essential medication change. Board keyword: dialysis + warfarin + painful skin necrosis + livedo reticularis = calciphylaxis → stop warfarin immediately.
Trap 08SGLT2 Inhibitors — Not Just for Diabetes
A 52-year-old woman with nondiabetic FSGS and CKD stage 3, persistent proteinuria on maximum ACE inhibitor. What additional therapy slows GFR decline and reduces cardiovascular events?
Correct Answer: SGLT2 inhibitor — indicated for BOTH diabetic AND nondiabetic CKD.
SGLT2 inhibitors restore tubuloglomerular feedback (afferent constriction via ↑ Na⁺ to macula densa) and provide direct tubular cytoprotection. The renoprotective mechanism is glucose-independent. Trials like DAPA-CKD showed benefit in nondiabetic CKD. Effective through stage 4.
⚠ Trap: Assuming gliflozins are only for diabetic CKD. Board keyword: "nondiabetic CKD" + "proteinuria" + "best add-on to ACEi" = SGLT2 inhibitor.
Trap 09Metabolic Acidosis Type — It Changes With CKD Progression
A 48-year-old man with CKD stage 3 secondary to obstructive uropathy has: pH 7.33, HCO₃⁻ 16 mmol/L, elevated chloride, and normal anion gap. How will his acid-base disorder evolve as CKD progresses?
Correct Answer: An anion-gap component will be added to the existing non-anion-gap acidosis as GFR declines further.
Early CKD: ↓ ammonia production → non-anion-gap (hyperchloremic) metabolic acidosis. Advanced CKD: total urinary net acid excretion severely limited (<30–40 mmol/day) → organic acid anion accumulation → added anion-gap. Both types coexist in advanced CKD.
⚠ Trap: Assuming one fixed type of metabolic acidosis in CKD. Board keyword: "CKD progresses" + "acid-base" = non-anion-gap evolves to mixed non-anion-gap + anion-gap. Obstructive uropathy causes this pattern earlier than other CKD types.
Trap 10APOL1 Testing — Who Needs It and Why
A 38-year-old Black man of West African descent presents with FSGS and CKD stage 2. Father had unexplained CKD. What genetic test is most relevant and what does it change?
Correct Answer: APOL1 high-risk genotype testing — changes diagnosis, prognosis, and therapeutic eligibility.
APOL1 high-risk variants (common in sub-Saharan African ancestry) confer several-fold higher risk for FSGS, HIV nephropathy, SARS-CoV-2 nephropathy, lupus nephritis, and CKD with HTN. Identifying high-risk genotype enables: accurate disease attribution, risk counseling, and eligibility for inaxaplin (APOL1 channel inhibitor — reduces proteinuria in APOL1-mediated FSGS).
⚠ Trap: Overlooking genetic testing in a patient with apparent "secondary" FSGS. Board keyword: Black patient + FSGS + family history of CKD = test APOL1. Positive result changes the treatment plan (inaxaplin is specifically indicated).
