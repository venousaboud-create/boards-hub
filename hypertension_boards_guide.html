01

Blood Pressure Physiology & Pathophysiology

Core Equation
Arterial BP = Cardiac Output × Peripheral Resistance. CO is influenced by stroke volume and heart rate; peripheral resistance by small arteries and arterioles.

The Control System

Arterial BP is primarily regulated by a renal-volume-endocrine pressure control system that slowly manipulates blood volume and total peripheral resistance. While this long-term control operates gradually, acute BP responses to stressors rely on changes in cardiac output.

  • Cardiac Output = stroke volume × heart rate. Stroke volume depends on myocardial contractility and intravascular compartment size.
  • Peripheral Vascular Resistance is determined by functional and anatomic changes in small arteries and arterioles.
  • Vascular Endothelium is a single cell layer regulating vascular tone via nitric oxide, arachidonic acid metabolites, endothelin, adenosine, purines, reactive oxygen species, and angiotensin II (via endothelial enzymes).
  • Endothelial dysfunction may play a critical role in the initiation or progression of hypertension and atherosclerosis.

Key Regulatory Systems

Vasoconstrictors
  • Sympathetic nervous system — stimulates vasoconstriction and ↑ heart rate
  • RAAS — angiotensin II vasoconstricts; aldosterone retains sodium
Vasodilators
  • Bradykinin system — promotes vasodilation
  • Natriuretic peptides — cause sodium excretion (atrial natriuretic factor)

Guyton's Pressure-Natriuresis Model

Arthur Guyton (1960s) hypothesized that the kidney's capacity to excrete sodium ultimately dictates long-term BP. Though challenged, it has profoundly influenced BP regulation understanding.

  • Commonly accepted sequence: initial ↑ BP due to ↑ cardiac output + expanded intravascular volume → peripheral resistance ↑ and cardiac output normalizes over time
  • As arterial pressure rises in response to high sodium chloride intake, urinary sodium excretion increases to maintain sodium balance — at the expense of elevated arterial pressure
  • Mechanism: subtle ↑ GFR, ↓ renal tubule sodium absorption capacity, hormonal factors (atrial natriuretic factor)
  • In patients with impaired sodium excretion capacity: greater BP increases required to achieve natriuresis
  • Salt can activate neural, endocrine, paracrine, and vascular mechanisms → ↑ arterial pressure

Autoregulation
Most vascular beds autoregulate blood flow. When peripheral arterial resistance ↑, autoregulatory systems further ↑ vascular resistance within that bed to maintain constant flow. This can perpetuate hypertension.
02

Blood Pressure Measurement

Why It Matters
Office BP is used for 3 purposes: (1) estimate average BP; (2) estimate CVD risk; (3) determine need for prevention/treatment. Most landmark trials used office BP — making it the gold standard.

Standardized Measurement Protocol

  • Pre-measurement: avoid full bladder; abstain from caffeine, smoking, alcohol, or exercise for 30 min prior
  • Environment: quiet room with comfortable ambient temperature
  • Rest: patient must rest for 3–5 min before first measurement; no talking or distractions (cell phones) during rest and measurements
  • Position: seated in chair with upright back support; feet flat on ground; measurement arm comfortably supported so cuff is at heart level
  • Cuff: applied to bare upper arm, approximately 2–3 cm above the elbow crease; correct size for patient's arm; measure at mid-brachial level
  • Device: clinically validated, preferably automated (oscillometric) BP measurement device — eliminates several sources of error; no periodic recalibration needed
  • Operator: trained, preferably certified health care team member; use the arm with the higher pressure at first visit
  • Interpretation: average of ≥2 readings at ≥2 visits; provide results and clinical implications to patient
Common Errors
SBP is overestimated by an average of ~7 mmHg in clinical practice → 15–20% overestimation of hypertension prevalence. Errors cannot be corrected by formulae — only solution is strict adherence to proper technique.

Validated Devices

  • US: Validate BP website — validatebp.org
  • Europe: STRIDE BP website — stridebp.org
  • Training: Free courses available via Pan American Health Organization / World Hypertension League
  • Having a trained non-physician staff member obtain BP measurements is efficient, cost-effective, and common practice in research

Classification Rules
When SBP and DBP are in different categories, choose the higher classification. Correct classification presumes accurate measurement and averaging of ≥2 readings at ≥2 occasions.

Pediatric Definition (Age <13)

  • Based on age-, sex-, and height-specific normative data
  • Hypertension = average of 3 SBP or DBP readings at or above the 95th percentile
  • OR SBP ≥130 or DBP ≥80 mmHg
This chapter focuses on adults ≥18 years
BP classification systems differ by country and have changed substantially over time. The thresholds above are specific to the ACC/AHA guideline for US adults.
04

Out-of-Office Blood Pressure Measurement

White Coat vs. Masked Hypertension

White Coat HTN
  • Office BP ≥130/80, out-of-office BP normal
  • Prevalence: 15–25%
  • CVD risk profile similar to normotensive patients
  • Treatment: nonpharmacologic only
  • Monitor carefully for transition to sustained HTN
Masked HTN
  • Office BP normal, out-of-office BP ≥130/80
  • Prevalence: 15–25%
  • CVD risk profile similar to sustained HTN
  • Treatment: antihypertensive drugs + nonpharmacologic
  • Suspect if elevated (but nonhypertensive) office BP + end-organ damage (LVH, proteinuria)

Home Blood Pressure Monitoring (HBPM)

  • Most practical approach for out-of-office readings; engages patients in their own care
  • Use a clinically validated device with same technique as office measurements
  • Guideline recommendation: 2–3 measurements in the morning (before antihypertensive medications) and evening
  • US practical option: ~3 days prior to office visit
  • Threshold same as office: SBP ≥130 or DBP ≥80 mmHg for hypertension diagnosis
  • Control defined as: SBP/DBP <130/80 mmHg in both settings
  • Many devices store readings on memory chip for telemetry transfer → prevents favorable self-reporting bias
  • Wearable devices (e.g., watches): hold promise but none currently clinically validated

Ambulatory BP Monitoring (ABPM)

  • Best obtained by practitioners with special expertise in this technique
  • Provides nighttime readings — best predictor of CVD risk among all BP measures
  • Nondippers: BP that fails to show the usual pronounced decrement at night → higher CVD risk; more common in non-Hispanic Blacks
Ambulatory BP Equivalents for Office 130/80 mmHg
Daytime: 130/80 mmHg  |  Nighttime: 110/65 mmHg  |  24-hour average: 125/75 mmHg
Limitation
ABPM is relatively expensive and intrusive. US patients are usually reluctant to undergo repeat ABPM — most useful for confirming diagnosis when uncertainty is a special concern.

Prevalence of Hypertension

US Epidemiology

  • By ACC/AHA criteria: >103 million US adults have HTN — one of the most common health conditions
  • Overall US prevalence: ~46% (stable in recent decades)
  • Age 20–44 years: 20–30% → Age ≥75 years: 80–85%
  • SBP increases progressively until ~8th decade; DBP increases until ~5th decade then plateaus or declines
  • Isolated systolic HTN is common late in life due to widening pulse pressure (SBP–DBP difference)

Global Epidemiology

  • Hypertension is the most common reason for primary care visits worldwide
  • Using SBP ≥140 / DBP ≥90 / on medication: 31.5% (1.04 billion) in low/middle-income countries; 28.5% (349 million) in high-income countries (2010 data)
  • Little or no age-related BP increase in many isolated populations — confirms hypertension is preventable
  • Migrant studies: progression to age-related BP increase after moving to non-native settings, associated with ↑ dietary sodium, ↓ potassium, ↓ physical activity, ↑ body weight, ↑ alcohol
06

Laboratory & Other Investigations

5 Purposes of Initial Workup
(1) Recognize target organ damage; (2) Identify secondary causes (kidney disease, primary aldosteronism); (3) Recognize comorbidities (DM, hyperlipidemia); (4) Estimate ASCVD risk; (5) Guide optimal drug choice

Minimum Required Tests in Newly Diagnosed HTN

  • Complete blood count (CBC)
  • Serum electrolytes: sodium, potassium, calcium
  • Serum creatinine + estimated GFR (eGFR)
  • Lipid profile
  • Glycemic status: HbA1c or fasting blood glucose
  • Thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH)
  • Urinalysis + urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio
  • 12-lead electrocardiogram (ECG)
WHO Guidance for Resource-Limited Settings
The 2021 WHO guideline states that inability to obtain minimum laboratory results should NOT be an impediment to BP reduction — treat the BP regardless.
07

Estimation of Cardiovascular Disease Risk

BP-CVD Risk Relationship

  • BP is associated with CVD risk in a continuous, progressive, log-linear fashion from low to high SBP and DBP
  • For SBP: risk relationship documented from as low as 90 mmHg to >180 mmHg
  • BP equally a CVD risk factor in men AND women
  • In younger patients: BP elevation is often an isolated risk predictor (steep relative risk slope)
  • In older patients: less steep relative risk slope, but much higher absolute risk due to other co-existing CVD risk factors
  • At any level of BP: >30-fold difference in 10-year predicted CVD risk between isolated elevation vs. multiple risk factors
Clinical Implication for Stage 1 HTN
Stage 1 HTN with isolated BP elevation: 5–10 year CVD risk may be small, limiting enthusiasm for drugs. Stage 1 HTN + other CVD risk factors: ASCVD risk may be quite high → drug therapy more appropriate. CVD risk assessment MUST be part of initial evaluation.

Risk Calculators

ACC/AHA Pooled Cohort Equations
  • For US adults aged 40–75 years
  • Validated in non-Hispanic White and Black adults
  • High risk = 10-year ASCVD risk ≥10%
  • Should be estimated in all without prior CVD history
PREVENT (Newer Model)
  • For US adults aged 30–70 years
  • Predicts 10- and 30-year CVD risk
  • Includes: kidney function (eGFR), urine albumin/creatinine ratio, zip code (social determinants), HbA1c
  • Also predicts heart failure risk
  • Expected to replace Pooled Cohort Equations over time

Patients with a prior CVD complication are already known to be at high risk for recurrent events — no calculator needed for them.

In antihypertensive clinical trials: relative risk reductions are similar across different baseline CVD risk groups, but absolute benefit is much greater in those at higher risk.

08

Causes of Hypertension — Primary

Primary (Essential) Hypertension
Most adults have primary hypertension with no obvious underlying anatomic cause. Genetic investigations have only identified modest polygenic associations — clinical implications are very limited.

General Environmental Exposures

  • Heavy metals: lead, mercury, cadmium, arsenic — all associated with higher BP
  • Air pollution: PM2.5 associated with higher SBP/DBP; average ↑ SBP ~3–5 mmHg; varies by geography, climate, season
  • Seasonal variation: BP higher in winter, lower in summer
  • High altitude: BP higher, possibly due to hypoxia and cold temperatures

The Six Key Personal Risk Exposures

These account for most age-related BP increases and are targets for nonpharmacologic intervention:

Diet Quality

Heart-healthy diets ↓ BP. DASH diet specifically formulated to lower BP.

Body Weight

~75% of US adults overweight (BMI 25–30, 31%) or obese (BMI ≥30, 42%)

Sodium Intake

>95% of US adults exceed 2000 mg/d threshold; ~100% exceed 1500 mg/d

Potassium Intake

Inversely associated with BP; almost all adults fail to meet recommended intake

Physical Inactivity

>25% report NO physical activity outside work; ~50% below ≥150 min/week

Alcohol

~85% consumed alcohol at some point; 25% report binge drinking in prior month

Causes of Hypertension — Secondary

When to Consider Secondary HTN
(1) New-onset HTN in all patients; AND in those with: (2) Treatment-resistant HTN; (3) Abrupt worsening of HTN; (4) Disproportionate target organ damage; (5) Unprovoked hypokalemia, proteinuria, or LVH

1. Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA) — Most Common

  • Prevalence: 15–30% of adult men, 10–15% of adult women in US
  • >50% of US adults with OSA have HTN; >30% of adults with HTN have OSA
  • Pathophysiology: sympathetic activation due to intermittent hypoxia; shared features with HTN including obesity, RAAS abnormalities, fluid distribution changes
  • Almost 75% of OSA patients are overweight or obese
  • Direct association between severity of OSA and BP level + resistance to antihypertensive therapy
  • Indicators: resistant HTN, snoring, poor quality sleep, breathing pauses, daytime sleepiness, obesity, upper airway narrowing
  • Screening: validated questionnaires (several available)
  • Diagnosis: polysomnography (sleep study)
  • Treatment: lifestyle (especially weight loss); CPAP is OSA-specific treatment BUT meta-analyses show limited BP lowering with CPAP → still need antihypertensive drugs
Board Trap
CPAP does NOT adequately lower BP in OSA patients. Hypertension in OSA should be treated with antihypertensive drug therapy in addition to nonpharmacologic therapy.

2. Medications & Other Substances

  • Prevalence: NSAID alone >26% of US adults report regular use
  • Mechanism: direct pressor effect OR drug-drug/drug-nondrug interactions
  • Prescription drugs that ↑ BP: amphetamines, angiogenesis inhibitors, antidepressants, antipsychotics, decongestants, oral contraceptives, NSAIDs, systemic corticosteroids
  • Illicit drugs: cocaine, marijuana, amphetamines, MDMA (methylenedioxymethamphetamine)
  • Herbal treatments: arnica, ephedra (Ma-Huang), ginseng, guarana, large quantities of licorice, St. John's wort
  • Other: caffeine, several anesthetic agents (short-term elevation)
  • Diagnosis: careful history taking (often all that is needed)
  • Treatment: discontinuation, substitution, or dose reduction; manage BP if cannot discontinue

3. Renal Parenchymal Disease

  • Prevalence: >5% of secondary HTN causes (among most common)
  • Diseases involved: polycystic renal disease, chronic glomerulonephritis, diabetic/hypertensive kidney disease, recurrent stones, kidney infections
  • More common with glomerular than interstitial disorders
  • Most patients with CKD have HTN; often difficult to determine if BP is cause or consequence
  • Pathophysiology: RAAS activation is major contributor
  • Indicators: resistant HTN, fatigue, renal mass, hematuria, proteinuria, ↑ creatinine, hyperglycemia
  • Workup: serum creatinine, albuminuria, glucose; imaging (ultrasound, CT, MRI); renal biopsy if glomerulonephritis suspected
  • Treatment: often requires ≥3 antihypertensive agents + lifestyle; diuretics + RAAS inhibitors (ACEIs/ARBs) are mainstays
New Evidence
Recent RCT showed chlorthalidone (long-acting diuretic) reduced ambulatory BP by 10.5 mmHg in stage 4 CKD (eGFR <23), challenging the old assumption that thiazides are ineffective when creatinine >3 mg/dL. Finerenone (newer MRA) also enhances BP reduction in advanced CKD.

4. Primary Aldosteronism

  • Prevalence: ~5% of non-OSA/medication-related secondary HTN
  • Causes: idiopathic bilateral adrenal hyperplasia (60–70%), benign adenoma — usually unilateral (30–40%), rarely familial or other
  • Clinical indicators: severe/resistant HTN, unprovoked hypokalemia, hypokalemia-related arrhythmias (e.g., atrial fibrillation), nonspecific fatigue/weakness/muscle cramps, incidental adrenal adenoma on imaging, family history of early-onset HTN or stroke, OSA
  • Physical exam: no specific findings
  • Screening: high plasma aldosterone/renin ratio (under standardized conditions) + CT abdomen
  • Confirmatory: saline suppression test or captopril challenge; adrenal vein sampling = gold standard
  • Treatment: unilateral adenoma → laparoscopic adrenalectomy (cures or substantially improves HTN); bilateral hyperplasia → medical treatment with MRA + ↓ sodium intake + ↓ body weight
  • Spironolactone: most patients tolerate, but adverse effects (hyperkalemia, breast tenderness, gynecomastia) may require newer MRAs (eplerenone, finerenone)

5. Renovascular Hypertension

  • Prevalence: ~5% of secondary HTN; renovascular disease found in 25% at autopsy, but renovascular HTN = only ~1% of all HTN
  • Causes: atherosclerosis (90%) — typically unilateral, discrete lesion near origin of renal artery; fibromuscular disease (10%) — typically younger, mostly White women; bilateral disease more common
Atherosclerotic Renovascular HTN
  • Older patients with other ASCVD evidence
  • Bruits over renal/carotid/femoral arteries
  • Medical treatment preferred
  • Angioplasty shows NO benefit in RCTs
Fibromuscular Disease
  • Younger White women
  • Abrupt-onset or resistant HTN
  • Diffuse, irregular "sawtooth" renal artery lesions in distal segments
  • Percutaneous transluminal angioplasty (PTA) can cure or substantially improve HTN
  • Screening: renal ultrasound, CT angiography, MR angiography, renograms, plasma renin activity
  • Gold standard: digital subtraction angiography (renal angiography)
  • Hemodynamically significant if >70% vessel lumen occlusion
  • Renal venous renin sampling: now rarely performed — high false-positive and false-negative rate

6. Hypertensive Disorders of Pregnancy

  • Prevalence: HTN in >10% of US pregnancies; preeclampsia ~3–5%; eclampsia 0.1–0.3%
  • HELLP syndrome: hemolysis + elevated liver enzymes + low platelets = more severe preeclampsia
  • Eclampsia: seizures; occurs in <1% with mild, ~3% with severe preeclampsia
  • Classification: (1) chronic HTN (pre-pregnancy); (2) gestational HTN (onset after 20 weeks); (3) preeclampsia/eclampsia; (4) chronic HTN with superimposed preeclampsia
  • Pathophysiology: new-onset → reduced placental perfusion → ↑ peripheral resistance (usually resolves after delivery)
  • Treatment: bed rest, nonpharmacologic, pharmacotherapy
Drugs Contraindicated in Pregnancy
ACEIs and ARBs are CONTRAINDICATED — teratogenic. Do NOT use in pregnancy or planned pregnancy.
Safe First-Line Agents in Pregnancy
Beta-blocker labetalol, CCB nifedipine, central α2-agonist methyldopa. One trial suggested labetalol and nifedipine may be superior to methyldopa for preeclampsia prevention. Direct vasodilator hydralazine is also commonly used in US practice.

Clinical trials have failed to show benefit for target SBP/DBP <130/80 mmHg vs <140/90 mmHg in pregnancy.

Less Common Secondary Causes

Cushing's Syndrome
  • Excess cortisol from: pituitary adenoma/ectopic ACTH tumor/adrenal adenoma/exogenous glucocorticoids
  • Signs: moon face, buffalo hump, central obesity, striae, easy bruising, acne
  • Screening: 24h urinary corticosteroids, imaging, overnight dexamethasone suppression test
  • >75% of Cushing's syndrome patients have HTN
Pheochromocytoma
  • Excessive catecholamines from chromaffin cell tumor: adrenal medulla (80–85%) or extra-adrenal paraganglioma
  • Presents at relatively young age (30–50 years)
  • Paroxysmal attacks: high BP, sweating, headaches, cardiac arrhythmias
  • Screening: blood and 24h urinary catecholamines; abdominal CT/MRI/PET
  • Treatment: complete or partial adrenalectomy (minimally invasive)
  • Small minority have inherited predisposition: MEN2, von Hippel-Lindau, neurofibromatosis, hereditary paraganglioma
Coarctation of the Aorta
  • Most common congenital cause of HTN
  • Congenital narrowing of aorta
  • Severe defect detected early; mild defect may not be recognized until early adulthood
  • Exam: delayed & diminished femoral/distal pulses; systolic murmur in posterior intrascapular area; LVH signs
  • Workup: echocardiography, CT, angiography
  • Treatment: balloon angioplasty, surgery, or medical management
Other Rare Causes
  • Hyperthyroidism / hypothyroidism
  • Acromegaly
  • Hypercalcemia

Nonpharmacologic Interventions (Lifestyle)

General Principles
Interventions exhibit linear dose-response for BP reduction. Greater BP lowering in adults WITH hypertension, and when 2+ interventions are combined. Nonpharmacologic therapy also enhances the effect of antihypertensive medications (especially sodium reduction).

1. Diet Quality — DASH Diet

  • DASH (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension): high in vegetables, fruits, whole grains, low-fat dairy; low in fats, saturated fats, cholesterol
  • Trials evaluating Mediterranean, low-carbohydrate, vegetarian/vegan diets also show consistent BP reduction
  • Expected BP reduction: ~5 mmHg SBP in patients WITH hypertension; ~2–3 mmHg WITHOUT hypertension
  • Short-term feeding trial: DASH vs usual US diet → SBP/DBP reduced by 5.5/3.0 mmHg
  • 6-month behavior change trial: DASH + other nonpharmacologic measures reduced SBP/DBP by ~4/3 mmHg vs advice-only
  • DASH diet community purchasable at supermarkets — no need for specialty/high-priced foods
  • Can be combined with weight loss, sodium reduction, or increased physical activity

2. Weight Loss

  • Weight loss lowers BP AND improves lipids AND prevents/manages DM
  • Rule: SBP reduced by ~1 mmHg per kilogram of weight loss
  • High-quality counseling → average 6–12 month loss of ~10 lb (4.5 kg) → ~5 mmHg SBP reduction
  • Most sustainable: gradual and progressive loss via behavioral counseling (calorie reduction + increased physical activity)
  • Bariatric surgery for BMI ≥40 or ≥35 with HTN or obesity-related comorbidity
  • GLP-1 receptor agonists (injectable): FDA-approved for weight loss; shown to reduce weight in clinical trials
  • SGLT2 inhibitors: off-label for weight loss; best reserved for FDA-approved indications

3. Dietary Sodium Reduction

  • Sodium-BP dose response is almost linear — any reduction is beneficial
  • Large dose-response meta-analysis: ~5.5 mmHg SBP reduction per 2300 mg (100 mmol) decrease in sodium intake
  • SBP/DBP difference of ~15/10 mmHg between highest (~7000 mg/d) vs lowest (<1000 mg/d) sodium intake groups
  • Expected reduction: ~5 mmHg SBP with HTN; ~2–3 mmHg without HTN
  • In US: >80% of sodium intake is added during food processing/commercial preparation; only ~10% from naturally occurring sources
  • Optimal targets: <1500–2300 mg sodium/day (recommendations vary)
  • Salt substitutes (replacing ~25% sodium with potassium): easy way to reduce Na AND increase K; Chinese RCT showed 14% reduction in stroke, 13% CVD, 12% all-cause mortality vs regular salt
  • Long-term posttrial follow-up (10–15 years): sodium reduction intervention → significant 30% reduction in new-onset CVD events
  • Behavioral strategies: scrutinize food labels, reduce portion size, low-sodium condiments/seasonings, use herbs/spices, home-prepared foods

4. Potassium Supplementation

  • Higher potassium intake → lower BP and reduced CVD risk (especially stroke)
  • Recommended intake for US adults: ~3500 mg/d; DASH diet provides 4700 mg/d
  • US Dietary Reference Intake adequate intake: 2600 mg/d (women), 3400 mg/d (men)
  • Supplementation: dietary preferred (tends to produce healthier diet + lower sodium); pill alternative
  • Optimal supplemental amount: ~1200 mg/d additional (dose-response meta-analysis; U-shaped curve)
  • Expected reduction: ~5 mmHg SBP with HTN; ~3 mmHg without; almost 3× greater in Black vs White adults
  • Strong linear association between BP reduction and consumption of potassium AND dietary sodium (high sodium → greater benefit from potassium)
Contraindications to Potassium Supplementation
Hyperkalemia, advanced kidney disease, patients taking potassium-sparing diuretics, RAAS inhibitors (ACEIs, ARBs), or MRA agents (aldosterone, eplerenone, finerenone)

High-potassium foods: fruits, vegetables, legumes, leafy greens

5. Physical Activity

  • Strong inverse association between physical activity (leisure or total) and BP + CVD
  • Aerobic exercise: brisk walking, swimming, dancing — most studied
  • Dynamic resistance: climbing stairs, push-ups, weightlifting, squats
  • Isometric resistance: yoga poses, wall sit, plank, bench press (muscles tightened without joint movement)
  • Largest meta-analysis (~400 trials):
    • Aerobic alone: ↓ SBP ~5 mmHg in adults with HTN
    • Aerobic + dynamic resistance: ↓ SBP ~6.5 mmHg
  • In trials comparing physical activity vs antihypertensive drugs: almost identical level of BP lowering
  • Optimal: aerobic exercise 40–60 min, ≥3×/week, total ≥150 min/week
  • "Weekend warrior" exercise also shown to lower BP

6. Alcohol Reduction

  • Alcohol = pressor; roughly linear association with BP/HTN — no threshold
  • Each 14 mg increase in alcohol (one US standard drink) → SBP ~1.5 mmHg higher
  • Expected BP reduction: overall ~5.5 mmHg SBP in largest meta-analysis (varies with baseline level)
  • US guideline target: ≤2 standard drinks/day (men), ≤1 standard drink/day (women)
  • WHO recommends complete abstinence from alcohol

Combination Drug Strategies

  • Starting with lower doses of a 2-drug combination provides superior BP control and fewer side effects vs single drug at high dose
  • Complementary physiologic combinations:
    • Dual therapy: diuretic OR CCB + ACEI or ARB
    • Triple therapy: diuretic + CCB + ACEI or ARB
  • Average drugs to achieve SBP/DBP <140/90 mmHg = 2 drugs
  • Average drugs to achieve SBP/DBP <130/80 mmHg = 3 drugs
  • Single-pill combinations → better adherence vs multiple pills
  • Initial combination drug therapy superior to stepped-care approach for rapid target achievement and medication adherence

  • Reassess after 1 month

BP Treatment Targets

Standard Goal
SBP/DBP <130/80 mmHg — recommended in ACC/AHA BP guideline for adults with HTN. Almost all patients with HTN seen in clinical practice are at high risk for ASCVD.
  • Older adults (≥65, community-dwelling): target SBP <130 mmHg
  • Older adults with high comorbidity burden and limited life expectancy: clinical judgment + patient preference should guide intensity of treatment
  • All 4 RCTs comparing SBP 120 vs 140 mmHg goal: fewer CVD complications in lower-goal group (statistically significant in 2 of 4)
  • Meta-analyses: achieved SBP <130 mmHg is superior to higher categories; incremental benefit diminishes as achieved BP gets progressively lower
  • English-language guidelines: SBP targets vary from 120–135 mmHg for high-risk adults with HTN

Initial Drug Selection for Uncomplicated HTN

Preferred First-Step Drugs (No Compelling Indication for Beta-Blocker)
Diuretics, CCBs, and ACEIs or ARBs — alone or in combination. Beta-blockers are first-step ONLY when clinically indicated (IHD, HF).
  • Most patients: initial combination drug therapy (especially Black adults and those with SBP >20 mmHg above target)
  • Selected non-Black adults with Stage 1 HTN near 130/80: initial monotherapy acceptable
  • Most patients with HTN require >1 antihypertensive agent — especially Stage 2 HTN and non-Hispanic Black adults

Resistant Hypertension

Definition
SBP/DBP ≥130/80 mmHg on 3 antihypertensive medications (preferably including a diuretic), OR taking ≥4 medications to achieve <130/80 mmHg

Investigation approach (sequential steps):

  • Step 1: Exclude pseudo-resistance (inaccurate BP measurement)
  • Step 2: Out-of-office BP to exclude white coat HTN
  • Step 3: Exclude lifestyle factors, OSA, medications that raise BP, and other secondary causes
  • Step 4: Confirm medication adherence
  • Step 5: Switch to long-acting diuretics (chlorthalidone or indapamide in place of HCTZ)
  • Step 6: Convert to single-pill combinations if possible
  • Step 7: Add an MRA (spironolactone) as 4th medication
  • Step 8: Add vasodilator (hydralazine or minoxidil); refer to specialist if uncontrolled after 6 months
FDA-Approved Renal Denervation (2023)
Ultrasound and radiofrequency renal denervation therapy approved as adjunctive treatment for resistant HTN. Restrict to patients with true resistant HTN and office SBP ≥160 mmHg.

Treatment in Special Patient Groups

Patients with CVD

  • Combination of HTN + CVD is very common
  • Preferred agents: ACEI, ARB, or beta-blockers (per CVD type) + diuretics, CCB, and MRA
  • SGLT2 inhibitors may be indicated, especially with HF + DM
  • Use non-DHP CCBs with caution in bradycardia or potential heart block risk
  • Target: SBP/DBP <130/80 mmHg (if well tolerated)

Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)

  • HTN prevalence in advanced CKD: 80–85%
  • BP-kidney disease relationship is complex (cause AND consequence)
  • CKD patients have high prevalence of traditional AND novel CVD risk factors → high CVD mortality (especially ESKD patients more likely to die from CVD than kidney failure)
  • ACEI/ARBs are central: preserve glomerular function via efferent arteriolar dilation → ↓ intraglomerular pressure; especially important with proteinuria
  • DO NOT combine ACEI + ARB: dangerous due to hyperkalemia and decline in kidney function
  • Diuretics: also important for ↓ peripheral resistance + intravascular volume
  • Traditionally: loop diuretics for eGFR <30, but recent RCT showed chlorthalidone is effective even in eGFR <23 (↓ 10.5 mmHg ambulatory BP + halved albumin/creatinine ratio)
  • CCBs: tend to dilate preglomerular afferent artery → good safety record when combined with ACEI/ARB
  • Monitor serum potassium carefully with potassium-sparing agents
  • Single-pill combinations especially useful (patients often on many medications)
  • SGLT2 inhibitors (gliflozins): useful for CKD management; modest BP reduction (~5/2 mmHg per meta-analyses)

Patients with Diabetes Mellitus

  • Combination of HTN + DM = substantial risk for CVD and kidney disease
  • Recommended target: SBP/DBP <130/90 mmHg
  • Most patients require combination therapy; many need 3–4 agents
  • Include ACEI or ARB (especially with heavy proteinuria or kidney insufficiency)
  • Most also require a diuretic; CCBs commonly part of regimen
  • Resistant HTN with DM: MRA effective, but monitor serum potassium carefully
  • SGLT2 inhibitors: SBP/DBP reduction ~5/2 mmHg (modest; does not meet FDA threshold for antihypertensive classification)
  • Watch for autonomic neuropathy or volume depletion → orthostatic hypotension (SBP difference ≥10 mmHg sitting vs upright) in longstanding severe DM
  • Single-pill dual or triple therapy especially useful given medication burden

Older Adults

  • Vascular stiffening → isolated systolic HTN + wide pulse pressure are common
  • Often have multiple comorbidities and drug interactions (delayed excretion due to kidney/liver disease)
  • RCT evidence for antihypertensive therapy mostly from studies with mean age ~70 years
  • Older adults at high risk for CVD → disproportionately large treatment benefit
  • Target: SBP <130 mmHg (for community-dwelling adults ≥65)
  • High comorbidity + limited life expectancy: base decisions on clinical judgment + patient preference
  • Start with lower doses than younger patients
  • Congestive HF increasingly common → prefer diuretic over CCB for dual therapy with ACEI/ARB
  • RCTs show no increase in syncope, falls, or infarctions with intensive antihypertensive treatment in older adults
  • J-curve in observational data = not reliable; randomized comparisons show benefit of intensive therapy throughout
  • More intensive antihypertensive treatment → reduced risk of cognitive impairment and possibly dementia

Race/Ethnicity — Non-Hispanic Black Adults

  • Highest prevalence of HTN (59%), awareness, and treatment rate among racial/ethnic groups in US
  • However, control rates are lower: <140/90 mmHg = 37% vs 52% in Whites (2017–2020 NHANES)
  • HTN onset at younger age; higher risk of CVD and kidney disease than Whites
  • Most disparity explained by social determinants of health (education, healthcare access, neighborhood, economic instability), NOT genetics
  • Diuretics and CCBs especially effective for BP lowering in Black Americans
  • Most require combination therapy including agents for comorbidities
  • Requires structural change + team-based care + multilevel effort to resolve the gap

Sex Differences

  • Similar CVD prevention benefit from antihypertensive therapy in both sexes
  • Women: more likely to be aware, treated, and controlled vs men (especially at younger ages)
  • ACEIs/ARBs: CI in pregnancy or women of child-bearing age contemplating pregnancy
  • Thiazide diuretics → reduced fracture risk in women (↓ urinary calcium excretion + ↑ osteoblast formation)
  • Loop diuretics: ↑ urinary calcium excretion; not associated with fractures in postmenopausal women per observational data
  • Women report more cough with ACEIs and more pedal edema with CCBs compared to men

Hypertensive Urgencies & Emergencies

Threshold
SBP/DBP ≥180/100 mmHg classifies as either a hypertensive urgency or emergency.
Hypertensive Urgency
  • SBP/DBP ≥180/100 + asymptomatic
  • FAR more common than emergency
  • Treatment: institution/reinstitution/intensification of oral antihypertensive agents
  • Setting: outpatient
Hypertensive Emergency
  • SBP/DBP ≥180/100 + active hypertensive end-organ damage:
  • Hypertensive encephalopathy (headaches, vision defects, nausea, vomiting, seizures)
  • Acute left ventricular failure
  • Acute kidney failure
  • Treatment: immediate, supervised intravenous antihypertensive agents
  • Setting: emergency room or inpatient
Especially Urgent — Requires Particularly Rapid Care
Hypertensive emergency associated with: acute aortic dissection | eclampsia/severe preeclampsia | pheochromocytoma in crisis

Prevalence, Awareness, Treatment & Control

US Control Rates (NHANES 2017–2020)

  • SBP/DBP <140/90 mmHg: ~48% controlled
  • SBP/DBP <130/80 mmHg: ~24% controlled
  • Control rates improved until 2009–2012 (peak ~53% for <140/90 and ~26% for <130/80) then progressively declined
  • Decrement most prominent in adults ≥75 years old
  • Non-Hispanic Blacks <140/90: 37% vs Whites: 52%; <130/80: 20% vs 26% — persistent gap
Global Control Rates
Control to <140/90 mmHg: <14% globally; <8% in low/middle-income countries. The situation is far worse than the US.

Improving Hypertension Control — Best Practices

  • Traditional one-on-one physician-patient care is inadequate — primary care physicians are overburdened
  • Team-based care has produced the largest improvements in RCTs and meta-analyses:
    • Average SBP reduction: ~7 mmHg; in best-executed RCT: >23 mmHg reduction
    • Team: nurses, pharmacists, community health workers, social workers, lifestyle counselors, medical technicians
    • Most effective when trained nonphysicians have authority (with physician oversight) to prescribe antihypertensives for uncomplicated HTN
  • Home BP Monitoring (HBPM): excellent way to engage patients; US strategy = 3 morning + 3 evening readings for 3 days before clinic visit
  • Kaiser Permanente, Northern California: multifaceted systemwide program → control (<140/90) improved from 44% (2001) to 90% (2015)
Six Best Practices for HTN Control
(1) Community-based patient-centered team care | (2) Simple evidence-based tools for lifestyle counseling and drug treatment | (3) Reliable access to effective/affordable medications | (4) Information systems to track progress and case management | (5) Engage patients with HBPM | (6) Commitment by provider/system/country to specific goals

Exam Traps

Trap 01CPAP and Blood Pressure in OSA
A 55-year-old obese man with OSA and resistant hypertension is started on CPAP. His sleep partner confirms he no longer snores. Which of the following is the most appropriate next step for his hypertension?
CORRECT: Add antihypertensive drug therapy. Meta-analyses demonstrate that CPAP has limited effect on BP reduction in OSA patients. HTN in OSA must still be treated with antihypertensive medications in addition to nonpharmacologic therapy — CPAP alone is not adequate treatment for HTN.
Trap 02ACEI + ARB Combination
A patient with CKD and proteinuria is already taking lisinopril. Their physician adds valsartan to improve proteinuria and blood pressure control. What is the major risk?
CORRECT: Do NOT combine ACEI + ARB. This combination unnecessarily increases the risk of hyperkalemia and decline in kidney function with little if any additional benefit over either drug class alone. This is specifically dangerous in CKD patients who are already at risk for hyperkalemia.
Trap 03Preferred Diuretic for HTN
A physician prescribes hydrochlorothiazide 25 mg daily for uncomplicated hypertension. What should be prescribed instead?
CORRECT: Chlorthalidone (12.5–25 mg daily). Chlorthalidone is preferred over hydrochlorothiazide because of its longer half-life (better nighttime and daytime control) and its use in almost all landmark US antihypertensive drug treatment trials. In resistant HTN specifically, hydrochlorothiazide should be replaced by chlorthalidone or indapamide.
Trap 04Antihypertensives in Pregnancy
A 28-year-old woman presents at 24 weeks gestation with BP 155/95 mmHg. She requires antihypertensive therapy. Which drug must be avoided?
CORRECT: Avoid ACEIs and ARBs — teratogenic. First-line safe agents in pregnancy are: labetalol (beta-blocker), nifedipine (CCB), and methyldopa (central α2-agonist). Hydralazine is also commonly used. One trial suggests labetalol and nifedipine may be superior to methyldopa for preeclampsia prevention.
Trap 05Gold Standard for Primary Aldosteronism
A patient with resistant HTN has a high aldosterone/renin ratio and a CT scan showing bilateral adrenal abnormalities. What is the gold standard confirmatory test?
CORRECT: Adrenal vein sampling for aldosterone levels. While saline suppression test and captopril challenge provide more definitive evidence than the aldosterone/renin ratio, adrenal vein sampling is the gold standard. This is critical because it distinguishes unilateral adenoma (→ surgical cure via adrenalectomy) from bilateral hyperplasia (→ medical treatment with MRA).
Trap 06White Coat vs Masked Hypertension CVD Risk
Which type of out-of-office hypertension carries similar CVD risk to sustained hypertension?
CORRECT: Masked hypertension (office BP normal, out-of-office elevated). White coat hypertension has a CVD risk profile more like normotensive patients. Masked hypertension has a CVD risk profile like sustained hypertension and should be treated with antihypertensive drugs + nonpharmacologic therapy. Suspect masked HTN when elevated-but-nonhypertensive office BP + end-organ damage (LVH, proteinuria).
Trap 07Hypertensive Emergency vs Urgency
A 60-year-old presents with BP 195/110 mmHg but is completely asymptomatic. What is the appropriate treatment setting and approach?
CORRECT: Hypertensive URGENCY → outpatient oral agents. End-organ damage is the distinguishing feature. Asymptomatic = urgency → oral antihypertensives, outpatient. If there were encephalopathy, acute LV failure, or acute kidney failure = emergency → IV agents in ER/inpatient. Do NOT aggressively drop BP in asymptomatic patients — aim for gradual reduction.
Trap 08Renovascular HTN: Angioplasty Decision
An elderly man with diffuse atherosclerosis presents with resistant hypertension. Imaging shows bilateral renal artery stenosis. Should he undergo percutaneous transluminal angioplasty (PTA)?
CORRECT: No — medical management is preferred. Multiple well-conducted RCTs failed to show benefit for PTA or renovascular surgery vs medical care in atherosclerotic renovascular HTN. PTA is most beneficial in fibromuscular disease (younger White women with discrete lesions) — where it can cure or substantially improve HTN. Use of agents blocking RAAS is central to medical management.
Trap 09Potassium Supplementation Contraindications
A hypertensive patient with stage 3 CKD is on lisinopril and spironolactone. The physician recommends adding dietary potassium supplementation to help lower BP. What is the concern?
CORRECT: Potassium supplementation is CONTRAINDICATED here. This patient has multiple risk factors for dangerous hyperkalemia: advanced CKD, ACEI (lisinopril), AND MRA (spironolactone) — all of which impair renal potassium excretion. Potassium supplementation is contraindicated in patients with hyperkalemia or advanced kidney disease, or who are taking RAAS inhibitors, potassium-sparing diuretics, or MRAs.
Trap 10Nondihydropyridine CCBs and Beta-Blockers
A patient with hypertension is already on metoprolol. Her physician wants to add a calcium channel blocker. Which type should be avoided?
CORRECT: Avoid non-dihydropyridine CCBs (diltiazem, verapamil). Do NOT combine non-DHP CCBs with beta-blockers — both slow the heart rate and can cause severe bradycardia, heart block, and hemodynamic compromise. Use a dihydropyridine CCB (amlodipine, felodipine, nifedipine) if a CCB is needed in a patient on beta-blockers.
Trap 11ACEI Cough — What to Do
A patient on lisinopril develops a persistent dry cough. What is the next step?
CORRECT: Switch to an ARB at a corresponding dose. ACEI-induced cough is caused by inhibition of bradykinin inactivation. The side effect resolves quickly after switching to an ARB. ARBs do not inhibit bradykinin degradation and have a rare side effect profile. Do NOT add an ARB on top of the ACEI — this increases hyperkalemia risk without additional BP benefit.
Trap 12Salt Substitutes — Major Trial Result
A large cluster-designed clinical trial of salt substitutes (25% potassium replacing sodium) in high-risk Chinese adults showed which outcomes beyond BP lowering?
CORRECT: 14% reduction in stroke, 13% reduction in other major CVD events, and 12% reduction in all-cause mortality. This is a high-yield boards number. There was no significant difference in hyperkalemic events between the two groups. Salt substitutes provide a relatively easy way to achieve sodium reduction and potassium increase simultaneously.
Trap 13Ambulatory BP and Nighttime Readings
Which ambulatory BP measurement pattern is associated with higher cardiovascular risk?
CORRECT: Nondipping pattern (BP fails to decrease at night). Those whose BPs fail to show the usual pronounced decrement during nighttime (nondippers) have higher CVD risk. Nondipping is more common in non-Hispanic Black adults than other racial/ethnic groups in the US. Nighttime readings may provide the best BP prediction of CVD risk overall.
Trap 14Chlorthalidone in Advanced CKD
A patient with stage 4 CKD (eGFR 19 mL/min) has uncontrolled hypertension. The team considers adding chlorthalidone. What does recent evidence say?
CORRECT: Chlorthalidone is effective even in advanced CKD. A recent high-quality RCT in patients with stage 4 kidney disease (average eGFR <23 mL/min) showed chlorthalidone (12.5–50 mg) produced a 10.5 mmHg reduction in ambulatory BP and halved the urinary albumin/creatinine ratio vs placebo. This challenges the traditional teaching that thiazides are ineffective when creatinine >3 mg/dL.
Trap 15Nicardipine IV — Contraindication
A 75-year-old with a hypertensive emergency is also found to have severe aortic stenosis on echo. IV nicardipine is ordered. Is this appropriate?
CORRECT: No — nicardipine IV is CONTRAINDICATED in severe aortic stenosis. Although nicardipine is the most commonly used IV agent for hypertensive emergencies in contemporary practice and requires no dose adjustment in older adults, it is specifically contraindicated in severe aortic stenosis. Use an alternative IV agent such as labetalol (if no airway/heart block contraindication) or nitroprusside.
Trap 16BP Target in Pregnancy
Clinical trials in pregnant women with hypertension have failed to demonstrate benefit for which intensive BP target?
CORRECT: SBP/DBP <130/80 mmHg vs <140/90 mmHg in pregnancy. Unlike in non-pregnant patients where <130/80 mmHg is the standard goal, clinical trials have not shown added benefit for more intensive BP targets during pregnancy. This means that the standard <140/90 mmHg target remains appropriate in pregnancy and more intensive targets should not be pursued.
