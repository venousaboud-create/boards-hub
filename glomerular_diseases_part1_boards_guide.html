Glomerular Architecture

Normal Anatomy

  • 1.8 million glomerular capillary tufts per kidney — each tuft resides within Bowman's space
  • Bowman's capsule lined by parietal epithelial cells that either transition into proximal tubular epithelia or migrate into the tuft to replenish podocytes
  • Blood flow circuit: Afferent arteriole → branching capillary bed embedded in mesangial matrix → efferent arteriole → cortical peritubular capillaries or medullary vasa recta. This makes the glomerular tuft an arteriolar portal system — the only one in the body
  • The glomerulus filters 120–180 L/day of plasma water containing solutes of varying size

The Three-Layer Filtration Barrier

Layer 1 — Fenestrated Endothelium

Lines glomerular capillaries. Large fenestrae allow plasma water and solutes through but retain blood cells and large proteins

Layer 2 — Glomerular Basement Membrane (GBM)

Pore radius = 4 nm. Strongly negatively charged → electrostatically repels albumin. Type IV collagen scaffold (α3, α4, α5 chains). Physical size barrier

Layer 3 — Podocyte Foot Processes

Wrap the outer capillary surface. Adjacent foot processes bridged by slit-pore membranes containing nephrin + podocin → the ultimate selectivity gate. Podocytes are terminally differentiated and cannot regenerate

Albumin Handling — Why It Matters

  • Albumin has a negative charge (repelled by GBM) and physical radius of 3.6 nm — smaller than the 4 nm pore
  • Result: a small amount inevitably crosses → reclaimed by megalin and cubilin receptors along proximal tubule
  • Normal urine albumin: 8–10 mg/day (~20–60% of total excreted urinary protein)
  • Glomerular injury → pore charge or size barrier breaks down → urine albumin rises to gram quantities
Boards Pearl
Normal albumin excretion = 8–10 mg/day. Microalbuminuria begins at 30 mg/day. Nephrotic range = >3,000 mg/day. These thresholds are extremely high yield for the boards.
02

Pathogenesis of Glomerular Disease

Pathogenesis is variably linked to: genetic mutations, infection, toxin exposure, autoimmunity, atherosclerosis, hypertension, emboli, thrombosis, or diabetes mellitus. Even after careful study, the cause often remains idiopathic.

Genetic Mutations

  • NPHS1 (nephrin) & NPHS2 (podocin): mutations → congenital nephrotic syndrome; disrupts slit-pore membrane from birth
  • TRPC6 (cation channel): mutations → FSGS in adulthood by allowing excess calcium into podocytes
  • APOL1 polymorphisms (G1/G2 alleles): account for ~70% of African Americans with nondiabetic ESKD — particularly FSGS and hypertensive nephrosclerosis
  • Type IV collagen mutations (COL4A3, COL4A4, COL4A5):
    • COL4A5 (X-linked) → Alport's syndrome → split GBM, progressive nephritis, sensorineural deafness
    • COL4A3/A4 → autosomal Alport's or thin basement membrane disease
    • Much of adult-onset FSGS thought to be hereditary via collagen IV mutations
  • Complement pathway mutations (CFH, CFI, CFB, C3, CFHR genes) → C3 glomerulopathies, MPGN, atypical HUS
  • IgG4 antibodies to PLA2R antigens on podocytes → primary membranous nephropathy (~70–80%); IgG3 antibodies to PLAR2gc → poor prognosis subset
  • Fabry's disease (α-galactosidase A deficiency, X-linked): lysosomal storage → podocyte and endothelial injury → FSGS; zebra body inclusions on EM
  • Type II partial lipodystrophy (lamin A/C or PPARγ mutations) → metabolic syndrome + MPGN

Hemodynamic & Systemic Disease Mechanisms

  • Systemic HTN + atherosclerosis: pressure stress, endothelial injury, lipid oxidants → chronic glomerulosclerosis (ischemic nephropathy)
  • Malignant hypertension: rapid rise in BP → fibrinoid necrosis of arterioles and glomeruli, thrombotic microangiopathy, AKI — may complicate pre-existing chronic glomerulosclerosis
  • Diabetic nephropathy: hyperglycemia + advanced glycosylation end products + reactive oxygen species → GBM thickening, mesangial expansion → Kimmelstiel-Wilson nodules

Immune-Mediated Injury (Glomerulonephritis)

Inflammation of glomerular capillaries = glomerulonephritis. Most antigens driving inflammation are unknown.

  • Glomerular/mesangial cells may shed or express epitopes mimicking other immunogenic proteins
  • Bacteria, fungi, viruses can directly infect the kidney, generating their own glomerular antigens
  • Autoimmune GN confined to kidney: idiopathic membranous GN, MPGN, IgA nephropathy
  • Systemic autoimmune diseases spreading to kidney: lupus nephritis, ANCA vasculitis
  • Anti-GBM disease = both kidney AND lung injured (α3 NC1 of type IV collagen has narrow tissue distribution)
Inflammatory Cascade
Local Toll-like receptor activation → immune complex deposition → complement pathway activation → mononuclear cell infiltration → T-cell adaptive response → neutrophils, macrophages drawn by chemokines → cytokines + proteases → mesangial, capillary, and GBM damage. Early T-cell activation is key; antigens presented by class II MHC on macrophages/dendritic cells.

Immune Deposit Locations — Essential for Biopsy Interpretation

Subendothelial

Preformed circulating immune complexes precipitate along the inner GBM surface. WHY: large complexes can't cross the GBM. Seen in: lupus, SBE, MPGN

Subepithelial

In situ deposits: circulating autoantibodies find antigen trapped on outer GBM surface. WHY: small antigen crosses barrier first, then antibody attaches. Seen in: membranous GN, poststrep "humps"

Mesangial

Preformed circulating complexes OR in situ reactions in the mesangial space. WHY: mesangium is the drain for glomerular debris. Seen in: IgA nephropathy, Class I–II lupus

Progression of Glomerular Disease

Most Important Concept in This Chapter
Persistent GN that worsens kidney function is always accompanied by interstitial nephritis, renal fibrosis, and tubular atrophy. Kidney failure in GN best correlates histologically with the degree of tubulointerstitial nephritis — NOT with the type of glomerular injury. This is why biopsy T score (tubular fibrosis) predicts ESKD across all GN types.

Three Mechanisms of Tubulointerstitial Damage

Mechanism 1 — Tubular Obstruction

Interstitial inflammation + fibrosis → tubular compression → obstruction → aglomerular nephrons (glomerulus persists but tubule is non-functional). Results in dead-end filtration units.

Mechanism 2 — Architectural Disruption

Interstitial edema/fibrosis alters tubular and vascular architecture → impaired tubular transport → isosthenuria + polyuria. Reduced renin from juxtaglomerular apparatus → less angiotensin II → decreased arteriolar tone → GFR falls further.

Mechanism 3 — Peritubular Capillary Damage

↓ cross-section of peritubular capillaries → (a) tubular ischemia + (b) impaired outflow from efferent arterioles → intraglomerular hypertension in remaining nephrons → glomerulosclerosis spreads injury to previously healthy nephrons.

Proteinuria as an Independent Driver of Injury

  • Primary route: Filtered protein in tubular fluid → cytokines + lipoproteins → reactive oxygen species production → T lymphocyte + macrophage infiltration into interstitium → fibrosis + tubular atrophy
  • Alternative route: Efferent arterioles from inflamed glomeruli carry inflammatory mediators downstream → interstitial nephritis in adjacent tubules
  • Third route: Filtrate from glomeruli adjacent to Bowman's capsule breaches misdirects to periglomerular interstitium
  • All three routes converge on the same endpoint: interstitial fibrosis → irreversible kidney loss

Fibrogenesis — The Molecular Mechanism of Scar Formation

  • Sources of renal fibroblasts (THREE sources, know the percentages):
    • Epithelial or endothelial-mesenchymal transitions — 15%
    • Bone marrow–derived circulating fibrocytes — 35%
    • Proliferation of resident interstitial fibroblasts — 50% (largest source)
  • Key growth factors driving fibrogenesis: TGF-β (master regulator), FGF-2, HIF-1α (hypoxia), PDGF
  • Fibroblasts → lay down tenascin and fibronectin scaffold → polymerization of new interstitial collagen types I and III → scar formation
  • Reversal possible early: BMP-7 and hepatocyte growth factor (HGF) can reverse early fibrogenesis and preserve tubular architecture — rationale for early treatment
  • When fibroblasts outpace survival factors → apoptosis → permanent acellular renal scar → irreversible ESKD
Prognostic Pearl
Early acute tubulointerstitial nephritis = potentially reversible with aggressive immunosuppression or treatment of cause. Chronic interstitial fibrosis and tubular atrophy on biopsy = permanent, irreversible kidney loss. Always correlate biopsy findings with whether the injury is acute vs chronic.

Approach to the Patient

Hematuria

  • Microscopic hematuria is typically asymptomatic — patients are completely unaware
  • Threshold for suspicion: As few as 3–5 RBCs in spun sediment from first-voided morning urine
  • Gross hematuria = only rarely present in GN. Important exceptions: IgA nephropathy (synpharyngitic gross hematuria) and sickle cell disease
  • Workup steps:
    • Step 1: Exclude anatomic lesions — especially malignancy in older men (bladder cancer, RCC)
    • Also consider: BPH, interstitial nephritis, papillary necrosis, hypercalciuria, kidney stones, cystic disease, renal vascular injury
    • Step 2: Look for markers of glomerular origin — RBC casts (highly specific for GN) and/or dysmorphic RBCs (acanthocytes) on microscopy

Diagnostic Serology Panel

Standard Workup for GN
Anti-GBM antibodies (α3 NC1 domain), antiphospholipid antibodies, ASO, PLA2R, THSD7A, anti-DNAse B, antihyaluronidase, ANCA (PR3 + MPO), anti-dsDNA, cryoglobulins, HIV, Hep B surface Ag + anti-HBc, Hep C antibody, complement components C3 and C4 (depletion is a critical diagnostic clue).

Acute vs. Chronic Assessment

  • When confronted with abnormal UA + elevated creatinine: is the GN acute or chronic?
  • Chronic GN → decreased kidney size on ultrasound (kidneys typically <9 cm in long axis)
  • Acute kidney failure: fatigue, weakness, uremic symptoms (nausea, vomiting, somnolence), fluid retention
  • Primary GN progressing slowly can be remarkably asymptomatic until late stages
  • Once clinically stable with adequate clotting parameters → encourage kidney biopsy — essential for definitive diagnosis and treatment planning

Clinical Syndromes — Master Classification

Six Clinical Syndrome Categories

Acute Nephritic Syndrome

1–2 g/24hr proteinuria, hematuria with RBC casts, pyuria, hypertension, fluid retention, ↑ creatinine with ↓ GFR. If creatinine rises rapidly over days → RPGN. Histopathologic equivalent of RPGN = crescentic GN (fibrocellular crescents in Bowman's space).

Pulmonary-Renal Syndrome

RPGN + lung hemorrhage. Causes: Goodpasture's (anti-GBM), ANCA vasculitis (GPA, MPA), lupus, cryoglobulinemia. Life-threatening emergency requiring urgent dialysis ± intubation.

Nephrotic Syndrome

Heavy proteinuria >3.0 g/24hr, hypertension, hypercholesterolemia, hypoalbuminemia, edema/anasarca ± microscopic hematuria. GFR may initially be normal but declines over months–years.

Basement Membrane Syndrome

Genetically abnormal BM (Alport's — COL4A mutations) OR autoimmune attack on type IV collagen (Goodpasture's — anti-α3NC1). → microscopic hematuria, mild–heavy proteinuria, ± ↑ creatinine.

Glomerular-Vascular Syndrome

Vascular injury → hematuria + moderate proteinuria. Causes: TMA (TTP, HUS, aHUS), antiphospholipid syndrome, HTN nephrosclerosis, cholesterol emboli, sickle cell disease, ANCA vasculitis.

Infectious Disease–Associated

Most common cause of GN worldwide. Malaria + schistosomiasis most common globally; HIV, chronic Hep B/C common; SBE important in developed world. Range from nephrotic to nephritic presentation.

Kidney Pathology & Biopsy Interpretation

Light Microscopy Stains

H&E

Assesses cellularity, architecture, necrosis, crescents

PAS (Periodic Acid-Schiff)

Stains carbohydrate moieties in GBM and tubular membranes — highlights mesangial expansion

Jones-Methenamine Silver

Enhances basement membrane → identifies spikes (membranous), splitting (Alport's), reduplication (MPGN tram-tracks)

Congo Red

Detects amyloid → apple-green birefringence under polarized light. DO NOT miss amyloidosis

Masson's Trichrome

Identifies collagen; assesses interstitial fibrosis and tubular atrophy (IFTA) — the prognostic fibrosis marker

Oil Red O

Detects lipid in tubules (nephrotic oval fat bodies) or in Fabry's disease podocytes

Immunofluorescence (IF) Patterns

  • Conjugated antibodies detect: IgG, IgM, IgA, C3, C4, C1q, fibrin, PLA2R, THSD7A, DNAJB9
  • "Lumpy-bumpy" / granular pattern → immune complex deposits (poststrep, lupus, membranous, IgA, SBE)
  • Linear pattern → anti-GBM disease (IgG lines the GBM smoothly) — pathognomonic
  • Dominant IgA in mesangium → IgA nephropathy or IgA vasculitis (HSP)
  • Dominant C3 without Ig → C3 glomerulopathies (DDD, C3GN)
  • Negative/pauci-immune → ANCA vasculitis (GPA, MPA, EGPA)

Electron Microscopy (EM)

  • High-resolution imaging to clarify: location of immune deposits + GBM status
  • Subepithelial electron-dense deposits → membranous GN (± poststrep "humps")
  • Subendothelial deposits → lupus, SBE, MPGN
  • Mesangial deposits → IgA, lupus Class I/II
  • Diffuse GBM thinning → thin basement membrane disease (<250 nm)
  • GBM splitting + "basketweave" pattern → Alport's syndrome
  • Dense ribbons within GBM → DDD (formerly MPGN Type II)
  • Zebra bodies (lamellated inclusions in podocytes) → Fabry's disease

Poststreptococcal Glomerulonephritis

Prototype Disease
Poststrep GN = the prototypical acute endocapillary proliferative GN and the model for immune complex–mediated nephritis. Understanding it deeply gives you a framework for ALL nephritic diseases.

Epidemiology

  • Incidence dramatically decreased in developed countries (now sporadic, related to skin infections); epidemic in developing countries
  • Developing countries: typically children 2–14 years
  • Developed countries: more typical in the elderly, especially with debilitating illness or immunocompromise
  • More common in males; familial/cohabitant incidence as high as 40%

Pathogenesis & Timing

  • Skin or throat infections with nephritogenic M-type streptococcal strains antedate glomerular injury by a latent period
    • After pharyngitis: latent period 1–3 weeks
    • After skin infection (impetigo): latent period 2–6 weeks (longer because skin is a poorer antigenic site)
  • Key candidate antigens:
    • SPEB (streptococcal pyrogenic exotoxin B) — cationic cysteine proteinase; found inside subepithelial deposits
    • NAPlr (nephritis-associated plasmin receptor)
  • Mechanism: circulating immune complexes containing strep antigens → deposit in glomerulus → activate alternate complement pathway → C3 consumed, C4 spared → endocapillary inflammation

Kidney Biopsy Findings

  • Light microscopy: Hypercellularity of mesangial AND endothelial cells; prominent PMN infiltrates (looks like pus in the glomerulus)
  • Immunofluorescence: Granular "lumpy-bumpy" deposits of IgG, IgM, C3, C4, C5–9
  • Electron microscopy: Subepithelial "humps" (hump-shaped subepithelial electron-dense deposits) — pathognomonic of poststrep GN

Clinical Presentation

  • Classic acute nephritic syndrome: hematuria (tea-colored), pyuria, RBC casts, edema, hypertension, oliguric AKI
  • Severe cases may appear as RPGN (creatinine rises rapidly)
  • Systemic symptoms (up to 50%): headache, malaise, anorexia, flank pain (capsular distension from swelling)
  • Nephrotic-range proteinuria: 5% of children, 20% of adults

Laboratory Findings

Complement (Most Important)

First week: 90% of patients have depressed CH50 and ↓C3 with normal C4. Reflects alternative pathway activation (classical pathway = C4 intact). Returns to normal in 8–10 weeks. Persistent ↓C3 after 8 weeks → reconsider diagnosis (think lupus, MPGN, C3 glomerulopathy)

Strep Serology

Streptozyme positive in 80–95%: includes ASO (best for pharyngitis), anti-DNAse B (best for skin infection), Altase, ASKase, anti-NAD. Blood cultures positive in only ~25% (inconsistent). RF (30–40%), cryoglobulins, CICs (60–70%), ANCA anti-MPO in 10%

Subclinical Disease — Board Favorite
Subclinical poststrep GN is 4–5× more common than clinical nephritis. It presents with asymptomatic microscopic hematuria + low serum C3 only — no edema, no HTN. Usually does NOT require biopsy.

Treatment

Treatment = Supportive Only
Control hypertension, manage edema (loop diuretics), dialysis as needed. Antibiotics for any active streptococcal infection (treat ALL cohabitants too). NO role for immunosuppressive therapy — even in the setting of crescents on biopsy. This is the single most important board trap in this chapter.

Prognosis

  • Children: Most have complete resolution of azotemia, hematuria, and proteinuria within 3–6 weeks. 3–10% have persistent microscopic hematuria or mild proteinuria for years but rarely progress to ESKD
  • Adults: Overall prognosis good; ESKD uncommon
  • Elderly: Worse prognosis — azotemia persists in up to 60%, nephrotic-range proteinuria more common, higher risk of ESKD
  • Recurrent poststrep GN = rare, despite repeated streptococcal infections

Subacute Bacterial Endocarditis & Infection-Associated GN

Overview

  • GN = typically a complication of SBE — patients untreated for a long time, negative blood cultures, or right-sided endocarditis
  • Comorbidities: valvular heart disease, IV drug use, hepatitis C, DM
  • WHY GN is unusual in ACUTE endocarditis: immune complex–mediated injury takes 10–14 days to develop; acute endocarditis patients are usually treated urgently (surgery ± antibiotics) before this occurs
  • Gross appearance: Kidneys have subcapsular hemorrhages → "flea-bitten" appearance

Biopsy Findings

  • LM: Focal or diffuse proliferative GN with crescents (RPGN picture common)
  • IF: Granular deposits of C3 (present in 94%), IgG, IgA in mesangial, subendothelial, and subepithelial locations
  • Embolic infarcts or septic abscesses may also be visible on CT

Clinical & Lab

  • Gross or microscopic hematuria, pyuria, mild proteinuria, AKI, or rapid decline (RPGN)
  • Normocytic anemia, ↑ ESR, hypocomplementemia, elevated rheumatoid factor titers, type III cryoglobulins, circulating immune complexes, occasionally ANCAs

Treatment

Primary Treatment
Eradicate the infection: 4–6 weeks of IV antibiotics (organism-directed). If accomplished expeditiously → good prognosis for kidney function recovery. Always rule out ANCA-associated vasculitis (different treatment, different prognosis).

Infection-Associated GN Variants

  • Also occurs with: ventriculoatrial/peritoneal shunts; pulmonary, intraabdominal, pelvic, or cutaneous infections; infected vascular prostheses
  • In developed countries: predominantly in immunocompromised adults; predominant organism = Staphylococcus aureus (dominates over strep in modern era)
  • Biopsy: MPGN pattern, DPGN, or mesangioproliferative GN — sometimes RPGN with crescents
  • Prognosis: guarded — dependent on speed of infection control

Lupus Nephritis

Overview

  • Common and serious complication of SLE — among the most important determinants of long-term prognosis
  • Clinical kidney manifestations present in 30% at time of SLE diagnosis; majority develop kidney abnormalities during the disease course
  • Most common clinical sign = proteinuria; also: hematuria, HTN, active sediment with RBC casts

Pathogenesis

  • Deposition of circulating immune complexes composed primarily of DNA + anti-DNA antibodies → complement activation (both classical AND alternative pathway → both C3 and C4 consumed)
  • Results in: complement-mediated damage, leukocyte infiltration, procoagulant factor activation, cytokine release, mesangial and endothelial injury
  • In situ immune complex formation ALSO plays a role in some classes
  • Deposits occur in: mesangial, subendothelial, and/or subepithelial spaces — location determines class
  • Anti-dsDNA antibodies that fix complement correlate best with presence of kidney disease
  • Hypocomplementemia in 70–90% with acute lupus nephritis; declining complement levels may herald an impending flare

When to Biopsy
Biopsy should be performed in most patients with kidney involvement to establish histologic class — class determines therapy. Patients can transform between classes (especially III→IV); repeat biopsies are often needed if disease flares or treatment fails.

Remission Definition (Class IV)

Return to near-normal kidney function + proteinuria ≤330 mg/dL/day. If remission is achieved → excellent long-term outcomes. Without treatment → worst kidney prognosis of all lupus classes.

Class V Thrombosis Risk

Antiphospholipid antibodies → glomerular microthromboses + TMA → worse kidney prognosis DESPITE anticoagulation. Class V patients: always check antiphospholipid panel (lupus anticoagulant, anti-cardiolipin, anti-β2-glycoprotein I).

Prognosis

  • Up to 20% of lupus nephritis patients reach ESKD requiring dialysis or transplantation
  • Markedly increased mortality vs. general population
  • Kidney transplantation after ~6 months of inactive disease → allograft survival comparable to other causes of ESKD

Anti-GBM Disease & Goodpasture's Syndrome

Definitions

  • Anti-GBM disease: autoantibodies attack the glomerular basement membrane → GN alone
  • Goodpasture's syndrome: anti-GBM disease + lung hemorrhage = pulmonary-renal syndrome
  • Anti-GBM disease = autoimmune "conformeropathy" — perturbation of the quaternary structure of the α345NC1 hexamer in type IV collagen

Pathogenesis — Why This Antigen?

  • Target epitope: quaternary structure of α3 NC1 domain of type IV collagen (α345NC1 hexamer)
  • Normally, this epitope is sequestered inside the collagen IV hexamer — not accessible to the immune system
  • Exposure triggers: infection, smoking, oxidants, solvents → disrupt hexameric structure → previously hidden epitopes exposed to immune system
  • MHC-restricted T cells initiate the autoantibody response → IgG anti-α3 NC1 antibodies form
  • WHY lung AND kidney? → α3 chain of type IV collagen has a narrow tissue distribution: alveolar basement membranes + glomerular basement membranes only

Epidemiology — Two Age Peaks

Young Men (Late 20s)

More likely to have full Goodpasture's syndrome (hemoptysis + GN). Smoking is a major trigger. Sudden fall in hemoglobin, fever, dyspnea. These patients often present dramatically. Paradoxically: lung hemorrhage group often does better (presents early, treated quickly)

Older Patients (60s–70s, Men & Women)

More likely to present with isolated GN without lung disease. Often present later when kidney injury is already advanced. Those who do have lung hemorrhage in this age group: do relatively better than those with prolonged asymptomatic kidney injury

Red Flag — Poor Prognosis Indicator
Oliguria at presentation is particularly associated with bad outcomes. Creatinine >5–6 mg/dL + oliguria → prognosis is very poor. Urgent biopsy is essential to confirm diagnosis and assess extent of crescents.

Biopsy & Serology

  • LM: Focal or segmental necrosis → crescent formation in Bowman's space; interstitial nephritis with fibrosis and tubular atrophy
  • IF: Linear IgG staining of the GBM (pathognomonic) — rarely IgA
  • CRITICAL serology point: Test using α3 NC1 domain of collagen IV SPECIFICALLY — NOT whole basement membrane fragments. Reason: antibodies against α1 NC1 domain are found in paraneoplastic syndromes and are NOT nephritic; whole GBM assays cannot distinguish them
  • Double positive: 10–15% of Goodpasture's patients also have ANCA anti-MPO — both conditions require treatment

Poor Prognostic Indicators at Presentation

Biopsy Findings

>50% crescents on biopsy with advanced interstitial fibrosis and tubular atrophy

Clinical Lab

Serum creatinine >5–6 mg/dL; presence of oliguria; requirement for acute dialysis at presentation

Treatment

  • Lung hemorrhage: responds to plasmapheresis (clears circulating anti-GBM antibodies rapidly)
  • Acute treatment: 8–10 sessions of plasmapheresis + oral prednisone + cyclophosphamide (to suppress new antibody production)
  • Maintenance: low-dose immunosuppression until antibody titers are negative
  • Limited data on rituximab or MMF; used in refractory cases
  • Kidney transplantation: wait at least 6 months AND until serum anti-GBM antibodies are completely undetectable

IgA Nephropathy

Epidemiology

  • First described by Jean Berger in 1968; one of the most common forms of GN worldwide
  • Male preponderance; peak incidence in 2nd–3rd decades of life; sporadic; rare familial clustering
  • Geographic prevalence: 30–45% of GN biopsies in Asian/Pacific Rim populations, 20% southern Europe, 10% northern Europe/North America (lower detection due to less aggressive biopsy practices)

IgA Nephropathy vs. IgA Vasculitis (HSP)

IgA Nephropathy

Kidney-limited IgA mesangial deposition. Sporadic. Peak in 2nd–3rd decade. Asymptomatic microscopic hematuria OR hematuria during/immediately after URI.

IgA Vasculitis (Henoch-Schönlein Purpura)

Distinguished by: prominent systemic symptoms (palpable purpura buttocks/legs, arthritis, abdominal pain), younger age (<20 years), preceding infection. Biopsy = identical to IgA nephropathy.

Secondary IgA Deposits — Not Usually Clinically Significant

Can be found in: chronic liver disease (especially cirrhosis), Crohn's disease, celiac disease, chronic bronchiectasis, idiopathic interstitial pneumonia, dermatitis herpetiformis, mycosis fungoides, ankylosing spondylitis, HIV, Sjögren's syndrome.

Important Exception
IgA-dominant Staphylococcus-associated infectious GN IS associated with clinically significant kidney disease (despite IgA dominance). Must treat underlying infection.

Pathogenesis — The Galactose-Deficient IgA1 Hypothesis

  • Pathognomonic finding: dominant or codominant mesangial IgA deposits (alone or with IgG, IgM, complement)
  • IgA deposits = J-chain containing polymeric IgA1 (not IgA2 and not monomeric IgA)
  • Step 1: Abnormal O-glycosylation of hinge region of polymeric IgA1 → synthesis of poorly galactosylated IgA1
  • Step 2: Exposed N-acetyl-galactosamine residues → recognized by IgG or IgA1 autoantibodies → form immune complexes
  • Step 3: Immune complexes → deposit in mesangium → activate complement via alternative and lectin pathways
  • Step 4: Complement activation → mesangial proliferation → proteinuria + hematuria + eventual scarring
  • Elevated serum IgA in 20–50% of patients; IgA deposits in skin biopsies in 15–55%
  • Kidney biopsy remains necessary to confirm the diagnosis

Oxford Classification — MEST-C Score (Know All Five)

M — Mesangial Hypercellularity

Score 0 or 1. Associated with adverse outcomes in multiple cohort studies

E — Endocapillary Hypercellularity

Score 0 or 1. Endocapillary proliferation in any glomerulus

S — Segmental Glomerulosclerosis

Score 0 or 1. Any segmental adhesion or scar in any glomerulus

T — Tubular Fibrosis/Atrophy

Score 0–2 (0: <25%, 1: 25–50%, 2: >50%). Strongest histologic predictor of ESKD — reflects irreversible interstitial damage

C — Crescents

Score 0–2 (0: none, 1: <25% glomeruli, 2: ≥25%). Crescents → RPGN presentation

Clinical Presentations

  • Presentation 1: Persistent asymptomatic microscopic hematuria ± mild proteinuria (found on routine urinalysis)
  • Presentation 2: Recurrent episodes of macroscopic (gross) hematuria during or immediately following a URI (synpharyngitic = concurrent, not delayed like poststrep)
  • Nephrotic syndrome: uncommon but possible
  • Rarely: AKI with rapidly progressive clinical picture → RPGN with crescents on biopsy

Prognosis & Risk Factors

  • Slow progression to ESKD in only 25–30% of patients over 20–25 years
  • Adverse risk factors: hypertension, proteinuria, ABSENCE of macroscopic hematuria episodes, male sex, older age of onset
  • Persistent proteinuria ≥6 months = single greatest predictive factor for adverse kidney outcomes
  • T score (tubular fibrosis) on MEST-C = strongest histologic predictor

Treatment

  • ACEi or ARB: first-line for any proteinuria or declining kidney function — reduce intraglomerular pressure and proteinuria
  • Oral targeted-release budesonide (Nefecon): 16mg/day × 9 months → ↓proteinuria, ↑eGFR in patients at risk of rapid progression. Targets Peyer's patches in the ileum where IgA is dysregulated
  • SGLT2i (dapagliflozin): reduced adverse kidney outcomes in clinical trials (DAPA-CKD subgroup)
  • Sparsentan (dual endothelin/angiotensin receptor antagonist): ↓proteinuria in Phase 3 trials (PROTECT study)
  • Tonsillectomy: considered in select patients with frequent tonsillitis-triggered hematuria
  • Fish oil (omega-3 fatty acids): some small studies support anti-inflammatory effect
  • RPGN with crescents: steroids + cytotoxic agents ± plasmapheresis

ANCA Small-Vessel Vasculitis

Group Overview

  • Small-vessel vasculitis (arterioles, capillaries, venules; rarely small arteries) + GN
  • Circulating antineutrophil cytoplasmic autoantibodies (ANCAs) are the defining feature
  • Most common ANCA antigens: proteinase 3 (PR3) and myeloperoxidase (MPO)
  • Pathogenesis: ANCA production → T cell–driven → ANCAs activate leukocytes + monocytes → damage walls of small vessels → endothelial injury → further leukocyte recruitment → extends inflammation
  • Pauci-immune GN: absence or paucity of immune complex deposition on immunofluorescence — key distinguishing feature
Anti-PR3 ANCA (cANCA pattern)

More common in GPA (Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis / Wegener's). Also: renal-limited vasculitis. More commonly C-ANCA pattern on indirect immunofluorescence.

Anti-MPO ANCA (pANCA pattern)

More common in MPA (Microscopic Polyangiitis) and EGPA (Eosinophilic GPA / Churg-Strauss). Also: renal-limited vasculitis. More commonly P-ANCA pattern on IF.

Treatment — Shared Across All ANCA Diseases

Mortality Without Treatment Is High
Untreated ANCA vasculitis is rapidly fatal. All active disease requires urgent therapy.
  • Induction: glucocorticoids + cyclophosphamide OR rituximab (equally effective; rituximab preferred if lymphoma risk or childbearing age)
  • Plasmapheresis: recommended in rapidly progressive kidney failure, pulmonary hemorrhage, or double-positive anti-GBM + ANCA
  • Remission in 75–90%; clinical relapse 25–50%
  • Reduced-dose corticosteroid regimen with discontinuation at 16 weeks now recommended (↓ infection risk)
  • Novel complement C5a receptor antagonists (avacopan): steroid-sparing; non-inferior to steroids in remission maintenance
  • Maintenance (up to 1–2 years post-remission): rituximab (preferred) OR cyclophosphamide OR azathioprine/methotrexate
  • Long-term: monitor for malignancy (especially bladder cancer with cyclophosphamide) and infection

GPA — Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (Wegener's)

  • Classic triad: upper respiratory tract + lower respiratory tract + kidney
  • Symptoms: fever, purulent rhinorrhea, nasal ulcers, sinus pain, polyarthralgia/arthritis, cough, hemoptysis, shortness of breath, hematuria, subnephrotic proteinuria
  • Occasionally: cutaneous purpura, mononeuritis multiplex, orbital granuloma (proptosis)
  • May present without kidney involvement initially; most develop GN later
  • Chest X-ray: Nodules, persistent infiltrates, sometimes cavities (cavitation = classic)
  • Biopsy: Small-vessel vasculitis + adjacent noncaseating granulomas (distinguishes from MPA)
  • Kidney biopsies: segmental necrotizing GN without immune deposits; classified as focal, mixed, crescentic, or sclerotic
  • Risk factors: silica dust exposure, α1-antitrypsin deficiency
  • Highest relapse rate of all ANCA vasculitides (PR3 > MPO)
  • Greatest threat = adverse events from treatment (NOT active vasculitis) — especially elderly patients in the first year of therapy

MPA — Microscopic Polyangiitis

  • Clinically similar to GPA but rarely significant lung disease or destructive sinusitis
  • Can cause pulmonary capillaritis → alveolar hemorrhage (less common than GPA)
  • Key distinction on biopsy: vasculitis WITHOUT granulomas

EGPA — Eosinophilic Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (Churg-Strauss)

  • Small-vessel vasculitis + peripheral eosinophilia + asthma + allergic rhinitis + cutaneous purpura + mononeuritis multiplex
  • Lung inflammation (asthma) precedes systemic vasculitis by years to decades
  • 1/3 may have exudative pleural effusions with eosinophils
  • Labs: hypergammaglobulinemia, elevated IgE, rheumatoid factor
  • Kidney biopsy: small-vessel vasculitis + focal segmental necrotizing GN WITHOUT immune deposits; usually no eosinophils or granulomas in kidney biopsy (despite peripheral eosinophilia)
  • Cause: thought to be abnormal immune function; specific inciting triggers unknown

C3 Glomerulopathies & MPGN

C3 Glomerulopathies — Overview

Defining Feature
Defined by glomerular accumulation of C3 with little or no immunoglobulin on IF. Encompasses Dense Deposit Disease (DDD) and C3 Glomerulonephritis (C3GN). Both result from complement pathway dysregulation — mutations in CFHR genes causing uncontrolled C3 activation.

DDD vs. C3GN — Comparison

Dense Deposit Disease (DDD)

Formerly MPGN Type II. Defined morphologically by dense deposits forming continuous ribbons within the GBM on EM.

  • Primarily children and young adults
  • LM: mesangial proliferative or membranoproliferative (most common)
  • IF: Dominant C3 with little/no Ig
  • Labs: ↓C3, normal C4; C3 nephritic factor positive in most DDD
  • Extrarenal: Partial lipodystrophy (adipose atrophy) + Drusen bodies in retina (sub-retinal deposits)
  • Prognosis POOR: 50% progress to ESKD
  • Transplant: recurs in allograft (~50% recurrence)
C3 Glomerulonephritis (C3GN)

No specific morphology required for diagnosis — just dominant C3 without Ig. Mean age ~30 years (older than DDD).

  • ~2/3 have hematuria; ~1/3 have proteinuria
  • Labs: ↓C3, normal C4; C3 nephritic factor less commonly than DDD
  • Morphologically often indistinguishable from recovering postinfectious GN — look at timing and complement recovery
  • No lipodystrophy or drusen
  • Prognosis: less severe than DDD but still significant progression risk

Workup

  • Complement: C3, C4, factor H, factor I, factor B, soluble C5b-9 (terminal complement complex)
  • Paraprotein detection (SPEP, UPEP, serum free light chains) — monoclonal Ig can drive complement activation
  • Specific CFHR genetic mutations
  • Screen first-degree family members

Treatment

  • RAAS inhibition + lipid lowering (supportive)
  • Immunosuppression: steroids ± mycophenolate mofetil
  • Eculizumab (anti-C5 monoclonal antibody): inhibits terminal complement; evidence for benefit, especially in C3 nephritic factor–positive DDD

MPGN — A Pattern, Not a Disease

Previously classified as MPGN types I/II/III. Now understood as a pattern of glomerular injury requiring determination of underlying cause.

MPGN Pattern Features
Increased mesangial and endocapillary hypercellularity + thickened GBMs with interposition of cellular elements between endothelial cell and GBM → double contours = "tram-tracking" on Jones silver stain. EM shows subendothelial and mesangial deposits with cellular interposition.

Causes of MPGN Pattern

  • Immune complex–mediated: Hepatitis B, hepatitis C + cryoglobulinemia (most common globally), other infection-associated GN
  • Complement-mediated: C3 glomerulopathies (inherited or acquired complement dysregulation)
  • Monoclonal immunoglobulin–mediated: Light chain deposition disease, heavy chain deposition disease, MGRS (monoclonal gammopathy of renal significance)
  • Other: Fibrillary GN, immunotactoid GN
Key Teaching Point
Each cause has distinct biopsy findings, natural history, and treatment. Seeing MPGN on biopsy is the START of the workup — not the end. Find the underlying etiology to direct therapy.

Mesangioproliferative GN

  • Expansion of mesangium ± mesangial hypercellularity; thin, single-contoured capillary walls; mesangial immune deposits
  • Seen in: IgA nephropathy, Plasmodium falciparum malaria, resolving postinfectious GN, Class II lupus nephritis
  • Primary mesangioproliferative GN (after excluding secondary causes) = <15% of kidney biopsies

Exam Traps — Board Exam Coach

Trap 01Poststrep GN: Which Complement Component Falls — and Why?
A 7-year-old presents with tea-colored urine and edema 2 weeks after strep throat. Labs show CH50 low, C3 low, C4 normal. What pathway is activated and what does normal C4 mean?
Alternative complement pathway is activated (C3 bypasses C4 and is consumed directly). C3 is low; C4 is NORMAL. This pattern distinguishes poststrep GN from lupus nephritis (where BOTH C3 and C4 are low because the classical pathway is activated first via anti-DNA immune complexes). Rule: ↓C3 + normal C4 → alternative pathway → poststrep, DDD, or C3 glomerulopathy. ↓C3 AND ↓C4 → classical pathway → lupus, SBE, cryoglobulinemia.
Trap 02Poststrep GN: NO Immunosuppression — Even With Crescents
Biopsy of a 10-year-old with poststrep GN shows 40% crescents and rapidly rising creatinine. What immunosuppressive agent should be added?
NONE. Treatment of poststrep GN is supportive only: control hypertension, manage edema with loop diuretics, dialysis if needed, and antibiotics for active strep infection. There is NO role for immunosuppressive therapy in poststrep GN — even with crescents on biopsy. This is the single most-tested fact in poststrep GN on boards. In contrast: crescentic GN from ANCA, anti-GBM, or severe lupus IV DOES require urgent immunosuppression. The distinction is critical.
Trap 03Lupus Nephritis: Classes With Best vs. Worst Prognosis, and the Remission Definition
Match each class to its prognosis and treatment intensity: Class I, Class IV-S, Class IV-G, Class VI.
Class I: Excellent — minimal/no therapy. Class II: Excellent — mild therapy. Class III: Variable — mild may need steroids only; severe treated like IV. Class IV-G (global): Very poor without treatment — standard aggressive induction (steroids + MMF or CYC). Class IV-S (segmental): Worst prognosis of ALL classes without treatment — worse than IV-G despite involving <50% of the tuft. Class VI: ESKD — transplant. Remission (Class IV) = near-normal kidney function + proteinuria ≤330 mg/dL/day.
Trap 04Anti-GBM: Which Domain to Test — α3 vs. α1 NC1
An elderly man with a known lung malignancy has a positive anti-GBM test using whole basement membrane fragments. Is this diagnostic for Goodpasture's syndrome?
No — not necessarily. Antibodies against the α1 NC1 domain of collagen IV are found in paraneoplastic syndromes and are NOT associated with glomerulonephritis. Assays using whole basement membrane fragments cannot distinguish these from true anti-GBM antibodies (which target the α3 NC1 domain). Always confirm using an α3 NC1 domain–specific assay. A positive paraneoplastic anti-α1 NC1 antibody is not Goodpasture's. Only α3 NC1 antibodies cause anti-GBM disease.
Trap 05Goodpasture's: When Can the Patient Receive a Kidney Transplant?
A patient with anti-GBM disease progressed to ESKD. They are dialysis-dependent. When should they receive a kidney transplant?
Wait at least 6 months from the last active disease, AND until serum anti-GBM antibodies are completely undetectable. If antibodies are still circulating, they will attack the transplanted kidney's GBM — causing immediate recurrence. Most Alport's patients also develop anti-GBM antibodies post-transplant (against newly introduced collagen IV epitopes absent from their native kidneys), but overt Goodpasture's in the allograft is rare. Timing matters: 6 months + negative antibodies = transplant safe.
Trap 06IgA Nephropathy vs. Poststrep GN — Timing Is Everything
A 22-year-old male develops gross hematuria DURING a sore throat. C3 is normal. Two weeks ago a different patient developed hematuria after pharyngitis. What are the two diagnoses?
Patient 1 (concurrent / synpharyngitic) = IgA nephropathy. Hematuria occurs during or within 1–2 days of the URI. Complement is normal. Patient 2 (1–3 week delay) = Poststrep GN. The latent period is 1–3 weeks for pharyngitis, 2–6 weeks for skin infection. Complement shows ↓C3, normal C4. Additional distinguishing features: IgA typically 2nd–3rd decade; poststrep is 2–14yo in developing countries or elderly in developed countries; macroscopic hematuria in IgA = better prognosis.
Trap 07ANCA: PR3 vs. MPO — Which Antibody Goes With Which Disease?
A patient has purulent sinusitis, nasal ulcers, cavitary lung lesions, and rapid-onset GN. ANCA positive. Which antibody is most likely? Another patient has pauci-immune GN + asthma + peripheral eosinophilia. Which antibody?
Patient 1 = GPA (Wegener's) → anti-PR3 ANCA (c-ANCA pattern). Granulomas + ENT + lung + kidney = GPA. Patient 2 = EGPA (Churg-Strauss) → anti-MPO ANCA (p-ANCA pattern). Asthma + eosinophilia + vasculitis = EGPA. MPA = anti-MPO, no granulomas, no eosinophilia, minimal ENT/lung. Memory rule: PR3 = GPA = Granulomas/Purulent/Rhinorrhea; MPO = MPA/EGPA = More Pulmonary Eosinophilia/Arteries. GPA has the highest relapse rate.
Trap 08Pauci-Immune GN — What IF Pattern Do You See?
Crescentic GN on kidney biopsy with ABSENT immunofluorescence staining for IgG, IgM, IgA, C3, and C4. What is the most likely diagnosis category?
Pauci-immune GN = ANCA-associated vasculitis (GPA, MPA, EGPA, or renal-limited vasculitis). The absence or paucity of immune complex staining on IF is the diagnostic hallmark. Compare: anti-GBM → LINEAR IgG staining; poststrep, lupus, SBE → GRANULAR (lumpy-bumpy) staining with IgG + C3 ± others; IgA nephropathy → dominant MESANGIAL IgA. The IF pattern is often the first clue to ANCA vasculitis even before ANCA serology returns.
Trap 09ANCA + Anti-GBM Double Positive — How Common and What Does It Mean?
A patient with Goodpasture's syndrome is also found to have a positive ANCA anti-MPO. How common is this? How does it change management?
10–15% of Goodpasture's/anti-GBM disease patients are also positive for ANCA (usually anti-MPO). Both conditions must be treated simultaneously: Plasmapheresis (for anti-GBM antibody clearance) PLUS immunosuppression (steroids + cyclophosphamide or rituximab, as for ANCA vasculitis). Double-positive patients typically have a worse prognosis than either condition alone. After treatment, continue to monitor ANCA titers for relapse of the ANCA component (ANCA vasculitis can relapse long-term).
Trap 10SBE GN: 10–14 Day Minimum — Early Hematuria Is NOT Endocarditis GN
A patient diagnosed with acute bacterial endocarditis develops microscopic hematuria on Day 3 of hospitalization. Is this endocarditis-associated GN?
No. Immune complex–mediated GN from bacterial endocarditis requires at least 10–14 days to develop. At Day 3, the hematuria has another explanation: septic emboli to kidneys, drug-induced interstitial nephritis (antibiotics), or pre-existing kidney disease. True endocarditis GN occurs in SBE — patients who have been bacteremic for weeks without treatment, or who have right-sided endocarditis (often IV drug users). The 10–14 day rule is tested frequently as a timing trap.
Trap 11Lupus Class V: Thrombosis and Antiphospholipid Antibodies
A lupus patient with Class V nephritis develops sudden unilateral leg swelling and pleuritic chest pain. What is happening and what is the relevance of antiphospholipid antibodies?
DVT/PE — classic thrombotic complication of Class V lupus nephritis (membranous pattern). Nephrotic syndrome (hypoalbuminemia → ↓antithrombin III, ↑clotting factors) + antiphospholipid antibodies (lupus anticoagulant, anti-cardiolipin, anti-β2GPI) → profound hypercoagulable state. Antiphospholipid antibodies in Class V lupus → glomerular microthromboses + TMA → worse kidney prognosis DESPITE anticoagulation. Always check antiphospholipid panel in all lupus nephritis patients. Renal vein thrombosis is the most feared thrombotic complication.
Trap 12DDD: Two Extrarenal Findings That Clinch the Diagnosis
A 14-year-old presents with nephrotic syndrome, low C3, normal C4, and an MPGN pattern on biopsy. Ophthalmology finds drusen deposits. What else might you find on exam?
Dense Deposit Disease (DDD). The two pathognomonic extrarenal associations are: (1) Partial lipodystrophy — adipose tissue atrophy, especially of the face and upper trunk; (2) Drusen bodies in the retina — sub-retinal pigment epithelium deposits causing a drusenoid maculopathy. Both result from complement dysregulation affecting non-renal tissues. Labs: ↓C3, normal C4, C3 nephritic factor positive in most DDD. EM: dense ribbons within the GBM (not just subendothelial — IN the membrane). Prognosis = 50% reach ESKD.
Trap 13IgA Nephropathy: Macroscopic Hematuria = BETTER Prognosis (Counterintuitive)
In IgA nephropathy, which group has a BETTER kidney prognosis: patients with recurrent episodes of gross hematuria, or those with only asymptomatic microscopic hematuria?
Counterintuitive: patients with recurrent macroscopic (gross) hematuria episodes have BETTER long-term kidney prognosis. The absence of macroscopic hematuria is listed as an adverse risk factor. Why? Macroscopic hematuria = acute inflammation with possible mesangial IgA clearance between attacks; patients with only microscopic hematuria may have more chronic, smoldering, subclinical inflammation. Other adverse factors: hypertension, persistent proteinuria ≥6 months (strongest predictor), male sex, older age. On biopsy: T score (tubular fibrosis) is the strongest histologic predictor of ESKD.
Trap 14ANCA Vasculitis: The Greatest Threat Is Treatment — Not the Vasculitis
A 74-year-old with GPA is started on high-dose steroids + cyclophosphamide. She achieves remission. What is now her greatest risk over the next 12 months?
Adverse events from treatment — NOT active vasculitis recurrence. In elderly patients in the first year of therapy, treatment complications (serious infections, PCP pneumonia, hemorrhagic cystitis from CYC, steroid-induced DM, avascular necrosis, malignancy) cause more morbidity and mortality than the underlying disease. This is why: (1) corticosteroid dose is now tapered and discontinued at 16 weeks; (2) rituximab is preferred over CYC in elderly; (3) long-term monitoring for malignancy (especially bladder cancer) is essential; (4) PCP prophylaxis (TMP-SMX) is mandatory. GPA also has the highest relapse rate of the ANCA diseases — close monitoring is needed regardless.
