01

Overview & Crystal Types

Crystal-induced arthropathies arise when microcrystals deposit in joints and connective tissue, triggering acute or chronic inflammatory responses via inflammasome activation and potent proinflammatory cytokines. Four major crystal types are responsible:

Monosodium Urate (MSU)

Needle/rod-shaped. Strongly negatively birefringent under compensated polarized light. Seen intracellularly + extracellularly. Causes gout.

Calcium Pyrophosphate (CPP)

Rectangular, rod-shaped, rhomboid. Weakly positively birefringent or nonbirefringent. Causes pseudogout (CPPD disease).

Calcium Apatite

1–20 μm globules. Nonbirefringent — invisible by polarized light; requires electron microscopy. Stain red with alizarin red S. Causes Milwaukee shoulder, calcific tendinitis.

Calcium Oxalate (CaOx)

Bipyramidal shape. Strongly birefringent. Seen in chronic renal failure / long-term dialysis. Stains with alizarin red S.

🔑 Board Pearl — Polarized Light Rule
Polarized light microscopy identifies MSU (negative) and CPP (weak positive). Apatite is NOT detectable by polarized light — requires electron microscopy. Always aspirate to rule out concurrent septic arthritis; bacterial infection can coexist with any crystal.
02

Pathogenesis of Gout

Why Humans Get Gout — Evolutionary Loss of Uricase

Humans are the only mammals known to develop gout spontaneously because of a species-wide evolutionary loss of uricase, the enzyme that converts urate into the highly water-soluble allantoin. Without uricase, uric acid accumulates at physiologic pH (pKa 5.8) as urate (the ionized form), predisposing to crystallization when serum concentrations exceed the saturation threshold of ~6.85 mg/dL.

Urate Balance — Production vs. Excretion

Overproduction (10% of cases)
  • Hematologic malignancies, psoriasis — ↑ cell turnover → ↑ purine salvage
  • Chemotherapy → ↑ urate synthesis
  • Tissue hypoxia → net ATP degradation → ADP → AMP → uric acid
  • Glycogen storage diseases (types I, III, V, VIII); fructose intolerance/infusion; ethanol
  • PRPP synthetase superactivity (X-linked) → ↑ PRPP → ↑ purines → ↑ urate
  • HPRT deficiency (Lesch-Nyhan) → hypoxanthine cannot be salvaged → degraded to urate
Impaired Excretion (90% — most common)
  • Renal proximal tubule: URAT1 + OAT10 (apical urate reabsorption, sodium-driven)
  • GLUT9 transporter — basolateral urate excretion
  • ABCG2 gene — high-capacity intestinal urate efflux transporter (also in renal proximal tubule)
  • ~2/3 eliminated renally; ~1/3 via GI tract (intestinal uricolysis by gut bacteria)
  • Anti-uricosuric drugs: thiazide/loop diuretics, low-dose aspirin, pyrazinoate (PZA), nicotinate

MSU Crystallization Cascade — From Hyperuricemia to Flare

  • Chronic hyperuricemia (SU >~6.85 mg/dL) — prerequisite but not sufficient alone; only a minority of hyperuricemic individuals develop gout. Pathophysiologic factors favoring crystallization: lower temperature (peripheral joints), ↓ pH, intra-articular dehydration, ↑ cation concentration, collagen, chondroitin sulfate, nonaggregating proteoglycans.
  • MSU crystal deposition → tophi formation — crystals deposit in joints and connective tissue, especially proteoglycan-rich tissues (joints, tendons). Synovial tophi typically walled off. Engage both innate and adaptive immunity.
  • Triggers → crystal shedding — purine-rich food, alcohol, diuresis, rapid urate changes with ULT initiation, local trauma, medical illness (CHF, hypoxia) loosen crystals from organic matrix into synovial space.
  • NLRP3 inflammasome activation — resident synovial phagocytes (monocytes/macrophages, mast cells) activated → NLRP3 inflammasome → pro-IL-1 and IL-1β, plus IL-6, IL-8, TNF-α.
  • Neutrophil recruitment → respiratory burst — neutrophils phagocytose MSU → lysosomal enzymes, superoxide anion, leukotriene B4, additional cytokines and chemokines. Cellular mechanisms involve kinases, phospholipases, chemoattractants, and adhesion molecules.

Genetic Causes of Hyperuricemia (High-Yield)

PRPP Synthetase Superactivity (X-linked)
  • ↑ PRPP → ↑ purine nucleotides → ↑ urate production
  • Causes gout + urate nephrolithiasis
  • X chromosome-linked inborn error of metabolism
HPRT Deficiency (X-linked) — Lesch-Nyhan
  • Spectrum: isolated hyperuricemia → Lesch-Nyhan syndrome (severe neurologic + behavioral abnormalities including self-mutilation); 250 different mutations described
  • Without HPRT: hypoxanthine only converted to urate (cannot be salvaged)
  • ↓ IMP and GMP → loss of feedback inhibition → ↑ de novo purine synthesis
  • Males homozygous for X-linked mutation → disease; carrier females (esp. postmenopausal) → gout + urinary tract stones
  • Hyperuricemia in prepubertal boys → always consider HPRT/PRPP defects
⚠ Cardiometabolic-Kidney Comorbidity Cluster
Gout is often part of the insulin resistance syndrome. Associated comorbidities: obesity, hypertension, type 2 diabetes, myocardial infarction, stroke, chronic kidney disease (CKD), urate nephrolithiasis. Modifiable risk factors: obesity, Western diet, alcohol, sedentary lifestyle, diuretics.
03

Clinical Manifestations of Gout

Acute Gout Flare — Classic Presentation

Who Gets It

Middle-aged to elderly men; postmenopausal women. Women = only 5–20% of all gout patients, typically postmenopausal with OA, hypertension, mild renal insufficiency, often on diuretics.

Timing

Onset at night to early morning. One of the most painful conditions humans experience. Subsides spontaneously in 1–2 weeks without treatment (intercritical gout between attacks).

Joint Pattern

First MTP (podagra): 70–90% of cases. Then tarsal joints, ankles, knees. Fingers, wrists, elbows more common in elderly / advanced disease. Oligo- and polyarticular patterns develop over time.

Physical Exam

Warm, red, tender, substantially swollen joint and periarticular area. Mimics cellulitis (pseudocellulitis) — classic board trap. Clinical appearance does not reliably distinguish from septic arthritis.

Disease Stages

  • Asymptomatic hyperuricemia — elevated serum urate without clinical manifestations. Only a minority progress to gout.
  • Acute recurrent gout flares — episodic inflammatory attacks separated by intercritical periods (asymptomatic intervals of varying length, no residual symptoms).
  • Chronic gouty arthritis — after years of untreated flares: ongoing synovitis, subcutaneous tophi, deformity, bony destruction. Less commonly, chronic arthritis or tophi only as sole manifestation.

Flare Triggers

Purine-rich food Alcohol Thiazide / loop diuretics ULT initiation (paradoxical) Local trauma Congestive heart failure Respiratory hypoxia Medical illness

Tophi — Chronic Gouty Arthritis

Tophi are deposits of MSU crystals surrounded by granulomatous inflammation, found in proteoglycan-rich tissues (joints, tendons). They represent chronic inflammation and engage both innate and adaptive immunity. Although they occasionally show acute inflammatory signs, tophi predominantly signify a chronic inflammatory state. Growing MSU crystal deposition leads to chronic gouty arthritis with subcutaneous tophi, erosive deformities, and bony destruction.

Special Populations

Women with Gout
  • Only 5–20% of all gout patients
  • Most postmenopausal and elderly
  • Often have OA, hypertension, or mild renal insufficiency
  • Usually receiving diuretics
  • Premenopausal gout is rare — suspect inherited enzymatic deficit (HPRT/PRPP) or ↓ renal urate clearance
Lesch-Nyhan / HPRT Spectrum
  • X-linked: males homozygous → disease; carrier females (postmenopausal) → gout + urinary tract stones
  • Spectrum: isolated hyperuricemia → full Lesch-Nyhan (self-mutilation, neurologic, behavioral abnormalities)
  • Hyperuricemia in prepubertal boys → always evaluate for HPRT/PRPP defects
04

Diagnosis of Gout

Gold Standard — Synovial Fluid Analysis

Even when clinical features strongly suggest gout, needle aspiration and synovial fluid analysis confirms the diagnosis. Acute septic arthritis, other crystal arthropathies, palindromic rheumatism, and psoriatic arthritis can all mimic gout clinically.

MSU Crystals Under Polarized Light
  • Needle-shaped and rod-shaped
  • Strongly negatively birefringent under compensated polarized light
  • Present both intracellularly and extracellularly
  • Leukocytes: 5,000–75,000/μL (cloudy fluid)
  • Large crystal loads → thick, pasty, chalky fluid from distended tophus
Critical Caveats
  • Always culture the fluid — bacterial infection can coexist with MSU crystals
  • MSU crystals can be demonstrated in first MTP and knees between flares — useful for intercritical diagnosis
  • Aspiration of asymptomatic joints is diagnostically valuable
⚠ Board Trap — Serum Urate During Flare
Serum urate can be normal or even LOW during an acute gout flare. Inflammatory cytokines (e.g., IL-6) have uricosuric properties that lower SU by ~2 mg/dL. Never use a normal SU to exclude gout in the acute setting! Seek historical or post-flare SU values as diagnostic clues and therapeutic targets.

Baseline Laboratory Tests

Serum creatinine Liver function tests Hemoglobin + WBC Hemoglobin A1c Serum lipids

Obtained at baseline to assess risk factors, comorbidities requiring treatment, and monitor for potential adverse effects of gout treatments.

Imaging

Musculoskeletal Ultrasound

Double-contour sign overlying articular cartilage — signifies MSU deposition. Useful for early diagnosis and timely confirmation.

Dual-Energy CT (DECT)

Uses two different energy beams to identify MSU by chemical composition. Can confirm specific presence of MSU crystals — useful when fluid aspiration is not feasible.

Plain Radiography

Only shows findings in advanced/chronic gout: cystic changes; well-defined erosions with sclerotic margins + overhanging bony edges; soft tissue masses (tophi). Typically absent in early gout — not useful for early diagnosis.

05

Acute Gout Treatment

🎯 Principle of Acute Management
Goal = anti-inflammatory therapy targeting downstream crystal-induced inflammation. Nonpharmacologic adjuncts (ice packs, rest of involved joints) are helpful. Drug choice depends on comorbidities, concurrent medications, and prior response. Early initiation aborts or reduces flare severity — provide established patients a supply ready to start at first sign of a flare.

Colchicine — First-Line (Used Early)

Colchicine — Microtubule Inhibitor

Mechanism

Inhibits microtubule polymerization → disrupts neutrophil chemotaxis and activation; also inhibits the NLRP3 inflammasome pathway upstream of IL-1β release.

Drugs & Doses

  • Acute (low-dose regimen): 1.2 mg at first sign of flare, then 0.6 mg 1 hour later; subsequent-day dosing per response. As effective as high-dose; better tolerated.
  • Prophylaxis: 0.6 mg once to twice daily (continue 3–6 months normouricemic + attack-free, or until tophi disappear)

Elimination / Drug Interactions

  • Eliminated via P-glycoprotein (MDR1) in liver, small intestine, kidneys, and via CYP3A4
  • Reduce dose with P-gp or CYP3A4 inhibitors: clarithromycin, tacrolimus, cyclosporine
  • Extra caution with hepatorenal impairment; reduce dose in renal disease for prolonged use

Side Effects / Contraindications

GI toxicity (high-dose) Myopathy CYP3A4 inhibitor co-administration Hepatorenal impairment (reduce dose)
💡 Clinical Pearl
Works best when used early in a flare. If delayed, efficacy declines sharply. The low-dose regimen (1.2 + 0.6 mg) = equivalent efficacy to high-dose with fewer GI side effects.

NSAIDs — Most Commonly Used

NSAIDs — COX Inhibitors

Drugs & Doses

  • Indomethacin: 25–50 mg TID
  • Naproxen: 500 mg BID
  • Ibuprofen: 800 mg TID
  • Celecoxib: 800 mg ×1, then 400 mg 12h later, then 400 mg BID

Side Effects / Contraindications

GI ulceration / bleeding Renal impairment Avoid in CKD, active peptic ulcer Avoid in heart failure
💡 Clinical Pearl
Best for individuals without complicating comorbidities. Must use full anti-inflammatory doses. Effective in the vast majority of patients when dosed adequately.

Glucocorticoids — When NSAIDs / Colchicine Contraindicated

Glucocorticoids — Systemic or Intraarticular

Drugs & Doses

  • Oral: Prednisone 30–50 mg/day initial dose, gradually tapered with resolution — effective even in polyarticular gout
  • Intramuscular: IM injection for polyarticular gout when oral route not feasible
  • Intraarticular: Effective and well tolerated for single joint or a few involved joints

Side Effects / Contraindications

Hyperglycemia Fluid retention Active infection

IL-1β Antagonists — Refractory Cases

Anakinra / Canakinumab — IL-1β Blockers

Mechanism

Block IL-1β — the central proinflammatory cytokine activated by the NLRP3 inflammasome in gout. Targets the upstream driver of neutrophil recruitment and synovial inflammation.

Indication

When other treatments are contraindicated or have failed. Useful in patients who cannot tolerate NSAIDs, colchicine, or glucocorticoids.

06

Urate-Lowering Therapy (ULT)

🎯 Goal of ULT
Correct the underlying chronic hyperuricemia. Target serum urate <5–6 mg/dL (<300–360 μmol/L) — a sub-saturation point to dissolve existing crystals and prevent new flares. Usually required for life.

When to Initiate ULT

  • Consider when hyperuricemia cannot be corrected by lifestyle changes alone
  • Indications: ≥2 gout flares/year; severe/prolonged flares; subcutaneous tophi; chronic gouty arthritis; known uric acid nephrolithiasis
  • Patient willingness to commit to long-term therapy
⚠ Critical Timing Rule
ULT is generally NOT initiated during active acute gout flares — acutely lowering SU causes paradoxical crystal shedding from dissolving synovial tophi, worsening the flare. Start during resolution phase or after a flare, with anti-inflammatory prophylaxis (colchicine 0.6 mg 1–2×/day or naproxen 250 mg BID). Faster SU reduction → higher paradoxical flare risk. Continue prophylaxis until normouricemic and attack-free for 3–6 months, or until tophi disappear.

Lifestyle Modifications First

Control body weight Healthy diet Limit alcohol ↓ Fructose-rich foods/drinks Avoid thiazide/loop diuretics

Allopurinol — First-Line

Allopurinol — Xanthine Oxidase Inhibitor (First-Line)

Mechanism

Inhibits xanthine oxidase → ↓ conversion of xanthine to uric acid → ↓ urate synthesis.

Drugs & Doses

  • Start 100 mg/day (or less) as a single morning dose
  • Titrate up to 800 mg/day maximum to achieve SU <5–6 mg/dL
  • CKD with GFR <30 mL/min: start at 50 mg/day, titrate slowly

Side Effects / Contraindications

Mild cutaneous reactions (~2%) SCARs (rare but life-threatening) Stevens-Johnson syndrome Toxic epidermal necrolysis HLA-B*5801 carriage (screen first) CKD (higher SCAR risk)

HLA-B*5801 Screening — Who Needs It

🚨 Critical Safety — HLA-B*5801
Screen before starting allopurinol in: Southeast Asian (except Japanese descendants), Pacific Islander/Native Hawaiian, and Black patients — high HLA-B*5801 carriage rate creates racial disparity in SCAR risk. If positive → use febuxostat instead. Starting at a low dose and titrating slowly also reduces SCAR risk regardless of HLA status. Presence of CKD and higher initial dosing (>100 mg/day in CKD) are additional SCAR risk factors.

Febuxostat — Alternative XO Inhibitor

Febuxostat — Xanthine Oxidase Inhibitor

Mechanism

Xanthine oxidase inhibitor (structurally distinct from allopurinol). Predominantly metabolized by glucuronide formation and oxidation in the liver.

Advantages

  • Does NOT require dose adjustment in moderate-to-severe CKD
  • Used in patients who carry HLA-B*5801 or who cannot tolerate allopurinol

Probenecid — Second-Line Uricosuric

Probenecid — Uricosuric Agent (Second-Line)

Mechanism

Inhibits renal tubular urate reabsorption (URAT1/OAT10) → ↑ urinary urate excretion. Requires adequate renal tubular function.

Drugs & Doses

  • Start 250 mg BID → increase gradually up to 3 g/day to maintain SU target
  • NOT effective when serum creatinine >177 μmol/L (2 mg/dL) — requires functioning kidneys
  • Benzbromarone: another uricosuric (not available in US) — more effective in CKD patients

Use Context

Second-line, alone or combined with xanthine oxidase inhibitors; for patients with good renal function who cannot tolerate or do not achieve targets with allopurinol alone.

Pegloticase — For Refractory Gout

Pegloticase — Pegylated Uricase

Mechanism

Converts urate to allantoin (highly water-soluble, easily excreted) — replaces the uricase activity lost in human evolution. Produces dramatic, rapid urate reduction.

Indication & Key Issue

  • For patients who do NOT tolerate or FAIL full doses of other ULT agents
  • Often combined with methotrexate to reduce immunogenicity (antibody formation → loss of efficacy + infusion reactions)

Cardiometabolic Drugs with Urate-Lowering Effects

SGLT2 Inhibitors

Offer both urate reduction AND comprehensive cardiometabolic-kidney comorbidity care. Beneficial in gout patients with multiple comorbidities given their approved multiple cardiometabolic-kidney indications.

Other Agents with Urate-Lowering Effects

Losartan (ARB), amlodipine (CCB), fenofibrate (lipid-lowering) — all have modest secondary urate-lowering effects that benefit gout comorbidity management.

07

Calcium Pyrophosphate Deposition (CPPD) Disease

Epidemiology & Pathogenesis

Epidemiology
  • Predominantly affects the elderly
  • Prevalence doubles with each decade after age 60
  • ~50% prevalence in those >85 years old
  • Most cases asymptomatic with uncertain underlying etiology
  • Biochemical changes in aging/diseased cartilage favor CPP crystal nucleation
Central Pathogenic Mechanism
  • ↑ Inorganic pyrophosphate (PPi) in cartilage matrix — analogous to hyperuricemia in gout
  • PPi combines with calcium → CPP crystals in matrix vesicles or on collagen fibers
  • Most PPi from breakdown of extracellular ATP
  • ANKH protein (multipass membrane protein) regulates ATP efflux and PPi transport; ANKH gene mutations → ↑ PPi elaboration (familial and sporadic CPPD)
  • ↑ ATP pyrophosphohydrolase and 5′-nucleotidase activity also ↑ PPi
  • CPP crystal release → phagocytosis by monocyte-macrophages and neutrophils → inflammasome activation (same pathway as MSU)

Associated Conditions — Investigate if Age <50!

Endocrine-Metabolic Causes
  • Primary hyperparathyroidism (↑ calcium)
  • Hemochromatosis (↑ iron)
  • Hypophosphatasia (↓ alkaline phosphatase)
  • Hypomagnesemia
  • Gitelman's syndrome; X-linked hypophosphatemic rickets; familial hypocalciuric hypercalcemia; malabsorption
  • Drugs: thiazide/loop diuretics; potentially proton pump inhibitors
Other Associations
  • Aging (strongest)
  • Osteoarthritis
  • Post-meniscectomy or joint trauma
  • Familial-genetic (ANKH mutations)
  • Gout (crystals can coexist with MSU and apatite)
🔑 Workup for Young CPPD (<50 yrs)
Order: serum calcium, phosphorus, alkaline phosphatase, magnesium, iron, and transferrin. Also inquire for familial aggregation. Treating the underlying metabolic condition may prevent progression.

Clinical Manifestations

Acute CPP Crystal Arthritis (“Pseudogout”)
  • Knee most commonly affected (vs. first MTP in gout)
  • Wrist second; first MTP (podagra) rarely affected — key distinction from gout
  • Other joints: shoulder, ankle, elbow, hands
  • Initial episodes last longer than gout: up to weeks to months (vs. 1–2 weeks)
  • Systemic signs in up to 50% of attacks: fever (occasionally 40°C/104°F), chills, ↑ acute-phase reactants — must culture fluid to exclude infection
CPPD Triggers
  • Trauma
  • Severe medical illness
  • Surgery — especially parathyroidectomy: rapid ↓ calcium and magnesium → CPP crystal dissolution and shedding

Chronic CPPD Patterns

  • Pseudo-osteoarthritis (most common) — polyarticular arthritis resembling OA; affects atypical OA joints (MCP, wrist, elbow, shoulder, ankle)
  • Chronic symmetric synovitis resembling rheumatoid arthritis
  • Severe destructive disease resembling neuropathic arthritis
  • Intervertebral disk and ligament calcification with restricted spine mobility; Crowned dens syndrome
  • Spinal stenosis (most common in elderly)
  • Rarely: tumoral deposits of CPP in soft tissues

Diagnosis of CPPD

Synovial Fluid

Rhomboid or rod-like crystals. Weakly positively birefringent or nonbirefringent under polarized light. WBC: several thousand to 100,000/μL (mean ~24,000); predominant cell = neutrophil. CPP crystals may be seen inside tissue fragments, fibrin clots, and neutrophils.

Chondrocalcinosis on Imaging

Punctate and/or linear radiodense deposits within fibrocartilaginous joint menisci or articular hyaline cartilage. Also visible on ultrasound. Presumptive of CPPD in absence of effusion. Exception: chondrocalcinosis due to CaOx in chronic renal failure.

Always Rule Out

Infection when fever present (cultures essential). Infection in a joint with any microcrystalline process can cause crystal shedding and dual-source synovitis. CPP crystals can coexist with MSU and apatite.

Treatment of CPPD

⚠ Key Distinction from Gout
Unlike gout, there is NO effective way to remove CPP crystal deposits from cartilage and synovium — no equivalent of urate-lowering therapy exists for CPPD.
  • Acute attacks: Ice packs + rest; joint fluid aspiration + glucocorticoid injection; oral colchicine; NSAIDs; systemic glucocorticoids
  • Severe polyarticular attacks: IL-1β antagonist anakinra
  • Prophylaxis (frequent recurrent attacks): Daily colchicine
  • Chronic synovitis: NSAIDs + gastric protection; colchicine; low-dose glucocorticoids; hydroxychloroquine; methotrexate
  • Progressive destructive large-joint arthropathy: May require joint replacement

Calcium Apatite Deposition Disease

Pathogenesis

Apatite is the primary mineral of normal bone and teeth. Abnormal accumulation of basic calcium phosphates (largely carbonate-substituted apatite) occurs in: areas of tissue damage (dystrophic calcification), hypercalcemic/hyperparathyroid states (metastatic calcification), connective tissue diseases (calcinosis — systemic sclerosis, dermatomyositis), and chronic renal failure (hyperphosphatemia → tumoral calcinosis, calciphylaxis). 30–50% of OA patients have apatite microcrystals in synovial fluid. Apatite causes synovial lining cells to release PGE2, cytokines, collagenases, and neutral proteases → destructive potential.

Clinical Manifestations

Periarticular
  • Calcific periarthritis (e.g., supraspinatus tendon rupture)
  • Bursitis and tendinitis
  • Hydroxyapatite pseudopodagra; polyarticular involvement
  • Asymptomatic deposition (most common)
Articular — Milwaukee Shoulder

Hemorrhagic shoulder effusions in elderly — apatite aggregates in synovial fluid. Also hips, knees, erosive OA of fingers. Joint destruction from cartilage damage + supporting structure failure → instability, deformity. Symptoms range from minimal to severe pain → may require joint replacement.

Diagnosis

  • Synovial fluid WBC usually <2,000/μL despite dramatic symptoms (predominantly mononuclear)
  • Individual crystals only visible by electron microscopy
  • Clumps: 1–20 μm shiny nonbirefringent globules; stain purplish with Wright's stain, bright red with alizarin red S
  • Definitive: electron microscopy + energy-dispersive elemental analysis, x-ray diffraction, infrared spectroscopy, or Raman microspectroscopy (usually not required clinically)
⚠ Polarized Light Rule for Apatite
Apatite crystals are NONBIREFRINGENT — cannot be identified by polarized light microscopy. This is a key distinguishing feature.

Treatment (Nonspecific)

  • Acute bursitis/synovitis may be self-limiting (days to weeks)
  • NSAIDs or oral colchicine for 2 weeks, OR intra/periarticular depot glucocorticoid → shortens duration and intensity
  • Periarticular deposits may resorb with attack resolution
  • In renal failure on hemodialysis: agents to ↓ serum phosphate may lead to deposit resorption
  • Underlying severe destructive changes → poor response to medical therapy

Calcium Oxalate (CaOx) Deposition Disease

Pathogenesis

  • CaOx deposits in chronic renal disease (visceral organs, blood vessels, bones, cartilage)
  • Reported patients on long-term hemodialysis or peritoneal dialysis
  • Many received ascorbic acid supplements: ascorbic acid → oxalate → inadequately cleared in uremia and by dialysis
  • CaOx aggregates in bone, cartilage, synovium, periarticular tissues → crystal shedding → acute synovitis
  • Like apatite and CPP: persistent aggregates → ↑ synovial cell proliferation + enzyme release → progressive articular destruction
  • Primary oxalosis: rare hereditary metabolic disorder → acute or chronic CaOx arthritis, periarthritis, bone disease
⚠ Prevention in Dialysis Patients
Avoid ascorbic acid supplements and oxalate-rich foods in dialysis patients because of risk of enhancing hyperoxalosis and its sequelae.

Clinical Manifestations & Diagnosis

  • Features may not be distinguishable from MSU, CPP, or apatite arthritis
  • Joints: fingers, wrists, elbows, knees, ankles, feet
  • Radiographs may show chondrocalcinosis or soft tissue calcifications
  • Synovial fluid: usually noninflammatory (<2,000/μL) or mildly inflammatory
  • Bipyramidal crystals (most easily recognized) — strongly birefringent; stain with alizarin red S; variable shapes possible

Treatment

  • NSAIDs, colchicine, intraarticular glucocorticoids, and/or ↑ dialysis frequency → only slight improvement
  • Primary oxalosis: liver transplantation → significant reduction in crystal deposits

High-Yield Distinctions

Gout Pseudocellulitis Trap

Acute gout flare produces warm, red, tender, swollen joint that closely mimics cellulitis. Key distinguishing feature: this is arthritis (joint space), not a skin infection. Synovial fluid aspiration confirms.

Serum Urate During Acute Flare

Serum urate may be normal or low during an acute gout attack (IL-6 uricosuric effect, ~2 mg/dL reduction). Never use a normal SU to exclude gout acutely.

Gout vs. Pseudogout — Joint Location

Gout: first MTP (podagra) most common. Pseudogout: knee most common; first MTP rarely affected. Wrist is more characteristic of CPPD. Duration: CPPD attacks last longer (weeks–months vs. 1–2 weeks).

ULT Timing Rule

Never start ULT during an active flare (worsens via crystal shedding). Always cover ULT initiation with anti-inflammatory prophylaxis. Faster SU reduction = higher paradoxical flare risk.

HLA-B*5801 Alert

Screen Southeast Asian (non-Japanese), Pacific Islander/Native Hawaiian, and Black patients for HLA-B*5801 before prescribing allopurinol. Positive → use febuxostat. Starting low and titrating slowly also reduces SCAR risk.

Parathyroidectomy → CPPD Flare

Surgery (especially parathyroidectomy) → rapid ↓ calcium and magnesium → CPP crystal dissolution and shedding → acute pseudogout attack. Classic precipitant in boards questions.

Exam Traps — Board Coach Mode

Expert board exam coaching — Stage A Internal Medicine Boards. Each trap shows exactly how the question is framed, the tempting distractor, and the mechanism behind the correct answer.

Trap 01 Look-Alike — Gout vs. Cellulitis with Normal Serum Urate
A 52-year-old man with hypertension presents with sudden onset of severe pain, redness, warmth, and swelling of his right first metatarsophalangeal joint that began last night. The skin appears erythematous and shiny. Serum uric acid is 5.8 mg/dL (normal). He is afebrile. What is the most likely diagnosis?
Correct Answer: Acute gout flare (pseudocellulitis)
Classic podagra presentation (first MTP, nocturnal onset, dramatic local inflammation). Confirm by synovial fluid aspiration — needle-shaped MSU crystals, strongly negatively birefringent under compensated polarized light. Serum urate may be normal or low during an acute flare because inflammatory cytokines (especially IL-6) have uricosuric properties that lower SU by approximately 2 mg/dL.
⚠ Trap: Most students choose cellulitis because the joint looks infected OR dismiss gout because SU is "normal." The key: never use a normal serum urate to exclude gout during an acute attack. Always aspirate the joint to confirm.
Trap 02 Number Trap — SU Saturation vs. ULT Target
A patient with established gout has a serum urate of 6.4 mg/dL on allopurinol. His physician considers this acceptable since it is below the MSU saturation point of ~6.85 mg/dL. Is this target sufficient?
Correct Answer: No — target SU <5–6 mg/dL, not just below saturation
The therapeutic goal is to normalize serum urate to a sub-saturation point of <300–360 μmol/L (5.0–6.0 mg/dL) — deliberately set BELOW the crystallization threshold to drive dissolution of existing tophi and prevent new flares. A SU of 6.4 mg/dL is below saturation but above the therapeutic target; titrate allopurinol further.
⚠ Trap: Confusing the saturation point (~6.85 mg/dL) with the treatment target (<5–6 mg/dL). Sub-saturation therapy is required to actively dissolve existing MSU crystal deposits.
Trap 03 Timing Trap — Starting ULT During Active Flare
A 58-year-old man presents with an acute gout flare. His primary care physician wants to start allopurinol immediately to address his hyperuricemia and prevent future attacks while treating the flare with colchicine simultaneously. What is the risk?
Correct Answer: Do NOT initiate ULT during an active gout flare — wait until resolution
Rapidly lowering serum urate during a flare causes paradoxical crystal shedding from dissolving synovial tophi, worsening the acute attack. ULT should be started during the resolution phase or after a flare has completely resolved, with anti-inflammatory prophylaxis (colchicine 0.6 mg 1–2×/day or naproxen 250 mg BID) to cover the paradoxical flare risk. Faster urate reduction is associated with higher flare risk.
⚠ Trap: It seems logical to treat both the flare and the underlying cause simultaneously. The paradoxical flare mechanism (crystal shedding during rapid SU reduction) makes this approach counterproductive acutely.
Trap 04 Population Trap — HLA-B*5801 Before Allopurinol
A 58-year-old Korean man with gout requires urate-lowering therapy. Allopurinol is first-line. The resident starts it at 100 mg/day. What important step was missed?
Correct Answer: Screen for HLA-B*5801 before starting allopurinol in this population
Korean patients (and other Southeast Asian populations except Japanese descendants; Pacific Islander/Native Hawaiian; and Black patients) have a high carriage rate of HLA-B*5801, which dramatically increases risk of life-threatening severe cutaneous adverse reactions (SCARs) including Stevens-Johnson syndrome and toxic epidermal necrolysis. If positive → use febuxostat instead. Additional SCAR risk factors: CKD and initial dosing >100 mg/day in CKD patients.
⚠ Trap: Allopurinol is first-line and the dose seems standard — the HLA screening step is easily overlooked. Screening creates racial equity in preventing a rare but life-threatening adverse reaction.
Trap 05 Look-Alike — Gout vs. Pseudogout: Crystal Characteristics
An 80-year-old woman presents with acute, severe knee joint inflammation with fever of 38.9°C and elevated ESR/CRP. Synovial fluid shows 30,000 WBC/μL. Crystals are rectangular/rhomboid and weakly positively birefringent. Cultures are pending. What is the diagnosis?
Correct Answer: Acute CPP crystal arthritis (pseudogout) — but infection must be actively excluded
Rhomboid/rectangular shape + weak positive birefringence = CPP (not MSU). Knee is the most commonly affected joint in CPPD (vs. first MTP in gout). Up to 50% of CPPD attacks feature fever, occasionally reaching 40°C. However, infection in a joint with microcrystalline deposition causes crystal shedding and dual synovitis — always culture the fluid regardless of crystal identification.
⚠ Trap: Fever + high WBC + elevated inflammatory markers leads to the reflex diagnosis of septic arthritis. Crystal morphology is the key distinguisher, but both conditions must be considered simultaneously.
Trap 06 Drug Interaction Trap — Colchicine + Clarithromycin
A gout patient on colchicine 0.6 mg twice daily for prophylaxis develops a respiratory tract infection and is prescribed clarithromycin. He is otherwise healthy with normal renal and hepatic function. What is the critical concern?
Correct Answer: Clarithromycin inhibits CYP3A4 and P-glycoprotein → ↑ colchicine levels → colchicine toxicity
Colchicine is eliminated via P-glycoprotein (MDR1) in the liver, small intestine, and kidneys, AND via enteric/hepatic CYP3A4. Inhibiting either pathway increases colchicine levels. The colchicine dose must be reduced substantially, or the combination avoided — especially in patients with any degree of renal or hepatic impairment. Colchicine has a narrow therapeutic index.
⚠ Trap: Both drugs are commonly prescribed and the interaction is easily missed. Colchicine toxicity (myopathy, cytopenias, GI toxicity, neurotoxicity) can be severe. Tacrolimus and cyclosporine are other common P-gp/CYP3A4 inhibitors that trigger this interaction.
Trap 07 Mechanism Trap — Probenecid in Renal Impairment
A 64-year-old man with gout has a serum creatinine of 2.3 mg/dL (estimated GFR 28 mL/min). He cannot tolerate allopurinol. The team considers probenecid as his urate-lowering agent. What is wrong with this plan?
Correct Answer: Probenecid is ineffective when serum creatinine >177 μmol/L (2 mg/dL)
Uricosuric agents require adequate renal tubular function to block urate reabsorption (URAT1/OAT10) and increase urate excretion. With creatinine of 2.3 mg/dL, this patient is above the threshold where probenecid is effective. Appropriate alternatives: febuxostat (xanthine oxidase inhibitor that does not require dose adjustment in moderate-to-severe CKD) or pegloticase for refractory cases.
⚠ Trap: Probenecid seems like a reasonable alternative when allopurinol is not tolerated, but the renal threshold is a hard contraindication to efficacy — not just safety. The drug simply won't work.
Trap 08 Population Trap — Young CPPD (<50 yrs) Must Get Metabolic Workup
A 42-year-old man presents with acute inflammatory arthritis of the knee. Synovial fluid reveals rhomboid crystals with weak positive birefringence. The ED team treats him with NSAIDs and sends him home with a CPPD diagnosis. What critical next step is missing?
Correct Answer: Metabolic workup for underlying conditions causing CPPD in a patient under 50
CPPD in patients younger than 50 years should always prompt evaluation for: primary hyperparathyroidism, hemochromatosis, hypophosphatasia, hypomagnesemia, Gitelman's syndrome, and hereditary CPPD (ANKH mutations). Order: serum calcium, phosphorus, alkaline phosphatase, magnesium, iron, and transferrin. Inquire about familial aggregation. Treating the underlying metabolic condition may prevent progression.
⚠ Trap: Treating the acute attack is appropriate but the underlying cause is always missed without an active metabolic workup. CPPD in a 42-year-old is NOT just "early aging" — it mandates investigation.
Trap 09 Mechanism Trap — Apatite Crystals Invisible by Polarized Light
An 82-year-old woman presents with a hemorrhagic shoulder effusion. Synovial fluid WBC is 800/μL (mononuclear predominance). Polarized light microscopy shows no birefringent crystals. The resident concludes no crystal arthropathy is present. What is the most likely missed diagnosis?
Correct Answer: Calcium apatite deposition disease (Milwaukee shoulder)
Apatite crystals are nonbirefringent and cannot be seen by polarized light microscopy. They appear as 1–20 μm shiny globules staining purplish with Wright's stain and bright red with alizarin red S. Definitive identification requires electron microscopy with energy-dispersive elemental analysis. Milwaukee shoulder (hemorrhagic shoulder effusion in the elderly from apatite aggregates) classically has a LOW synovial WBC despite dramatic symptoms.
⚠ Trap: Negative polarized light = no crystals? Wrong. Apatite is the one crystal type that is completely invisible by polarized light. The low WBC + hemorrhagic effusion in an elderly patient with shoulder pain is the clinical signature of Milwaukee shoulder.
Trap 10 Special Population — Dialysis + Vitamin C + Bipyramidal Crystals = CaOx
A 66-year-old woman on long-term hemodialysis presents with acute wrist arthritis. She has been taking high-dose vitamin C supplements for several months at her family's suggestion. Synovial fluid shows bipyramidal, strongly birefringent crystals. What is the diagnosis and what change is needed?
Correct Answer: Calcium oxalate (CaOx) crystal arthritis — discontinue ascorbic acid supplements
In renal failure/dialysis patients, ascorbic acid (vitamin C) is metabolized to oxalate, which is inadequately cleared by uremia and dialysis → hyperoxalosis → CaOx crystal deposition in joints and periarticular tissues. Bipyramidal, strongly birefringent crystals on synovial fluid analysis are diagnostic. Ascorbic acid supplements and oxalate-rich foods must be strictly avoided in dialysis patients.
⚠ Trap: Vitamin C is perceived as harmless and beneficial, so families often recommend it. In dialysis patients, it is a direct cause of oxalate crystal arthropathy. The bipyramidal crystal shape (vs. rhomboid for CPP, needle for MSU) is the crystal identifier.
