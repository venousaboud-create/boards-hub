01

Pathophysiology of CKD

▾

Two Broad Mechanisms of Damage

CKD results from two converging pathways — an initiating disease-specific insult, followed by a universal maladaptive response that perpetuates injury regardless of the original cause.

Mechanism 1 — Disease Specific

Initiating injury specific to the underlying etiology:

  • Genetic abnormalities in renal development
  • Immune complex deposition and inflammation (glomerulonephritis)
  • Metabolic injury (hyperglycemia → diabetic nephropathy)
  • Microvascular perturbation (hypertension)
  • Toxin exposure affecting vascular, glomerular, or tubulointerstitial compartments
Mechanism 2 — Nonspecific Universal

Once nephron number falls, surviving nephrons undergo compensatory hyperfiltration and hypertrophy — initially helpful, ultimately maladaptive:

  • Increased intraglomerular pressure → distortion of glomerular architecture
  • Abnormal podocyte function → disruption of filtration barrier
  • Sclerosis and dropout of remaining nephrons
  • Mediated by vasoactive hormones, cytokines, and growth factors

The RAS and Tubuloglomerular Feedback Loop

Increased intrarenal activity of the renin-angiotensin system (RAS), together with reduced tubuloglomerular feedback, contributes both to initial compensatory hyperfiltration AND to the subsequent maladaptive hypertrophy and sclerosis. This is the mechanistic basis for why a reduction in nephron number may lead to progressive decline in kidney function over many years — even after the initial injury resolves.

🔑 Core Concept
The short-term adaptations of hyperfiltration and hypertrophy become maladaptive over time — increased pressure and flow within the nephron predisposes to glomerular scarring and further nephron dropout. This self-perpetuating cycle explains why CKD tends to progress even after the original insult is removed.

Risk Factors & Staging

▾

Risk Factors for CKD

Noncommunicable Diseases
  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Increased BMI / sedentary lifestyle
  • Autoinflammatory disease (lupus, vasculitis, cancer immunotherapy)
  • Nephrotoxic exposures (antineoplastic agents)
  • Hypertension (risk factor, cause, OR consequence)
Communicable Diseases
  • Streptococcal infection
  • Mycobacterial infection
  • HIV infection (HIVAN)
  • SARS-CoV-2
  • HBV, HCV
Genetic Factors
  • Monogenic inheritance — high OR low/medium penetrance
  • Polygenic risk factors
  • APOL1 variants (sub-Saharan African ancestry) — non-Mendelian, odds ratio >2
  • Accounts for up to 20% of adult-onset CKD
Childhood-Related
  • Premature and SGA birth
  • Persistent asymptomatic microscopic hematuria
  • Childhood kidney disease (even if resolved)
  • Treated childhood cancer
Demographic / Other
  • Age, male sex
  • Population ancestry
  • Family history of kidney disease
  • Prior acute kidney injury
  • Preeclampsia
  • Kidney donation or other nephrectomy
  • Tobacco use
✅ Monitoring
Adults with risk factors should be monitored yearly for: urinary albumin excretion, decline in eGFR, and blood pressure.

Staging of CKD — GFR + Albuminuria

CKD staging uses both GFR and urinary albumin excretion rate (UACR) — together these predict risk of all-cause mortality, cardiovascular mortality, and CKD progression.

⚠ Age-Related GFR Decline
Normal annual GFR decline is ~1 mL/min/year after the 3rd decade (peak GFR ~120). Mean GFR at age 70 = ~70 mL/min. Even a mild serum creatinine elevation in an elderly woman may represent a GFR <50 mL/min — never dismiss a "normal" creatinine without calculating eGFR.

eGFR Estimation

GFR is estimated (eGFR) using serum creatinine + patient parameters. The two recommended equations:

MDRD Equation

eGFR = 1.86 × (SCr)⁻¹·¹⁵⁴ × (age)⁻⁰·²⁰³
× 0.742 if female

Race multiplier (1.21 for African ancestry) currently under review

CKD-EPI Equation

GFR = 141 × min(SCr/κ,1)^α × max(SCr/κ,1)⁻¹·²⁰⁹ × 0.993^Age × 1.018 (female)

κ=0.7 female, 0.9 male; α=−0.329 female, −0.411 male. Race multiplier under review.

🔑 eGFR Validity Rule
eGFR is only valid in steady state — serum creatinine must be neither rising nor falling over days. New race-free equations are recommended. Cystatin-C can be used when precise GFR is needed for dosing decisions.

Albuminuria (UACR)

Urinary albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR) in spot first-morning urine has replaced 24-hour collections. Persistently elevated UACR (>2.5 mg/mmol male, >3.5 mg/mmol female) on 2–3 occasions marks both primary kidney disease and systemic microvascular disease — even with a negative conventional dipstick.

💜 Kidney Failure Risk Equation
Available at www.kidneyfailurerisk.com. Uses: age, sex, region (North American vs. non–North American), GFR, and UACR. Validated globally; risk appears higher in North America.

Genetics of CKD

Up to 20% of adult-onset CKD has a genetic basis. Key monogenic causes include:

Most Common Mendelian Causes
  • ADPKD: ADPKD1, ADPKD2, IFT140, GANAB, DNAJB2, ALG9
  • Type IV collagen nephropathy (Alport): COL4A3, COL4A4, COL4A5
  • Autosomal dominant tubulointerstitial kidney disease: UMOD, MUC1, HNF1B
  • Nephronophthisis: NPHP genes
APOL1 — Non-Mendelian High-Risk
  • Sub-Saharan African ancestry; odds ratio >2
  • Associated with: FSGS, HIV nephropathy, SARS-CoV-2 nephropathy, CKD with HTN, lupus nephritis
  • Arose as evolutionary protection against tropical pathogens
  • Requires acquired "second hit" (e.g., elevated IFN-γ) to trigger disease
  • Specific APOL1 inhibitor: Inaxaplin — reduces proteinuria in FSGS with high-risk genotype
🔭 When to Consider Genetic Testing
Family history of unexplained kidney failure; parental consanguinity; extrarenal features (dysmorphic features, hearing/vision loss, autism spectrum); FSGS; steroid-resistant nephrotic syndrome; young-onset unexplained progressive CKD; cystic kidney disease.

Etiology & Epidemiology

▾

Prevalence

  • ≥6% of US adults have CKD stages 1–2
  • 4.5% of US adults have CKD stages 3–4
  • Projected global years of life lost (YLL) due to CKD: ~26 million (2016) → 52.5 million (2040)
  • CKD predicted to rise from 16th to 5th in global YLL rankings by 2040

Leading Etiologies (accounting for >90% worldwide)

EtiologyKey Points
Diabetic NephropathyMost common in North America & Europe; usually type 2 DM
GlomerulonephritisVariable geographic contribution; includes IgA, membranous, FSGS
Hypertension-Associated CKDIncludes vascular/ischemic kidney disease and primary glomerulopathy with secondary HTN. Note: some "hypertensive CKD" may actually be subclinical primary glomerulopathy (FSGS/global glomerulosclerosis) causing the HTN
ADPKDMost common Mendelian CKD; gene-specific therapy now available
Other Cystic & Tubulointerstitial NephropathyIncludes nephronophthisis, medullary cystic disease
⚠ Hypertension — Cause or Consequence?
When no primary glomerular/tubulointerstitial process is evident, CKD is often attributed to hypertension. However, some patients may have a subclinical primary glomerulopathy (FSGS) where elevated blood pressure is actually a consequence of the kidney disease, not the cause.

Pathophysiology & Biochemistry of Uremia

▾

What Is Uremia?

Uremia is the syndrome of symptoms, signs, and laboratory disturbances resulting from reduced kidney function. Urea and creatinine rise with reduced excretory capacity, but these do not themselves cause the uremic syndrome — they are merely measurable but incomplete surrogate markers for retained toxins.

🚨 Board Pearl
Monitoring urea and creatinine alone in a patient with impaired kidney function represents a "vast oversimplification" of the uremic state. Large numbers of water-soluble, hydrophobic, protein-bound, charged, and uncharged nitrogen-containing compounds accumulate when GFR declines.

Three Spheres of Uremic Dysfunction

Sphere 1 — Toxin Accumulation

Retained solutes that normally undergo renal excretion. Includes water-soluble, protein-bound, charged/uncharged nitrogenous metabolites. PTH itself is considered a uremic toxin.

Sphere 2 — Loss of Kidney Functions

Beyond excretion: loss of fluid/electrolyte homeostasis, erythropoietin production, calcitriol synthesis, insulin degradation, hormone regulation (PTH, FGF-23, sex hormones, prolactin).

Sphere 3 — Systemic Inflammation

Elevated CRP and acute-phase reactants; decreased albumin and fetuin (negative acute-phase reactants). Drives the malnutrition-inflammation-atherosclerosis/calcification syndrome → accelerated vascular disease.

05

Fluid, Electrolyte & Acid-Base Disorders

▾

Sodium and Water Homeostasis

Many forms of kidney disease disrupt sodium balance so that dietary intake exceeds excretion → sodium retention → extracellular fluid volume (ECFV) expansion. ECFV expansion contributes to hypertension, which accelerates nephron hyperfiltration and injury.

  • Isonatremic ECFV expansion is the typical result (water intake does not exceed renal clearance capacity)
  • Hyponatremia is uncommon in CKD; when present → water restriction
  • ECFV depletion (from GI loss, overzealous diuretics) → prerenal component → acute-on-chronic renal failure
  • "Sick day rule": Patients on diuretics should hold them if experiencing vomiting/diarrhea until resuming normal intake
🔑 Treatment of Sodium Excess
  • Salt restriction (first line)
  • Loop diuretics (furosemide, bumetanide, torsemide) — higher doses needed in CKD due to diuretic resistance
  • Thiazide (chlorthalidone) — shown to reduce blood pressure even in stage 4 CKD
  • Loop + metolazone combination if resistant
  • Intractable edema + hypertension → indication to initiate dialysis

Potassium Homeostasis

GFR decline is NOT always paralleled by a decline in urinary potassium excretion, because potassium secretion is predominantly mediated by aldosterone-dependent mechanisms in the distal nephron. Additionally, augmented GI potassium excretion serves as a backup defense.

Causes of Hyperkalemia in CKD

  • Increased dietary potassium intake
  • Hemolysis; transfusion of stored red blood cells
  • Metabolic acidosis (shifts K⁺ out of cells)
  • Medications: RAS inhibitors, spironolactone, amiloride, eplerenone, triamterene, nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists
  • Conditions with hyporeninemic hypoaldosteronism: diabetes mellitus, obstructive uropathy, sickle cell nephropathy — cause disproportionate hyperkalemia relative to GFR decline
✅ Treatment of Hyperkalemia
  • Dietary potassium restriction
  • Kaliuretic diuretics (promote urinary K⁺ excretion)
  • Potassium-binding resins: calcium resonium, sodium polystyrene, patiromer, calcium zirconium cyclosilicate (newer agents)
  • Dose adjustment/avoidance of potassium-retaining medications
  • Gliflozins (SGLT2i) — counterbalancing effects on potassium handling → net preservation of K⁺ homeostasis
  • Intractable hyperkalemia → dialysis indication (uncommon)
💜 RAS Inhibitor Dilemma
Despite the risk of hyperkalemia, the benefits of RAS inhibitors in slowing CKD progression and reducing cardiovascular complications usually outweigh risks — use cautiously with close potassium monitoring. Co-administration of potassium-lowering agents (patiromer) can enable continued RAS inhibitor use.

Metabolic Acidosis

Common in CKD. Kidneys can still acidify the urine but produce less ammonia → cannot excrete sufficient protons for most diets.

Declining GFR → Reduced ammonia production
↓
Early CKD: Non-anion-gap (hyperchloremic) metabolic acidosis
↓
Advanced CKD: Anion retention → Anion-gap metabolic acidosis added
  • pH rarely <7.32; usually mild
  • Diabetic nephropathy and tubulointerstitial disease (obstructive uropathy) → non-anion-gap + hyperkalemia at earlier stages
  • Even modest metabolic acidosis → protein catabolism + CKD progression
✅ Treatment
Alkali supplementation (sodium bicarbonate) when serum HCO₃⁻ falls below 20–23 mmol/L. Studies suggest bicarbonate supplementation in stages 3–5 CKD is associated with slower progression to dialysis. Account for sodium load when ECFV expansion is present.
⚠ Hyperkalemia Worsens Acidosis
Hyperkalemia further depresses ammonia production → worsens acid-base balance. This explains the common pairing of hyperkalemia + metabolic acidosis in CKD, especially diabetic nephropathy.

Disorders of Calcium, Phosphate & Bone

▾

Pathophysiology of Secondary Hyperparathyroidism

Changes begin when GFR falls below 60 mL/min, through a series of interrelated mechanisms:

↓ GFR → ↓ Phosphate excretion → Phosphate retention
↓
↑ FGF-23 (by osteocytes) + ↑ PTH + Parathyroid gland hypertrophy
↓
FGF-23 → ↓ Calcitriol production + ↑ Calcitriol degradation
↓
↓ Calcitriol → Hypocalcemia → More PTH (direct + via Ca-sensing receptor)
Loss of Klotho-dependent FGF-23 inhibition of PTH
↓
Secondary Hyperparathyroidism → High-Turnover Bone Disease
🚨 FGF-23 — Independent Cardiovascular Risk
Elevated FGF-23 is an independent risk factor for left ventricular hypertrophy and is associated with increased mortality in CKD, dialysis, and kidney transplant patients.

Types of CKD Bone Disease

High-Turnover Bone Disease (Osteitis Fibrosa Cystica)

Cause: Secondary hyperparathyroidism (elevated PTH)

Histology: Abnormal osteoid, bone marrow fibrosis, bone cysts ("brown tumors" due to hemorrhage)

Clinical: Bone pain, fragility, brown tumors with compression syndromes, resistance to erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (bone marrow fibrosis). PTH itself causes muscle weakness + cardiac fibrosis.

Adynamic Bone Disease

Cause: Excessive PTH suppression, chronic inflammation. More common in diabetics and older patients.

Mechanism: Reduced bone volume and mineralization; inability to repair normal microfractures

Complications: ↑ Fractures, bone pain, ↑ vascular and cardiac calcification, tumoral calcinosis (calcium deposits in soft tissue)

Note: Most severe musculoskeletal pain often comes from this type

Osteomalacia

Cause: Reduced production and action of 1,25(OH)₂D₃ (calcitriol)

Result: Accumulation of nonmineralized osteoid

Distinct from adynamic disease and osteitis fibrosa

Cardiovascular Calcification

  • Strong association between hyperphosphatemia and cardiovascular mortality
  • CKD patients have coronary artery and heart valve calcification orders of magnitude greater than general population
  • Proportional to age and hyperphosphatemia; associated with low PTH and low bone turnover
  • Mechanism: Ingested calcium cannot be incorporated into low-turnover bone → deposits in vascular bed
  • Hyperphosphatemia induces vascular smooth muscle cells to adopt an osteoblast-like phenotype → vascular ossification

Calciphylaxis

Devastating condition, nearly exclusive to advanced CKD. Heralded by painful livedo reticularis → subcutaneous nodules → patches of ischemic necrosis (especially on legs, thighs, abdomen, breasts). Pathology: vascular occlusion + extensive calcification.

  • Originally linked to severe Ca/P dysregulation + advanced hyperparathyroidism
  • Increasingly seen without severe hyperparathyroidism
  • Warfarin is a risk factor: decreases vitamin K–dependent activation of matrix GLA protein (prevents vascular calcification) → if calciphylaxis develops, discontinue warfarin and use alternative anticoagulation

Treatment of Ca/Phos Disorders

Phosphate-Binding Agents (taken with meals)
  • Calcium acetate / calcium carbonate: Effective but risk hypercalcemia (especially in low-turnover bone disease)
  • Sevelamer: Non-calcium polymer; does NOT cause hypercalcemia; may attenuate vascular calcification
  • Lanthanum: Non-calcium polymer; similar benefits to sevelamer
  • Tenapanor: Sodium-proton inhibitor that decreases GI phosphate absorption
💜 Pearl
Sevelamer and lanthanum preferred when hypercalcemia is a concern or in adynamic bone disease.
Vitamin D & Calcimimetics
  • Calcitriol: Directly suppresses PTH; risk of hypercalcemia and hyperphosphatemia via increased GI absorption
  • Paricalcitol: Calcitriol analogue; suppresses PTH with less hypercalcemia
  • Cinacalcet / Etelcalcetide: Calcimimetics — enhance Ca-sensing receptor sensitivity in parathyroid gland → dose-dependent reduction in PTH and plasma calcium
🔑 PTH Target
KDOQI guidelines: PTH target = 2–9× upper limit of normal. Very low PTH → adynamic bone disease + ectopic calcification risk.

Cardiovascular Abnormalities

▾

Overview

Cardiovascular disease is the leading cause of morbidity and mortality at every stage of CKD. Incremental cardiovascular risk vs. age/sex-matched population: 10- to 200-fold depending on stage. Most patients with CKD die of cardiovascular disease before reaching stage 5. Of those who reach stage 5, 30–45% already have advanced cardiovascular complications.

Vascular Disease — Traditional + CKD-Specific Risk Factors

Traditional ("Classic") Risk Factors
  • Hypertension
  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Hypervolemia
  • Dyslipidemia
  • Sympathetic overactivity
  • Hyperhomocysteinemia
CKD-Specific (Nontraditional) Risk Factors
  • Anemia
  • Hyperphosphatemia
  • Hyperparathyroidism
  • Increased FGF-23
  • Sleep apnea
  • Systemic inflammation (↓ fetuin → ↑ vascular calcification)
⚠ Cardiac Troponin in CKD
Frequently elevated in CKD without acute ischemia. This complicates the diagnosis of acute MI. Use serial measurements and trends over hours — a single elevated level is not diagnostic. Consistently elevated levels = independent prognostic factor for adverse CV events.

Heart Failure in CKD

  • Myocardial ischemia + LV hypertrophy + diastolic dysfunction + cardiomyopathy + salt/water retention → heart failure
  • Can occur with preserved (diastolic) or reduced (systolic) ejection fraction
  • "Low-pressure" pulmonary edema in advanced CKD: "bat wing" distribution on CXR, responds to dialysis (increased alveolar capillary permeability in uremic state)
  • AV fistula for hemodialysis → high cardiac output → high-output heart failure
  • Advanced malnutrition-inflammation state can reverse classic risk factors (HTN, hyperlipidemia) → reduced LV function, weight loss, poor prognosis

Hypertension Management in CKD

🔑 Blood Pressure Target
<130/80 mmHg in CKD patients with diabetes OR proteinuria >1g/24h, if achievable without prohibitive adverse effects.
ACE Inhibitors / ARBs — First-Line for Proteinuric CKD

Mechanism

Slow CKD decline beyond blood pressure reduction alone. Reduce intraglomerular hypertension by disproportionately reducing efferent arteriolar vasoconstriction → ↓ intraglomerular filtration pressure.

Key Considerations

  • Never combine ACE inhibitor + ARB — greater AKI and cardiac event risk
  • GFR drop up to 30% of baseline is acceptable and reflects effective glomerular unloading (tolerate if stable)
  • Can be continued even at eGFR <30 mL/min with monitoring — discontinuation associated with ↑ risk of death and CV events (but lower risk of starting RRT)
  • Risk of hyperkalemia — co-administer potassium-lowering agents (patiromer) if needed
  • Can precipitate AKI in ischemic renovascular disease (especially in combination)
SGLT2 Inhibitors (Gliflozins) — Emerging Cornerstone

Mechanism (see tubuloglomerular feedback)

Inhibit SGLT2 in the proximal tubule → ↓ Na⁺ reabsorption → ↑ Na⁺ delivery to macula densa → afferent arteriolar constriction (restored tubuloglomerular feedback) → ↓ intraglomerular pressure. Also: direct proximal tubule cytoprotection.

  • Indicated in both diabetic and nondiabetic kidney disease to slow GFR decline
  • Reduces major cardiovascular events in CKD including stage 4
  • Counterbalancing effects on potassium homeostasis
  • Net preservation of K⁺ balance allows RAS inhibitor co-use
💜 Clinical Pearl
GLP-1 receptor agonists have also been shown to reduce major cardiovascular events and CKD in at-risk patients.
🔭 Calcium Channel Blockers
Among CCBs, diltiazem and verapamil may have superior antiproteinuric and renoprotective effects compared to dihydropyridines (amlodipine, nifedipine). However, all can be useful for systemic hypertension control in conditions like ADPKD where intraglomerular hypertension is less prominent.

Pericardial Disease

  • Chest pain with respiratory accentuation + friction rub = uremic pericarditis
  • ECG: PR-interval depression + diffuse ST elevation
  • Observed in advanced uremia; less common with timely dialysis; now mainly in underdialyzed nonadherent patients
🚨 Treatment
Uremic pericarditis = absolute indication for urgent initiation of dialysis (or intensification if already on dialysis). Because of hemorrhage propensity, hemodialysis should be performed without heparin. Consider drainage for recurrent effusion or tamponade signs.

Hematologic Abnormalities

▾

Anemia of CKD

Normocytic, normochromic anemia evident as early as stage 3; nearly universal by stage 4. Primary cause: insufficient erythropoietin (EPO) production by the diseased kidney.

Cause of AnemiaMechanism
EPO deficiencyDiseased kidney cannot produce sufficient EPO
Reduced RBC lifespanUremic toxins damage red cell membrane
Bleeding diathesisUremic platelet dysfunction → chronic blood loss
Iron deficiencyPoor dietary absorption + GI blood loss
Hyperparathyroidism / bone marrow fibrosisInhibits erythropoiesis; resistance to ESA
Chronic inflammationInflammatory cytokines inhibit erythropoiesis
Folate / B12 deficiencyPoor intake or losses
ComorbiditiesHypothyroidism, HIV, autoimmune disease, immunosuppressives

Consequences of Anemia

  • Decreased tissue oxygen delivery; increased cardiac output; ventricular dilation + hypertrophy
  • Fatigue, reduced exercise tolerance, angina, heart failure
  • Decreased cognition and mental acuity
  • Impaired host defense against infection
  • Growth restriction in children

Treatment of Anemia

Erythropoiesis-Stimulating Agents (ESAs)

Recombinant EPO has transformed CKD care — eliminated regular transfusions, reducing transfusion infections, iron overload, and allosensitization (critical for future transplant).

Iron Supplementation (Essential)

  • Must ensure adequate iron stores before starting ESA
  • Demand by marrow often exceeds available iron (check transferrin saturation + ferritin)
  • Oral iron for pre-dialysis or peritoneal dialysis patients (if GI tolerated)
  • IV iron infusion if GI intolerance — note: ↑ susceptibility to bacterial infection
  • Also ensure adequate B12 and folate

ESA Targets

Normalization of hemoglobin NOT beneficial in CKD. Current target: Hgb 100–115 g/L. Higher targets associated with ↑ stroke risk (type 2 DM) and thromboembolic events.

💜 ESA Resistance — Think About
Acute or chronic inflammation, inadequate dialysis, severe hyperparathyroidism (bone marrow fibrosis), chronic blood loss/hemolysis, infection, or malignancy.
HIF Prolyl-Hydroxylase Inhibitors (New Class)

Inhibit endogenous hypoxia-inducible factor (HIF) degradation → ↑ endogenous EPO production + ↑ GI iron absorption. Similar effectiveness to ESAs in trials, but some cardiovascular, retinal, and tumor vasculogenesis concerns remain under investigation.

Abnormal Hemostasis

CKD causes both a bleeding tendency AND a thrombotic tendency — a unique coexistence.

Bleeding Tendency
  • Prolonged bleeding time
  • ↓ Activity of platelet factor III
  • Abnormal platelet aggregation and adhesiveness
  • Impaired prothrombin consumption
  • Clinical: easy bruising, prolonged surgical bleeding, menorrhagia, GI bleeding

Treatment: DDAVP (desmopressin), cryoprecipitate, IV conjugated estrogens, blood transfusions, ESA therapy, tranexamic acid (severe). Optimal dialysis corrects prolonged bleeding time.

Thrombotic Tendency
  • Greater susceptibility to thromboembolism
  • Especially if nephrotic-range proteinuria → hypoalbuminemia + renal loss of anticoagulant factors → thrombophilic state

Anticoagulation nuances: Warfarin for AFib must be individualized — greater bleeding risk + calciphylaxis risk. Low-molecular-weight heparin may need dose adjustment (monitor factor Xa). Unfractionated heparin preferred in hospitalized patients. Apixaban increasingly used in CKD/dialysis. Most new oral anticoagulants are renally eliminated.

Neuromuscular, GI, Endocrine & Dermatologic Manifestations

▾

Neuromuscular Abnormalities

CNS, peripheral, and autonomic neuropathy plus muscle abnormalities are well-recognized. Subtle manifestations begin at stage 3 CKD.

CNS Complications
  • Early (stage 3): Mild memory disturbances, concentration difficulty, sleep disturbance
  • Later: Neuromuscular irritability — hiccups, cramps, twitching
  • Advanced (untreated): Asterixis, myoclonus, seizures, coma
Peripheral Neuropathy (evident at stage 4)
  • Sensory > motor involvement
  • Lower extremities > upper
  • Distal > proximal
  • Restless leg syndrome: Ill-defined discomfort in legs/feet, relieved by movement
  • Peripheral neuropathy without another cause (e.g., diabetes) = indication for renal replacement therapy

Gastrointestinal & Nutritional Abnormalities

  • Uremic fetor: Urine-like breath odor from urea breakdown → ammonia in saliva; often accompanied by metallic taste (dysgeusia)
  • Gastritis, peptic disease, mucosal ulcerations at any GI level → abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, GI bleeding
  • Constipation (worsened by calcium and iron supplements)
  • Uremic toxin retention → anorexia, nausea, vomiting
  • Protein-energy malnutrition is common in advanced CKD — often indication for RRT initiation
  • Metabolic acidosis + inflammatory cytokines → protein catabolism
⚠ Protein Restriction
May reduce nausea/vomiting but risks malnutrition. Should only be done in consultation with a registered dietitian specializing in CKD.

Endocrine & Metabolic Disturbances

Glucose Metabolism
  • Glucose intolerance common but fasting glucose usually normal or only slightly elevated
  • ↓ Renal degradation of insulin → elevated plasma insulin levels; insulin dose reduction often needed as CKD worsens
  • Metformin contraindicated when GFR < ~half of normal
  • Sulfonylureas contraindicated when GFR < normal/2
  • Gliptins require dose reduction in renal failure
  • SGLT2 inhibitors: Stabilize GFR via TGF loop correction; major renoprotective and CV benefit
Reproductive & Hormonal
  • Women: Low estrogen, menstrual abnormalities, infertility, ↑ spontaneous abortion. At GFR ~40 mL/min → only ~20% of pregnancies lead to live births; pregnancy may hasten CKD progression
  • Men: Reduced testosterone, sexual dysfunction, oligospermia
  • Adolescents: Delayed sexual maturation even on dialysis
  • Many abnormalities improve with intensive dialysis or successful transplantation

Dermatologic Abnormalities

  • Pruritus: Common, one of most vexing uremic manifestations. First line: rule out unrelated skin disorders (scabies), treat hyperphosphatemia. Treatments: moisturizers, mild topical glucocorticoids, oral antihistamines, UV radiation. Kappa opioid receptor agonists reduce pruritus intensity and improve quality of life.
  • Pigmentation: Increased in advanced CKD — deposition of retained urochromes
  • Nephrogenic fibrosing dermopathy: Progressive subcutaneous induration on arms and legs; seen in CKD patients exposed to gadolinium-based MRI contrast. CKD stage 3 → minimize gadolinium exposure; stages 4–5 → avoid unless medically necessary. Rapid hemodialysis after exposure may mitigate risk.

Evaluation & Diagnosis of CKD

▾

History — Key Elements

  • CKD symptoms are often subtle or absent until renal failure supervenes — patients are frequently surprised by the diagnosis
  • Pain in the kidney region and decreased urine volume are NOT typical features
  • History of hypertension, diabetes, abnormal urinalyses, preeclampsia, early pregnancy loss
  • Drug history: NSAIDs, COX-2 inhibitors, antimicrobials, chemotherapy, antiretrovirals, proton pump inhibitors, phosphate-containing bowel cathartics, lithium
  • Uremic symptoms: appetite, weight loss, nausea, hiccups, peripheral edema, cramps, pruritus, restless legs
  • Family history of kidney disease + manifestations in auditory/visual/integumentary → consider heritable CKD (Alport's, Fabry's, cystinosis)

Physical Examination

  • Blood pressure + target organ damage from hypertension
  • Fundoscopy especially important in diabetics (diabetic retinopathy = associated with diabetic nephropathy)
  • Edema, sensory polyneuropathy
  • Asterixis or pericardial friction rub (not attributable to other causes) = uremic syndrome is present

Laboratory Workup

  • Serum and urine protein electrophoresis (all patients >35 with unexplained CKD + anemia → rule out multiple myeloma)
  • If hematuria/proteinuria: test for lupus, hepatitis B and C, HIV
  • Serial renal function measurements → confirm chronicity, pace of deterioration
  • Ca, phosphorus, PTH → metabolic bone disease
  • Hemoglobin, iron, B12, folate
  • 24-hour urine collection (or UACR): protein >300 mg → indication for ACE inhibitor, ARB, SGLT2 inhibitors; associated with higher progression risk

Imaging

Renal ultrasound is the most useful imaging study:

  • Verifies two kidneys, symmetry, size, masses, obstruction
  • Bilaterally small kidneys → CKD of long-standing duration
  • Normal-sized kidneys despite CKD: exceptions are diabetic nephropathy (enlarged at onset), amyloidosis, HIV nephropathy
  • Discrepancy >1 cm in length → consider unilateral developmental abnormality or renovascular disease
  • ADPKD: Enlarged kidneys with multiple cysts
🚨 Contrast Caution
IV or intraarterial contrast dye should be avoided in CKD (especially diabetic nephropathy). When unavoidable: avoid hypovolemia, minimize dye load, use least nephrotoxic preparation, consider sodium bicarbonate ± N-acetylcysteine (evidence less robust). Gadolinium: minimize in stage 3; avoid in stages 4–5 unless medically necessary.

Kidney Biopsy

Contraindicated with bilaterally small kidneys (technically difficult, high bleeding risk, too much scarring to yield diagnosis, window for disease-specific therapy has passed).

Other contraindications: uncontrolled hypertension, active UTI, bleeding diathesis (including ongoing anticoagulation), severe obesity.

  • Preferred approach: ultrasound-guided percutaneous
  • If indicated (suspicion of concomitant active process): measure bleeding time first; if elevated → give desmopressin immediately before procedure
  • Biopsy is NOT always indicated — in a patient with 15–20 years of type 1 DM + retinopathy + nephrotic proteinuria + no hematuria, diabetic nephropathy is very likely → biopsy unnecessary

Distinguishing CKD from AKI

🔑 Critical Initial Step
Distinguish newly diagnosed CKD from acute or subacute renal failure — the latter may respond to targeted therapy. Previous serum creatinine measurements are key: normal creatinine in recent months → consider reversible process; elevated creatinine in past → chronic process. Even confirmed CKD may have a superimposed acute process.

Treatment & Slowing CKD Progression

▾

Framework of Therapy

CKD therapy divides into: (1) interventions directed at the specific etiology, and (2) interventions that attenuate the common pathway of glomerular hyperfiltration. Fortunately, the same interventions that slow CKD also reduce cardiovascular complications.

🔑 Emergent Etiology-Directed Therapies
  • ADPKD: Genome-specific therapies (tolvaptan)
  • Type 1 hyperoxaluria: siRNA therapy (lumasiran)
  • APOL1-mediated kidney disease (AMKD): Inaxaplin — specific inhibitor of APOL1 channel function; decreases proteinuria in FSGS with high-risk APOL1 genotype

Slowing CKD Progression — Key Interventions

⚠ Monitor for Reversible Acceleration of CKD Decline
Any accelerated rate of GFR decline should prompt search for: ECFV depletion, uncontrolled HTN, UTI, new obstruction, nephrotoxin exposure (NSAIDs, radiocontrast), flare of underlying disease (lupus, vasculitis).

Medication Dose Adjustment in CKD

  • Loading dose: usually not affected by CKD (renal elimination not used in calculation)
  • Maintenance doses: require adjustment for many drugs — reduced dosage or extended intervals
  • Avoid: metformin (contraindicated when GFR <~30–45), meperidine, most sulfonylureas, NSAIDs/COX-2 inhibitors
  • Many antibiotics, antihypertensives, antiarrhythmics require dose adjustment
  • Online resources: http://www.globalrph.com/index_renal.htm

Preparation for Renal Replacement Therapy (RRT)

Clear Indications for RRT Initiation

  • Anorexia and nausea not attributable to reversible causes
  • Evidence of malnutrition
  • Fluid/electrolyte abnormalities refractory to other measures: hyperkalemia or ECFV overload
  • Uremic pericarditis (absolute indication — see cardiovascular section)
  • Peripheral neuropathy without another cause
  • Encephalopathy (now a late indication with timely dialysis)
🔑 Timing of RRT
Do NOT use an arbitrary urea or creatinine level to determine timing. Patients may be accustomed to chronic uremia and deny symptoms, then feel dramatically better with dialysis. Early preparation avoids problems with vascular access (fistula) or peritoneal catheter malfunction.

Patient Education — Start No Later Than Stage 4

  • Hemodialysis (in-center and home-based)
  • Peritoneal dialysis
  • Kidney transplantation
  • Nondialytic conservative care
  • Patients with education are more likely to choose home-based therapy (less expensive; associated with improved quality of life)

Kidney Transplantation

Offers the best potential for complete rehabilitation — dialysis replaces only a small fraction of kidney's filtration function and none of its endocrine/anti-inflammatory functions. Preemptive transplantation (from living donor) is preferred over transplanting after a period of dialysis when renal failure is irreversible.

High-Yield Distinctions

ACE Inhibitor Alone vs. Combination ACEi + ARB

ACE inhibitor OR ARB — recommended for proteinuric CKD. NEVER combine ACE inhibitor + ARB — demonstrated greater AKI and adverse cardiac events from combination therapy.

Calcium-Containing vs. Non-Calcium Phosphate Binders

Calcium acetate/carbonate: Risk of hypercalcemia (especially in low-turnover/adynamic bone disease). Sevelamer/lanthanum: No hypercalcemia risk; may attenuate vascular calcification — preferred when hypercalcemia is a concern.

ESA Use — Normalized Hgb Target?

Do NOT normalize hemoglobin in CKD — shown NOT beneficial and may increase stroke risk (type 2 DM) and thromboembolic events. Target: Hgb 100–115 g/L.

Warfarin in CKD

Warfarin decreases matrix GLA protein activation → risk of vascular calcification and calciphylaxis. In CKD patients requiring anticoagulation, warfarin avoidance is preferred; apixaban increasingly used. Warfarin for AFib: individualize — bleeding risk must be weighed.

Hypertension as Cause vs. Consequence

Hypertension in CKD may be the CAUSE (essential hypertension → nephrosclerosis) OR the CONSEQUENCE (primary glomerulopathy such as FSGS → secondary HTN). A patient labeled as "hypertensive CKD" may actually have primary FSGS.

SGLT2 Inhibitors — Beyond Diabetes

Gliflozins are now indicated for CKD protection in BOTH diabetic and nondiabetic kidney disease, and even in stage 4 CKD. They counterbalance RAS inhibitor–induced hyperkalemia.

Exam Traps

▾

Expert board exam coaching — Stage A Internal Medicine Boards. Each trap shows exactly how the question is framed and the reasoning to the correct answer.

Trap 01The "Normal Creatinine" in an Elderly Woman
An 82-year-old woman is found to have a serum creatinine of 1.0 mg/dL, which is listed as "within normal limits" on her lab report. She is being started on a renally dosed antibiotic. Is dose adjustment needed?
Correct Answer: YES — dose adjustment is needed.
Even a "normal" creatinine in an elderly woman may reflect a GFR <50 mL/min due to reduced muscle mass and lower creatinine production. The serum creatinine concentration must be used to calculate eGFR; it cannot be interpreted in isolation. Creatinine ≤1.0 mg/dL in an 80-year-old woman commonly corresponds to CKD stage 3.
⚠ Trap: Students see "normal creatinine" and assume normal kidney function. The key: creatinine is a surrogate that must be interpreted in the context of muscle mass, age, and sex. The eGFR equation is mandatory.
Trap 02ACE Inhibitor + ARB Combination
A 55-year-old man with diabetic nephropathy and persistent proteinuria despite maximum-dose lisinopril has his eGFR decline from 55 to 48 mL/min over 6 months. His physician considers adding losartan to further reduce proteinuria. What is the most important concern with this approach?
Correct Answer: Do NOT combine ACE inhibitor + ARB.
While additive antiproteinuric effects might seem appealing, the combination of ACE inhibitor and ARB has been demonstrated to cause a greater incidence of acute kidney injury and adverse cardiac events, with no net benefit. The correct approach is to optimize other agents (e.g., add SGLT2 inhibitor) rather than dual RAS blockade.
⚠ Trap: The logic "more RAS blockade = more renoprotection" seems sound mechanistically. The key: clinical trial data show harm from ACE inhibitor + ARB combination. SGLT2 inhibitors are the preferred add-on to ACE inhibitors/ARBs for further renoprotection.
Trap 03Phosphate Binder Selection — Hypercalcemia Risk
A 62-year-old woman with stage 4 CKD on hemodialysis has markedly elevated serum phosphate. Bone biopsy shows adynamic bone disease with very low PTH. Which phosphate binder is most appropriate?
Correct Answer: Sevelamer or Lanthanum (non-calcium-containing binder)
In adynamic bone disease, ingested calcium cannot be incorporated into bones with low turnover and instead deposits in vascular and soft tissues. Calcium-based binders (calcium acetate, calcium carbonate) would worsen hypercalcemia and accelerate vascular calcification. Sevelamer/lanthanum do not predispose to hypercalcemia and may actually attenuate vascular calcium deposition.
⚠ Trap: Students default to "calcium-based" phosphate binders as first choice. The key: adynamic bone disease + low-turnover = calcium binders contraindicated due to hypercalcemia and vascular calcification risk.
Trap 04Uremic Pericarditis — Urgent Action
A 58-year-old man with stage 5 CKD not yet on dialysis presents with pleuritic chest pain and a friction rub on auscultation. ECG shows PR-interval depression and diffuse ST elevation. What is the most appropriate next step?
Correct Answer: Urgent initiation of hemodialysis (without heparin)
Uremic pericarditis is an absolute indication for urgent initiation of dialysis. Because of the propensity for hemorrhage into pericardial fluid, hemodialysis must be performed without heparin. NSAIDs or colchicine could adversely affect renal function and are not first-line here.
⚠ Trap: Students may choose NSAIDs or colchicine (standard pericarditis treatment). The key distinguishing word: "uremic" — always means urgent dialysis, and always without anticoagulation due to hemorrhagic risk.
Trap 05Hemoglobin Target in CKD — Don't Normalize
A 68-year-old man with stage 4 CKD has a hemoglobin of 9.2 g/dL. He is started on ESA therapy. The nurse asks about the hemoglobin target. What is the correct target range?
Correct Answer: Target Hgb 100–115 g/L (10–11.5 g/dL) — do NOT normalize to 12–13 g/dL
Randomized controlled trials of ESAs in CKD failed to show improved cardiovascular outcomes when targeting normal hemoglobin. ESA use to achieve near-normal Hgb was associated with increased risk of stroke (type 2 DM) and thromboembolic events, and possibly faster CKD progression.
⚠ Trap: Students assume "higher is better" for hemoglobin. The key: normalization of Hgb in CKD = harm, not benefit. This is a trial trap — know that the TREAT and CREATE trials showed excess cardiovascular events with higher Hgb targets.
Trap 06Warfarin and Calciphylaxis
A 65-year-old woman on peritoneal dialysis for ESKD, on chronic warfarin for atrial fibrillation, develops a painful nodule on the abdomen that progresses to ischemic skin necrosis. The most important intervention (beyond local wound care) is:
Correct Answer: Discontinue warfarin and switch to an alternative anticoagulant
Warfarin inhibits vitamin K–dependent activation of matrix GLA protein, which normally prevents vascular calcification. Loss of this protection → calciphylaxis. Discontinuation of warfarin is mandatory once calciphylaxis develops. Alternative anticoagulation based on the specific indication should be chosen (apixaban is used increasingly in dialysis patients).
⚠ Trap: Students focus on pain management or local care and miss the essential step. The key: warfarin + CKD/dialysis = calciphylaxis risk. The board keyword is "warfarin" in a dialysis patient with progressive skin necrosis.
Trap 07SGLT2 Inhibitors Beyond Diabetes
A 52-year-old woman with non-diabetic focal segmental glomerulosclerosis and CKD stage 3 with persistent proteinuria is well-controlled on an ACE inhibitor. What additional therapy has been shown to slow GFR decline and reduce cardiovascular events in this patient?
Correct Answer: SGLT2 inhibitor (gliflozin) — indicated in both diabetic AND nondiabetic CKD
SGLT2 inhibitors restore tubuloglomerular feedback by increasing sodium delivery to the macula densa → afferent arteriolar constriction → reduced intraglomerular hyperfiltration. This mechanism and clinical benefit applies to nondiabetic CKD and extends to stage 4 CKD. They also preserve potassium homeostasis.
⚠ Trap: Students assume gliflozins are only for diabetic kidney disease. The key: SGLT2 inhibitors are now indicated for nondiabetic CKD as well, based on trials like DAPA-CKD. The mechanism is glucose-independent.
Trap 08GFR Drop After Starting ACE Inhibitor — Stop or Continue?
A 60-year-old man with CKD stage 3 and proteinuria starts lisinopril. Two weeks later, his creatinine rises and eGFR falls from 52 to 38 mL/min (a 27% drop). He feels well. What is the correct management?
Correct Answer: Continue the ACE inhibitor with close monitoring — this is an acceptable response
A nonprogressive decline in eGFR up to 30% may reflect effective reduction of intraglomerular hypertension (reduced hyperfiltration) and is tolerable if stable over time. Discontinuing the ACE inhibitor in this scenario was associated with increased risk of death and cardiovascular events in clinical studies, with only a modest reduction in starting kidney replacement therapy. A progressive rise in creatinine beyond this range should prompt evaluation for renovascular disease.
⚠ Trap: Students immediately stop the ACE inhibitor when creatinine rises. The key: up to 30% eGFR decline is the acceptable threshold; beyond 30% or with a progressive rise → investigate for renovascular disease. Stable 27% drop = continue with monitoring.
Trap 09Metabolic Acidosis Type Progression in CKD
A 48-year-old man with CKD stage 3 secondary to obstructive uropathy has blood gas showing pH 7.33, HCO₃⁻ 16 mmol/L, and an elevated chloride with normal anion gap. Which statement best describes expected progression of his acid-base disorder?
Correct Answer: As CKD progresses, an anion-gap metabolic acidosis will be ADDED to the existing non-anion-gap acidosis
In early CKD, reduced ammonia production → non-anion-gap (hyperchloremic) metabolic acidosis. As GFR declines further, total acid excretion becomes severely limited (<30–40 mmol/day) and accumulation of organic acid anions adds an anion-gap component. The two types of metabolic acidosis can coexist in advanced CKD. Obstructive uropathy and sickle cell nephropathy tend to cause earlier and more severe distal nephron potassium secretion abnormalities + hyperchloremic acidosis.
⚠ Trap: Students expect a single acidosis type in CKD. The key: CKD acidosis evolves from non-anion-gap → mixed non-anion-gap + anion-gap as GFR falls. Don't stop at "non-anion-gap" — it will change.
Trap 10APOL1 — When to Test and Why
A 38-year-old Black man of West African descent presents with focal segmental glomerulosclerosis (FSGS) and CKD stage 2. Family history: his father had unexplained CKD. What genetic test is most relevant and why?
Correct Answer: APOL1 high-risk genotype testing — important for diagnosis, risk stratification, and potential targeted therapy
APOL1 variants (common in sub-Saharan African ancestry) confer a several-fold higher risk for FSGS, HIV nephropathy, SARS-CoV-2–associated nephropathy, and CKD with hypertension. Identifying high-risk genotypes enables: (1) accurate disease attribution, (2) prognostic counseling, (3) eligibility for inaxaplin (APOL1 channel inhibitor shown to reduce proteinuria in APOL1-mediated FSGS). Acquired triggers (e.g., elevated IFN-γ) transform genetic risk into disease.
⚠ Trap: Students overlook genetic testing in a patient with apparent "secondary" FSGS. The key: APOL1 status fundamentally changes diagnosis, prognosis, and treatment eligibility — inaxaplin is a targeted therapy only for APOL1 high-risk genotypes.
