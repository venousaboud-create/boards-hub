Section 1

Definition & Staging

AKI is the impairment of kidney filtration and excretory function occurring over days to weeks — generally within 7 days. The most important thing to understand from the start: AKI is a clinical diagnosis, not a structural one. A patient can have AKI with perfectly intact kidney cells. The classification into prerenal, intrinsic, and postrenal reflects where the problem is, not how severe the damage is.

📋 KDIGO CriteriaDiagnosis — Any ONE of these is sufficient
▼
  • Serum creatinine rise ≥ 0.3 mg/dL within 48 hours
  • Serum creatinine rise ≥ 1.5× baseline within 7 days
  • Urine output <0.5 mL/kg/h for more than 6 hours

Staging uses the higher of the SCr or UO criterion — a patient can be Stage 2 by creatinine and Stage 1 by urine output; they are Stage 2 overall.

The Three-Category Framework

Every cause of AKI fits into one of three anatomical locations. Before running any tests, asking this single question orients everything else:

1 · Prerenal

Not enough blood reaching the glomerulus. The kidney itself is intact — it just isn't being perfused. Rapidly reversible when perfusion is restored.

2 · Intrinsic

Direct damage to the kidney parenchyma — tubules, glomeruli, interstitium, or vessels. The problem is inside the kidney.

3 · Postrenal

Obstruction to urine outflow increases back-pressure, reducing filtration. Remove the obstruction, restore function.

Section 2

Prerenal Azotemia

Prerenal is the most common form of AKI. The key concept: no parenchymal damage — GFR falls because perfusion pressure falls. When you restore blood flow, function returns. But if you ignore it, prolonged prerenal ischemia damages tubular cells and becomes ATN — a continuum, not a binary.

How the Kidney Defends Its GFR

When perfusion pressure drops, the kidney activates two simultaneous compensatory responses. Understand these two responses — almost every prerenal drug interaction comes back to disrupting them:

Afferent Vasodilation

The afferent arteriole dilates to let more blood in. Triggered by:

  • Prostaglandins (prostacyclin, PGE₂)
  • Nitric oxide (via tubuloglomerular feedback)
  • Myogenic reflex in the vessel wall

Blocked by: NSAIDs — remove prostaglandins, the afferent cannot dilate

+
Efferent Vasoconstriction

The efferent arteriole constricts to maintain glomerular pressure. Driven by:

  • Angiotensin II (from RAAS activation)
  • Raises filtration fraction
  • ↑ peritubular oncotic pressure → ↑ Na reabsorption

Blocked by: ACE-I / ARB — remove angiotensin II, the efferent cannot constrict

🚨
When both mechanisms are blocked simultaneously — NSAIDs + ACE-I/ARB — GFR collapses. This is the highest-risk drug combination for prerenal AKI. Each drug alone is manageable; together, there is no compensatory mechanism left. Especially dangerous in patients with bilateral renal artery stenosis, CKD, elderly, or heart failure.
⚠️
Autoregulation has a hard limit. Even in a perfectly healthy adult, it fails when systolic BP falls below 80 mmHg. Below this threshold, GFR drops proportionally with pressure regardless of compensatory attempts.

NSAIDs — Prerenal, Not Intrinsic

This distinction is repeatedly tested. NSAIDs cause AKI by blocking prostaglandins → the afferent arteriole cannot dilate → GFR falls. This is a hemodynamic (prerenal) mechanism — there is no parenchymal damage. The AKI is rapidly reversible when the drug is stopped. NSAIDs can also cause AIN through an immune mechanism, but their primary kidney effect is prerenal — they belong in the prerenal category, not alongside tubular toxins like aminoglycosides or cisplatin.

SGLT-2 Inhibitors — A Common Misconception

SGLT-2 inhibitors lower intraglomerular pressure and cause natriuresis — which sounds like it should increase AKI risk by the same logic as ACE-I/ARBs. It doesn't. Recent data show SGLT-2 inhibitors are actually protective against AKI. Do not conflate them with ACE-I/ARBs.

Section 3

Intrinsic AKI — Sepsis & Ischemia

The three most common causes of intrinsic AKI in hospitalized patients are sepsis, ischemia, and nephrotoxins. This section covers sepsis and ischemia; nephrotoxins get their own dedicated section because they are the most heavily examined.

Sepsis — More Than Just Hypotension

AKI complicates more than 50% of severe sepsis cases and dramatically raises mortality. The critical teaching point: sepsis-AKI can occur without overt hypotension. It is not simply "not enough blood flow." The mechanisms are more complex:

  • Cytokine storm → ↑ inducible NO synthase → generalized vasodilation → variable renal vascular effects (can be vasodilatory or vasoconstrictive)
  • Endothelial damage → microvascular leukocyte adhesion, thrombosis, ↑ permeability, ↑ interstitial pressure, ↓ local tubular blood flow, reactive oxygen species
  • Mitochondrial dysfunction → impaired ATP generation even when O₂ delivery is adequate
  • Postmortem studies of sepsis kidneys: frank tubular necrosis is often absent — inflammation, mitochondrial dysfunction, and interstitial edema dominate the pathology
💎
Sepsis and AKI are bidirectional: sepsis causes AKI, and AKI predisposes to further sepsis (impaired immune defense). SARS-CoV-2 causes AKI via cytokine storm → intrarenal vasoconstriction + systemic hypercoagulable state → impaired intrarenal blood flow.

Ischemia — Why the Outer Medulla Bears the Brunt

The kidney receives 20% of cardiac output and consumes 10% of resting O₂ despite being only 0.5% of body mass. Paradoxically, it contains one of the most hypoxic regions in the body: the outer medulla. The S3 segment of the proximal tubule sits here and depends entirely on oxidative phosphorylation — making it the most vulnerable segment to ischemia.

When ischemia hits, three mechanisms combine to reduce GFR:

  1. Preglomerular vasoconstriction — injured proximal tubule delivers excess solute to the macula densa → tubuloglomerular feedback → afferent constriction
  2. Backleak — filtrate leaks across denuded tubular epithelium back into the interstitium
  3. Tubular obstruction — necrotic cell debris falls into the tubular lumen and forms obstructing casts
⚠️ ClinicalPostoperative AKI — High-Risk Procedures
▼

Severe AKI requiring dialysis occurs in approximately 1% of cardiac and vascular surgery cases. The highest-risk procedures:

  • Cardiac surgery with cardiopulmonary bypass (CPB), especially combined valve + bypass: ischemia from hypoperfusion + leukocyte activation + hemolysis (pigment nephropathy) + atheroemboli. Longer CPB duration = higher AKI risk.
  • Aortic cross-clamping during vascular surgery
  • Major intraperitoneal procedures

Abdominal compartment syndrome complicates massive fluid resuscitation (trauma, burns, pancreatitis). When intra-abdominal pressure exceeds 20 mmHg → renal vein compression → ↓ GFR. Burns cause AKI in 25% of patients with >10% TBSA involvement.

Section 4 · Most Tested

Nephrotoxin-Associated AKI

The kidney is uniquely vulnerable to toxins — it receives massive blood flow and progressively concentrates substances along the nephron. The critical skill here is not memorizing a list of drugs, but understanding which part of the kidney each toxin attacks, because that determines every downstream clinical feature: the AKI pattern, the urine sediment, the electrolyte disturbances, and the FeNa.

Aminoglycosides — The Prototype Non-Oliguric ATN

Aminoglycosides (Gentamicin, Amikacin, Tobramycin)

GentamicinAmikacinTobramycinStreptomycin
Mechanism
Freely filtered at the glomerulus → accumulate in the proximal tubular cells of the renal cortex at concentrations far exceeding plasma levels → bind anionic phospholipids in cell membranes → lysosomal dysfunction → cell necrosis
AKI Pattern
Non-oliguric ATN in 10–30% of courses, even when plasma drug levels are in the therapeutic range. The damage is patchy — enough tubular cells survive to maintain urine output, but creatinine still rises.
Timing
AKI onset after 5–7 days of therapy. Critically: AKI can appear and worsen after the drug is stopped — aminoglycosides accumulate in cortical stores and slowly release for days after plasma levels reach zero.
Electrolytes
Hypomagnesemia is the hallmark — persists even after creatinine normalizes. Also: hypokalemia, hypophosphatemia from tubular wasting.
FeNa
>1% — damaged proximal tubular cells cannot reabsorb Na normally

Amphotericin B — The Unique Triad

Amphotericin B

Conventional AmBAmBisome (lipid — less nephrotoxic)
Mechanisms
Three simultaneous mechanisms: (1) ↑ tubuloglomerular feedback → afferent vasoconstriction → ↓ GFR; (2) reactive oxygen species → direct tubular toxicity; (3) binds tubular membrane cholesterol → inserts pores → tubular membranes leak electrolytes and cannot secrete H⁺
Clinical Triad
Polyuria (the pores make tubular cells leaky → nephrogenic DI pattern) + Hypomagnesemia (Mg wasting through the pores) + Non-anion gap metabolic acidosis (impaired distal H⁺ secretion → distal RTA pattern). Also: hypocalcemia, hypokalemia.
AKI Pattern
Non-oliguric — the patient makes too much urine (polyuria), not too little
Dose-dependence
Nephrotoxicity is cumulative dose + duration dependent. Liposomal formulation (AmBisome) dramatically reduces nephrotoxicity.
💎
The Amphotericin B triad is unique among nephrotoxins: polyuria + hypoMg + non-anion gap metabolic acidosis. No other drug produces this exact combination. If you see these three together, the answer is amphotericin B.

Cisplatin — Direct Proximal Tubular Uptake

Cisplatin & Carboplatin

Cisplatin is actively transported into proximal tubular cells via organic cation transporters → inside the cell, it forms DNA crosslinks → oxidative stress → necrosis + apoptosis of proximal tubular cells

ATN — the dose-limiting toxicity of cisplatin. Intensive pre-hydration regimens reduce incidence but do not eliminate it. AKI develops 3–5 days into therapy.

Cisplatin → active intracellular uptake → cell death. Acyclovir → crystal deposition in lumen → obstruction + AIN. Rifampin/quinolones → immune AIN. These are completely different mechanisms — do not confuse them.

TMA-Causing Drugs — The Glomerular Targets

An entirely separate mechanism from tubular toxins: these drugs damage the glomerular endothelium, causing microvascular thrombosis. The result is thrombotic microangiopathy (TMA) — not ATN, not AIN.

🚨
Gemcitabine causes TMA, not ATN. This distinction is consistently tested. The word "chemotherapy" should not lead you to automatically think "tubular toxicity." Gemcitabine → endothelial damage → glomerular microvascular thrombosis → HUS-like syndrome (schistocytes + thrombocytopenia + ↑ LDH + AKI). The correct answer is always TMA, never ATN.
🚨 TMA-Causing Drugs
  • Bevacizumab (anti-VEGF): blocks VEGF → glomerular endothelial loss → TMA → proteinuria + hypertension
  • Gemcitabine: endothelial toxicity → glomerular TMA → HUS-like syndrome
  • Mitomycin C: TMA
  • Calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus, cyclosporine): arteriolar vasoconstriction → small vessel TMA
🔵 How to Recognize TMA
  • AKI + schistocytes on peripheral blood smear
  • ↑ LDH, ↓ haptoglobin (hemolysis)
  • Thrombocytopenia
  • Dysmorphic RBCs in urine
  • Hypertension + proteinuria (especially with anti-VEGF agents)

Acute Interstitial Nephritis (AIN) — Immune-Mediated

AIN is a type IV hypersensitivity reaction in the tubulointerstitium. Drugs account for ~75% of biopsy-proven AIN. The most commonly implicated: PPIs, NSAIDs, quinolones, sulfonamides, rifampin, beta-lactams, and checkpoint inhibitors.

⚠️
The classic triad of fever + rash + eosinophilia is present in only a minority of AIN cases. Absence of this triad does not exclude AIN. If the clinical story fits (drug exposure + non-oliguric AKI with sterile pyuria), biopsy may be needed to confirm the diagnosis. Stop the offending drug. Consider glucocorticoids if AKI persists after stopping.

Rhabdomyolysis — The Endogenous Toxin

Rhabdomyolysis (Myoglobin Nephropathy)

Mechanism
Muscle cell death → myoglobin released into blood → three simultaneous kidney injuries: (1) intrarenal vasoconstriction, (2) direct proximal tubular toxicity via ROS, (3) myoglobin precipitates with Tamm-Horsfall protein (uromodulin) in acidic urine → obstructing casts in distal nephron
Causes
Crush injury, prolonged immobilization, seizures, excessive exercise, heat stroke, neuroleptic malignant syndrome, statins, alcohol, severe hypothyroidism, severe hypokalemia/hypophosphatemia
Labs
↑ CPK (gold standard, often >1,000–10,000 U/L); ↑ AST, ↑ LDH; hyperkalemia, hyperphosphatemia, hypocalcemia (Ca²⁺ deposits in necrotic tissue); BUN:Cr ratio <10:1 (muscle creatine → creatinine raises Cr independently of GFR)
Urine Finding
Heme-positive dipstick + FEW or NO RBCs on microscopy — myoglobin reacts with the peroxidase reagent on the dipstick exactly like hemoglobin, but there are no actual RBCs. This is the classic presentation and a repeatedly-tested pattern.
FeNa
<1% early (vasoconstriction dominates) → may rise to >1% as ATN develops
Treatment
Aggressive IVF — up to 10 L/day initially. Target urine output 200–300 mL/h. Alkalinize urine with NaHCO₃ (75 mmol/L in 0.45% saline) → raises urinary pH → prevents myoglobin-uromodulin precipitation. Risk: worsening hypocalcemia.
🔵
Why the BUN:Creatinine ratio is LOW in rhabdomyolysis: Skeletal muscle contains large amounts of creatine. When muscle lyses, creatine floods the blood and is non-enzymatically converted to creatinine — raising plasma Cr independently of how well the kidney filters. BUN does not share this mechanism. Result: Cr rises disproportionately → BUN:Cr <10:1. In contrast, prerenal, GI bleeding, and steroids all raise BUN disproportionately → BUN:Cr >20:1.

Tumor Lysis Syndrome — Can Happen Before Chemotherapy

Tumor Lysis Syndrome

Trigger
Massive, rapid cell death. Occurs with cytotoxic therapy in high-grade lymphomas and leukemia — but can also occur spontaneously, before any treatment, in aggressive tumors (DLBCL, Burkitt lymphoma, ALL). High intrinsic tumor cell turnover alone can trigger TLS.
Mechanism
Purine release → massive hyperuricemia (often >15 mg/dL) → uric acid precipitates in renal tubular lumen in acidic urine → intratubular obstruction → AKI
4 Abnormalities
Hyperuricemia + Hyperkalemia + Hyperphosphatemia + Hypocalcemia (calcium deposits in tissues as phosphate precipitates)
Urine Finding
Uric acid crystals — these are the diagnostic finding in the urine. Distinct from oxalate crystals (ethylene glycol) or no crystals (ATN).
Treatment
Allopurinol or Febuxostat (prophylaxis — block xanthine oxidase → ↓ uric acid production). Rasburicase (acute treatment — recombinant uricase breaks down uric acid; contraindicated in G6PD deficiency → risk of hemolysis). Aggressive IVF.

Cholesterol Emboli — A Subacute Course That Fools Everyone

After manipulation of the aorta (surgery, cardiac catheterization, percutaneous procedures) or rarely spontaneously or after anticoagulation, cholesterol crystals embolize from disrupted atherosclerotic plaques into small renal arteries. The critical teaching point is the timeline:

  • Contrast nephropathy: AKI peaks at 3–5 days, resolves by 1 week
  • Cholesterol emboli: AKI develops over weeks to months — subacute and progressive

Why subacute? A foreign body reaction to the cholesterol crystals causes intimal proliferation and giant cell formation — progressively narrowing the vascular lumen over weeks, long after the initial embolization event.

🔵 Signs
  • Livedo reticularis (mottled skin)
  • Blue toe syndrome
  • Hollenhorst plaques in retinal vessels on fundoscopy
🔵 Labs
  • Eosinophilia
  • Eosinophiluria (variable)
  • Hypocomplementemia
  • Biopsy: cholesterol clefts (pathognomonic)

Ethylene Glycol — The Tubular Toxin With a Distinctive Lab Pattern

Ethylene glycol (automobile antifreeze) is metabolized to oxalic acid, glycolaldehyde, and glyoxylate. These metabolites cause direct tubular cell toxicity and form calcium oxalate crystals that obstruct the tubular lumen. Unlike most conditions causing AKI with reduced perfusion (which have FeNa <1%), ethylene glycol destroys tubular cells → impaired Na reabsorption → FeNa >1%. The complete laboratory triad: ↑ anion gap + ↑ osmolal gap + oxalate crystalluria.

Section 5

Postrenal AKI

Obstruction to urine outflow increases retrograde hydrostatic pressure → impairs glomerular filtration. The most important clinical lesson: normal urine output does not rule out significant postrenal obstruction. GFR is approximately 100× higher than urine flow rate under normal conditions — a patient can lose most of their GFR and still produce enough urine to look "OK."

Causes: BPH, prostate cancer, neurogenic bladder, urethral strictures, calculi, retroperitoneal fibrosis, pelvic malignancy, sloughed renal papillae, anticholinergic drugs, obstructed Foley catheter. Bilateral obstruction is needed for significant AKI unless the patient has a solitary functioning kidney or underlying CKD.

💎
Postrenal AKI carries a better prognosis than intrinsic AKI — if you recognize and relieve the obstruction promptly. Always image the urinary tract (ultrasound or CT) when obstruction is clinically possible, even if urine output seems preserved. Obstruction can be present without hydronephrosis if volume-depleted, encased by tumor, or very early in its course.
Section 6 · Most Tested Diagnostic Concept

FeNa, BUN:Creatinine Ratio & Diagnosis

FeNa is the most tested diagnostic index in AKI — and the most misused. The key is understanding not just the cutoffs, but why the cutoffs exist, and when they break down.

FeNa — The Concept Behind the Formula

FeNa (Fractional Excretion of Sodium) measures what percentage of filtered sodium is excreted rather than reabsorbed. It reflects two things simultaneously: tubular reabsorptive capacity and perceived intravascular volume.

Formula: FeNa (%) = (Urine Na × Plasma Cr) / (Plasma Na × Urine Cr) × 100

🔵
The question FeNa is really asking: "Are the tubular cells working hard to hold onto sodium?" If yes (<1%) → either volume-depleted, or glomerular disease with intact tubules. If no (>1%) → tubular dysfunction, or diuretics forcing Na excretion regardless of volume status.
🔵 Complete MapFeNa — Every Scenario, Including All Exceptions
▼
FeNa <1% — Avid Na Retention

Tubules are working hard to reabsorb Na:

  • Prerenal azotemia — classic cause
  • Glomerulonephritis (early) — glomerular damage, tubules intact → still reabsorb avidly
  • Rhabdomyolysis (early) — intrarenal vasoconstriction dominates initially
  • Contrast nephropathy (early) — tubular function relatively preserved
  • Sepsis (early in course) — tubular function initially intact
  • Hepatorenal syndrome
  • Post-STEMI / cardiogenic shock
FeNa >1% — Na Wasting

Tubules cannot reabsorb Na, OR forced to excrete:

  • ATN (ischemic, aminoglycosides, cisplatin) — damaged tubular cells
  • Fanconi syndrome — generalized proximal tubular transport failure
  • Diuretics — force Na excretion even in volume depletion; INVALIDATES FeNa
  • Ethylene glycol — direct tubular toxicity
  • CKD with baseline tubular dysfunction
  • AIN (tubular inflammation)
🚨
The FeNa only works in very specific conditions: oliguric patient + NOT on diuretics + no baseline CKD. If the patient is on diuretics, FeNa becomes meaningless for distinguishing prerenal from ATN — diuretics force Na excretion regardless of intravascular volume. A "high" FeNa in a diuretic-treated patient tells you nothing.
⚠️
Low FeNa (<1%) is suggestive of, but not synonymous with, volume depletion. It also occurs in early GN, rhabdomyolysis, and contrast nephropathy — all of which are intrinsic AKI. A low FeNa should never be used as the sole reason to give fluids.

Distinguishing AKI from CKD

Suggests AKI
  • Normal or near-normal kidney size on ultrasound
  • Recently documented normal renal function
  • Rising SCr on serial measurements
  • No normocytic anemia (unless from other cause)
Suggests CKD

Novel Biomarkers

💎 EmergingBeyond Creatinine — What's Now FDA-Approved
▼
  • KIM-1 (Kidney Injury Molecule-1): Transmembrane protein, highly expressed in injured proximal tubular cells. Not expressed without tubular injury. Detectable in urine/plasma after ischemic or nephrotoxic injury. Excellent tubular injury marker.
  • NGAL (Neutrophil Gelatinase-Associated Lipocalin): Detected in urine/plasma within 2 hours of CPB-associated AKI. FDA-approved 2023 for early identification of AKI in pediatric patients.

The heme-positive dipstick trap: A dipstick that is positive for "blood" detects the peroxidase activity of the heme group — it cannot distinguish RBCs from free hemoglobin from myoglobin. The only way to tell the difference is microscopy. Heme+ with abundant RBCs = true hematuria (urologic evaluation). 💎
Dysmorphic RBCs (acanthocytes) = glomerular disease, always. When RBCs are forced through a disrupted glomerular basement membrane or through a thrombotic microangiopathy in the glomerular capillaries, they are physically deformed. This deformation is the signature of glomerular origin — it does not occur with tubular disease. Dysmorphic RBCs in urine → think GN or TMA, never ATN.
Section 8

Complications of AKI

🚨 Life-Threatening
  • Hyperkalemia: Especially severe in rhabdomyolysis, hemolysis, TLS (massive intracellular K⁺ release). Affects cardiac conduction → fatal arrhythmias. This is why these three conditions are high-risk for sudden cardiac death.
  • Pulmonary edema: Oliguric/anuric AKI → impaired salt/water excretion → volume overload → acute lung injury (AKI also increases pulmonary vascular permeability independently)
  • Uremic encephalopathy: Asterixis (flapping tremor), confusion, seizures — a dialysis indication
  • Uremic pericarditis / pericardial effusion
  • Uremic bleeding: Platelet dysfunction from retained uremic toxins
⚠️ Metabolic
  • Metabolic acidosis: ↑ Anion gap (retained phosphate, hippurate, sulfate, urate). Treat only if pH <7.2 and HCO₃ <15 mmol/L.
  • Hyperphosphatemia → Hypocalcemia: Especially severe in rhabdomyolysis, TLS. Hypocalcemia → perioral paresthesias, muscle cramps, seizures, carpopedal spasms, prolonged QT.
  • Hyponatremia: Excess hypotonic fluids → seizures
  • Anemia: Multifactorial — ↓ erythropoiesis (uremia) + platelet dysfunction. Not responsive to EPO acutely.
  • Malnutrition: AKI is a hypercatabolic state
💎
The recovery phase: Resolution of AKI is signaled by a polyuric phase — osmotic diuresis from retained urea + delayed recovery of tubular concentrating ability. Monitor electrolytes carefully during this phase — hypokalemia and dehydration can occur. The kidney has remarkable regenerative capacity even after severe, dialysis-requiring AKI when baseline function was intact.
Section 9

Treatment of AKI

Fluid Management

Critical rule: Oliguria alone is NOT an indication for fluid administration. Only documented intravascular hypovolemia justifies fluids. Giving fluids to an oliguric patient with intrinsic AKI (ATN from sepsis, for example) can cause dangerous volume overload without benefit.

Dialysis — Indications and Timing

🚨 CriticalAEIOU — Indications for Renal Replacement Therapy
▼
  • A — Acidosis: Refractory metabolic acidosis (pH <7.2, HCO₃ <15 mmol/L, not responding to bicarbonate)
  • E — Electrolytes: Refractory hyperkalemia (especially with EKG changes)
  • I — Ingestions: Toxic ingestions removable by dialysis (ethylene glycol, methanol, lithium, salicylates)
  • O — Overload: Volume overload causing pulmonary edema, refractory to diuretics
  • U — Uremia: Asterixis, pericardial rub or effusion, uremic encephalopathy, uremic bleeding. Asterixis = uremic encephalopathy = most urgent indication.

Many nephrologists also initiate dialysis empirically when BUN exceeds 100 mg/dL without signs of clinical recovery — but AEIOU criteria take precedence for urgency. A number alone (BUN 80, bicarbonate 15) without uremic symptoms is not an urgent indication.

⚠️
Timing: Randomized trials show NO survival benefit from early vs. late dialysis initiation. Do not wait for a life-threatening complication — but do not initiate unnecessarily early either. Early dialysis risks: IV line complications, infection, bleeding, hypotension that perpetuates AKI by ischemia to the recovering kidney.

✅ Treatment by Etiology
  • GN / Vasculitis: Immunosuppression, anticomplement therapy, plasmapheresis

  • Scleroderma renal crisis: ACE inhibitors

★ Section 11 · Exam Coach

Board Exam Traps

These are the patterns that most commonly trick students — presented as clinical vignettes so you recognize the setup when it appears on your exam.

★ Trap 01 · The Diuretic Invalidates FeNa
An elderly woman with CHF on furosemide and metolazone develops worsening creatinine. Her FeNa is 2.4%. The team concludes she has ATN and withholds fluids. Was this the right interpretation?
What the trap does

Presents a patient with seemingly high FeNa (>1%) and leads you to diagnose intrinsic AKI. But the patient is on two diuretics.

Why it's wrong

Diuretics invalidate FeNa entirely. Furosemide + metolazone block tubular Na reabsorption regardless of volume status — FeNa will be >1% even in true volume depletion. This patient likely has cardiorenal syndrome from poor cardiac output. Clinical assessment (JVP, mucous membranes, response to careful fluid challenge) must replace FeNa as the guide.

Keyword: Diuretics → FeNa unreliable
★ Trap 02 · Contrast Nephropathy vs. Cholesterol Emboli
A 72-year-old man undergoes coronary angiography. Three weeks later his creatinine rises from 1.2 to 3.1. He has livedo reticularis and discoloration of his toes. What caused his AKI?
What the trap does

Anchors on the angiography procedure (contrast exposure) and leads you to diagnose contrast nephropathy.

Why it's wrong

Contrast nephropathy peaks at 3–5 days and resolves within 1 week. Three weeks later is impossible for contrast. The correct answer is cholesterol emboli — aortic plaque disruption during catheterization → cholesterol crystals embolize → subacute, progressive AKI over weeks. Livedo reticularis and blue toe confirm the diagnosis.

Keyword: AKI at 3 WEEKS post-procedure + livedo = Cholesterol Emboli
★ Trap 03 · Gemcitabine = TMA, Not ATN
A woman on gemcitabine for pancreatic cancer develops AKI with schistocytes on blood smear and thrombocytopenia. A student diagnoses "acute toxic ATN from chemotherapy." Correct?
What the trap does

Uses the word "chemotherapy" to imply tubular toxicity (ATN), as with cisplatin.

Why it's wrong

Gemcitabine causes TMA, not ATN. Cisplatin accumulates in proximal tubular cells → ATN. Gemcitabine damages glomerular endothelium → TMA. Schistocytes + thrombocytopenia + ↑LDH + AKI = TMA pattern. Do not conflate all chemotherapy drugs — their renal targets differ completely.

Keyword: Gemcitabine + schistocytes + thrombocytopenia = TMA (not ATN)
★ Trap 04 · GN Has FeNa <1% — It's Not Always Prerenal
A patient with AKI has FeNa of 0.6%. The team diagnoses prerenal azotemia and gives 2L of saline. Creatinine continues to rise. Urine sediment shows RBC casts. What happened?
What the trap does

Uses a low FeNa as proof of prerenal AKI, bypassing the urine sediment.

Why it's wrong

Glomerulonephritis can have FeNa <1% because the primary injury is the glomerular filtration barrier — tubular cells remain intact and continue to avidly reabsorb Na. The RBC casts are the diagnostic key. Fluids don't fix GN; immunosuppression might. Low FeNa plus RBC casts = GN until proven otherwise.

Keyword: FeNa <1% + RBC casts = GN, not prerenal
★ Trap 05 · Heme-Positive Dipstick Is Not Always Hematuria
A patient found unconscious (prolonged immobilization) has a urine dipstick positive for blood 2+. Microscopy shows very few red blood cells. The team orders a CT urogram to evaluate for hematuria. What's wrong?
What the trap does

Equates a heme-positive dipstick with urologic bleeding.

Why it's wrong

The urine dipstick detects the peroxidase activity of the heme group — not the protein structure. Myoglobin reacts identically to hemoglobin in RBCs. Heme positive + few RBCs on microscopy = pigment nephropathy from rhabdomyolysis. Check CPK, not CT urogram. Also: BUN:Cr ratio will be low (<10:1) from muscle creatine release.

Keyword: Heme+ dipstick + few RBCs = pigment nephropathy → check CPK
★ Trap 06 · Aminoglycoside AKI After Stopping the Drug
A patient received 5 days of gentamicin. The drug was stopped. On day 8, creatinine begins rising. The family is reassured: "The drug was stopped 3 days ago — the kidney injury must be from something else." Correct?
What the trap does

Uses temporal logic: drug stopped → kidney injury cannot be from that drug.

Why it's wrong

Aminoglycosides accumulate in renal cortical cells and persist there for days after plasma levels normalize. The cortical drug stores slowly release → ongoing tubular injury after the drug is discontinued. AKI can appear and worsen 5–10 days into therapy and continue after stopping. Hypomagnesemia also persists even after creatinine normalizes.

Keyword: Aminoglycosides — nephrotoxicity can appear/worsen AFTER stopping
★ Trap 07 · TLS Can Occur Before Chemotherapy
A 75-year-old man with newly diagnosed DLBCL is scheduled to start R-CHOP next week. Today his creatinine rises and urine shows crystals. A student says "TLS cannot occur before chemotherapy starts." Correct?
What the trap does

Limits TLS to a chemotherapy-triggered phenomenon.

Why it's wrong

High-grade lymphomas (DLBCL, Burkitt, ALL) have massive intrinsic tumor cell turnover. TLS can occur spontaneously from this baseline turnover, before any treatment. The uric acid crystals in the urine confirm the diagnosis. Treatment is the same: rasburicase (acute) and allopurinol/febuxostat (prevention), but note: rasburicase is contraindicated in G6PD deficiency.

Keyword: TLS can occur spontaneously BEFORE chemotherapy in aggressive lymphoma
★ Trap 08 · Amphotericin B — NOT Interstitial Nephritis
A patient on amphotericin B develops polyuria, hypomagnesemia, and a non-anion gap metabolic acidosis. A student diagnoses drug-induced AIN because "amphotericin B is a drug that causes kidney inflammation." Correct?
What the trap does

Assumes all drug-induced kidney injury = AIN (interstitial nephritis).

Why it's wrong

Amphotericin B causes direct tubular toxicity, not immune-mediated interstitial nephritis. Its mechanism is membrane pore insertion via cholesterol binding → the triad of polyuria (nephrogenic DI) + hypomagnesemia + non-anion gap metabolic acidosis (distal H⁺ secretion failure). AIN presents with WBC casts, eosinophilia, and often fever/rash — none of which are present here.

Keyword: AmB = tubular pores = polyuria + hypoMg + non-gap metabolic acidosis (NOT AIN)
★ Trap 09 · NSAIDs = Prerenal, Not Non-Oliguric ATN
A question asks which drug is the LEAST LIKELY cause of non-oliguric acute tubular necrosis. Options: Cisplatin, Amikacin, Amphotericin B, NSAIDs. Many students pick Amphotericin B (because it has the complex mechanism) or Cisplatin (because it's less familiar).
What the trap does

Relies on familiarity with cisplatin and amphotericin to distract from the correct answer.

Why it's wrong

NSAIDs cause prerenal AKI — hemodynamic, not tubular. They block prostaglandins → afferent cannot dilate → GFR falls. No tubular cell death, no muddy brown casts, no non-oliguric ATN pattern. Cisplatin, amikacin, and amphotericin B are all established tubular toxins that cause non-oliguric ATN via different mechanisms. NSAIDs simply do not belong in this category.

Keyword: NSAIDs = prerenal (not non-oliguric ATN). Tubular toxins = aminoglycosides, cisplatin, amphotericin B.
★ Trap 10 · 0.9% Saline Worsens Metabolic Acidosis
An ICU patient with AKI and mild metabolic acidosis receives 4L of 0.9% normal saline. Two hours later, the acidosis has worsened. A student is confused — "We gave volume, why is the acidosis worse?"
What the trap does

Assumes that giving "normal" saline is neutral and cannot worsen the metabolic picture.

Why it's wrong

0.9% saline contains 154 mEq/L of chloride — supraphysiologic compared to plasma (~103 mEq/L). Large volumes → dilution of bicarbonate + chloride loading → hyperchloremic non-anion gap metabolic acidosis. The correct choice for resuscitation in a patient with metabolic acidosis is a balanced crystalloid (Plasmalyte, pH 7.4), which has near-physiologic electrolyte composition.

Keyword: 0.9% saline → hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis. Use Plasmalyte in acidosis.
