Overview & Key Concepts

Why DILI Matters

  • Leading cause of acute liver failure in the Western world — more than viral hepatitis
  • Most common reason for failure of new compounds to reach drug approval — hepatotoxicity detected in clinical trials kills more candidates than any other organ signal
  • Any xenobiotic can cause injury: prescription drugs, OTC drugs, industrial toxins, herbal/CAM products
  • A thorough drug history must include: prescription medications, OTC agents, herbal and dietary supplements, industrial/occupational exposures, body-building supplements

Six Mechanisms of Hepatocyte Injury

Drugs can damage the liver through six distinct pathways — understanding the mechanism predicts the clinical and histologic pattern:

Mechanism A

Rupture of cell membrane — disruption of intracellular calcium homeostasis → disassembly of actin fibrils → membrane blebbing → cell lysis

Mechanism B

Bile canaliculus injury — disruption of actin filaments next to canaliculus → loss of villous processes → interruption of transport pumps (MRP3) → failure to excrete bilirubin & organic compounds → cholestasis

Mechanism C

P-450 drug covalent binding — cytochrome P450 binds covalently to drug → nonfunctioning adducts formed in endoplasmic reticulum

Mechanism D

Drug adducts as immunogens — adducts migrate to cell surface in vesicles → targeted by cytolytic T cells (CTLs) and cytokines → immune-mediated hepatocyte destruction

Mechanism E

Apoptosis via TNF-α / Fas — activation of death domain (DD) receptors → caspase cascade → programmed cell death

Mechanism F

Mitochondrial dysfunction — dual inhibition of β-oxidation AND respiratory chain → failure of fatty acid metabolism → lactic acidosis + accumulation of reactive oxygen species → microvesicular steatosis histologically

💡 Board Pearl — Immune-Mediated Injury
Current data implicate the adaptive immune system as the dominant driver of idiosyncratic DILI: phase I metabolism of the offending drug generates immune-stimulatory compounds that trigger an adaptive response. HLA haplotypes (e.g., specific HLA-B and HLA-DR alleles) have been linked to hepatotoxicity from amoxicillin-clavulanate and flucloxacillin. Nuclear transporters (CAR, PXR) also play a role in drug hepatotoxicity induction.

Drug Metabolism

Metabolic Pathway

Most drugs are water-insoluble and must be converted to water-soluble forms for excretion. The two-phase process:

Water-insoluble Drug enters body
↓
Phase I — Oxidation / methylation via CYP450 (mixed-function oxygenases)
→ generates reactive intermediate metabolites
↓
Phase II
Glucuronidation or sulfation
→ innocuous, water-soluble metabolites → renal/biliary excretion
Glutathione pathway
GSH binds toxic metabolites → inactivated → excreted as mercapturic acid
⚠ Critical Concept
Most drug hepatotoxicity results from phase I toxic metabolites. When these are produced in excess, or when glutathione levels are depleted, the glutathione S-transferase inactivation pathway is overwhelmed → toxic metabolites bind covalently to hepatocyte macromolecules → hepatocyte injury. Glutathione depletion is central to acetaminophen toxicity (see Section 6).

Two Types of DILI

Type 1 — Direct Toxic
  • Predictable — occurs in all individuals exposed to sufficient dose
  • Dose-dependent — clear dose-response relationship
  • Short latency — usually hours; clinical manifestations possibly delayed 24–48 h
  • Reproducible characteristic histologic pattern for each toxin
  • Agents: systemic poisons or hepatically metabolized to toxic intermediates
  • Examples: acetaminophen (prime example), carbon tetrachloride (centrilobular zonal necrosis), trichloroethylene, Amanita phalloides toxin (massive necrosis; lethal dose ~10 mg — found in a single deathcap mushroom)
Type 2 — Idiosyncratic
  • Unpredictable — incidence 1 in 10³–10⁵ patients
  • Not clearly dose-dependent (but most agents given at doses >100 mg/day — higher dose → more off-target effects)
  • Variable latency — typically 5–90 days after starting the drug
  • More variable morphologic pattern; a single agent can cause multiple patterns
  • Adaptation: mild, transient, nonprogressive aminotransferase elevations that resolve despite continued drug use (recognized for INH, valproate, phenytoin, statins) — unknown mechanism
  • Extrahepatic hypersensitivity features (rash, fever, eosinophilia, arthralgias) occur in a minority — characteristic for phenytoin, TMP-SMX; absent in others
  • Examples: isoniazid, amoxicillin-clavulanate, nitrofurantoin, statins

Hy's Law — A Board Favourite

📋 Hy's Law (Dr. Hyman Zimmerman)
If jaundice occurs in a patient enrolled in a clinical drug trial, this predicts serious hepatotoxicity:
  • Ratio of 10:1 — jaundice cases : acute liver failure cases
  • 10 patients with jaundice in a trial → 1 will develop acute liver failure
  • Troglitazone is the classic example: Hy's Law cases occurred in Phase 3 trials but went unrecognized until post-marketing liver failure emerged → withdrawn 2001
  • Rosiglitazone and pioglitazone (second-generation TZDs) do NOT have this hepatotoxicity — aminotransferase elevations same as placebo
  • Since troglitazone withdrawal in 2001, no fully approved drugs have been withdrawn by the FDA for hepatotoxicity

Causality Assessment

DILI is nearly always a presumptive diagnosis. Many disorders mimic DILI. Establishing causality requires:

  • Temporal association — time of onset, time to resolution after withdrawal
  • Clinical-biochemical features and type of injury (hepatocellular vs cholestatic)
  • Extrahepatic features (rash, eosinophilia)
  • Prior record of the suspect agent's hepatotoxicity
  • Exclusion of other causes (viral hepatitis, autoimmune hepatitis, etc.)
📊 Causality Tools
  • RUCAM (Roussel-Uclaf Causality Assessment Method) — yields residual uncertainty; not widely adopted
  • DILIN (Drug-Induced Liver Injury Network) — structured expert consensus on 5-degree likelihood scale (definite, highly likely, probable, possible, unlikely); not practical for routine clinical use
  • RECAM (Revised Electronic Causality Assessment Method) — newest, most improved tool; available at dilirecam.com; not yet in widespread use

Morphologic Patterns of Injury

R-Value — Hepatocellular vs Cholestatic

🔢 R-Value Formula
R = (ALT / ULN) ÷ (ALP / ULN)
Both expressed as multiples of the upper limit of normal.
R > 5.0

Hepatocellular injury

R 2.0 – 5.0

Mixed hepatocellular-cholestatic

R < 2.0

Cholestatic injury

Cholestatic Injury — Four Grades of Severity

  • Bland cholestasis (limited hepatocellular injury) — estrogens, 17α-substituted androgens
  • Inflammatory cholestasis — amoxicillin-clavulanic acid (most frequently implicated antibiotic in DILI), oxacillin, erythromycin estolate
  • Sclerosing cholangitis — intrahepatic infusion of floxuridine (chemotherapy for hepatic metastases from colon cancer); also seen after long-term ketamine use
  • Vanishing bile duct syndrome (ductopenic cholestasis) — disappearance of bile ducts, similar to chronic rejection after liver transplant. Caused by carbamazepine, levofloxacin, amoxicillin-clavulanate (occasionally)

Mechanisms of cholestasis: binding of drugs to canalicular membrane transporters; accumulation of toxic bile acids from canalicular pump failure; genetic defects in canalicular transporter proteins.

💡 DILI in Pre-existing Liver Disease
DILI is generally not more frequent in persons with underlying chronic liver disease — but severity may be amplified. Exceptions where DILI risk IS increased in liver disease: aspirin, methotrexate, INH (some data), antiretroviral therapy for HIV, and conditioning regimens for bone marrow transplant in patients with hepatitis C.

Acetaminophen Hepatotoxicity — Direct Toxin Prototype

🔴 Big Picture
Acetaminophen is the #1 cause of acute liver failure in the Western world. Up to 72% of patients with acetaminophen hepatotoxicity in Scandinavia (somewhat lower in UK and USA) progress to encephalopathy and coagulopathy.

Mechanism — Why Acetaminophen Is Toxic

Acetaminophen ingested
↓
Phase II (major pathway)
Sulfation + glucuronidation
→ innocuous, excreted renally
Phase I (minor pathway, CYP2E1)
→ NAPQI
(N-acetyl-p-benzoquinone-imine)
↓
Normal state
NAPQI + glutathione (GSH) → mercapturic acid → renal excretion ✓
Overdose / GSH depletion
GSH overwhelmed → NAPQI binds covalently to hepatocyte macromolecules → protein adducts → hepatocyte necrosis
⚡ Potentiating Factors — These Lower the Toxic Threshold
  • Alcohol (chronic use) — induces CYP2E1 (↑ NAPQI production) AND suppresses hepatic glutathione synthesis → toxic dose may be as low as 2 g/day in alcoholics
  • Fasting / starvation — depletes hepatic glutathione (↓ GSH available to neutralize NAPQI); includes inability to maintain oral intake during febrile illness
  • Phenobarbital, rifampin, INH — all induce CYP450 → ↑ NAPQI generation
  • Hepatitis C co-infection — associated with increased risk of acute liver injury at overdose doses
  • Opioid-acetaminophen combinations — habituation to the opioid leads to gradual dose escalation over days/weeks, inadvertently increasing acetaminophen intake

Clinical Presentation — Timeline

TimepointClinical Features
0–12 h (early)Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, shock — direct GI and hemodynamic effects
24–48 hEarly features abate → hepatic injury becomes apparent
3–5 daysMaximal hepatic abnormalities and liver failure — aminotransferases >10,000 IU/L are not uncommon (far exceeding viral hepatitis levels)
ConcurrentRenal failure, myocardial injury may co-occur
🎯 Biochemical Signature — Boards Favourite
Acetaminophen hepatotoxicity produces a hyperacute pattern:
  • Extremely high aminotransferases (>10,000 IU/L)
  • Disproportionately LOW bilirubin (early stage)
  • This combination is highly characteristic — should trigger further questioning even if history is denied
  • Acute renal injury occurs in nearly 75% of severe cases but is virtually always self-limited

Dose Thresholds

  • Single dose of 10–15 g (occasionally less) → clinical liver injury
  • Fatal fulminant disease usually with ingestion of ≥25 g (though not invariably)
  • Chronic unintentional overdose: 8 g/day over several days (twice the daily maximum) → liver failure
  • Blood level >300 μg/mL at 4 h after ingestion → predictive of severe damage
  • Blood level <150 μg/mL at 4 h → hepatic injury highly unlikely
  • For patients with high acetaminophen blood levels (>200 μg/mL at 4 h OR >100 μg/mL at 8 h) → administer N-acetylcysteine
  • Chronic alcoholics: warn about dangers even at standard doses; toxic dose as low as 2 g/day
  • FDA-recommended daily limit: reduced from 4 g to 3 g/day (even lower for chronic alcohol users); acetaminophen component in opioid combinations limited to 325 mg per tablet

Treatment of Acetaminophen Overdose

  • Gastric lavage — early decontamination
  • Activated charcoal or cholestyramine — effective only if given within 30 min of ingestion; stomach lavage should precede oral agents
  • N-acetylcysteine (NAC) — the proven antidote; provides sulfhydryl groups to replete glutathione → prevents covalent binding of NAPQI to hepatocyte proteins
    • Effective if given within 8 h of ingestion (best outcome)
    • At least partially effective when given up to 24–36 h post-overdose
    • IV route preferred; loading dose 140 mg/kg over 1 h, then 70 mg/kg q4h × 15–20 doses
    • Routine use has substantially reduced fatal acetaminophen hepatotoxicity
    • NAC for non-acetaminophen acute liver failure showed benefit (especially early encephalopathy) in a double-blind RCT but not yet FDA-approved for this indication
  • Nomogram — plot acetaminophen levels at 4–8 h to determine risk (readily available in emergency departments)
  • Contact local poison control center whenever acetaminophen hepatotoxicity is suspected
  • Liver transplantation — if progressive jaundice, coagulopathy, confusion despite NAC therapy
  • Early arterial blood lactate >3.5 mmol/L → high likelihood of requiring transplantation
✓ Survivors
Survivors of acute acetaminophen overdose rarely have ongoing liver injury or sequelae, but may be at risk for repeat overdosing episodes.

Key Drug Profiles — High-Yield for Boards

Isoniazid (INH) — Idiosyncratic

  • 10% of patients develop elevated aminotransferases in first few weeks — usually mild (ALT <200 IU/L), self-limited, resolves despite continued use (adaptation)
  • Serious hepatocellular DILI has variable latency up to 6 months
  • More frequent in: alcoholics and those taking barbiturates, rifampin, pyrazinamide concurrently
  • Age-related risk: substantially increases after age 35; highest frequency >50 years; lowest <20 years. Even in >50 with careful monitoring, incidence only ~2%
  • Fever, rash, eosinophilia are distinctly unusual (unlike many other idiosyncratic reactions)
  • Liver biopsy: morphology similar to viral hepatitis or bridging hepatic necrosis
  • Fatal outcome almost always occurs in the setting of continued INH use despite clinically apparent illness → educate patients about symptoms (nausea, fatigue, jaundice)
  • A clinical picture resembling chronic hepatitis has been observed in a few patients

Sodium Valproate — Toxic + Idiosyncratic

  • 45% of patients have asymptomatic aminotransferase elevations — "adaptive," clinically unimportant (majority can continue therapy)
  • Rare patients develop jaundice, encephalopathy, hepatic failure: biopsy shows microvesicular fat + bridging hepatic necrosis in centrilobular zone
  • Most likely not directly hepatotoxic — metabolite 4-pentenoic acid appears to be responsible
  • Predominantly affects children (#1 antiepileptic drug causing liver transplant candidacy in children) but also adults
  • Mitochondrial enzyme deficiencies markedly increase susceptibility
  • Carnitine depletion — valproate depletes carnitine; IV carnitine may ameliorate toxicity
  • HLA associations: DR4 and B*1502; also mutations in mitochondrial DNA polymerase gamma 1
  • Bile duct injury may also occur

Nitrofurantoin — Idiosyncratic

  • Common UTI antibiotic — frequently used and reused for recurrent cystitis in women → prolonged, intermittent cumulative exposure
  • May cause acute hepatitis (can be fatal) OR more frequently chronic hepatitis indistinguishable from autoimmune hepatitis
  • Longer latency than most DILI agents — because of intermittent, recurrent use
  • Autoantibodies: anti-nuclear (ANA), anti-smooth muscle (SMA), anti-mitochondrial (AMA) — may subside after drug withdrawal
  • Glucocorticoids or other immunosuppressives may be needed to resolve the autoimmune injury
  • Cirrhosis can develop if not recognized quickly
  • Also causes interstitial pulmonary fibrosis (chronic cough and dyspnea) — resolves slowly after drug withdrawal
  • Similar pattern seen with minocycline (repeat acne treatment in teenagers), hydralazine, and α-methyldopa

Amoxicillin-Clavulanate — Idiosyncratic Mixed

  • #1 most common DILI agent in the United States AND Europe
  • Mixed or primarily cholestatic injury
  • Delayed onset — liver injury may manifest after the drug has been stopped (long latency)
  • High prevalence reflects the very frequent use for respiratory infections (community-acquired pneumonia)
  • Mechanism unclear: amoxicillin is thought responsible, potentiated by clavulanate (which itself is not toxic)
  • HLA haplotypes linked (like flucloxacillin)
  • Symptoms: nausea, anorexia, fatigue, jaundice (may be prolonged), pruritus; rash is uncommon
  • Occasionally → vanishing bile duct syndrome (permanent injury to small bile ducts → progressive paucity of ducts on biopsy)

Amiodarone — Toxic + Idiosyncratic

  • 15–50% of patients develop modest aminotransferase elevations — may remain stable or diminish despite continuation; also causes hepatomegaly in a proportion
  • Clinically important liver disease in <5%
  • Direct effects common to all long-term users: ultrastructural phospholipidosis (no clinical disease) + interference with CYP450 metabolism of other drugs
  • Drug and its metabolite (desethylamiodarone) accumulate in hepatocyte lysosomes and mitochondria and in bile duct epithelium
  • Rare clinically apparent disease: pseudoalcoholic liver injury — ranges from steatosis → alcoholic hepatitis–like picture (neutrophilic infiltration + Mallory's hyaline) → cirrhosis
  • Phospholipid-laden lysosomal lamellar bodies on electron microscopy — distinguishes amiodarone from true alcoholic hepatitis
  • Rarely: acute idiosyncratic hepatocellular or cholestatic hepatitis; granulomas
  • Long half-life → liver injury may persist for months after stopping the drug

Anabolic Steroids — Cholestatic Reaction

  • Most common CAM-type DILI = profound cholestasis from anabolic steroids used by body builders
  • Young male + cholestatic profile (rather than hepatitic) → almost always body-building supplement or androgen congener
  • Mechanism: injury to bile transport pumps → intense cholestasis
  • Serum ALT usually <100 IU/L; serum bilirubin frequently >342 μmol/L (20 mg/dL)
  • ALP moderately elevated; no substantial inflammation or necrosis on biopsy
  • Resolution is the rule but may take 10 weeks or more of jaundice
  • In prescription use for bone marrow failure: also associated with hepatic centrizonal sinusoidal dilatation, peliosis hepatis, hepatic adenoma, hepatocellular carcinoma

Statins (HMG-CoA Reductase Inhibitors) — Idiosyncratic

  • 1–2% of patients taking any statin have asymptomatic, reversible aminotransferase elevations >3× ULN
  • Minor aminotransferase rises in larger proportion during first few weeks — usually transient
  • Clinically meaningful aminotransferase elevations are so rare that they do not differ in meta-analyses from placebo recipients
  • Expert consensus (National Lipid Association): liver test monitoring NOT necessary in patients treated with statins; statin therapy need not be stopped for asymptomatic aminotransferase elevations
  • Statin hepatotoxicity is NOT increased in chronic hepatitis C, hepatic steatosis, or other underlying liver diseases → statins can be used safely in these patients

TMP-SMX — Idiosyncratic Hypersensitivity

  • Hepatotoxicity caused by the sulfamethoxazole component
  • Relatively uniform latency of several weeks
  • Often accompanied by eosinophilia, rash, and other hypersensitivity features — including DRESS syndrome
  • Biochemically and histologically: predominant acute hepatocellular necrosis, cholestatic features also frequent; rarely severe cholangiolytic pattern
  • Tissue eosinophilia and granulomas may be seen on biopsy
  • Most cases self-limited; rare fatalities
  • Risk increased in HIV-infected persons
  • Unique HLA associations in European Americans and African Americans identified

Herbal/CAM & Immunotherapy DILI

Herbal and Complementary Medicines

  • Account for >20% of DILI in the United States currently
  • Lack prospective safety oversight; efficacy often unproven
  • Most common category besides anabolic steroids: weight loss agents
  • Implicated herbal products: Jin Bu Huan, xiao-chai-hu-tang, germander, chaparral, senna, mistletoe, skullcap, ma huang (ephedra), kava, celandine, comfrey (pyrrolizidine alkaloids), green tea extract (Camellia sinensis), Hydroxycut, OxyElite Pro, LipoKinetix, Herbalife
  • Jin Bu Huan histology: focal hepatocellular necrosis, mixed mononuclear portal tract infiltration, coagulative necrosis, apoptotic degeneration, tissue eosinophilia, microvesicular steatosis
  • Pyrrolizidine alkaloids (often contaminants in Chinese herbal preparations) → venoocclusive injury → sinusoidal hepatic vein obstruction
  • Megadoses of vitamin A → hepatic injury
  • Herbal medicines may induce CYP450 → amplify toxicity of conventional drugs (and vice versa)
  • Many "herbal" products actually contain synthetic chemicals (e.g., "antirheumatic herbs" found to contain diclofenac)
  • Always ask about herbal/dietary supplements in every patient with liver disease — they often don't disclose without direct questioning
📋 Reference Resource
LiverTox website (livertox.nih.gov) — comprehensive, authoritative, up-to-date DILI information maintained by NIH/National Library of Medicine. Use as reference for any drug-liver association.

Immune Checkpoint Inhibitor Hepatotoxicity

  • New class of cancer immunotherapy → new kind of hepatotoxicity via immune activation
  • Three classes:
    • CTLA-4 inhibitor: ipilimumab
    • PD-1 inhibitors: pembrolizumab, nivolumab
    • PD-L1 inhibitors
  • Active hepatitis develops within weeks of starting any of these agents
  • Associated with positive ANAs; responds to glucocorticoid therapy
  • Histology: nonspecific acute hepatic injury mediated predominantly by CD8+ lymphocytes; does NOT resemble chronic autoimmune hepatitis
  • Immune-mediated injury to other organs also seen: thyroid, muscle, colon
  • Rarely fatal; agents may need to be halted temporarily but can often be restarted if patient showing favorable antitumor response (and tolerated better on retreatment)

HAART (HIV Antiretroviral Therapy)

  • Drug hepatotoxicity is common but complex in HIV due to many competing causes (viral co-infections, fatty liver, infiltrative disease, mycobacterial infection)
  • Most implicated agents:
    • NRTI: zidovudine, didanosine, stavudine (mitochondrial toxicity → microvesicular steatosis + lactic acidosis)
    • Protease inhibitors: ritonavir, indinavir, tipranavir, amprenavir (with ritonavir)
    • NNRTI: nevirapine (most), efavirenz (less)
  • Didanosine or stavudine + ribavirin = contraindicated in HIV/HCV co-infection → severe mitochondrial toxicity and lactic acidosis
  • HAART immune reconstitution can cause immunologically mediated liver injury in HBV co-infection if HBV-active antivirals are withdrawn
  • HIV + low CD4+ → ↑ rate of hepatic fibrosis in HCV co-infection
  • HAART can ↑ aminotransferases and HCV RNA in HCV co-infection

Treatment of DILI — General Principles

  • Treatment is largely supportive — except acetaminophen (NAC) and a few special cases
  • Withdraw the suspected agent at the first sign of adverse reaction OR when aminotransferases reach 5× ULN
    • Multiple studies show lethal outcomes follow continued use despite symptoms and signs of liver injury
  • Acute liver failure develops in 10% of DILI patients; once reached:
    • Spontaneous recovery: <30%
    • Liver transplantation: >40% of those who reach liver failure severity

⚠ Direct Toxin — Don't Miss Other Organs
In cases of direct hepatotoxins (e.g., acetaminophen, carbon tetrachloride), liver involvement should not divert attention from renal or other organ involvement, which may also threaten survival independently.

Exam Traps

Trap 01Amoxicillin-clavulanate hepatotoxicity — the timing trap
A 55-year-old woman was treated for community-acquired pneumonia with amoxicillin-clavulanate 10 days ago. The antibiotic course has been completed. She now presents with jaundice and pruritus. Her bilirubin is 8 mg/dL, ALP is 4× ULN, ALT is 2.5× ULN. What is the most likely diagnosis?
Drug-induced cholestatic hepatitis from amoxicillin-clavulanate. The key trap: liver injury from amoxicillin-clavulanate can manifest AFTER the drug has already been stopped (delayed onset is characteristic). The cholestatic pattern (low R-value, high ALP, pruritus) and the recent antibiotic course (even though completed) are the clues. This is the #1 most common DILI agent.
Trap 02Acetaminophen — the low bilirubin paradox
A patient arrives in the ED. Aminotransferases are 11,000 IU/L. Bilirubin is 2.1 mg/dL. The patient denies any drug ingestion. What is the diagnosis and what should you do?
Presumptive acetaminophen hepatotoxicity. The biochemical signature is extremely high aminotransferases (far exceeding viral hepatitis) with disproportionately LOW bilirubin early — this combination is the hallmark of acetaminophen's hyperacute injury. Do NOT let denial or altered mentation stop you. Measure acetaminophen levels, start N-acetylcysteine immediately, contact poison control. NAC is safe even if diagnosis is uncertain.
Trap 03INH adaptation — when to continue vs stop
A 48-year-old with a positive QuantiFERON test is started on INH prophylaxis. At month 1, ALT is 180 IU/L (ULN = 40). She is asymptomatic. What do you do?
Continue INH and monitor closely. Up to 10% of patients on INH develop mild aminotransferase elevations (ALT <200 IU/L) in the first few weeks — this is adaptive and resolves despite continued drug use. The threshold for stopping INH is: ALT >5× ULN, OR any elevation with symptoms (nausea, fatigue, jaundice, RUQ pain). Stopping for adaptive mild elevations is unnecessary. Fatal outcomes almost always occur when the drug is continued DESPITE clinically apparent illness — educate patients about symptom awareness.
Trap 04Autoimmune hepatitis — or is it a drug?
A 35-year-old woman presents with chronic hepatitis — elevated aminotransferases, positive ANA, elevated IgG. Liver biopsy shows interface hepatitis identical to autoimmune hepatitis. What is the most important step before diagnosing autoimmune hepatitis?
Obtain a thorough drug history, specifically asking about nitrofurantoin, minocycline, hydralazine, and α-methyldopa. These four drugs are the most likely to produce a phenotype of autoimmune hepatitis with high-likelihood positive ANA. Drug-induced autoimmune hepatitis is histologically indistinguishable from idiopathic AIH. If a drug is the cause, stopping it (with or without a course of corticosteroids) may resolve the disease — whereas idiopathic AIH requires long-term immunosuppression.
Trap 05Anabolic steroid DILI — the profile mismatch
A 22-year-old male bodybuilder presents with jaundice. Bilirubin = 22 mg/dL. ALT = 85 IU/L. ALP is mildly elevated. No fever, no rash, no eosinophilia. He says he is only using "supplements from the gym." What is happening?
Anabolic steroid-induced cholestasis. The mismatch between profound jaundice (very high bilirubin) and very low ALT is the key clue — this is bland cholestasis (disruption of bile transport pumps) with minimal hepatocellular injury. Young male + cholestatic biochemistry = assume anabolic steroid until proven otherwise. "Gym supplements" often contain androgen congeners that are not declared. Resolution typically takes 10+ weeks of jaundice but is the rule.
Trap 06Statins in liver disease — are they safe?
A 58-year-old with well-compensated chronic hepatitis C and hypercholesterolemia is started on atorvastatin. Should his liver enzymes be monitored more closely?
No. Statin hepatotoxicity is NOT increased in patients with chronic hepatitis C, hepatic steatosis, or other underlying liver diseases. Statins are safe to use in these patients. Expert consensus (National Lipid Association) states that routine liver test monitoring is not required for patients on statins, and that therapy need not be discontinued for asymptomatic aminotransferase elevations. Clinically meaningful statin-related hepatotoxicity occurs at the same frequency as placebo in meta-analyses.
Trap 07Valproate toxicity — the wrong patient gets the blame
An 8-year-old child on valproate for epilepsy develops jaundice, encephalopathy, and coagulopathy. ALT and bilirubin are elevated. Biopsy shows microvesicular fat and centrilobular bridging necrosis. What special workup should be considered?
Evaluate for mitochondrial enzyme deficiencies. Valproate hepatotoxicity is predominantly seen in children and is MORE common in persons with mitochondrial enzyme deficiencies. The mechanism involves the toxic metabolite 4-pentenoic acid, and valproate also depletes carnitine — IV carnitine may ameliorate toxicity. HLA-DR4 and B*1502 haplotypes and mutations in mitochondrial DNA polymerase gamma 1 are associated. Valproate is the #1 antiepileptic drug causing candidacy for liver transplantation in children.
Trap 08The "safe" dose of acetaminophen in alcoholics
A known alcoholic takes 2 g/day of acetaminophen for chronic back pain, staying well within the standard recommended dose of 4 g/day. Can he safely continue this?
No — the toxic threshold in chronic alcoholics is as low as 2 g/day. Chronic alcohol use both induces CYP2E1 (increasing NAPQI production) AND suppresses hepatic glutathione synthesis (reducing the ability to neutralize NAPQI). Alcoholics should be explicitly warned about the dangers of even standard doses of acetaminophen. The FDA has recommended the general daily limit be reduced from 4 g to 3 g/day, with an even lower limit for chronic alcohol users.
Trap 09Hy's Law — what it means and what it predicts
During Phase 3 trials of a new drug, 5 of 1,200 enrolled patients develop jaundice. No cases of liver failure have occurred yet. Should this prompt concern?
Yes — this is a strong Hy's Law signal that should raise serious concern. Hy's Law (named after Dr. Hyman Zimmerman) states that jaundice in clinical trials predicts a 10:1 ratio: for every 10 jaundice cases, approximately 1 acute liver failure will occur. Even if no liver failures have been seen yet in the trial, the presence of Hy's Law cases often portends failure of regulatory approval. Troglitazone is the classic example where post-marketing failure occurred because this signal was not acted upon during trials.
Trap 10CAM hepatotoxicity — asking the right question
A 40-year-old woman presents with unexplained elevated aminotransferases for 3 months. She denies taking any medications. Viral hepatitis serology is negative. What critical question remains?
Ask specifically about herbal remedies, dietary supplements, vitamins, and products bought at health food stores or online. Herbal and dietary supplement-related DILI now accounts for >20% of all DILI cases in the USA. Patients frequently do not consider these "medications" and will not mention them unless specifically asked. Common culprits include green tea extract (Camellia sinensis), Hydroxycut, OxyElite Pro, kava, and weight-loss supplements. LiverTox (livertox.nih.gov) is the go-to reference.
