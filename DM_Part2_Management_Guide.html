01

Overall Goals of Therapy

The Three Goals
(1) Eliminate symptoms related to hyperglycemia  •  (2) Reduce or eliminate long-term microvascular and macrovascular complications  •  (3) Allow the patient to achieve as normal a lifestyle as possible.

Symptoms of diabetes (polyuria, polydipsia, blurry vision) usually resolve when plasma glucose is <11.1 mmol/L (200 mg/dL). Therefore, most DM treatment focuses on goals 2 and 3 — preventing long-term complications, not just relieving symptoms.

Comprehensive Diabetes Care — Key Elements

  • Individualized glycemic goal + therapeutic plan (shared decision-making)
  • Blood glucose monitoring (CGM or capillary fingerstick BGM)
  • HbA1c testing 2–4 times per year
  • Lifestyle management: nutrition therapy, physical activity, psychosocial care
  • Detection/prevention/management of DM-related complications (eye, foot, neuropathy, kidney)
  • Management of associated conditions: blood pressure, lipids, obesity
  • Consider screening for MASLD (metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease) in T2DM if other risk factors present
  • Immunizations: influenza, pneumococcal, hepatitis B, COVID-19, RSV (>60 years)
Key Concept
Comprehensive diabetes care is far more than glucose management. Treating associated conditions (hypertension, dyslipidemia, obesity) and detecting complications early reduces morbidity and mortality as much as glycemic control itself — especially in T2DM.
02

Monitoring Glycemic Control

Two Complementary Monitoring Approaches

CGM / BGM — Short-Term

Provides a picture of short-term glycemic patterns

CGM: interstitial glucose sensor; data every 5 minutes; may lag when glucose changes rapidly or during exercise

Sensors replaced every 10–14 days (subcutaneous); alternative device placed surgically, replaced every 6–12 months

Output: Ambulatory Glucose Profile (AGP) — includes TIR, time above/below range, glucose variability, GMI (correlates with HbA1c)

BGM (fingerstick): <2 μL blood; essential backup when CGM malfunctions

HbA1c — Long-Term

Reflects average glycemia over previous 2–3 months

Erythrocyte lifespan ~120 days; preceding month contributes ~50% to HbA1c value

Does NOT detect glycemic variability or recent intercurrent illnesses

Measure at least twice/year if at goal; every 3 months if not at goal or therapy changed

Must be correlated to DCCT reference assay (NGSP-approved method)

Conditions That Falsely Alter HbA1c

HbA1c Unreliable — Use Glucose-Based Tests Instead
Hemoglobinopathies (sickle cell, thalassemia), anemias, reticulocytosis, transfusions, uremia, G6PD variants, hemodialysis, erythropoietin therapy, HIV treatment, certain ancestries. Also: African Americans have HbA1c ~0.3% higher than non-Hispanic whites or Hispanics for the same mean glucose.

Alternative glycation markers when HbA1c is unreliable: fructosamine or glycated albumin — reflect glycemia over prior 2–4 weeks.

Glycemic Targets

Core Principle
Glycemic targets must be individualized. Regardless of baseline level, any improvement in glycemic control reduces risk of complications — most notably microvascular. However, hypoglycemia must be avoided, especially in high-risk patients.

Why Not Always Target <7%?
In patients with established ASCVD and type 2 DM, intense glycemic control trials (ACCORD, ADVANCE, VADT) showed that hypoglycemia in high-risk populations is associated with cardiovascular events and mortality. Near-normal glycemia is therefore NOT the goal in this population.

Key Clinical Trials on Glycemic Control

DCCT/EDIC — Type 1 DM

Intensive glycemic control in early T1DM → reduced nonfatal MI, stroke, and CV death almost two decades later (metabolic memory)

Intensive control reduces microvascular complications

ACCORD / ADVANCE / VADT — Type 2 DM

Intense glycemic control beneficial for ASCVD in some T2DM populations

But: hypoglycemia in high-risk ASCVD patients linked to CV events and mortality

These trials predate GLP-1 RA and SGLT-2i — those agents show greater CV benefit

Why Rapid > Regular for Prandial Coverage
Rapid-acting analogues (aspart, lispro, glulisine) have less tendency for self-aggregation → more rapid absorption and onset, shorter duration. This means their decay corresponds to the decline in plasma glucose after a meal → fewer hypoglycemic episodes. Regular is reserved for IV infusion (less expensive, equally effective IV).

Type 1 DM — Insulin Regimen Principles

  • Goal: mimic physiologic insulin secretion (basal + prandial)
  • Total daily dose T1DM: 0.4–1.0 units/kg/day
  • 30–50% as basal (glargine or degludec); remainder as prandial (aspart, lispro, or glulisine)
  • Insulin-to-carbohydrate ratio: typically 1 unit per 10–15 g carbohydrate (individualized)
  • Correction factor: estimated from 1500 ÷ total daily insulin dose = mg/dL drop per 1 unit
  • Long-acting insulin dose adjusted based on fasting glucose; prandial adjusted for pre-meal glucose + carb intake
  • All T1DM patients should have a filled glucagon prescription
Key Limitation of Exogenous Insulin
Injected insulin enters the systemic circulation immediately, whereas endogenous insulin is secreted into the portal venous system. This exposes the liver to subphysiologic insulin levels — requiring higher peripheral insulin levels to restrain hepatic glucose production. No current regimen precisely reproduces physiologic secretion.

Insulin Delivery Systems

MDI (Multiple Daily Injections)

Basal (glargine/degludec) + bolus (aspart/lispro/glulisine)

Most flexibility; best chance for near normoglycemia in T1DM

CSII (Continuous Subcutaneous Insulin Infusion)

Insulin pump; requires manual input for boluses

Challenges: infusion site infection, occlusion → hyperglycemia or DKA

Short half-life of rapid-acting analogue → DKA quickly if delivery interrupted

AID (Automated Insulin Delivery)

Pump + CGM + algorithm = closes the loop

Preferred delivery for most T1DM (cost/coverage are barriers)

Hybrid closed-loop: automates basal; patient inputs prandial bolus

Can predictively suspend basal for hypoglycemia prevention

Combination Insulins — NOT for T1DM

  • 70/30 (NPH + regular), 75/25 (protamine lispro + lispro), 50/50 — convenient (2 injections/day)
  • Do NOT allow independent adjustment of short-acting and long-acting insulin
  • Not appropriate in T1DM management

Pramlintide (Amylin Analogue) — Adjunct to Insulin

  • Approved for insulin-treated T1DM and T2DM
  • Mechanism: slows gastric emptying + suppresses glucagon (does NOT alter insulin levels)
  • HbA1c reduction: 0.25–0.5%
  • T1DM dose: start 15 μg SC before each meal → max 30–60 μg
  • T2DM dose: start 60 μg SC before each meal → max 120 μg
  • SE: nausea, vomiting (titrate slowly); ↑ risk of hypoglycemia with insulin → reduce rapid-acting insulin initially
  • CI: hypoglycemia unawareness (pramlintide suppresses glucagon → worsens hypoglycemia recovery)
  • Do NOT combine with other drugs that slow GI motility

Glucose-Lowering Drug Classes

Master Framework — Before Memorizing Each Drug
Drugs that do NOT cause hypoglycemia alone: metformin, SGLT-2i, DPP-4i, GLP-1 RA, TZD, α-glucosidase inhibitors. Drugs that DO cause hypoglycemia: sulfonylureas, glinides, insulin, pramlintide (when combined with insulin).

1. Metformin (Biguanide)

Mechanism & Benefits

↓ Hepatic glucose production (primary) + modest ↑ peripheral glucose utilization + influences gut function

HbA1c reduction: 1–2%

Weight neutral; no hypoglycemia; inexpensive; modest ↓ CV events; lowers FPG and insulin levels; improves lipid profile

Extended-release: fewer GI side effects

Start low → escalate every 1–2 weeks to max 2000 mg/day

Side Effects & Contraindications

SE: diarrhea, nausea, anorexia, metallic taste, lactic acidosis (rare), vitamin B12 deficiency (monitor)

CI:

  • GFR <30 mL/min (moderate renal insufficiency)
  • Any form of acidosis
  • Unstable CHF
  • Liver disease
  • Radiographic contrast (hold; restart after renal function confirmed)
  • Hospitalized patients; patients with nothing orally

2. SGLT-2 Inhibitors

Mechanism & Benefits

↑ Renal glucose excretion — inhibits SGLT-2 in proximal convoluted tubule → glucose-lowering is insulin-independent

Drugs: canagliflozin, dapagliflozin, empagliflozin, ertugliflozin, bexagliflozin, sotagliflozin (SGLT-1/2)

HbA1c: 0.5–1.0%

Renal protective, ↓ CV events, ↓ heart failure hospitalizations, modest weight loss, modest BP reduction (3–6 mmHg systolic), no hypoglycemia

Empagliflozin or canagliflozin → ↓ ASCVD events and all-cause CV mortality in T2DM + established ASCVD

Side Effects & Contraindications

SE:

  • ↑ Genital mycotic infections (both sexes) + necrotizing fasciitis of perineum (Fournier’s gangrene)
  • Polyuria, dehydration, acutely impaired kidney function
  • Euglycemic DKA — SGLT-2i ↑ glucagon → ↑ hepatic ketone production; glucose may be normal or mildly elevated
  • Hyperkalemia (impair proximal Na/K reabsorption)
  • Dapagliflozin: possible ↑ bladder cancer risk

CI: T1DM or pancreatogenic DM (insulin deficiency); moderate renal insufficiency for initiation (eGFR <45 mL/min); do NOT use in stage 4 CKD (eGFR <30)

Perioperative: discontinue 3–4 days before surgery; stop during serious illness

3. GLP-1 Receptor Agonists

Mechanism & Benefits

Mechanism: ↑ glucose-stimulated insulin, ↓ glucagon, slow gastric emptying, satiety (GLP-1 receptor expressed in brain)

Drugs: dulaglutide, exenatide, liraglutide, lixisenatide, semaglutide (oral available), tirzepatide (GLP-1+GIP)

HbA1c: GLP-1 RA 0.5–1.0%; tirzepatide 1.8–2.4%

Weight loss; no hypoglycemia (glucose-dependent — only works when glucose > fasting); ↓ CV events; modest renoprotection

Long-acting (weekly): sustained-release exenatide, dulaglutide, lixisenatide, semaglutide

Tirzepatide (GLP-1+GIP dual agonist) → greatest weight loss of any glucose-lowering drug class

Liraglutide: ↓ CVD events + ↓ diabetic kidney disease in T2DM + established CVD

Semaglutide: ↓ CVD events + ↓ diabetic kidney disease, but ↑ retinopathy-related complications (with rapid HbA1c improvement)

Dulaglutide: ↓ CVD events + ↓ composite microvascular retinopathy/nephropathy

Side Effects & Contraindications

SE: nausea/vomiting (dose-limiting; start low and titrate), possibly associated with pancreatitis, possibly worsen retinopathy (with rapid glycemic improvement)

Black box warning: thyroid C-cell tumors in rodents → CI: medullary carcinoma of thyroid or MEN

CI: medullary thyroid carcinoma/MEN; renal disease; history of pancreatitis; agents that also slow GI motility; previous ileus; gastroparesis history (tirzepatide)

Slow gastric emptying → may influence absorption of other drugs

GLP-1 RA levels of activity > DPP-4 inhibitors (which only prolong endogenous GLP-1)

Higher doses of liraglutide and semaglutide approved for obesity treatment

4. DPP-4 Inhibitors

Mechanism & Benefits

Prolong endogenous GLP-1 (and GIP) action by inhibiting DPP-4 enzyme → ↑ insulin, ↓ glucagon

Drugs: sitagliptin, saxagliptin, linagliptin, alogliptin, vildagliptin

HbA1c: 0.5–0.8%

Well tolerated; no hypoglycemia; no weight gain; preferential effect on postprandial glucose

GLP-1 RA action > DPP-4 inhibitor action (DPP-4i only restores endogenous levels)

Side Effects

Angioedema/urticaria; immune-mediated dermatologic effects (rash, hypersensitivity including anaphylaxis, Stevens-Johnson)

Rarely associated with pancreatitis

Severe joint pain reported

↓ Dose with renal insufficiency (except linagliptin — hepatically cleared)

5. Sulfonylureas & Glinides (Insulin Secretagogues)

Mechanism & Key Facts

Bind SUR subunit of K-ATP channel → close it → depolarization → insulin secretion (independent of glucose)

Most effective in T2DM of recent onset (<5 years) with residual beta cell function

Sulfonylureas: glimepiride, glipizide, glyburide; HbA1c 1–2%

Glimepiride and glipizide → once daily; preferred over glyburide in elderly

Glinides: repaglinide, nateglinide; HbA1c 0.5–1%; short half-life → give immediately before each meal; target postprandial glucose excursions

Repaglinide: may be used cautiously in CKD (exception to renal CI)

Side Effects & Interactions

Hypoglycemia (most important; related to: delayed meals, exercise, alcohol, renal insufficiency)

Weight gain (from ↑ insulin levels)

Metabolized in liver → active metabolites cleared by kidney → CI: significant hepatic or renal dysfunction

Drug interactions: warfarin, aspirin, ketoconazole, fluconazole, α-glucosidase inhibitors

Antibiotic interactions: discontinue sulfonylureas when adding fluoroquinolones, clarithromycin, TMP-SMX, metronidazole, fluconazole

Durability of glycemic control slightly less for sulfonylureas vs metformin or TZD

6. Thiazolidinediones (TZD)

Mechanism & Benefits

Agonists of PPAR-γ nuclear receptor (found at highest levels in adipocytes) → ↓ insulin resistance; promote adipocyte differentiation; ↓ hepatic fat; redistribute fat from central to peripheral

Drugs: pioglitazone, rosiglitazone

HbA1c: 0.5–1.4%

↓ Insulin requirements; no hypoglycemia

Effects take several days (vs immediate for sulfonylureas, GLP-1 RA)

Pioglitazone: ↑ HDL more, ↓ LDL less, ↓ TG; may ↓ stroke/MI in insulin-resistant non-diabetics with prior stroke/TIA

Side Effects & Contraindications

Weight gain 2–3 kg; peripheral edema; mild ↓ hematocrit; mild ↑ plasma volume

CHF — more common; CI: CHF class III/IV; hepatic insufficiency

Fractures — ↑ risk in postmenopausal women

Worsening of diabetic macular edema (rare FDA alert)

Pioglitazone: possible ↑ bladder cancer risk (FDA review)

Induces ovulation in premenopausal women with PCOS → warn about pregnancy risk (safety not established)

Rosiglitazone: ↑ LDL, HDL, TG slightly

7. α-Glucosidase Inhibitors

  • Mechanism: ↓ GI glucose absorption by inhibiting enzyme that cleaves oligosaccharides into simple sugars
  • Drugs: acarbose, miglitol; HbA1c 0.5–0.8%
  • Taken just before each meal; reduce postprandial glucose (unique mechanism)
  • SE: GI flatulence, diarrhea, abdominal distention (from oligosaccharides reaching large bowel); elevated LFTs
  • CI: inflammatory bowel disease, gastroparesis, serum creatinine >177 μmol/L (2 mg/dL)
  • Key board pearl: If hypoglycemia occurs while on α-glucosidase inhibitors → use pure glucose (not complex carbohydrates, which won’t be absorbed)
  • Can ↑ sulfonylurea levels and increase hypoglycemia risk
  • Used commonly in South Asian patients; less common in US/Europe

T2DM Treatment Algorithm

The Paradigm Shift
The approach to T2DM has changed dramatically. GLP-1 RAs and SGLT-2 inhibitors reduce cardiovascular events AND slow renal disease progression — meaning a GLP-1 RA or SGLT-2 inhibitor should be used in most individuals with T2DM, not just when metformin fails.

Initial Choice Based on Hyperglycemia Severity

  • Mild FPG 126–199 mg/dL: often responds to single oral agent
  • Moderate FPG 200–250 mg/dL: usually requires >1 oral agent or insulin
  • Severe FPG >250 mg/dL: may require insulin initially — reduces “glucose toxicity” to beta cells, may then allow oral agents alone
  • Severe FPG 250–300 mg/dL or symptomatic: consider insulin as initial therapy

Key Rules for T2DM Combination Therapy

  • Most individuals will eventually require multiple agents — T2DM is progressive
  • Reassess HbA1c every 3 months; if still above target, add third agent including basal insulin
  • GRADE study: adding liraglutide or basal insulin to metformin → better glycemic control than glimepiride or sitagliptin
  • Effective combinations: metformin + SGLT-2i, metformin + GLP-1 RA, metformin + insulin, long-acting insulin + GLP-1 RA
  • Adding GLP-1 RA limits weight gain from insulin and may ↓ insulin dose needed

T2DM Insulin Initiation

  • Start with long-acting insulin at bedtime: 0.1–0.4 U/kg/day (NPH, glargine, or degludec)
  • Bedtime insulin is more effective than morning for fasting hyperglycemia (hepatic glucose production peak at night)
  • Glargine at bedtime → less nocturnal hypoglycemia than NPH at bedtime
  • Adjust dose in 10–20% increments based on CGM/BGM
  • As T2DM progresses → add prandial insulin (basal/bolus regimen)
  • Large insulin requirements (>200 units/day) → use concentrated forms (U-500) to reduce injection volume
  • “Sliding scale” alone is inadequate for inpatient glucose management

Lifestyle Management

Medical Nutrition Therapy (MNT)

  • T1DM focus: match carbohydrate intake temporally and quantitatively with insulin dose (informed by CGM)
  • T2DM focus: weight loss (goal 5–10%), address CV risk factors; very-low-carbohydrate diets may produce rapid, dramatic glucose lowering in new-onset T2DM
  • Avoid fructose- and sucrose-containing beverages
  • Use glycemic index: estimates postprandial glucose rise per food
  • Sleep deprivation and shift work → ↑ weight gain and insulin resistance
  • Routine supplements of vitamins or antioxidants not supported by evidence; vitamin D and calcium as per general population recommendations

Physical Activity

  • ADA recommendation: 150 min/week of moderate aerobic activity, distributed over ≥3 days, no gaps >2 days
  • Benefits: CV risk reduction, ↓ BP, maintained muscle mass, ↓ body fat, ↑ insulin sensitivity
  • Untreated proliferative retinopathy: relative contraindication to vigorous exercise (risk of vitreous hemorrhage or retinal detachment)
Exercise in T1DM — High Risk of Both Hyper- and Hypoglycemia
T1DM patients lack normal glucoregulatory response (normally insulin ↓, glucagon ↑ during exercise). Management rules:
  • Monitor glucose before, during, and after exercise
  • Delay exercise if glucose >250 mg/dL AND ketones present (low insulin → catecholamines ↑ → ↑ glucose, ↑ ketones → DKA)
  • If glucose <90 mg/dL: ingest carbohydrate before exercising
  • Monitor glucose during exercise; ingest carbohydrate as needed
  • Decrease insulin doses before AND after exercise; inject into non-exercising area
  • T2DM: exercise-related hypoglycemia less common but can occur with insulin or secretagogues

Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)

Core Definition
DKA = relative or absolute insulin deficiency + counterregulatory hormone excess (glucagon, catecholamines, cortisol, GH) → hyperglycemia + ketosis + metabolic acidosis. Most cases in T1DM. Also occurs in: obese young adults of Hispanic/African descent (ketosis-prone T2DM), SGLT-2 inhibitor use.

Pathophysiology

↓ Insulin / ↑ Glucagon, Catecholamines, Cortisol, GH
↓
↑ Hepatic gluconeogenesis + glycogenolysis
→ Hyperglycemia → glucosuria → osmotic diuresis → volume depletion
↑ Lipolysis from adipocytes → ↑ Free fatty acids → ↑ Hepatic ketone body synthesis
→ Ketonemia + metabolic acidosis (↑ anion gap)

Precipitating Events

Common Precipitants
  • Inadequate insulin — most common; omission, pump failure, eating disorder, rising insulin costs
  • Infection — pneumonia, UTI, gastroenteritis, sepsis (look for even without fever)
  • Infarction — cerebral, coronary, mesenteric, peripheral
  • Pancreatitis; cocaine use; pregnancy
  • SGLT-2 inhibitor therapy (euglycemic DKA)
Clinical Features
  • Symptoms develop over 24 hours
  • Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain (may mimic acute abdomen)
  • Thirst, polyuria
  • Tachycardia, dehydration, hypotension
  • Kussmaul respirations + fruity breath odor (metabolic acidosis + acetone)
  • Lethargy → coma with severe DKA
  • Cerebral edema — most serious non-metabolic complication; most often in children

Key Laboratory Pearls

  • K⁺ paradox: Total body K⁺ deficit 3–5 meq/kg BUT serum K⁺ may be normal or elevated at presentation (acidosis drives K⁺ out of cells + volume depletion concentrates serum K⁺). During treatment, K⁺ drops rapidly.
  • Sodium correction: measured serum Na ↓ due to hyperglycemia. Add 1.6 meq Na per 100 mg/dL rise in glucose. Normal measured Na in DKA = more profound water deficit (not truly normal).
  • β-hydroxybutyrate preferred over nitroprusside for ketone measurement. Nitroprusside detects only acetoacetate and acetone (not β-OHB which is produced 3× more). Drugs causing false-positive nitroprusside: captopril, penicillamine, valproic acid.
  • Hyperamylasemia common in DKA — usually salivary origin, not diagnostic of pancreatitis; get serum lipase if pancreatitis suspected.
  • Degree of acidosis and hyperglycemia do NOT necessarily correlate (ketonemia is the consistent finding — distinguishes DKA from simple hyperglycemia).
  • BUN and creatinine elevated → intravascular volume depletion
  • Differential: starvation ketosis, alcoholic ketoacidosis (HCO3 usually >15), other increased anion gap acidoses

DKA Treatment — Step by Step

Step 1: Confirm diagnosis (↑ glucose, ↑ β-OHB, metabolic acidosis) + assess precipitating event
↓

→ then 0.45% saline at 250–500 mL/h
→ switch to 5–10% dextrose + 0.45% saline when glucose reaches 250 mg/dL
Ringer’s lactate: faster DKA resolution, less hyperchloremia than NS
↓
Check K⁺ FIRST: If K⁺ <3.3 meq/L → hold insulin; replece K⁺ first (giving insulin will worsen hypokalemia → fatal arrhythmia)
↓
Step 3 — Insulin: IV bolus 0.1 units/kg regular insulin → then continuous IV infusion 0.1 units/kg/h
If no response in 2–4h: 2–3× infusion rate
Mild–moderate DKA: SC rapid-acting insulin acceptable (0.1 U/kg then q1h)
↓

K⁺ <3.5: 10–20 meq/h IV until K⁺ >3.5

↓

↓
Resolution: Glucose goal 150–200 mg/dL; HCO3 ≥18; normal ketones and pH
Add long-acting insulin as soon as patient is eating — allow 2–4 hour overlap with IV infusion before stopping it
Important Sequence of Resolution
Hyperglycemia resolves faster than ketoacidosis. The anion gap normalizes before bicarbonate. A hyperchloremic acidosis (HCO3 15–18) often follows successful treatment — this is expected and gradually resolves. Resolution of euglycemic DKA should be based on bicarbonate correction, not glucose.
  • Mortality of DKA: <1% — related more to underlying precipitating event (infection, MI)
  • Even a single DKA episode → greatly increased 1-year mortality rate
  • Major non-metabolic complication: cerebral edema (mostly children during DKA resolution)
  • Other complications: venous thrombosis, upper GI bleeding, ARDS

Hyperglycemic Hyperosmolar State (HHS)

Core Distinction from DKA
HHS = extreme hyperglycemia + hyperosmolality + profound dehydration WITHOUT significant ketosis or acidosis. Occurs in elderly T2DM. Relative insulin deficiency (not absolute) — enough insulin to suppress ketogenesis but not hyperglycemia. Mortality up to 15% — far higher than DKA.

Clinical Presentation

  • Typical patient: elderly T2DM with several-week history of polyuria, weight loss, diminished oral intake
  • Culminates in mental confusion, lethargy, or coma
  • Absent: nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, Kussmaul respirations (distinguishes from DKA)
  • Physical exam: profound dehydration, hyperosmolality, hypotension, tachycardia, altered mental status
  • Precipitants: MI, stroke, sepsis, pneumonia; debilitating condition (prior stroke, dementia) limiting water intake

Why No Ketosis in HHS?

Mechanism — Not Fully Understood
Insulin deficiency in HHS is only relative and less severe than in DKA. Lower levels of counterregulatory hormones and free fatty acids than in DKA. Possible: liver is less capable of ketone body synthesis, or the insulin/glucagon ratio does not favor ketogenesis. Small amount of insulin is still present — enough to prevent uncontrolled lipolysis and ketogenesis.

Treatment

  • Fluid replacement more profound than DKA (fluid deficit often 9–10 L accumulated over days to weeks)
  • Initial: 1–3L of 0.9% NS over first 2–3h to stabilize hemodynamics
  • If Na >150 meq/L → use 0.45% saline
  • After hemodynamic stability → free water deficit reversal with hypotonic fluids (0.45% saline → D5W) over 1–2 days (200–300 mL/h); too rapid reversal may worsen neurologic function
  • Insulin: IV bolus 0.1 unit/kg → continuous infusion 0.1 unit/kg/h; same escalation as DKA
  • K⁺ repletion as dictated by serial measurements (patients on diuretics may have large K⁺ deficit + magnesium deficiency)
  • After recovery: discharge on insulin; some patients can later switch to oral agents

Diabetes Management in Hospital / Perioperative

Inpatient Glycemic Goals

Critically / Non-Critically Ill

Target: 140–180 mg/dL

NICE-SUGAR trial: strict control (81–108 mg/dL) → ↑ mortality + ↑ severe hypoglycemia. Avoid very tight control in ICU.

Selected Patients

Target: 110–140 mg/dL with hypoglycemia avoidance

Perioperative

Target: 80–180 mg/dL

Principles of Inpatient Insulin Management

  • Sliding scale alone is inadequate — treats glucose reactively; no basal coverage; not standard of care
  • Preferred: basal-bolus regimen — long-acting (basal) + prandial (rapid-acting) + corrective insulin
  • IV insulin infusion preferred in: ICU, clinically unstable patients — half-life of IV insulin is minutes (very short), allowing rapid adjustment
  • For relatively short (<4h) procedures → SC insulin may suffice
  • Regular insulin used IV (not analogues) — less expensive, equally effective IV
  • When stopping IV insulin infusion → give long-acting SC insulin 2–4 hours before stopping to avoid insulin deficiency gap

T1DM Perioperative Management

  • Should receive continuous insulin (IV infusion, insulin pump, or reduced SC long-acting)
  • Rapid-acting insulin alone is insufficient — prolonged procedure → insulin deficiency → DKA
  • If brief procedure (<4h) → reduced SC insulin (20–50% basal reduction + corrective rapid-acting as needed)
  • Prolonged perioperative period: IV insulin infusion preferred (0.5–1.0 units/h regular insulin)

Oral Agents in Hospital — Stop on Admission

  • All oral glucose-lowering agents should be discontinued on hospital admission
  • SGLT-2 inhibitors: stop up to 1 week prior to planned admission (euglycemic DKA risk, especially perioperative)
  • Metformin: stop if: fasting, contrast media, declining kidney function, unstable CHF (lactic acidosis risk)
  • Sulfonylureas: stop if fasting (hypoglycemia risk)
  • Once stable → oral agents may be resumed in anticipation of discharge

Special Inpatient Situations

TPN/TEN

TPN: IV insulin infusion preferred; after dose determined, portion may be added directly to TPN solution

TEN (enteral bolus): SC rapid-acting insulin before each bolus; start 1 unit per 10–15g carbohydrate

T1DM/pancreatogenic DM on TPN/TEN: also give long-acting insulin 0.1–0.2 units/kg/day (basal coverage if TPN is interrupted)

Glucocorticoids

↑ Insulin resistance + ↓ glucose utilization + ↑ hepatic glucose production + ↓ insulin secretion

Effects: dose-dependent, usually reversible, most pronounced postprandially

If FPG near normal → oral agents (sulfonylureas, metformin) may suffice

If FPG >200 mg/dL → insulin required

AM steroids → short-acting + NPH in the morning (target postprandial glucose excursions)

If hyperglycemia persists during chronic supraphysiologic steroids (>5 mg prednisone equivalent) → “steroid-induced diabetes”

Special Situations

Pregnancy and DM

  • GDM complicates ~7% (range 1–14%) of pregnancies; ↑ in Black and Hispanic populations
  • Glucose crosses placenta freely; insulin does NOT → maternal hyperglycemia → fetal hyperinsulinism → macrosomia
  • GDM treatment: MNT + insulin if hyperglycemia persists; oral agents not approved (metformin/glyburide studied but not FDA-approved)
  • Preconception DM: HbA1c target <6.5% before and throughout pregnancy
  • Risk of fetal malformations: 4–10× increased with uncontrolled DM at time of conception
  • HbA1c <6.0–6.5% → ↓ fetal macrosomia and neonatal hypoglycemia
  • Monitor HbA1c every 2 months throughout gestation

Older Adults

  • DM present in ~25% of individuals >65 years
  • Individualize goals based on: biologic age, functional status, living situation, cognitive status, other medications
  • Critical priority: avoidance of hypoglycemia — can worsen cognitive impairment and CVD
  • Robust elderly (80y+): HbA1c target <7.0–7.5%; complex/poor health: <8.0–8.5%
  • Consider adverse effects of drugs: heart failure (TZD), renal insufficiency, propensity for hypoglycemia (long-acting sulfonylureas)

Other Therapies — Bariatric Surgery

  • ADA guideline: consider metabolic surgery in T2DM with BMI >30 kg/m² if hyperglycemia inadequately controlled despite optimal medical therapy
  • Mechanism: caloric restriction + ↓ insulin resistance + altered gut hormones (GLP-1, GIP effects)
  • Very-low-calorie diet (800–1000 cal/day): dramatically improves T2DM, especially recent-onset; must be supervised + followed by weight-maintenance program

Emerging Therapies

  • Transplantation of insulin-producing cells from human pluripotent stem cells — clinical trials showing promise
  • Teplizumab (anti-CD3 monoclonal antibody, targets T lymphocytes): FDA approved for Stage 2 T1DM to delay onset of Stage 3
  • Agents targeting TXNIP (thioredoxin-interacting protein), especially Ca²⁺ channel blockers: promise in recent-onset T1DM

Exam Traps

Trap 01SGLT-2 Inhibitor + Normal Glucose = Still DKA
Patient on empagliflozin, presents with nausea, vomiting, Kussmaul respirations, anion gap metabolic acidosis. Blood glucose: 160 mg/dL. Question: “What is the most likely diagnosis?”
Correct Answer: Euglycemic DKA. SGLT-2 inhibitors increase glucagon and ketone body production. The ongoing glucosuria masks the expected hyperglycemia. Diagnosis is based on β-hydroxybutyrate >3.0 mmol/L + metabolic acidosis + anion gap — not glucose level. Resolution of euglycemic DKA is based on bicarbonate correction, NOT glucose correction. The trap is dismissing DKA because glucose appears near-normal.
Trap 02DKA Potassium — Start Insulin Without Checking K⁺?
A patient in DKA has serum K⁺ of 3.1 meq/L. The intern starts IV regular insulin immediately. Is this correct?
Correct Answer: NO — if K⁺ <3.3 meq/L, hold insulin and replace potassium first. Insulin drives K⁺ into cells, worsening hypokalemia → fatal cardiac arrhythmia. Total body K⁺ is depleted in all DKA (3–5 meq/kg deficit), even when serum K⁺ appears normal or high. The rule: K⁺ <3.3 → hold insulin + aggressive K⁺ replacement first. K⁺ >5.0 → start insulin, hold K⁺ supplementation until K⁺ falls.
Trap 03DKA vs HHS — Key Differentiators
Elderly T2DM patient, glucose 900 mg/dL, osmolality 340, HCO3 22 meq/L, no ketones, no Kussmaul respirations, altered mental status. Question: DKA or HHS? Next step?
Correct Answer: HHS. Key distinguishers: glucose typically >600 (vs 250–600 DKA); HCO3 >18 (vs <18 DKA); pH >7.3 (vs 6.8–7.3 DKA); NO ketosis; NO Kussmaul. The treatment shares elements with DKA but: fluid deficit is 9–10L (larger than DKA); replace free water deficit over 1–2 days (too rapid reversal → cerebral damage); use 0.45% saline if Na >150. HHS mortality up to 15% — much higher than DKA (<1%).
Trap 04GLP-1 RA + Medullary Thyroid Carcinoma = Contraindicated
A T2DM patient with a family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma. The physician wants to start a GLP-1 RA for weight loss and HbA1c control. Is this safe?
Correct Answer: Contraindicated. GLP-1 RAs carry a black box warning for thyroid C-cell tumors in rodents and are contraindicated in individuals with medullary carcinoma of the thyroid or multiple endocrine neoplasia (MEN). This applies to all GLP-1 RAs (liraglutide, semaglutide, dulaglutide, tirzepatide). Choose an alternative — SGLT-2 inhibitor is a good option for this patient’s goals.
Trap 05Sulfonylurea + Fluoroquinolone Antibiotic = Dangerous Interaction
A T2DM patient on glipizide develops a UTI and is started on ciprofloxacin. 2 days later she is found unresponsive with glucose of 28 mg/dL. What happened?
Correct Answer: Sulfonylurea + fluoroquinolone interaction. Fluoroquinolones, clarithromycin, TMP-SMX, metronidazole, and fluconazole interact with sulfonylureas and can potentiate hypoglycemia. When these antibiotics are added, sulfonylureas should be discontinued or the patient monitored very closely. This is a classic boards interaction — the answer to “what should you do when adding fluoroquinolone?” is: discontinue or reduce the sulfonylurea.
Trap 06α-Glucosidase Inhibitor Hypoglycemia — Use Glucose, NOT Sucrose
A patient on both acarbose and a sulfonylurea develops hypoglycemia. They reach for orange juice. Is this the right treatment?
Correct Answer: No — use pure glucose tablets, not orange juice or complex carbohydrates. α-Glucosidase inhibitors inhibit the enzyme that cleaves oligosaccharides to simple sugars. Therefore, sucrose, starches, and complex carbohydrates will NOT be absorbed quickly enough to treat hypoglycemia. Only pure glucose (or glucose tablets/IV dextrose) will work. This is the unique antidote for hypoglycemia in patients taking α-glucosidase inhibitors.
Trap 07Metformin + Contrast Media — What to Do
A T2DM patient on metformin is scheduled for a CT with contrast. What should you do about their metformin?
Correct Answer: Hold metformin before/during the procedure; restart only after confirmed preserved renal function (typically 48h post-contrast). Contrast media can cause acute kidney injury → accumulation of metformin → lactic acidosis (rare but potentially fatal). This is a standard CI for metformin. The mechanism: metformin itself doesn’t cause the problem — but if AKI occurs from contrast, metformin cannot be cleared and lactic acidosis results.
Trap 08Pramlintide + Hypoglycemia Unawareness = Contraindicated
A T1DM patient with known hypoglycemia unawareness wants to add pramlintide to better control postprandial glucose. Is this appropriate?
Correct Answer: Contraindicated. Pramlintide suppresses glucagon, which is one of the key counter-regulatory hormones for hypoglycemia recovery. In a patient who already has impaired hypoglycemia awareness (and therefore depends on glucagon for counter-regulation), pramlintide will further worsen hypoglycemia recovery and increase risk of severe, prolonged hypoglycemia. This is an absolute contraindication. For postprandial control in T1DM with hypoglycemia unawareness, CGM and AID are safer options.
Trap 09T1DM Surgery — Rapid-Acting Insulin Alone Is Not Enough
A T1DM patient is going for an elective hernia repair (3-hour procedure). The anesthesiologist plans to give rapid-acting insulin corrective doses as needed. Is this adequate?
Correct Answer: No — rapid-acting insulin alone is insufficient for T1DM perioperatively. T1DM patients require continuous insulin even when not eating — for basal glucose regulation and to prevent DKA. A prolonged procedure with only corrective rapid-acting doses can lead to insulin deficiency → DKA. The correct approach: IV insulin infusion or SC reduced long-acting insulin (20–50% basal reduction). For procedures <4h, reduced SC insulin may suffice but must include basal coverage.
Trap 10NICE-SUGAR — Tight Control in ICU Worsens Outcomes
An ICU nurse proposes targeting glucose 81–108 mg/dL in all diabetic patients on mechanical ventilation. Is this evidence-based?
Correct Answer: No — the NICE-SUGAR trial showed that targeting 81–108 mg/dL in ICU patients INCREASED mortality and severe hypoglycemia compared to a target of <180 mg/dL. The current ADA recommendation for critically ill patients is 140–180 mg/dL. Very strict glycemic control in acutely ill patients likely worsens outcomes. The selected patients target of 110–140 is for specific situations with avoidance of hypoglycemia — not the routine ICU goal.
Trap 11HbA1c + Sickle Cell Disease = Unreliable
A patient with sickle cell disease is being followed for pre-diabetes. HbA1c comes back at 5.4%. The physician concludes they do not have pre-diabetes. Is this conclusion reliable?
Correct Answer: HbA1c is unreliable in hemoglobinopathies. Sickle cell disease (and other hemoglobinopathies, hemolytic anemias) falsely lowers HbA1c because RBC turnover is accelerated — cells are younger on average and have less time to glycate. The “normal” HbA1c may mask true hyperglycemia. Use FPG or OGTT for accurate diagnosis. This applies to thalassemia, G6PD deficiency, anemias, reticulocytosis, uremia, hemodialysis, and HIV treatment.
Trap 12Combination Insulin in T1DM — Wrong Choice
A physician switches a T1DM patient from MDI to 70/30 (NPH + regular) twice daily for convenience. Is this appropriate?
Correct Answer: Not appropriate for T1DM. Combination (premixed) insulins do not allow independent adjustment of rapid-acting and long-acting doses. T1DM requires precise, individualized titration based on CGM data, carbohydrate intake, and activity — which premixed combinations cannot provide. Premixed insulins are sometimes used in T2DM for convenience but are never appropriate for T1DM management. The correct T1DM regimen: basal (glargine/degludec) + bolus (aspart/lispro/glulisine) with CGM or MDI, or AID system.
