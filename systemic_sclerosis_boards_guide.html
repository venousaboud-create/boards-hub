Definition, Classification & Subsets

Core Definition
Systemic Sclerosis (SSc) — Scleroderma — is an acquired, sporadic, autoimmune disease characterized by three simultaneous, interacting pathological processes: (1) Diffuse microangiopathy (small vessel disease), (2) Inflammation and autoimmunity, and (3) Visceral and vascular fibrosis affecting multiple organs. The word "systemic" is critical — this is not a skin disease with occasional organ complications; it is a multi-organ disease in which the skin is simply the most visible component.

ACR/EULAR Classification Criteria — Score ≥9 Classifies as SSc

>90% sensitivity and specificity. Designed for clinical research, not routine clinical practice.

Why These Scores
Bilateral skin induration crossing the MCP joints is essentially pathognomonic for SSc — hence 9 points alone classifies the patient. SSc-specific autoantibodies (ACA, Scl-70, RNA pol III) each earn 3 points because they are mutually exclusive (one patient, one dominant antibody), highly specific, and simultaneously define disease subtype and organ risk profile — they are diagnostically and prognostically powerful.

The Two Major Subsets — A Fundamental Clinical Division

The subset determines likely organ complications, autoantibody profile, rate of progression, and prognosis. This is not classification for its own sake — it is clinical decision-making architecture.

Limited Cutaneous SSc (lcSSc)

Skin rule: Confined to fingers, distal limbs (below elbows and knees), and face. Trunk always spared. Slow, indolent progression.

Vascular: Raynaud's precedes other manifestations by years–decades (10–15 yr prodrome). This long prodrome creates a diagnostic opportunity: Raynaud's + SSc-specific autoantibody + abnormal nailfold capillaries = close monitoring even without overt skin disease.

Late complications: Isolated PAH (obliterative pulmonary vasculopathy without ILD — different from dcSSc where PAH usually co-exists with ILD). Digital ischemic ulcers common (50%). Hypothyroidism, Sjögren's features, primary biliary cirrhosis. Renal crisis very rare (ACA is protective).

Characteristic antibody: Anti-centromere (ACA)
Prognosis: Generally more benign; long survival; morbidity driven by vascular complications.

Diffuse Cutaneous SSc (dcSSc)

Skin rule: Starts in fingers (sclerodactyly) and ascends from distal to proximal extremities and onto the trunk — this proximal/truncal involvement is the hallmark of dcSSc. Rapid onset. Early edema → rapid fibrosis.

Temporal: Interval between Raynaud's and other manifestations = weeks to months (not years). Rapid progression reflects more aggressive systemic inflammatory/fibrotic process.

Why truncal skin matters: The same fibrotic process infiltrating the trunk also reaches the viscera — lungs, renal vasculature, heart, GI tract — at an earlier point and with greater intensity. Truncal skin involvement = surrogate biomarker for systemic severity of fibrosis.

Dominant complications: ILD (65%), Scleroderma Renal Crisis (10–15%, almost exclusively within first 4 years), Tendon friction rubs (marker of active, aggressive disease).

Characteristic antibodies: Anti-Scl-70 (topoisomerase I) or Anti-RNA polymerase III
Critical window: First 4 years from onset = most rapidly evolving and potentially irreversible lung and renal damage.

CREST — Phenotypic Subset of lcSSc (NOT a Separate Disease)

  • C — Calcinosis cutis (calcium hydroxyapatite deposits under the skin)
  • R — Raynaud's phenomenon
  • E — Esophageal dysmotility (reduced LES pressure, reflux, dysphagia)
  • S — Sclerodactyly (tight skin of the fingers)
  • T — Telangiectasia (dilated skin capillaries)
Digital Ulcers Paradox — Critical Exam Point
Digital ischemic ulcers are MORE common in lcSSc (50%) than in dcSSc (25%) — despite the "limited" designation. The reason: lcSSc patients have a longer disease duration, and cumulative vascular damage accumulates over a more extended timeline. "Limited" refers to skin distribution, not severity of vascular disease.

SSc Sine Scleroderma & Autoantibody Profiles

SSc Sine Scleroderma: Patient has Raynaud's, SSc-specific autoantibodies, and characteristic clinical/laboratory features of SSc — but NO detectable skin thickening. These patients can still develop significant pulmonary, cardiac, and renal complications. Without visible skin changes, the diagnosis is frequently delayed or missed.

Cancer Alert — Anti-RNA Pol III
Anti-RNA pol III–positive SSc has a paraneoplastic mechanism: the tumor harbors genomic alterations in RNApol3 → T-cell immunity → cross-reactive autoantibodies (molecular mimicry). Any patient with anti-RNA pol III must be screened for occult malignancy — breast, lung, ovarian carcinomas, and lymphomas tend to occur in close temporal association with SSc onset.

Differential Diagnosis — Scleroderma-Like Skin Changes

Skin induration is NOT synonymous with SSc. The key distinguishing feature: none of the localized forms cause Raynaud's phenomenon or significant internal organ involvement.

  • Morphea: Localized skin fibrosis — no systemic involvement, no Raynaud's, no SSc-specific autoantibodies
  • Linear scleroderma: Unilateral linear bands of fibrosis — can affect bone growth in children
  • Coup de sabre: Linear scleroderma on forehead/scalp — can involve calvaria and brain
  • Nephrogenic systemic fibrosis: Gadolinium exposure in renal failure patients — critical iatrogenic consideration in any MRI patient
  • Bleomycin, PD-1 checkpoint inhibitors: Drug-induced SSc-like illness
  • Eosinophilia-myalgia syndrome: Contaminated L-tryptophan supplements
  • Paraneoplastic SSc: Especially with anti-RNA pol III antibodies

Epidemiology & Genetics

Key Epidemiological Numbers

Incidence (US)

9–46 cases per million per year. Lower in England, northern Europe, Japan vs. North America and Australia — reflects differences in case definition, environmental exposures, and genetic susceptibility across ancestral populations.

Sex Ratio — 4.6:1 F:M

Strong female predominance. Most pronounced during childbearing years. Declines after menopause — directly implicates sex hormones. Multiple mechanisms including estrogen-driven immune stimulation and skewed X-inactivation (TLR7, TLR8, CD40L overexpression).

Ancestry — Risk Modifier

Black patients: Higher incidence rates, higher mortality, earlier age of onset, greater likelihood of dcSSc, higher rates of ILD, worse overall prognosis. Peak onset in women: 65–74 years for both limited and diffuse forms.

Familial Risk — Genetics Loads the Gun, Environment Pulls the Trigger

  • 1.6% of SSc patients have a first-degree relative with SSc
  • Having an affected first-degree relative increases risk 13-fold
  • First-degree relatives also have increased risk of Raynaud's, ILD, SLE, RA, autoimmune thyroiditis
  • Monozygotic twin concordance: only 4.7% — the most important conceptual anchor. Even in genetically identical individuals, SSc develops in only ~1 in 20 affected twins. Environmental and epigenetic factors account for the vast majority of disease susceptibility.
Genetic Associations (GWAS Data)
HLA region (Chromosome 6): Strongest and most consistent association.
B and T cell activation genes: BANK1, BLK, CD247, IL2RA, CCR6, PTPN22, TNFSF4/OX40L, TNIP1 — collectively lower the threshold for lymphocyte activation and impair immunological tolerance.
Type I IFN pathway: STAT4, IRF5 — pDCs detect self-nucleic acids → IRF5 → type I IFN → amplified immune activation → self-sustaining loop.
ECM/Fibrosis genes: CSK, CAV1, PPARG, GRB10
ADRA2A: Genetic basis for Raynaud's (α2-adrenergic receptor heightened sensitivity on vascular smooth muscle).
No single variant is sufficient — combinations create a permissive immunological state.

Environmental Factors & Occupational Exposures

  • Particulate silica (quartz): Most consistently associated occupational exposure
  • Polyvinyl chloride (PVC): Associated with acro-osteolysis and scleroderma-like skin changes
  • Epoxy resins, welding fumes, organic solvents: Aromatic hydrocarbons (toluene, xylene, trichloroethylene) — linked in occupational cohorts
  • Bleomycin: The classic mouse model of SSc uses bleomycin injection to induce skin and lung fibrosis
  • PD-1 checkpoint inhibitors: Remove immunological brakes on T cells → SSc-like illness
  • Radiation therapy: Linked to de novo SSc onset and exacerbation of pre-existing SSc
  • Infectious triggers (parvovirus B19, EBV, CMV): Proposed but unproven — molecular mimicry, TLR activation, epigenetic dysregulation
Important Negatives — Know for Boards
Cigarette smoking does NOT increase SSc risk.
Silicone breast implants do NOT increase SSc risk.
These are classic exam "traps" — don't attribute SSc to smoking in a patient history.

Pathogenesis I — The Vascular Pillar

The Three Pillars of SSc Pathogenesis
SSc arises from the simultaneous interaction of three pathological processes: (1) Vasculopathy — small vessel injury and structural remodeling; (2) Immune activation and autoimmunity — T and B cells, autoantibodies, type I interferon; (3) Fibroblast activation and fibrosis — TGF-β-driven myofibroblast transdifferentiation and ECM deposition. The vascular pillar is considered the initiating event in most models, but all three pillars become self-sustaining and interact bidirectionally once established.

Vascular Injury — The Initiating Event

  • Endothelial injury: The trigger is unknown but results in endothelial activation, surface expression of adhesion molecules (ICAM-1, E-selectin, VCAM-1), and increased vascular permeability
  • CXCL4 (PF4) — the master vascular mediator: Released by activated platelets and plasmacytoid dendritic cells (pDCs). CXCL4 levels correlate with PAH severity, skin score, and ILD progression. It inhibits the anti-fibrotic PPAR-γ axis, promotes fibroblast activation, and suppresses angiogenesis — a triple threat
  • Endothelin-1 (ET-1) and VEGF paradox: ET-1 is elevated and drives vasoconstriction and fibrosis. VEGF is also elevated but leads to abnormal, disorganized angiogenesis (telangiectasias, GAVE) rather than productive vasculogenesis — vessels are formed but functionally insufficient
  • Loss of eNOS: Reduced nitric oxide production removes the primary vasodilatory and anti-proliferative brake on vascular smooth muscle
EndoMT — Endothelial-to-Mesenchymal Transition
A critical and underappreciated mechanism: endothelial cells lose their identity and acquire mesenchymal (fibroblast-like) characteristics under the influence of TGF-β and other signals. The cell that was supposed to maintain vascular health becomes a source of activated myofibroblasts — directly linking vascular injury to fibrosis at the cellular level. EndoMT reduces endothelial cell number, contributes to intimal thickening, and populates the perivascular fibrotic niche.

Raynaud's Phenomenon — Mechanism and Significance

Raynaud's is present in virtually all SSc patients (>95%) and is the most common presenting symptom. Understanding its mechanism is essential for understanding SSc vascular biology.

Mechanism of Vasospasm

Structural: Intimal proliferation + adventitial fibrosis = fixed narrowing of digital arteries. Even at rest, luminal area is reduced.

Functional: α2-adrenergic receptor upregulation on vascular smooth muscle (encoded by ADRA2A) — cold or emotional stress triggers exaggerated vasospasm via norepinephrine-mediated vasoconstriction. Reduced NO and prostacyclin remove the vasodilatory counterbalance.

Neuropeptide deficiency: Loss of CGRP (calcitonin gene-related peptide) and substance P from sensory nerves removes the axon reflex vasodilation that should occur after vasospasm. This loss of "neurogenic inflammation" as a protective rebound mechanism is unique to secondary Raynaud's.

Nailfold Capillaroscopy — The Window into SSc Vasculopathy

  • Normal nailfold: Regular U-shaped loops, uniform diameter, no hemorrhages
  • "Early" SSc pattern: Giant/enlarged capillaries, rare hemorrhages, relatively preserved capillary density
  • "Active" SSc pattern: Giant capillaries + frequent hemorrhages + moderate capillary loss (avascular areas beginning)
  • "Late" SSc pattern: Severe capillary loss, extensive avascular areas, irregular remaining vessels — correlates with digital ulcers, PAH, and organ involvement
Clinical Application
Nailfold capillaroscopy is non-invasive, inexpensive, and highly informative. The "late" pattern correlates with digital ulcers, PAH, and poor outcomes. A normal nailfold in a patient with Raynaud's strongly suggests primary (benign) Raynaud's. Abnormal findings in a patient with SSc-specific autoantibody = high-risk profile requiring close surveillance even before skin changes appear.

The Vascular Cascade — From Injury to End-Stage Vasculopathy

Step-by-Step Progression
1. Endothelial injury (trigger unknown: ischemia, infection, autoimmune, chemical) →
2. Platelet activation + pDC recruitment → CXCL4 release → type I IFN production →
3. Endothelial activation: ICAM-1↑, E-selectin↑, ET-1↑, NO↓ →
4. Vasospasm + structural narrowing (intimal proliferation, medial hypertrophy, adventitial fibrosis) →
5. Impaired angiogenesis (VEGF present but nonfunctional; telangiectasias form instead of functional vessels) →
6. EndoMT → endothelial cells → myofibroblasts → perivascular fibrosis →
7. End-stage: Obliterative vasculopathy, digital ischemia, pulmonary hypertension, renal crisis, myocardial "Band-like" fibrosis

Pathogenesis II — Immunity & Fibrosis Pillars

The Immune Pillar — Cellular and Humoral Mechanisms

T Cell Responses

CD4+ helper T cells predominate in early SSc lesions. Th2 skewing (IL-4, IL-13) promotes fibroblast activation and M2 macrophage polarization. Th17 cells (IL-17) promote inflammation and directly activate fibroblasts.

Regulatory T cells (Tregs): Numerically reduced and functionally impaired in SSc. Loss of Treg suppression removes the brake on both effector T cells and B cells — autoreactive clones expand unchecked.

CD8+ cytotoxic T cells: Present in inflammatory infiltrates; may directly damage endothelium and small muscle fibers in the heart and skeletal muscle (SSc myopathy).

B Cell Responses & Autoantibodies

B cells are hyperactivated with elevated serum immunoglobulins and characteristic autoantibodies. The three major autoantibodies (ACA, Scl-70, RNA pol III) are mutually exclusive in individual patients — one antibody, one dominant clinical phenotype.

Mechanisms: Autoantibodies may directly stimulate fibroblasts through surface receptors (PDGFR), activate complement on endothelium, or mediate antibody-dependent cytotoxicity against endothelial cells.

B cell depletion (rituximab): Emerging evidence for efficacy in skin and lung fibrosis — supports pathogenic B cell role.

Plasmacytoid Dendritic Cells (pDCs) — Type I IFN Axis

pDCs recognize self-nucleic acids (released from injured cells) via TLR7/TLR8/TLR9 → produce massive type I IFN (IFN-α/β). Type I IFN: activates autoreactive B and T cells, upregulates MHC-I on fibroblasts, promotes EndoMT, and sustains CXCL4 production. The "IFN signature" (measurable in blood) tracks disease activity.

Macrophage Polarization

M1 (classical): Pro-inflammatory, early lesions
M2 (alternative): Dominant in established SSc. M2 macrophages secrete TGF-β, IL-10, PDGF, and CTGF — directly driving fibroblast activation and ECM deposition. They also impair Treg function and sustain the profibrotic microenvironment.

The Fibrosis Pillar — TGF-β and Myofibroblast Activation

TGF-β — Master Regulator of Fibrosis
TGF-β (transforming growth factor beta) is the central mediator of SSc fibrosis. Sources: T cells, macrophages, platelets, endothelial cells (via EndoMT), and activated fibroblasts themselves (autocrine loop). Signals via Smad2/3 phosphorylation → nucleus → transcription of collagen I, collagen III, fibronectin, CTGF (CCN2), and PAI-1 (blocks matrix degradation). Non-Smad pathways (ERK, p38 MAPK, PI3K/AKT) amplify the profibrotic response and are increasingly targeted therapeutically.
  • Myofibroblast transdifferentiation: Resident fibroblasts (and endothelial cells via EndoMT, and epithelial cells via EMT) acquire α-smooth muscle actin (α-SMA) expression → contractile, ECM-secreting myofibroblasts. Once activated, they are remarkably persistent — continue producing collagen even after the inflammatory stimulus resolves (epigenetic locking)
  • Mechanosensing: Stiff fibrotic ECM itself signals back to fibroblasts through integrins and focal adhesions → YAP/TAZ nuclear translocation → sustained fibroblast activation even without external cytokine stimulation. A self-amplifying, mechano-fibrotic feedback loop that explains why SSc fibrosis is so difficult to reverse
  • CTGF (CCN2): TGF-β induces CTGF, which amplifies and sustains fibrosis independently of TGF-β. CTGF blockade (pamrevlumab) is in clinical trials
  • Epigenetic dysregulation: SSc fibroblasts show reduced DNMT3A (hypomethylation), altered histone acetylation, and altered miRNA expression — these epigenetic changes lock fibroblasts in the activated state across cell generations, long after the initial inflammatory trigger resolves
Why Fibrosis Is So Hard to Reverse
Three mechanisms create a self-sustaining fibrotic state: (1) Autocrine TGF-β loops — activated fibroblasts produce their own TGF-β; (2) Mechanosensing — the stiff ECM itself drives continued fibroblast activation even without cytokines; (3) Epigenetic locking — heritable changes in gene expression maintain the myofibroblast phenotype across cell divisions. This is why anti-fibrotic therapy (nintedanib, pirfenidone) slows progression but rarely achieves reversal.

Cross-Pillar Interactions — The Unified Model

How the Three Pillars Reinforce Each Other
Vascular → Immune: Injured endothelium releases CXCL4 → pDC recruitment → type I IFN → immune activation.
Immune → Fibrosis: T cells (Th2/Th17) + M2 macrophages → TGF-β, IL-4, IL-13, PDGF → fibroblast activation.
Fibrosis → Vascular: Periarteriolar fibrosis compresses vessel lumens → ischemia → more vascular injury → EndoMT → more myofibroblasts.
Vascular → Fibrosis (direct): EndoMT → endothelial cells become myofibroblasts → perivascular and organ fibrosis.
Fibrosis → Immune: Fibrotic ECM creates an altered cytokine milieu, traps inflammatory cells, and generates mechanical signals that sustain macrophage M2 polarization.

Result: A self-sustaining fibroinflammatory circuit that becomes progressively independent of the original triggering event.

Pathology — Organ-by-Organ

Skin Pathology — The Three Phases

Phase 1: Inflammatory/Edematous

Early dcSSc. Skin is swollen, pitting edema of the hands ("puffy hands"). Histology: perivascular lymphocytic infiltrate (CD4+ T cells), mast cells, activated macrophages. Endothelial swelling and early vessel wall changes. Clinically — this is the window of opportunity for immunosuppression before irreversible fibrosis.

Phase 2: Fibrotic/Indurated

Dense dermal collagen deposition. Epidermis is thinned and flattened. Skin appendages (hair follicles, sweat glands, sebaceous glands) are destroyed — explains alopecia, dry skin, anhidrosis in SSc. Dermis expands at the expense of subcutaneous fat. Skin becomes board-like and immobile. Microvascular loss continues.

Phase 3: Atrophic (Late)

Paradoxically, in some dcSSc patients after years of disease, the skin softens. The inflammatory drive has exhausted itself. BUT visceral fibrosis (lung, heart, kidney) continues to progress despite skin softening — do NOT interpret skin softening as disease remission.

Lung Pathology — The Most Important Organ for Prognosis

ILD — Interstitial Lung Disease

Dominant pattern: NSIP (Non-Specific Interstitial Pneumonia) — ground-glass opacities, fine reticulation, subpleural sparing on HRCT. Bilateral, symmetric, lower-lobe predominant. Histology: uniform interstitial inflammation and fibrosis without temporal heterogeneity.

Less common: UIP (Usual Interstitial Pneumonia) — honeycombing, traction bronchiectasis, no subpleural sparing. Portends worse prognosis when present in SSc-ILD compared to NSIP.

Key numbers: ILD in 65% dcSSc, 35% lcSSc. ILD = most common cause of SSc-related death overall.

PAH — Pulmonary Arterial Hypertension

Histology: plexiform lesions (chaotic proliferation of endothelial cells and smooth muscle in pulmonary arterioles) + intimal proliferation + medial hypertrophy + adventitial fibrosis. These are essentially vascular SSc lesions in the pulmonary arterioles.

Key numbers: 8–12% of all SSc patients develop PAH. Accounts for ~30% of SSc deaths. Particularly common in lcSSc (isolated obliterative vasculopathy). In dcSSc, PAH often co-exists with ILD.

Important: SSc-PAH has worse prognosis than idiopathic PAH — concurrent ILD, reduced cardiac reserve from myocardial fibrosis, and venoocclusive component make it more difficult to treat.

Gastrointestinal Pathology

GI tract is involved in up to 90% of SSc patients — most common internal organ involved overall. The ENS (enteric nervous system) is injured early; smooth muscle fibrosis follows.

Renal Pathology — The "Onion-Skin" Lesion

  • Interlobular arteries: Concentric intimal proliferation of myofibroblasts → classic "onion-skin" appearance on histology
  • Fibrinoid necrosis: Present in afferent arterioles and glomerular tuft during acute renal crisis — identical to malignant hypertension / TMA
  • Glomeruli: Ischemic collapse from reduced perfusion. Glomerulonephritis is distinctly rare in SSc — if present, consider overlap with ANCA or lupus
  • Pathophysiology: Vasospasm + structural narrowing → renal cortical ischemia → renin-angiotensin axis activation → angiotensin II → further vasoconstriction → more ischemia. A runaway positive feedback loop. ACEi breaks this loop.
SRC vs TTP — Critical Distinction
Scleroderma Renal Crisis (SRC) and Thrombotic Thrombocytopenic Purpura (TTP) both present with MAHA + thrombocytopenia + renal failure in an SSc patient. ADAMTS13 is severely reduced in TTP but NORMAL in SRC. This lab distinguishes them. The clinical urgency: TTP requires plasma exchange/rituximab; SRC requires immediate ACEi (captopril). Getting this wrong is potentially fatal.

Cardiac Pathology

  • "Band-like" myocardial fibrosis: Patchy replacement fibrosis not confined to coronary territory — unlike MI scar, which follows a vascular distribution. This patchy pattern causes arrhythmias and conduction defects
  • Contraction band necrosis: Evidence of recurrent ischemia-reperfusion — pathological Raynaud's of coronary microvessels
  • Pericardial disease: Pericardial effusion and fibrinous pericarditis — usually clinically silent but may cause tamponade in severe cases
  • RV failure: Ultimately, PAH leads to right ventricular pressure overload → RV hypertrophy → RV dilation → tricuspid regurgitation → RV failure — the final common pathway of PAH death

Clinical Features I — Skin & Raynaud's

Skin Manifestations — From Fingers to Trunk

Sclerodactyly & Facial Changes

Sclerodactyly: Tight, shiny, bound-down skin of the fingers. Start at fingertips, progress proximally. Loss of finger flexion creases. Cannot make a full fist. "Tapered" fingertip appearance.

Facial features: Reduced mouth opening (microstomia) → peri-oral furrows ("purse-string" mouth). Beaked nose, taut shiny face. Difficulty eating large food pieces. Dental hygiene impaired by limited oral aperture.

Modified Rodnan Skin Score (mRSS): 0–3 scale at 17 body sites (max 51). Primary clinical trial endpoint for skin assessment. Changes in mRSS parallel organ involvement in dcSSc — an improving mRSS in the first 2 years is a favorable prognostic sign.

Digital Complications

Digital ischemic ulcers: Common, painful, slow to heal. MORE common in lcSSc (50%) than dcSSc (25%) because cumulative vascular damage accumulates over a longer disease duration. Predilect fingertip pads and over bony prominences (PIP joints). Secondary infection is common — can lead to osteomyelitis and amputation.

Pitting scars: Residual atrophic scars at fingertip pads from healed ischemic ulcers.

Calcinosis cutis: Calcium hydroxyapatite deposits in subcutaneous tissue. Most common in lcSSc/CREST. Over the fingers, forearms, elbows. Can ulcerate through the skin → painful, with chalky discharge. Can become superinfected. No reliable treatment — excision rarely curative as deposits recur.

Acro-osteolysis: Resorption of the distal phalangeal tufts from ischemia → "pencil-pointing" on X-ray. Unique to SSc among rheumatic diseases.

Telangiectasia & Other Skin Features

  • Telangiectasia: Mat-like dilated capillaries on face (especially lips and tongue), hands, and trunk. Result of abnormal angiogenesis (VEGF-driven disorganized vessel formation). Important in CREST/lcSSc. Can occur on GI mucosa → occult GI bleeding
  • Hyperpigmentation and hypopigmentation: "Salt-and-pepper" appearance — mixed hyper- and hypopigmented macules, especially on trunk. Pathognomonic pattern when present
  • Pruritus: Very common in early inflammatory phase. Driven by mast cell degranulation in the dermis. Responds to antihistamines; may respond to narrow-band UVB
  • Alopecia: Destruction of hair follicles by fibrosis → diffuse scalp hair loss
  • Dry eyes/mouth (sicca syndrome): Secondary Sjögren's overlap common in lcSSc

Raynaud's Phenomenon — Clinical Assessment

The Classic Three-Phase Color Change
White (pallor): Vasospasm → cessation of blood flow → ischemia. Triggered by cold or emotional stress. Sharply demarcated at a digital joint.
Blue (cyanosis): Deoxygenation of static blood in the digit.
Red (rubor): Reactive hyperemia as the spasm releases and blood returns — can be painful, throbbing. Not all patients have all three phases. The classic sequence is White → Blue → Red but may be incomplete.
  • Prevalence: >95% of SSc patients; virtually universal. Often the presenting symptom by years before other manifestations
  • Assessment: Nailfold capillaroscopy (see §3) is the key investigation to distinguish primary from secondary
  • Severity grading: Frequency per week, number of digits, duration of attacks, presence of digital ulcers, healing time
  • Triggers: Cold exposure, emotional stress, caffeine, nicotine, sympathomimetic drugs (decongestants, amphetamines)
  • Complications: Digital ischemic ulcers → chronic pain, non-healing, secondary infection, osteomyelitis, digit amputation

Clinical Features II — Internal Organs

Pulmonary Manifestations — The Main Killer

ILD — Detection & Monitoring

Symptoms: Exertional dyspnea (most common), dry cough, reduced exercise tolerance. Often asymptomatic in early stages — screening is essential.

Auscultation: Fine "Velcro" bibasilar crackles (like walking on fresh snow). Identical to IPF crackles.

PFTs: Restriction = ↓TLC, ↓FVC, ↓DLCO, FEV1/FVC ratio normal or increased. DLCO is the most sensitive individual PFT parameter — falls before FVC changes. FVC <70% predicted = high-risk threshold for ILD progression.

HRCT: Ground-glass (active alveolitis) + fine reticulation (fibrosis), lower-lobe predominant, subpleural sparing (NSIP pattern). CT is superior to CXR — do not rely on CXR alone for SSc-ILD assessment.

BAL: Alveolar lymphocytosis or neutrophilia suggests active alveolitis — may support decision to treat, but not routinely required.

PAH — Detection & Monitoring

Symptoms: Progressive exertional dyspnea, fatigue, syncope (late), signs of RV failure (JVD, RV heave, loud P2, peripheral edema). Clinically silent until advanced — screening is critical.

Screening protocol (annual): Echocardiography (TR jet velocity, RVSP), PFTs, NT-proBNP, 6-minute walk distance (6MWD), anti-centromere antibody status (highest risk for isolated PAH).

Key PFT clue: Disproportionately reduced DLCO relative to FVC → think PAH (not ILD alone). Isolated DLCO reduction in lcSSc = screen aggressively for PAH.

Definitive diagnosis: Right heart catheterization (RHC) — mPAP ≥20 mmHg at rest with PVR ≥2 Wood units = PAH.

NT-proBNP: Elevated = increased risk, correlates with RV dysfunction. Useful for monitoring response to therapy.

Scleroderma Renal Crisis (SRC)

Scleroderma Renal Crisis — High-Stakes Emergency
Who: Mainly dcSSc, first 4 years of disease, anti-RNA pol III positive.
What: Abrupt-onset accelerated hypertension (BP >150/85 typically) + acute oliguric renal failure. May progress to dialysis within days without treatment.
Normotensive SRC: 10% of SRC cases — no hypertension, but renal function still deteriorates rapidly. WORSE outcome than hypertensive SRC because the diagnosis is delayed (no hypertension alert).
Triggers: High-dose glucocorticoids (≥15 mg/day prednisone). Do NOT use glucocorticoids in early dcSSc without recognizing this risk.
Labs: Rising creatinine, MAHA (microangiopathic hemolytic anemia — fragmented RBCs/schistocytes), thrombocytopenia, elevated plasma renin activity.
Treatment: Captopril (short-acting ACEi) — start immediately, titrate aggressively to control BP. Continue even if dialysis is required — renal recovery can occur months later if ACEi is maintained. Pre-ACEi survival <10% at 1 year; post-ACEi >70% at 3 years.

Cardiac Manifestations

  • Arrhythmias: Most common cardiac manifestation. Ventricular arrhythmias and conduction blocks from patchy myocardial fibrosis. Can be sudden cardiac death (SCD) without warning
  • Heart block: AV block, bundle branch block from fibrosis of the conduction system. May require pacemaker
  • Diastolic dysfunction: Common, from myocardial fibrosis — impairs ventricular filling and exercise capacity. Exacerbates PAH-related symptoms
  • Myocardial "Raynaud's": Coronary microvascular spasm → contraction band necrosis → regional ischemia → troponin elevation without epicardial coronary disease (normal coronary angiogram)
  • Pericardial disease: Clinically silent effusion is common; tamponade is rare but recognized complication

Musculoskeletal & Laboratory Features

  • Tendon friction rubs: Palpable and/or audible leathery crepitus over tendon sheaths (wrist extensors, ankle tendons). Pathognomonic of active dcSSc. Predicts renal crisis, cardiac involvement, and reduced survival. A critical exam finding — always ask about and examine for.
  • Arthritis: Inflammatory, symmetrical, small joint arthritis resembling RA. Erosions are less common than RA but occur. Overlap with RA/MCTD must be considered if prominent.
  • Myopathy: Two types — (1) Non-inflammatory: simple atrophy and replacement fibrosis, mild CK elevation; (2) Inflammatory (SSc-myositis overlap): elevated CK/aldolase, active inflammation — treat with immunosuppression
Laboratory Profile of SSc
ANA: Positive in >95% (nucleolar, speckled, centromere patterns — pattern guides antibody testing)
Specific antibodies: ACA (lcSSc), Scl-70 (dcSSc + ILD), RNA pol III (dcSSc + renal crisis + cancer)
ESR/CRP: Usually NORMAL in SSc. If significantly elevated → suspect myositis, malignancy, or infection
CBC: Anemia of chronic disease common; MAHA in SRC; thrombocytopenia in SRC or TTP overlap
Complement: Usually normal (not a complement-consuming disease unlike SLE)
NT-proBNP: Elevated = PAH or cardiac involvement — annual screening biomarker

Diagnosis & Monitoring

Diagnostic Approach — Confirming SSc

  • Clinical diagnosis first: SSc is a clinical diagnosis supported by serology and investigations. Apply ACR/EULAR 2013 criteria (score ≥9)
  • Key history: Onset of Raynaud's (and interval to other symptoms), skin tightening progression, dysphagia, dyspnea, joint pain, family history
  • Physical exam priorities: Skin score (mRSS), nailfold capillaroscopy, tendon friction rubs, lung auscultation, BP (both arms), heart exam (P2, RV heave)
  • ANA first: If ANA positive, send subset panel: ACA, Scl-70, RNA pol III. These guide the risk profile
  • Subset classification: Limited vs diffuse (skin above elbows/knees/trunk = diffuse)

Decision Points for ILD Management
Step 1: Baseline HRCT + PFTs at diagnosis
Step 2: Risk stratify — dcSSc + Scl-70 + FVC <70% = high-risk profile

Step 7: Consider HRCT for characterization before changing therapy (NSIP vs UIP pattern affects treatment response expectation)

PAH Screening Algorithm

High-Risk Profile for PAH
Highest risk: lcSSc + anti-centromere antibody + disease duration >10 years + isolated DLCO reduction (<60% predicted)
Annual screening: TRV on echo ≥2.5 m/s OR NT-proBNP elevated OR 6MWD declining → proceed to RHC
DETECT algorithm: Validated screening algorithm using 6 variables (FVC/DLCO ratio, NT-proBNP, anti-centromere, telangiectasias, right axis deviation on ECG, RVSP on echo) → guides RHC referral
Definitive: RHC — mPAP ≥20 mmHg + PVR ≥2 Wood units confirms PAH. Essential before starting PAH-specific therapy.

High-Risk Monitoring Profile — Integrating Subset and Antibody

Anti-RNA pol III

Watch for: Renal crisis (BP monitoring q2 weeks in early dcSSc), rapid skin progression, GAVE. Screen for occult malignancy at diagnosis (age-appropriate cancer screening + chest CT + mammography).

Anti-Scl-70

Watch for: ILD progression (more aggressive PFT monitoring), extensive skin disease, digital ulcers, cardiac involvement. PFTs every 3 months initially.

Anti-centromere (ACA)

Watch for: PAH (annual echo mandatory), digital ulcers, calcinosis, Sjögren's overlap, primary biliary cirrhosis. Measure NT-proBNP annually.

Treatment

Treatment Philosophy in SSc
There is NO treatment that reverses established SSc fibrosis. Current therapy aims to: (1) Slow progression, (2) Prevent organ damage, (3) Treat complications, (4) Improve quality of life. Treatment is organ-specific and subset-specific. The most important determinant of outcome is early diagnosis and early initiation of organ-appropriate therapy before irreversible damage occurs.

Antifibrotic Therapy — ILD Progression

Nintedanib (Ofev)

Triple kinase inhibitor: PDGFR, VEGFR, FGFR. SENSCIS trial: Significantly reduced rate of FVC decline in SSc-ILD vs placebo (−41 mL/year vs −93 mL/year). FDA-approved for SSc-ILD.

Key boards point: Nintedanib is lung-specific — it slows ILD progression. It does NOT improve skin score, Raynaud's, or other non-pulmonary manifestations. Use in combination with MMF if needed (not contraindicated).

Adverse effects: Diarrhea most common (manage with dose reduction, loperamide), nausea, elevated liver enzymes. Monitor LFTs monthly.

Pirfenidone

Mechanism: anti-fibrotic/anti-inflammatory (TGF-β pathway inhibitor). Approved for IPF. Some off-label use in SSc-ILD but weaker evidence base than nintedanib for SSc specifically.

HCTS (HSCT): Autologous hematopoietic stem cell transplant — ASTIS and SCOT trials showed survival benefit over CYC in carefully selected dcSSc patients with significant internal organ involvement but preserved cardiac and pulmonary function. Treatment-related mortality ~10% → patient selection is critical. Consider in young (<50) dcSSc patients with rapidly progressive disease.

Renal Crisis — ACEi Protocol

Captopril First — Even on Dialysis
Start captopril immediately — short-acting ACEi preferred for rapid titration. Target: SBP reduction of ~10–20 mmHg/24 hours initially; goal <130/80 ultimately.
Continue ACEi even if dialysis is required — patients may recover renal function months to years after SRC with sustained renin-angiotensin blockade. Documented cases of dialysis patients recovering enough to come off dialysis after 6–18 months of ACEi.
Do NOT withhold ACEi in SRC — this is the therapeutic cornerstone. The concern about ACEi causing hyperkalemia in CKD does NOT outweigh the benefit in acute SRC.
ARBs: Alternative if ACEi not tolerated, but ACEi (especially captopril) is the established agent with the most evidence.

GI Management

  • GERD/Esophageal dysmotility: High-dose PPI (twice daily). Elevate head of bed 30°. Prokinetics (metoclopramide — limited use due to CNS effects; erythromycin as prokinetic; prucalopride for intestinal dysmotility). Avoid Nissen fundoplication — CONTRAINDICATED.
  • GAVE: Argon plasma coagulation (APC) endoscopically. Repeat PRN. IV iron for anemia. Anti-RNA pol III screening for concurrent malignancy.
  • SIBO: Rotating antibiotics — rifaximin (first line, minimal absorption), then metronidazole, ciprofloxacin, trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole. Cycle 10 days on/20 days off.
  • Malnutrition: Nutritional assessment and supplementation. Enteral nutrition (NG/NJ tube) if oral intake insufficient. Consider parenteral nutrition for refractory cases with significant small bowel dysmotility.

Advanced & Emerging Therapy

  • CAR-T cell therapy: CD19-directed CAR-T cells have produced dramatic sustained remissions in early dcSSc cases (2023–2024 reports from Erlangen group). Deep molecular remissions with normalization of skin scores and PFTs. Still experimental but potentially paradigm-shifting. Not yet standard of care.
  • Lenabasum: CB2 cannabinoid receptor agonist — anti-inflammatory and anti-fibrotic. Phase III trials in dcSSc ongoing.
  • Pamrevlumab: Anti-CTGF (CCN2) monoclonal antibody — completed Phase III in SSc-ILD. Results pending.
  • Belimumab + HSCT combinations: B cell targeting prior to HSCT to reduce relapse rates under investigation.

Prognosis & Related Disorders

Prognosis by Subset

lcSSc Natural History

Generally more benign overall, but NOT without significant morbidity.

  • Long prodrome (Raynaud's for 10–15 years before other symptoms)
  • Slow skin progression — may stabilize for years
  • Main threats: Isolated PAH (long-term), digital ulcers, calcinosis complications, Sjögren's overlap
  • 10-year survival: ~70–80% in most series (PAH-free); drops significantly with PAH onset
  • Quality of life often impaired by digital pain, calcinosis, GI symptoms despite absence of major organ failure
dcSSc Natural History

Biphasic course:

  • First 4 years (critical window): Rapid skin progression, inflammatory phase, peak risk for ILD, SRC, and cardiac involvement. Disease most active and modifiable here.
  • After 4–5 years: Skin often softens (do not interpret as remission — visceral disease may continue or stabilize independently)
  • 5-year survival: ~70–80%; 10-year survival: ~55–65%
  • Main causes of death: ILD, PAH, cardiac fibrosis/arrhythmia, SRC (pre-ACEi era predominantly; now rarer)

Markers of Poor Prognosis

  • dcSSc subset > lcSSc
  • Anti-Scl-70 (extensive ILD risk) or anti-RNA pol III (renal crisis, cancer)
  • Black/African-American ancestry — earlier onset, more severe disease, worse outcomes
  • Male sex — worse prognosis than female despite lower prevalence
  • ILD with FVC <70% at presentation
  • PAH — especially if SSc-PAH (worse than idiopathic PAH at comparable hemodynamics)
  • Tendon friction rubs — predicts SRC, cardiac involvement, reduced survival
  • Elevated NT-proBNP at baseline
  • Malnutrition/cachexia — weight loss >10% body weight in the first year is a poor prognostic sign
  • Normotensive SRC — worse than hypertensive SRC

The ACEi Revolution in Renal Crisis

Most Dramatic Therapeutic Achievement in SSc History
Pre-ACEi era: SRC 1-year survival <10%. Dialysis-dependent, death from uremia or cardiac failure within weeks to months.
Post-ACEi era: 3-year survival >70%. Renal recovery even after dialysis documented. SRC transformed from a near-universal death sentence to a manageable crisis.
Key lesson: This demonstrates that targeted, mechanism-based intervention (blocking the renin-angiotensin axis that drives the SRC positive feedback loop) can dramatically alter outcomes. It also explains why avoiding glucocorticoids in early dcSSc (which precipitate SRC) is so important.

Related Disorders

Localized Scleroderma

Skin-only fibrosis — NO systemic involvement, NO Raynaud's, NO SSc-specific autoantibodies. Does NOT progress to systemic SSc (rarely, if ever). Types:

  • Morphea: Round/oval plaques of skin induration. Inflammatory center, lilac border. Self-limited in most cases.
  • Linear scleroderma: Linear bands of fibrosis following lines of Blaschko. Can cross joints → joint contractures. Can affect underlying bone → leg-length discrepancy in children. En coup de sabre = linear scleroderma on forehead/scalp → can involve underlying bone and brain → rarely causes seizures.
  • Treatment: Methotrexate ± pulse IV corticosteroids for active/expanding lesions. Phototherapy (UVA1, PUVA) for extensive morphea.
Mixed Connective Tissue Disease (MCTD)

Key features: Overlap of SSc, SLE, PM/DM, and RA. Defined by high-titer anti-U1-RNP antibody (required for diagnosis).

Clinical: Raynaud's (universal), puffy hands, inflammatory arthritis, esophageal dysmotility, myositis, mild ILD. PAH is the most serious complication.

Prognosis: Generally more benign than SSc, more steroid-responsive than SSc. ILD and PAH drive morbidity.

Boards point: MCTD = U1-RNP. If you see U1-RNP without ACA or Scl-70, think MCTD, not pure SSc.

Eosinophilic Fasciitis (EF) — The "NONE Rule"

Scleroderma-like skin induration involving the fascia (deep to dermis), NOT the skin itself. Skin can be "tethered" (pulled toward underlying fascia) giving an "orange-peel" (peau d'orange) appearance.

The "NONE" Rule:

  • No Raynaud's phenomenon
  • No SSc-specific autoantibodies (ANA may be weakly positive)
  • No internal organ involvement
  • No nailfold capillary changes

Positive features: Peripheral eosinophilia + ↑ESR, fasciitis on MRI/biopsy (plasma cells + eosinophils in fascia). Associated with strenuous exercise and occasionally hematologic malignancy (aplastic anemia, lymphoma — screen!).

Treatment: Prednisone — dramatic response (unlike true SSc). Excellent prognosis when malignancy excluded.

⚠ Exam Traps & High-Yield Distinctions

How to Use This Section
These are the most commonly tested SSc concepts at boards level — presented as the distinction you must make under exam pressure. Each trap is a real answer choice error waiting to happen. Read once, mark the ones that surprise you, and review those flagged ones the night before.

The 17 Critical Traps

TRAP 1 — ACEi Is for Renal Crisis, CCBs Are for Raynaud's

The most commonly tested SSc pharmacology distinction. ACEi (captopril) = treat SRC. CCBs (nifedipine/amlodipine) = treat Raynaud's. If the vignette says "Raynaud's → give captopril" — that is WRONG. ACEi has no role in Raynaud's treatment. CCB is not used for SRC.

TRAP 2 — ACA Protects Against Renal Crisis (NOT a Risk Factor)

Anti-centromere antibody (ACA) = lcSSc. SRC is a dcSSc complication, associated with anti-RNA pol III (not ACA). ACA is explicitly protective against renal crisis. A vignette giving ACA + asking about SRC risk: the answer is LOW RISK, not high.

TRAP 3 — Glucocorticoids Precipitate Renal Crisis

Prednisone ≥15 mg/day is the most important iatrogenic trigger of SRC in early dcSSc patients, especially those with anti-RNA pol III. Never use high-dose steroids without acknowledging this risk and monitoring BP closely. A vignette where a newly diagnosed dcSSc patient is started on prednisone and develops acute hypertension + renal failure = SRC triggered by steroids.

TRAP 4 — Digital Ulcers Are MORE Common in lcSSc Than dcSSc

Counter-intuitive. lcSSc = 50% prevalence of digital ulcers; dcSSc = 25%. "Limited" refers to skin distribution, NOT vascular disease severity. lcSSc patients live longer → cumulative vascular damage over a longer disease course → more digital ischemia.

TRAP 5 — Anti-RNA Pol III = Screen for Occult Malignancy

Anti-RNA pol III is a paraneoplastic marker — the tumor drives the autoimmune response via molecular mimicry (tumor-specific RNApol3 mutations → T cell response → cross-reactive autoantibodies against self RNApol3). Any SSc patient with anti-RNA pol III requires thorough age-appropriate cancer screening (mammography, CT chest/abdomen/pelvis, GI scope if indicated). Malignancy typically presents simultaneously with or shortly before SSc onset.

TRAP 6 — Normotensive SRC Is Worse, Not Better

Normotensive SRC (10% of SRC cases) — renal function deteriorates without hypertension as a warning sign. Diagnosis is therefore delayed. Prognosis is worse than hypertensive SRC precisely because treatment is started later. Do not reassure a patient with SRC features because BP is "only mildly elevated" or normal.

TRAP 7 — ESR Is NORMAL in SSc

SSc is not a "hot" inflammatory arthritis. CRP and ESR are characteristically normal (or minimally elevated). If ESR is significantly elevated in an SSc patient → investigate for: (1) Inflammatory myositis overlap, (2) Malignancy, (3) Intercurrent infection. Don't attribute a markedly elevated ESR to SSc itself.

TRAP 8 — Disproportionate DLCO Reduction → Think PAH First

In SSc, DLCO can fall for two reasons: ILD (reduced gas exchange surface) or PAH (reduced pulmonary blood flow). When DLCO falls disproportionately to FVC (i.e., DLCO very low, FVC relatively preserved) — this pattern favors PAH over ILD. In lcSSc especially, isolated DLCO <60% predicted = highest-risk PAH signal → proceed to echo and RHC. Do not assume "it's just the ILD."

TRAP 9 — SSc-ILD Pattern Is NSIP, Not UIP

IPF = UIP pattern (honeycombing, traction bronchiectasis, no subpleural sparing). SSc-ILD = NSIP pattern (ground-glass opacities, fine reticulation, bilateral symmetric, lower-lobe, subpleural sparing). When a boards question shows SSc + HRCT + asks for the dominant ILD pattern, the answer is NSIP. If UIP is present in SSc, it portends worse prognosis.

TRAP 10 — Nissen Fundoplication Is CONTRAINDICATED in SSc

Esophageal peristalsis is absent or severely impaired in SSc (smooth muscle atrophy of the lower 2/3 esophagus). Nissen fundoplication creates a 360° wrap around the LES — in a patient without peristalsis, this creates a functional obstruction. The result: severe dysphagia, food impaction, aspiration. Treat GERD in SSc with high-dose PPI + prokinetics, NOT surgical fundoplication.

TRAP 11 — SRC vs TTP: ADAMTS13 Distinguishes Them

Both present with MAHA + thrombocytopenia + renal failure in SSc patients. ADAMTS13 severely reduced (<10%) = TTP. ADAMTS13 normal = SRC. This single lab test determines treatment: TTP → plasma exchange + rituximab; SRC → captopril immediately. Getting them confused is potentially fatal.

TRAP 12 — Tendon Friction Rubs = Danger Sign

Palpable leathery crepitus over tendon sheaths (wrist, knee, ankle) is pathognomonic of active, aggressive dcSSc. They predict: (1) renal crisis risk, (2) cardiac involvement, (3) reduced survival. A vignette with SSc + tendon friction rubs → high-risk classification, intensified monitoring, consider aggressive immunosuppression.

TRAP 13 — Nintedanib Is Lung-Specific, No Skin Benefit

Nintedanib slows ILD progression (FVC decline reduced by ~50% vs placebo in SENSCIS). It does NOT improve skin score, Raynaud's, or other non-pulmonary SSc manifestations. A boards question asking what nintedanib improves → answer: rate of FVC decline in ILD. It does not reverse fibrosis — it slows the rate of lung function loss.

TRAP 14 — GERD Worsens ILD via Microaspiration

GER → microaspiration of acid/pepsin into the airways → triggers and worsens ILD independently of primary fibrosis. This is a critical therapeutic implication: aggressive GERD management (PPI + prokinetics + head-of-bed elevation) is part of ILD management in SSc, not just a GI intervention. An SSc-ILD patient with uncontrolled GERD should be treated for both conditions simultaneously.

TRAP 15 — Twin Concordance 4.7% → Environment Drives SSc

Monozygotic twins are genetically identical. If SSc were primarily genetic, concordance should be high (approaching 100% in a purely genetic disease). Only 4.7% concordance in MZ twins = genetics provides susceptibility but environmental and epigenetic triggers are responsible for the vast majority of disease risk. On an exam: "what does the low twin concordance in SSc demonstrate?" → Answer: the predominant role of environmental/epigenetic factors.

TRAP 16 — SSc-PAH Has WORSE Prognosis Than Idiopathic PAH

Despite similar hemodynamics at diagnosis, SSc-PAH patients do worse than idiopathic PAH patients on the same PAH-specific therapy. Reasons: (1) Concurrent ILD reduces pulmonary vascular reserve; (2) Myocardial fibrosis reduces cardiac reserve — RV cannot compensate as effectively; (3) Venoocclusive component in some SSc-PAH cases; (4) BMPR2 mutations (the signature of heritable PAH) are NOT associated with SSc-PAH. This explains why SSc-PAH requires earlier and more aggressive PAH therapy.

TRAP 17 — MMF Is Preferred Over CYC for SSc-ILD

SLS-II (Scleroderma Lung Study II) showed MMF and CYC had comparable efficacy for SSc-ILD (FVC stabilization), but MMF had significantly better tolerability (less leukopenia, less GI toxicity, no gonadotoxicity, no hemorrhagic cystitis). MMF is therefore now the preferred first-line agent. CYC is reserved for severe, rapidly progressive ILD where rapid response is needed, or as a bridge. A question asking "first-line therapy for SSc-ILD" → MMF (not CYC).
