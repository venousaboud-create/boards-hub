01

Overview — What Is Cirrhosis?

  • Defined histopathologically: fibrosis → architectural distortion → formation of regenerative nodules → decreased hepatocellular mass + altered blood flow
  • Fibrosis is induced by activation of hepatic stellate cells → increased collagen + extracellular matrix deposition
  • Advanced fibrosis = bridging fibrosis with nodularity (stage 3); cirrhosis = stage 4
  • Cirrhosis CAN regress when the underlying insult is removed — most apparent with HCV cure; also seen in hemochromatosis (phlebotomy) and ALD (abstinence)
Compensated Cirrhosis
  • Stable; well-functioning
  • HVPG 5–10 mmHg (asymptomatic)
  • Can last >10 years
  • Labs may be completely normal
Decompensated Cirrhosis
  • Ascites, hepatic encephalopathy, or variceal bleeding
  • HVPG ≥10 mmHg = CSPH (clinically significant portal hypertension)
  • Median survival <2 years after first decompensation
  • Consider liver transplantation
⚠ Complications of Cirrhosis — Full List
Portal hypertension (varices, portal hypertensive gastropathy, hypersplenism) · Ascites · Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP) · Hepatorenal syndrome (HRS) · Hepatic encephalopathy · Hepatopulmonary syndrome · Portopulmonary hypertension · Coagulopathy · Bone disease (osteoporosis/osteopenia/osteomalacia) · Hematologic abnormalities (anemia, thrombocytopenia, neutropenia) · Malnutrition/sarcopenia · Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) · Acute-on-chronic liver failure (ACLF)
02

Causes of Cirrhosis

Most Common
  • Alcohol — 48% of cirrhosis deaths
  • Chronic HCV — ~80% develop chronic HCV; ~20–30% → cirrhosis over 20–30 yrs
  • Chronic HBV — ~5% of adults develop chronic HBV; ~20% → cirrhosis
  • MASH (MASLD-related)
Biliary
  • Primary biliary cholangitis (PBC)
  • Primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC)
  • Autoimmune cholangiopathy (AIC)
  • Secondary biliary cirrhosis
Metabolic / Inherited
  • Hemochromatosis (1 in 250 genetically susceptible; <5% → severe disease)
  • Wilson's disease (1 in 30,000)
  • α1-Antitrypsin deficiency (ZZ phenotype, ~10–20% → CLD)
  • Cystic fibrosis
  • Autoimmune hepatitis
Other
  • Cardiac cirrhosis (right heart failure — increasingly rare)
  • Cryptogenic (many = "burned out" MASH)
  • Budd-Chiari syndrome

Alcohol-Associated Cirrhosis — Histology

  • Nodules usually <3 mm = micronodular
  • With alcohol cessation → larger nodules form → mixed micronodular + macronodular
  • Fibrosis can be centrilobular, pericellular, or periportal
  • Connective tissue connects portal triads with central veins → regenerative nodules
  • Zieve's syndrome — unique hemolytic anemia with spur cells and acanthocytes; occurs in severe alcoholic hepatitis
📋 Prognosis — Alcohol-Associated Cirrhosis
With ongoing alcohol use + complications of cirrhosis: <50% 5-year survival. With sustained abstinence: significantly improved prognosis. New biomarker: phosphatidylethanol (PEth) — clinically available marker of recent alcohol consumption, useful for evaluating abstinence.

MASH Cirrhosis — The Cryptogenic Cirrhosis Trap

Many patients previously labeled as "cryptogenic cirrhosis" in fact had MASH. As MASH cirrhosis progresses, patients become catabolic and lose the telltale histologic signs of steatosis — so biopsy appears "cryptogenic." Positive ANA or ASMA in AIH-related cirrhosis; if burned-out AIH (no inflammation), immunosuppression will not help.

Portal Hypertension

🔢 Key Thresholds — HVPG (Hepatic Venous Pressure Gradient)
HVPG >5 mmHg

Definition of portal hypertension

HVPG ≥10 mmHg

Clinically Significant Portal Hypertension (CSPH) — risk of decompensation

HVPG >12 mmHg

Risk of variceal hemorrhage

Pathogenesis — Two Simultaneous Processes

  • (1) ↑ Intrahepatic resistance — due to cirrhosis, regenerative nodules, microthrombi → blood cannot flow through liver freely
  • (2) ↑ Splanchnic blood flow — splanchnic arterial vasodilation (mediated by nitric oxide and other vasodilators) → increased portal venous inflow
  • Advanced stage: activation of neurohumoral systems (RAAS + sympathetic nervous system) → sodium and water retention → ↑ blood volume → hyperdynamic circulation → more portal hypertension

Classification of Portal Hypertension

CategoryCauses
PrehepaticPortal vein thrombosis, splenic vein thrombosis, massive splenomegaly (Banti's syndrome)
Intrahepatic — PresinusoidalSchistosomiasis, congenital hepatic fibrosis
Intrahepatic — SinusoidalCirrhosis (all causes), alcoholic hepatitis — >95% of all portal HTN cases
Intrahepatic — PostsinusoidalHepatic sinusoidal obstruction (venoocclusive syndrome/SOS)
PosthepaticBudd-Chiari syndrome, IVC webs, cardiac causes (restrictive cardiomyopathy, constrictive pericarditis, severe CHF)

Portal vein thrombosis may be idiopathic or associated with cirrhosis, infection, pancreatitis, or abdominal trauma. Coagulation disorders (polycythemia vera, essential thrombocytosis, protein C/S deficiency, antithrombin III deficiency, factor V Leiden) can also cause PVT.

Clinical Features of Portal Hypertension

The three primary complications are: (1) gastroesophageal varices with hemorrhage, (2) ascites, (3) hypersplenism

  • Portal hypertension first indicated by: thrombocytopenia, enlarged spleen, ascites, encephalopathy, esophageal varices
  • Splenomegaly is usually the first indication; causes thrombocytopenia and leukopenia
  • Portal hypertensive gastropathy: snakeskin-like mosaic pattern + central red/brown spots on endoscopy; can cause acute or chronic bleeding; treat with NSBB + iron; refractory → TIPS

Esophageal Varices & Variceal Hemorrhage

🔴 Variceal Hemorrhage — Key Numbers
  • 20–30% mortality per bleeding episode
  • ~1/3 of cirrhotic patients have varices at diagnosis
  • 5–15% of cirrhotics per year develop new varices
  • ~1/3 of patients with varices will eventually bleed

Primary Prophylaxis — Prevention of First Bleed

Screening Endoscopy Intervals

  • Compensated cirrhosis: endoscopy at diagnosis, then every 2 years (active disease) or every 3 years (inactive — alcohol cessation, HCV eradication)
  • Screening NOT needed if: no thrombocytopenia AND liver stiffness by TE <20 kPa (high-risk varices are rare)
  • Endoscopy recommended at time of hepatic decompensation

Who Gets Prophylaxis?

  • Medium or large varices, OR small varices with high-risk stigmata, OR decompensated cirrhosis

Prophylaxis Options

Non-Selective Beta Blockers (NSBB)
  • Propranolol or nadolol — titrate to HR 55–60 bpm with SBP >90 mmHg
  • Carvedilol — becoming NSBB of choice; additional α1-adrenergic vasodilating properties → more effectively ↓ portal pressure; no HR goal needed; emerging data showing prevention of hepatic decompensation and improved survival in CSPH
  • NSBBs recommended as first-line if tolerated (additional systemic benefits)
Endoscopic Variceal Ligation (EVL)
  • Equivalent efficacy to NSBB for primary prophylaxis
  • Used when NSBB not tolerated
  • Once primary prophylaxis started, repeat surveillance endoscopy is unnecessary

Acute Variceal Bleeding — Treatment

  • Fluid + RBC replacement to stabilize hemodynamics
  • Restrictive transfusion strategy: start transfusion when Hgb <7 g/dL; goal Hgb 7–9 g/dL → reduced rebleeding and mortality vs liberal strategy
  • Do NOT correct elevated PT with FFP unless coagulopathy causing bleeding at other sites (IV lines etc.) — routine FFP correction not recommended
  • Vasoconstricting agents: somatostatin or octreotide → ↓ bleeding, ↓ transfusion requirements, ↓ all-cause mortality
  • Prophylactic antibiotics (ceftriaxone, started BEFORE endoscopy) → ↓ infections, ↓ rebleeding, ↓ mortality
  • Upper endoscopy = first-line treatment to diagnose and control bleeding with EVL
  • Balloon tamponade (Sengstaken-Blakemore or Minnesota tube) or self-expandable metal stents — stabilization bridge to endoscopy or TIPS, after endoscopic failure

TIPS (Transjugular Intrahepatic Portosystemic Shunt)

  • Percutaneous approach; expandable metal stent creates direct portocaval shunt through liver substance
  • Used when endoscopic therapy fails, or for gastric varices (band ligation often unsuccessful)
  • Encephalopathy in up to 20% post-TIPS — particularly problematic in elderly and those with pre-existing HE
  • Effective at managing ascites but does NOT improve survival
  • Emerging evidence that early TIPS in high-risk bleeders improves outcome
  • Monitor with Doppler ultrasound for stenosis
  • All patients requiring TIPS should be considered for transplant evaluation

Gastric Varices

  • When esophageal varices extend into proximal stomach or bleeding entirely gastric → band ligation often unsuccessful
  • Consider TIPS; also BRTO (balloon-occluded retrograde transvenous obliteration) for varices associated with splenorenal shunt

Secondary Prophylaxis (Prevention of Rebleeding)

  • Repeated EVL until varices obliterated + NSBB
  • Recurrent bleeding → TIPS for long-term prevention
  • After TIPS: no further endoscopy for variceal surveillance needed; monitor TIPS with Doppler US

Ascites

Pathogenesis

Cirrhosis → Portal hypertension
↓
Splanchnic arterial vasodilation (nitric oxide) → ↑ portal venous inflow + arterial underfilling
↓
Activation of RAAS + SNS + ADH → sodium + water retention
↓
↑ Extracellular fluid volume + ↓ plasma oncotic pressure (hypoalbuminemia) → fluid leaks into peritoneal cavity

Diagnosis — SAAG

🔢 SAAG (Serum Ascites-to-Albumin Gradient)
SAAG = Serum albumin − Ascitic fluid albumin
Replaces the old "exudate vs transudate" classification.
SAAG ≥ 1.1 g/dL

Portal hypertension cause (usually cirrhosis)

Cardiac ascites: SAAG ≥1.1 AND ascitic protein >2.5 g/dL

SAAG < 1.1 g/dL

Consider infectious or malignant causes

Ascitic Fluid Analysis — Key Values

  • Ascitic protein <2.5 g/dL — typical of cirrhotic ascites
  • Ascitic protein <1.5 g/dL — increased risk of developing SBP
  • PMN >250/μL — SBP is likely
  • High RBC in ascites — usually traumatic tap; rarely HCC or ruptured omental varix
  • More than 2 organisms on culture → consider secondary bacterial peritonitis (perforated viscus)

Treatment of Ascites

  • Dietary sodium restriction: goal 2 g/day (~¾ teaspoon of salt); avoid canned/processed foods
  • Diuretics:
    • Start: spironolactone 100 mg/day + furosemide 40 mg/day
    • If not mobilizing fluid: increase incrementally to spironolactone max 400 mg/day + furosemide max 160 mg/day
  • Refractory ascites = persistent ascites on maximum diuretics with low-sodium diet compliance → large-volume paracentesis (LVP) or TIPS
  • After LVP ≥5 L: give IV 25% albumin at 6–8 g per liter of ascites removed to prevent circulatory dysfunction
  • TIPS effective for ascites control but does NOT improve survival and ↑ encephalopathy risk
  • Ascites prognosis: <50% survive 2 years after onset → consider liver transplant
🚫 Drugs to Avoid in Cirrhosis with Ascites
  • NSAIDs — risk of renal failure
  • Aminoglycosides — nephrotoxic
  • ACE inhibitors / ARBs — use cautiously with close BP and renal monitoring

Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis (SBP)

  • Spontaneous infection of ascitic fluid without intraabdominal source
  • Occurs in up to 30% of hospitalized cirrhotic patients with ascites
  • In-hospital mortality: 25%
  • Mechanism: bacterial translocation — gut flora traverse the intestine → mesenteric lymph nodes → bacteremia → seeding of ascitic fluid

Organisms

  • Most common: E. coli and other gut gram-negative bacteria
  • Gram-positives also: Streptococcus viridans, Staphylococcus aureus, Enterococcus spp.
  • More than 2 organisms → think secondary bacterial peritonitis (perforated viscus), NOT SBP

Diagnosis

🔢 SBP Diagnosis = Absolute Neutrophil Count (PMN) > 250/μL in Ascitic Fluid
Diagnosis made on PMN count — do NOT wait for culture results to start treatment. Cultures via direct injection into blood culture bottles at bedside. Patients may present with fever, altered mental status, ↑ WBC, abdominal pain, AKI — or may present WITHOUT any of these features (high clinical suspicion required).

Treatment

  • IV third-generation cephalosporin × 5 days (e.g., cefotaxime)
  • IV albumin: 1.5 g/kg on day 1 + 1.0 g/kg on day 3 → reduces risk of renal failure and improves survival

Prevention / Prophylaxis

  • Variceal hemorrhage: SBP frequency significantly increased → prophylaxis recommended at presentation with upper GI bleeding
  • After a prior SBP episode: quinolone antibiotic prophylaxis (e.g., norfloxacin) to prevent recurrence
  • Diagnostic paracentesis recommended for any hospitalized cirrhotic patient with ascites and complications or signs of infection
07

Hepatorenal Syndrome (HRS)

  • Functional renal failure without renal pathology — occurs in ~10% of patients with advanced cirrhosis or acute liver failure
  • Mechanism: marked renal vasoconstriction (likely multifactorial) + ↓ systemic vascular resistance

Types — New Terminology

HRS-AKI (formerly Type 1 HRS)
  • Progressive, rapid impairment of renal function
  • AKI defined: creatinine rise ≥0.3 mg/dL over 48 h OR ≥50% rise from baseline
  • Poor prognosis
HRS-CKD (formerly Type 2 HRS)
  • Reduction in GFR with elevated creatinine — stable
  • Better outcome than Type 1/HRS-AKI
  • Associated with refractory ascites

Diagnosis — Exclusion-Based

  • Stop diuretics; give albumin 1 g/kg/day × 48 h without significant improvement
  • Exclude other AKI causes: volume depletion, intrinsic renal (ATN, AIN, glomerulonephritis), obstructive

Treatment

  • First-line vasoconstrictors: terlipressin (preferred where available) or low-dose norepinephrine (requires ICU monitoring)
  • Third-line (US): midodrine (α-agonist) + octreotide + IV albumin
  • If treatment fails: renal replacement therapy
  • Best therapy = liver transplantation — recovery of renal function is typical post-transplant
  • Prognosis without transplant: poor (AKI-HRS or CKD-HRS)
08

Hepatic Encephalopathy (HE)

  • Alteration in mental status and cognitive function from liver failure
  • Mechanism: gut-derived neurotoxins (especially ammonia) not removed by failing liver → vascular shunting + decreased hepatic mass → reach brain
  • Ammonia levels poorly correlate with severity — do not use alone for diagnosis or monitoring
  • Other contributing compounds: false neurotransmitters, mercaptans

Precipitating Factors

  • Volume depletion
  • GI bleeding (large nitrogen load)
  • Hyponatremia
  • Infection (always tap ascites to rule out SBP)
  • Constipation
  • Medications (sedatives, narcotics)

Clinical Features

  • Confusion, personality change, violent behavior, excessive somnolence
  • Asterixis (flapping tremor / "liver flap") — arms extended, wrists flexed back → sudden forward wrist movement
  • In acute liver failure: brain edema can occur → cerebral herniation is feared; treat with hypertonic saline or mannitol
  • Development of HE = poor prognostic sign

Treatment

  • Identify and treat precipitating factors — most important step
  • Lactulose (nonabsorbable disaccharide) — colonic acidification → catharsis → elimination of nitrogenous products; goal = 2–3 soft stools/day
  • Rifaximin 550 mg twice daily — poorly absorbed antibiotic; replaced neomycin and metronidazole (lower toxicity); effective in preventing recurrent HE
  • Zinc supplementation — sometimes helpful; relatively harmless
  • Protein restriction strongly discouraged — negative nutritional impact outweighs benefit; protein restriction is outdated
  • In recurrent HE despite treatment: closure of large portosystemic shunts can help
  • Electrolyte correction (esp. hyponatremia) essential

Acute-on-Chronic Liver Failure (ACLF)

🔴 ACLF
  • Acutely decompensating cirrhosis + failure of ≥1 organ systems (liver, kidneys, brain, lung, circulatory, coagulation)
  • Most common precipitants: alcoholic hepatitis (most common), infection (bacterial/fungal), GI bleeding, post-op state, flaring viral/autoimmune hepatitis, DILI
  • 28-day mortality: 20–70%; increases with number of organ failures
  • Treat precipitating cause + ICU if needed + consider immediate LT referral
  • If after 3–7 days ICU support: ≥4 organ failures + LT not an option → consider palliative care

Primary Biliary Cholangitis (PBC) & Primary Sclerosing Cholangitis (PSC)

PBC — Primary Biliary Cholangitis
  • 100–200 per million; strong female predominance; median age ~50
  • Portal inflammation + necrosis of cholangiocytes in small- and medium-sized bile ducts
  • AMAs (antimitochondrial antibodies) present in ~95% — target pyruvate dehydrogenase complex (PDC) on inner mitochondrial membrane
  • 5–10% are AMA-negative → check sp100 or gp210 antibodies; biopsy most important in this group
  • 4 histologic stages (chronic nonsuppurative destructive cholangitis → duct reduction → bridging fibrosis → cirrhosis)
  • Labs: ↑ ALP + GGT, mild ↑ ALT/AST, ↑ IgM, hyperbilirubinemia once cirrhotic, thrombocytopenia/leukopenia/anemia in portal HTN
  • Symptoms: fatigue (disproportionate to disease severity), pruritus (~50% at diagnosis) — intermittent, worst in evenings; pruritus before jaundice = severe disease + poor prognosis
  • Unique features: hyperpigmentation (trunk + arms), xanthelasma, xanthomata (↑ cholesterol), bone pain (osteoporosis)
  • "Overlap" syndrome: ~10% of PBC patients have features of AIH → usually treated as PBC; may need immunosuppressives
PSC — Primary Sclerosing Cholangitis
  • Chronic cholestatic syndrome; diffuse inflammation + fibrosis involving entire biliary tree (intra- + extrahepatic)
  • → Obliteration of biliary tree → biliary cirrhosis + portal HTN + liver failure
  • Diagnosis requires cholangiography — biopsy changes NOT pathognomonic; biopsy shows periductal fibrosis, ductopenia, bile duct proliferation
  • Cholangiographic findings: multifocal strictures + beading (short strictures + normal/dilated segments between them) → classic beaded appearance
  • MRCP = imaging of choice first; ERCP if MRCP suboptimal or dominant stricture or newly ↑ bilirubin
  • pANCA positive in ~65% of PSC patients
  • 60–80% of PSC patients have IBD, predominantly ulcerative colitis → colonoscopy at diagnosis
  • Labs: ALP ≥2× ULN + mild ↑ aminotransferases; ↓ albumin; ↑ PT; some have AIH overlap
  • Gallbladder and cystic duct involved in up to 15%
  • Dreaded complication: cholangiocarcinoma (relative contraindication to liver transplantation)

Other Complications of Cirrhosis

Hepatopulmonary Syndrome (HPS)
  • Arterial hypoxemia in cirrhosis without significant lung disease
  • Intrapulmonary vascular dilations → blood shunts past alveoli → V/Q mismatch
  • Symptoms: dyspnea, platypnea (dyspnea worse when upright → improved lying down)
  • Occurs in 4–32% of cirrhotic patients; often mild
  • Diagnosis: hypoxemia + bubble echocardiography (shunt)
  • Treatment: oxygen + liver transplantation
Portopulmonary Hypertension (PPHT)
  • Pulmonary hypertension in a patient with portal hypertension
  • Portal HTN → vasoconstrictor substances affect pulmonary artery
  • Rare — ~5% of advanced cirrhosis
  • Diagnosis: echocardiogram → confirm with right heart catheterization (↑ mean PAP, ↑ PVR, normal PCWP)
  • Treatment: vasodilatory medications → then liver transplantation if PAP normalized
Coagulopathy in Cirrhosis
  • Both pro- and anticoagulant factors are decreased → "rebalanced" coagulation
  • PT/INR do NOT reliably reflect clotting ability in cirrhosis
  • Platelet function is impaired but offset by ↑ von Willebrand factor
  • Adequate thrombin can form with platelets >50,000–60,000/L
  • Vitamin K-dependent factors (II, VII, IX, X) deficient in cholestatic disease → IV/IM vitamin K can correct
  • Routine FFP before procedures not required; clinical judgment over labs
  • Standard DVT prophylaxis when hospitalized is recommended
Malnutrition & Sarcopenia
  • Patients become catabolic with ↑ energy needs but ↓ intake
  • Sarcopenia = loss in muscle mass and function → frailty → ↑ mortality
  • Nutrition goals: 35 kcal/kg/day + 1.2–1.5 g/kg/day protein
  • Multiple small meals recommended throughout waking hours
  • Exercise: 150–300 min/week aerobic + 2 days muscle-strengthening
  • Protein restriction is strongly discouraged
Bone Disease
  • Osteoporosis common — especially in cholestatic liver disease (malabsorption of vitamin D + ↓ calcium)
  • Rate of bone resorption > new bone formation
  • DEXA scan to assess; bisphosphonates if osteoporosis confirmed
  • Screen fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) if bilirubin >2 mg/dL
Hematologic Abnormalities
  • Anemia — multiple causes: hypersplenism, hemolysis, iron deficiency, folate deficiency
  • Macrocytosis — common in chronic liver disease
  • Neutropenia — from hypersplenism
  • Thrombocytopenia — portal HTN → hypersplenism; also platelet dysfunction
  • Zieve's syndrome — hemolytic anemia with spur cells/acanthocytes in severe alcoholic hepatitis

Exam Traps

Trap 01Transfuse liberally in variceal bleeding? — No, restrictive strategy wins
A cirrhotic patient presents with massive variceal bleeding. Hemoglobin is 8.5 g/dL. Should packed red blood cells be transfused?
Not yet — the threshold is Hgb <7 g/dL (restrictive strategy). A randomized trial demonstrated that starting transfusion at Hgb <7 g/dL with a target of 7–9 g/dL reduced early rebleeding and mortality compared to a liberal strategy. Liberal transfusion increases portal pressure, worsening bleeding. The restrictive strategy is now the standard. Note: adjustments should be made based on cardiac risks and hemodynamics.
Trap 02Give FFP to correct the PT before endoscopy? — Not recommended
A cirrhotic patient is actively bleeding from varices. PT is prolonged at 21 seconds (INR 2.3). Should FFP be given to "correct" the coagulopathy before endoscopy?
No — routine correction of elevated PT/INR with FFP is NOT recommended in variceal bleeding. The PT/INR does not reliably reflect true coagulation capacity in cirrhosis (both pro- and anticoagulant factors are reduced; the system is rebalanced). FFP correction is only warranted if there is evidence of active coagulopathy causing bleeding at other sites. Proceeding directly to endoscopy (with antibiotics and octreotide) is the correct approach.
Trap 03SAAG ≥1.1 only = cirrhosis? — No, also includes cardiac ascites
A patient with severe CHF develops ascites. SAAG is 1.4 g/dL. Does this confirm cirrhotic ascites?
No — SAAG ≥1.1 indicates portal hypertension, not specifically cirrhosis. Cardiac ascites also gives SAAG ≥1.1 because right-sided heart failure elevates hepatic vein pressure, creating portal hypertension. The distinguishing feature is ascitic fluid PROTEIN: in cardiac ascites, protein is typically >2.5 g/dL (rich exudate). In cirrhotic ascites, protein is typically <2.5 g/dL. Always check both SAAG and ascitic protein.
Trap 04SBP — "No symptoms" does NOT exclude it
A hospitalized cirrhotic patient with ascites has no fever, no abdominal pain, and no change in mental status. Is SBP excluded?
No — SBP can be entirely asymptomatic. The classic features (fever, abdominal pain, altered mental status) may all be absent in SBP. The diagnosis is made on ascitic fluid PMN count (>250/μL), NOT on clinical symptoms. Any hospitalized cirrhotic with ascites who develops complications or signs of infection should have a diagnostic paracentesis. Missing SBP in a "well-appearing" patient is a serious boards (and clinical) trap.
Trap 05HRS — always rule out other AKI first
A cirrhotic patient with large ascites develops rising creatinine. How is HRS diagnosed?
HRS is a diagnosis of exclusion. First, stop diuretics and give albumin challenge (1 g/kg/day × 48 h). If creatinine does not improve significantly and other causes (ATN, AIN, glomerulonephritis, obstruction, volume depletion) are excluded, then HRS-AKI is diagnosed. Volume depletion is the most common alternative cause and must always be excluded first. The albumin challenge step is essential — skipping it before diagnosing HRS is a board trap.
Trap 06Restrict protein in HE? — Strongly discouraged
A cirrhotic patient is admitted with hepatic encephalopathy. Should dietary protein be restricted to reduce ammonia production?
No — protein restriction is strongly discouraged in cirrhosis with HE. While protein generates ammonia, restricting protein causes severe malnutrition, worsens sarcopenia, and increases mortality. The negative impact on nutrition outweighs any benefit in reducing ammonia. Patients with cirrhosis need INCREASED protein (1.2–1.5 g/kg/day). Lactulose and rifaximin address ammonia without sacrificing nutrition. This was standard teaching 30 years ago but is now explicitly reversed.
Trap 07PBC — AMA negative does NOT exclude it
A 52-year-old woman presents with cholestatic liver enzymes, fatigue, and pruritus. AMA is negative. Is PBC excluded?
No — AMA is negative in 5–10% of PBC patients. In AMA-negative patients, check sp100 and gp210 antibodies (PBC-specific, though not universally available). Liver biopsy is most important in AMA-negative patients. Additionally, PSC must be ruled out with cholangiography (MRCP) in AMA-negative cholestatic disease. AMA-negative PBC still shows the same histologic features and progresses at the same rate as typical PBC.
Trap 08High-dose UDCA in PSC is harmful — not just ineffective
A patient with PSC is not responding to standard-dose UDCA (13–15 mg/kg/day). Should the dose be escalated to 28–30 mg/kg/day for a better effect?
No — high-dose UDCA (28–30 mg/kg/day) in PSC is specifically shown to be HARMFUL. This is a high-yield board distinction: in PBC, UDCA at 13–15 mg/kg/day is beneficial. In PSC, standard UDCA is of unproven benefit (anecdotal only), and high-dose UDCA was harmful in a clinical study. The only effective treatment for end-stage PSC is liver transplantation. Endoscopic dilation of dominant strictures may help symptomatically.
Trap 09TIPS improves ascites but does NOT improve survival
A patient with refractory ascites is told that TIPS will improve survival compared to repeated large-volume paracentesis. Is this true?
No — TIPS is effective at managing ascites but multiple studies have shown it does NOT improve survival in patients with refractory ascites. It is also associated with increased frequency of hepatic encephalopathy (up to 20% post-TIPS). TIPS is appropriate for ascites refractory to diuretics and LVP, but the primary treatment goal for end-stage ascites remains liver transplantation evaluation. TIPS can serve as a bridge to transplant, not a substitute for it.
Trap 10Cryptogenic cirrhosis = often burned-out MASH
A 58-year-old obese diabetic man has cirrhosis. Liver biopsy shows no steatosis, no inflammation, and no specific etiology. All viral and autoimmune markers are negative. He is labeled "cryptogenic cirrhosis." Is this the correct final diagnosis?
Not necessarily — this is likely burned-out MASH (MASLD-related cirrhosis). As MASH cirrhosis progresses, patients become catabolic and lose the histologic hallmarks of steatosis. The biopsy then appears "cryptogenic" — fat is no longer visible. Clues: obesity, T2DM, metabolic syndrome. Many patients previously labeled cryptogenic were retrospectively found to have MASH. The diagnosis of MASH-related cirrhosis should be considered in any patient with metabolic risk factors and unexplained cirrhosis.
