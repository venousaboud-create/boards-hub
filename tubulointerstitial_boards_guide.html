Overview & Classification

The Core Concept

Inflammation or fibrosis of the renal interstitium with tubular atrophy is a common secondary consequence of glomerular or vascular disease. However, a distinct group of disorders primarily targets the tubules and interstitium, with relative sparing of glomeruli and renal vessels. These are the tubulointerstitial nephritides (TIN).

Key Concept
The fundamental distinction: Primary TIN targets tubules/interstitium first → glomeruli and vessels are relatively spared. Secondary TIN occurs downstream of glomerular or vascular disease.

Acute TIN — Clinical Features

  • Most often presents as acute kidney injury (AKI)
  • Mechanism of acuity:
    • Aggressive inflammatory infiltrates → tissue edema + tubular cell injury + compromised tubular flow
    • OR frank obstruction of tubules with casts, cellular debris, or crystals
  • Sometimes flank pain due to distention of the renal capsule
  • Urinary sediment: often active with leukocytes and cellular casts (depends on exact disorder)

Chronic TIN — Clinical Features

More indolent. Manifests with disorders of tubular function:

Distal Tubule Dysfunction

Nephrogenic DI: polyuria from impaired concentrating ability

Type IV RTA: non-anion-gap metabolic acidosis + hyperkalemia due to impaired ammoniagenesis

Proximal Tubule Dysfunction

Fanconi's Syndrome: glycosuria + phosphaturia + aminoaciduria + hypokalemia + Type II RTA (bicarbonaturia)

Proteinuria

Often modest (<2 g/d) due to decreased tubular reabsorption of filtered proteins. Nephrotic-range albuminuria possible if secondary FSGS develops.

Azotemia

Progressive rise in creatinine and BUN. Chronic TIN is insidious — often advanced before recognition.

Imaging — "Medical Renal Disease"
Renal ultrasound may show: increased echogenicity with loss of corticomedullary differentiation · prominence of renal pyramids · cortical scarring
Histopathology of Chronic TIN
Interstitial fibrosis + patchy mononuclear cell infiltration + widespread tubular atrophy + luminal dilation + thickening of tubular basement membranes. Nonspecific — rarely gives specific diagnosis. Diagnosis relies on history, drug/toxin exposure, symptoms, and imaging.

Allergic Interstitial Nephritis (Drug-Induced AIN)

Epidemiology

Biopsy-proven AIN accounts for ~15% of cases of unexplained AKI — but this is a substantial underestimate because offending medications are often identified and empirically discontinued without biopsy confirmation.

Historical Note
In 1897, Councilman described AIN as "an acute inflammation of the kidney characterized by cellular and fluid exudation in the interstitial tissue, accompanied by, but not dependent on, degeneration of the epithelium." Today it is far more often an allergic drug reaction.

Causative Drugs

Antibiotics

β-lactams · sulfonamides · quinolones · vancomycin · erythromycin · linezolid · minocycline · rifampin · ethambutol · acyclovir

NSAIDs / COX-2 inh

NSAIDs · COX-2 inhibitors (atypical presentation — no classic triad)

GI/CV Drugs

Proton pump inhibitors · H₂ blockers · captopril · mesalazine · indinavir · allopurinol

Diuretics

Rarely: thiazides · loop diuretics · triamterene

Anticonvulsants

Phenytoin · valproate · carbamazepine · phenobarbital

Immune Modulators

Immune checkpoint inhibitors · vedolizumab · lenalidomide

Clinical Presentation

⚠ Board High-Yield — Classic Triad Is the EXCEPTION
The classic triad of fever + rash + peripheral eosinophilia + oliguric AKI after 7–10 days of methicillin/β-lactam is the exception rather than the rule. More often, patients are found incidentally to have a rising serum creatinine or present with nonspecific AKI symptoms.
Typical Presentation

Most common: Incidental rising creatinine or nonspecific AKI symptoms

Fever + rash + eosinophilia only in minority

Onset 7–10 days after offending drug (β-lactams)

NSAID-Induced AIN (Atypical)

Fever, rash, and eosinophilia are RARE

But: AKI + heavy proteinuria is common

Heavy proteinuria distinguishes NSAIDs from other drug AIN

Rifampin — Special Warning
A particularly severe and rapid-onset AIN may occur upon reintroduction of rifampin after a drug-free period. This is a classic board trap — discontinuing rifampin and restarting is high risk.

More insidious reactions to PPIs, sulfonamides, 5-aminosalicylates (mesalazine/sulfasalazine), and antiretrovirals may lead to progressive tubulointerstitial damage. Whether PPIs cause chronic kidney disease via subclinical prolonged interstitial nephritis is still unclear.

Diagnosis

  • Primary finding: Otherwise unexplained AKI (with or without oliguria) + exposure to a potentially offending agent
  • Peripheral eosinophilia: Supporting evidence but present in only a minority of patients
  • Urinalysis: Pyuria + white blood cell casts + hematuria
  • Urinary eosinophils: Neither sensitive nor specific for AIN — testing is NOT recommended
  • Kidney biopsy: Generally not required but reveals extensive interstitial + tubular infiltration of leukocytes including eosinophils
Board Trap
Urinary eosinophils are neither sensitive nor specific — do NOT rely on them to rule in or rule out AIN.

Treatment Principles
1. Discontinue the offending agent — often sufficient; may not be completely reversible if prolonged exposure + tubular atrophy + interstitial fibrosis have occurred.

2. Glucocorticoids — may accelerate recovery; not required in most cases. Best reserved for: severe AKI where dialysis is imminent + start promptly if function continues to deteriorate despite drug cessation.

Key point: Delay in initiating dialysis leads to worse long-term outcomes.

Absolute Indications for Corticosteroids/Immunosuppressives

  • Sjögren's syndrome
  • Sarcoidosis
  • SLE interstitial nephritis
  • Adults with TINU
  • Interstitial nephritis from IgG4-related disease
  • Idiopathic and other granulomatous interstitial nephritis

Relative Indications

  • Drug-induced or idiopathic AIN with: rapid progression of renal failure · diffuse infiltrates on biopsy · impending need for dialysis · delayed recovery
  • Children with TINU
  • Postinfectious AIN with delayed recovery (?)

Sjögren's Syndrome & TINU

Sjögren's Syndrome

Core Concept
Sjögren's is a systemic autoimmune disorder primarily targeting exocrine glands (lacrimal + salivary) → "sicca syndrome" (dry eyes + dry mouth). TIN is its most common renal manifestation.
  • Renal pathology: TIN with predominant lymphocytic infiltrate (most common renal manifestation)
  • Renal manifestations:
    • Impaired kidney function
    • Distal RTA
    • Nephrogenic diabetes insipidus
  • Serology: anti-Ro (SS-A) + anti-La (SS-B) antibodies → strongly supports diagnosis
  • Other labs: polyclonal hypergammaglobulinemia in a large proportion of patients
Treatment
Initial: Glucocorticoids
Maintenance: Azathioprine or mycophenolate mofetil (MMF) to prevent relapse

Tubulointerstitial Nephritis with Uveitis (TINU)

Epidemiology

<5% of all AIN · Females 3× more often than males · Median age onset: 15 years

Hallmarks

Lymphocyte-predominant interstitial nephritis + painful anterior uveitis (bilateral, blurred vision, photophobia)

Temporal Relationship

Ocular symptoms precede or accompany kidney disease in only 1/3 of cases — diagnosis often confounded!

Additional Extrarenal Features

  • Fever · anorexia · weight loss · abdominal pain · arthralgia

Laboratory Clues to Suspect TINU

  • Elevated creatinine
  • Sterile pyuria
  • Mild proteinuria
  • Features of Fanconi's syndrome
  • Elevated ESR
  • Serologies for common autoimmune diseases are usually negative
Diagnosis of Exclusion
TINU is a diagnosis of exclusion after ruling out: Sjögren's syndrome · Behçet's disease · Sarcoidosis · SLE. All of these can cause both uveitis and kidney disease.
Children

Clinical symptoms are typically self-limited

Adults

More apt to follow a relapsing course

Treatment
Renal and ocular manifestations: Respond well to oral glucocorticoids
Maintenance (to prevent relapses): Methotrexate · azathioprine · or mycophenolate
Note: Adults with TINU = ABSOLUTE indication for corticosteroids; children with TINU = relative indication

SLE, Granulomatous & IgG4-Related AIN

Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)

  • An interstitial mononuclear cell inflammatory reaction accompanies the glomerular lesion in most cases of class III or IV lupus nephritis
  • Immune complex deposits can be identified in tubular basement membranes in ~50% of cases
  • Occasionally, tubulointerstitial inflammation predominates and may manifest with:
    • Azotemia
    • Type IV RTA
    • Lacking features of glomerulonephritis
Board Pearl
When SLE-related kidney disease presents with azotemia + Type IV RTA without nephritic/nephrotic features, think tubulointerstitial predominance. SLE interstitial nephritis = absolute indication for corticosteroids.

Granulomatous Interstitial Nephritis

  • Course: Protracted and relapsing — distinct from typical drug-induced AIN
  • Biopsy: Chronic inflammatory infiltrate with granulomas + multinucleated giant cells
  • Most often: No associated disease or cause found
  • Some patients subsequently develop pulmonary, cutaneous, or other systemic manifestations of sarcoidosis (e.g., hypercalcemia)
Critical: Rule Out TB First
Tuberculosis is a rare cause of granulomatous interstitial nephritis. Always rule out TB before starting glucocorticoids — starting steroids in undiagnosed TB is dangerous.
Treatment
Most patients: Some improvement in kidney function if treated early with glucocorticoids — before significant interstitial fibrosis and tubular atrophy develop.
Frequent relapsers upon steroid withdrawal: Other immunosuppressive agents required.

IgG4-Related Systemic Disease

What It Is
A form of AIN characterized by a dense inflammatory infiltrate containing IgG4-expressing plasma cells, occurring as part of IgG4-related systemic disease.

Associated Conditions (variable)

  • Autoimmune pancreatitis
  • Sclerosing cholangitis
  • Retroperitoneal fibrosis
  • Chronic sclerosing sialadenitis (mimicking Sjögren's syndrome)
The Pseudotumor Trap
Fibrotic lesions form pseudotumors in affected organs, soon replacing the initial inflammatory infiltrates. These often lead to biopsy or excision for fear of true malignancy. This is the clinical red flag to consider IgG4-RD.
Treatment
Glucocorticoids are successfully used as first-line treatment once correctly diagnosed. The role of IgG4 in pathogenesis is not yet fully understood.

Checkpoint Inhibitors, Idiopathic & Infection-Associated AIN

AIN from Immune Checkpoint Inhibitors

Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) have revolutionized cancer care by disrupting mechanisms that allow tumor cells to evade immune surveillance. However, this immunostimulation comes at the cost of autoimmune side effects.

Incidence of Renal Impact

2% with monotherapy
Up to 5% with dual checkpoint inhibitor therapy

Timing — Key Exam Point

Creatinine rise typically within 15 weeks after starting, but can occur up to 2 months after the final dose

  • Biopsy: Typically shows acute interstitial inflammation; glomerular pathologies may also be found
  • Confounders: Patients often concurrently taking PPIs or NSAIDs (themselves causes of drug-induced TIN)
Treatment of ICI-Induced AKI
1. Corticosteroids (for severe AKI)
2. Discontinue potential inciting medications (PPIs, NSAIDs)
3. Avoid further checkpoint inhibitor doses until kidney function recovers
4. Reinitiation for treatment of underlying malignancy may be possible in some cases

Idiopathic AIN

  • Typical clinical and histologic features of AIN but no drug exposure + no clinical/serologic features of autoimmune disease
  • Pathogenesis clue: Some cases have autoantibodies to a tubular antigen (similar to those identified in rats with induced interstitial nephritis) → suggests autoimmune response
  • Recently identified: autoantibodies targeting antigens expressed by:
    • Collecting duct epithelium
    • Proximal tubular brush border (anti-LRP2 / anti-brush border disease)
  • Course: Like TINU and granulomatous IN — responsive to glucocorticoids but may follow relapsing course requiring maintenance IS therapy

Infection-Associated AIN

Distinguish from Bacterial Pyelonephritis
Acute bacterial pyelonephritis does NOT generally cause AKI unless it affects both kidneys or causes septic shock. Infection-associated AIN is a different entity — local inflammatory reaction to microbial infection.

Common Pathogens

Bacteria

Streptococcus · Staphylococcus · Legionella · Salmonella · Brucella · Yersinia · Corynebacterium diphtheriae

Viruses

EBV · CMV · hantavirus · polyomavirus BK · HIV

Miscellaneous

Leptospira · Rickettsia · Mycoplasma · Histoplasma

Modern Relevance

Polyomavirus BK reactivation in kidney transplant recipients (immunocompromised) = most common current scenario

SARS-CoV-2: Significant increase in acute TIN (±uveitis / TINU) in children post-infection

Crystal Deposition Disorders & Light Chain Cast Nephropathy

Crystal Deposition — Overview

AKI occurs when crystals are deposited in tubular cells and interstitium, or when they obstruct tubules. Often accompanied by flank pain from tubular obstruction.

Drug-Induced Crystal Nephropathy

Treatment
Generally precipitated by hypovolemia → reversible with saline volume repletion + drug withdrawal. Note: Indinavir can also cause a frank AIN from crystal deposition (distinct from obstructive mechanism).

Acute Urate Nephropathy

  • Mechanism: Severe hyperuricemia → uric acid crystallization in tubules + collecting system → partial or complete obstruction of collecting ducts, renal pelvis, or ureter
  • Classic setting: Tumor lysis syndrome in patients with lympho- or myeloproliferative disorders treated with cytotoxic agents; can occur spontaneously before treatment
  • Urine: Dense precipitate of birefringent uric acid crystals + microscopic or gross hematuria
Board Trap — Allopurinol Timing
Prophylactic allopurinol reduces risk of uric acid nephropathy before tumor lysis, but is of NO benefit once tumor lysis has occurred.

Once oliguria develops: emergent treatment with hemodialysis OR rasburicase (recombinant urate oxidase) is required to rapidly lower uric acid levels and restore kidney function.
Alkaline diuresis may provide some benefit as well.

Other Crystal Disorders

Calcium Oxalate

Settings: ethylene glycol intoxication · enteric hyperoxaluria (ileal resection or small-bowel bypass) · hereditary hyperoxaluria
May lead to permanent kidney dysfunction

Acute Phosphate Nephropathy

Oral Phosphosoda (laxative/colonoscopy prep) → calcium phosphate crystals in tubules and interstitium
Occurs especially in: underlying CKD + hypovolemia
Phosphosoda should be avoided in CKD patients — no longer widely available in the US

Light Chain Cast Nephropathy (Myeloma Kidney)

When to Think of It
In any elderly patient with otherwise unexplained AKI. Also: myeloma patients who fail to recover once a precipitating factor is corrected. Consider even in known MGUS (monoclonal gammopathy) without frank myeloma.

Pathogenesis — Step by Step

  • Filtered monoclonal immunoglobulin light chains (Bence-Jones proteins) form intratubular aggregates with secreted Tamm-Horsfall protein in the distal tubule
  • Casts obstruct tubular flow in affected nephrons
  • Casts incite a giant cell / foreign-body reaction → can lead to tubular rupture → interstitial fibrosis
  • Direct toxicity: Filtered monoclonal light chains also cause proximal tubular cell toxicity + intracellular crystal formation → isolated tubular disorders (RTA or full Fanconi's syndrome)
Precipitating Factors in Myeloma Patients
Hypovolemia · infection · hypercalcemia · NSAIDs · radiographic contrast media

Diagnosis

Clinical Clues

Anemia · bone pain · hypercalcemia · abnormally narrow anion gap (due to hypoalbuminemia + hypergammaglobulinemia)

Urine Key Point

Urinary dipstick detects albumin but NOT immunoglobulin light chains. Increased protein on laboratory testing + negative dipstick = highly suggestive of Bence-Jones protein

  • Send: Serum and urine protein electrophoresis (SPEP + UPEP) + immunofixation for detection and identification of potential monoclonal band
  • Sensitive method: serum and urine free light chain assay
Treatment Goals
1. Correct precipitating factors (hypovolemia, hypercalcemia)
2. Discontinue potential nephrotoxic agents
3. Treat underlying plasma cell dyscrasia
4. Plasmapheresis to remove light chains = questionable value for LCCN

Lymphomatous Infiltration of the Kidney

  • Interstitial infiltration by malignant B lymphocytes — common autopsy finding in chronic lymphocytic leukemia and non-Hodgkin's lymphoma; usually incidental
  • Rarely: massive kidney enlargement + oliguric AKI
  • Treatment: high-dose glucocorticoids + chemotherapy may recover kidney function, but prognosis is generally poor

Chronic TIN: Structural Causes

Vesicoureteral Reflux (VUR) & Reflux Nephropathy

Historical Name
Previously called "chronic pyelonephritis" because it was believed to result from recurrent UTIs. We now know the mechanism is VUR-related.

Pathogenesis

  • VUR = abnormal retrograde urine flow from bladder into one or both ureters and kidneys due to mislocated and incompetent ureterovesical valves
  • High-pressure sterile reflux → impairs normal kidney growth
  • When coupled with recurrent UTIs in early childhood → patchy interstitial scarring + tubular atrophy
  • Loss of functioning nephrons → hypertrophy of remnant glomeruli → eventual secondary FSGS

Clinical Presentation

  • Often unnoticed until early adulthood — detected during routine evaluation or pregnancy
  • Adults: frequently asymptomatic; history of prolonged bed-wetting or recurrent UTIs in childhood
  • Variable degrees of kidney injury + hypertension + mild-to-moderate proteinuria + unremarkable urine sediment
  • Bilateral involvement: Often progresses inexorably over several years to ESRD despite absence of ongoing UTIs or reflux
  • Unilateral: May go undetected except for hypertension
Ultrasound in Adults
Asymmetric small kidneys · irregular outlines · thinned cortices · regions of compensatory hypertrophy
Treatment
Surgical reimplantation of ureters: Indicated in young children with persistent high-grade reflux. NOT effective and NOT indicated in adolescents or adults after scarring has occurred.

Medical management: Aggressive BP control + ACEI or ARB + SGLT2 inhibitors → reduces proteinuria + may significantly forestall further kidney deterioration

Sickle Cell Nephropathy

  • Early tubular injury (childhood and early adolescence) — appears years before significant nephron loss and proteinuria from secondary FSGS:
    • Polyuria from decreased concentrating ability
    • Type IV RTA
  • Early recognition: Microalbuminuria → nephrologist consultation + low-dose ACEIs
  • Papillary necrosis may result from:
    • Ischemia due to sickling of red cells in the relatively hypoxemic + hypertonic medullary vasculature
    • Presents with: gross hematuria + ureteric obstruction by sloughed ischemic papillae
Major Causes of Papillary Necrosis
Analgesic nephropathy · Sickle cell nephropathy · Diabetes with urinary tract infection · Prolonged NSAID use (rare)
Mnemonic: ASDN

Tubulointerstitial Abnormalities with Glomerulonephritis

Primary glomerulopathies are often associated with damage to tubules and interstitium. This can occur via:

  • Same pathologic process (e.g., immune-complex deposition in lupus nephritis)
  • Secondary consequences of prolonged glomerular dysfunction:
    • Proteinuria-mediated damage to epithelial cells
    • Activation of tubular cells by cytokines and complement
    • Reduced peritubular blood flow → downstream tubulointerstitial ischemia (especially with globally obsolescent glomeruli from severe GN)
Clinical Dilemma
Often difficult to discern the initial cause of injury by kidney biopsy in a patient presenting with advanced kidney disease in this setting — the secondary changes obscure the primary pathology.

Chronic TIN: Drugs & Toxins

Analgesic Nephropathy

Classic Agent
Long-term use of compound analgesic preparations containing phenacetin (banned in US since 1983) + aspirin + caffeine.

Classic Features

  • Impaired kidney function
  • Papillary necrosis — attributed to presumed concentration of drug to toxic levels in the inner medulla
  • Radiographic constellation: small, scarred kidneys with papillary calcifications
  • Polyuria due to impaired concentrating ability
  • Non-anion-gap metabolic acidosis from tubular damage
  • Shedding of a sloughed necrotic papilla → gross hematuria + ureteric colic (ureteral obstruction)
Long-Term Risk
Individuals with ESRD from analgesic nephropathy are at increased risk of urothelial malignancy compared to patients with other causes of kidney failure.

Aristolochic Acid Nephropathy (AAN)

Two Conditions — One Cause
Chinese herbal nephropathy + Balkan endemic nephropathy — two seemingly unrelated forms of CIN — are now linked by the common etiologic agent aristolochic acid from the plant Aristolochia.

Two Sources

Chinese Herbal Nephropathy

First described early 1990s in young women taking traditional Chinese herbal preparations for weight loss. Aristolochic acid identified as the offending agent. Aristolochia species used in herbal remedies for centuries — still available despite official bans in many countries.

Balkan Endemic Nephropathy

Chronic TIN found primarily in towns along tributaries of the Danube River — first described in 1950s. Most likely route: contamination of local grain preparations with seeds of Aristolochia species. Molecular evidence implicates aristolochic acid.

Pathology & Presentation

  • Renal interstitial fibrosis with a relative paucity of cellular infiltrates
  • Bland urine sediment (rare leukocytes)
  • Only mild proteinuria
  • Anemia may be disproportionately severe relative to degree of kidney dysfunction
Diagnosis — Requires 2 of 3 Features
1. Characteristic histology on kidney biopsy
2. Confirmation of aristolochic acid ingestion
3. Detection of aristolactam-DNA adducts in kidney or urinary tract tissue — represent molecular signature of aristolochic acid–derived DNA damage; often A:T-to-T:A transversions
Malignancy Risk — High Priority
Due to mutagenic activity, AAN is associated with a very high incidence of upper urinary tract urothelial cancers, with risk related to cumulative dose.

Surveillance: CT + ureteroscopy + urine cytology
At ESRD: Consideration should be given to bilateral nephroureterectomy

Karyomegalic Interstitial Nephritis

  • Unusual form of slowly progressive CKD with mild proteinuria + interstitial fibrosis + tubular atrophy
  • Distinguishing feature: oddly enlarged nuclei of proximal tubular epithelial cells
  • Linked to mutations in FAN1 (a nuclease involved in DNA repair) → may render carriers susceptible to environmental DNA-damaging agents

Lithium-Associated Nephropathy

Spectrum of Renal Effects

Most Common (Reversible)

Nephrogenic Diabetes Insipidus → polyuria + polydipsia

Less Common (Irreversible)

Chronic TIN after prolonged (>10–20 years) use; most likely with repeated episodes of toxic lithium levels

Mechanism of Nephrogenic DI

  • Lithium accumulates in principal cells of the collecting duct by entering through the epithelial sodium channel (ENaC)
  • → Inhibits glycogen synthase kinase 3β
  • → Downregulates vasopressin-regulated aquaporin water channels
  • → Collecting duct becomes unresponsive to ADH → free water loss
Biopsy Findings (Highly Characteristic)
Interstitial fibrosis + tubular atrophy out of proportion to glomerulosclerosis or vascular disease · sparse lymphocytic infiltrate · small cysts or dilation of distal tubule and collecting duct (HIGHLY CHARACTERISTIC of lithium nephropathy)
  • Degree of interstitial fibrosis correlates with both duration and cumulative dose of lithium
  • Individuals are typically asymptomatic: minimal proteinuria + few urinary leukocytes + normal blood pressure
  • Some develop more severe proteinuria due to secondary FSGS
Treatment
Monitor: Kidney function regularly in all patients taking lithium; caution in underlying CKD
For nephrogenic DI: Amiloride (inhibits ENaC) — effective to prevent and treat; not clear if prevents CIN
Target lithium level: <1 meq/L — especially important as kidney function declines (lithium cleared less effectively)
Key challenge: Lithium is an effective mood stabilizer often incompletely substituted by other agents. Despite discontinuation, CKD is often irreversible and can slowly progress to ESRD. The most prudent approach is frequent monitoring and dose adjustment to avoid toxic levels.

Calcineurin Inhibitor (CNI) Nephrotoxicity

Agents
Cyclosporine + Tacrolimus — both can cause acute AND chronic kidney injury
Acute CNI Toxicity

Vascular: vasoconstriction or thrombotic microangiopathy
Toxic tubulopathy

Chronic CNI Toxicity

Typically in solid organ transplant recipients (heart-lung, liver)
Slow but irreversible reduction in GFR + mild proteinuria + arterial hypertension
Hyperkalemia (relatively common) from tubular resistance to aldosterone

Histologic Pattern
Patchy interstitial fibrosis + tubular atrophy in "striped" pattern · intrarenal vascular hyalinosis · focal glomerulosclerosis. Similar changes occur in CNI use for autoimmune diseases (lower doses).
Management
Dose reduction or CNI avoidance → mitigates chronic tubulointerstitial changes but may increase risk of rejection and graft loss. Clinical trade-off.

Heavy Metal (Lead) Nephropathy

Current Relevance
No longer commonly diagnosed in developed nations (lead removed from most products/fuels). Still relevant in: battery manufacturing/destruction · lead paint removal · alloy/electrical equipment manufacture · moonshine whiskey distilled in lead-tainted containers · countries with less stringent industrial regulation.

Pathogenesis & Clinical Features

  • Early signs: Proximal tubule dysfunction → particularly hyperuricemia from diminished urate secretion
  • Classic triad: "Saturnine gout" + hypertension + impaired kidney function → prompt specific inquiry about lead exposure
Evaluating Lead Burden
Blood test is NOT sufficient. Preferred methods:
1. Measuring urinary lead after infusion of a chelating agent
2. Radiographic fluoroscopy of bone
Treatment
In patients with CIN of unclear origin + elevated total body lead burden: repeated lead chelation therapy has been shown to slow decline in kidney function.

Metabolic Disorders Causing CIN

Chronic Uric Acid Nephropathy (Gouty Nephropathy)

Context
Very uncommon now (gout typically well managed with allopurinol). More historical interest than clinical importance. Emerging evidence: hyperuricemia is an independent risk factor for CKD, perhaps through endothelial damage.

Pathology

  • Crystalline deposits of uric acid + monosodium urate salts in the kidney parenchyma
  • Deposits → intrarenal obstruction + inflammatory response (lymphocytic infiltration + foreign-body giant cell reaction + fibrosis) especially in medullary and papillary regions
  • Since patients with gout frequently have hypertension + hyperlipidemia → degenerative changes of renal arterioles may be striking

Clinical Features

  • Insidious cause of CKD
  • Early in course: GFR may be near normal despite morphologic changes, proteinuria, and diminished urinary concentrating ability
Treatment
Allopurinol + urine alkalinization: generally effective in preventing uric acid nephrolithiasis and consequences of recurrent kidney stones. However, gouty nephropathy may be intractable. Allopurinol in asymptomatic hyperuricemia has not been consistently shown to improve kidney function.

Hypercalcemic Nephropathy

Causes of Chronic Hypercalcemia Leading to TIN

  • Primary hyperparathyroidism · Sarcoidosis · Multiple myeloma · Vitamin D intoxication · Metastatic bone disease

Pathogenesis — Sequential

  • Earliest lesion: Focal degenerative change in renal epithelia — primarily in collecting ducts, distal tubules, and loops of Henle
  • Tubular cell necrosis → nephron obstruction + stasis of intrarenal urine → local calcium salt precipitation + infection
  • Dilation + atrophy of tubules → interstitial fibrosis + mononuclear leukocyte infiltration + interstitial calcium deposition (nephrocalcinosis)
  • Calcium deposition may also occur in glomeruli + walls of renal arterioles

Clinical Features

  • Most striking defect: Inability to maximally concentrate urine
    • Due to reduced collecting duct responsiveness to arginine vasopressin
    • Defective transport of Na⁺ and Cl⁻ in the loop of Henle
  • Reductions in GFR and renal blood flow (both acute and chronic hypercalcemia)
  • Abdominal x-rays: nephrocalcinosis + nephrolithiasis (from hypercalciuria)
Treatment
Reduce serum calcium toward normal + correct primary abnormality of calcium metabolism.
Acute hypercalcemia: May be completely reversible.
Chronic hypercalcemia: Gradual progressive kidney dysfunction may not improve even with correction of the calcium disorder.

Hypokalemic Nephropathy

Causes

  • Chronic laxative or diuretic abuse · surreptitious vomiting · primary aldosteronism

Pathogenesis & Course

  • Early (reversible): Vacuolar degeneration of proximal and distal tubular cells
  • Eventually: Tubular atrophy + cystic dilation + interstitial fibrosis → irreversible CKD
Treatment
Timely correction of hypokalemia will prevent further progression. Persistent hypokalemia can cause ESRD.

Global Perspective

The causes of AIN and CIN vary widely across the globe:

  • Analgesic nephropathy: still seen in countries where phenacetin-containing analgesics are available
  • Traditional herbal preparations: aristolochic acid contamination remains prevalent in many Asian countries
  • Lead and cadmium: still a risk in countries with less stringent industrial regulation
  • New endemic forms (CINAC/CKDu): Chronic interstitial nephritis in agricultural communities (Mesoamerican nephropathy in Central America · Sri Lankan nephropathy · Uddanam nephropathy in southern India) — may be related to heat exposure, dehydration, volume depletion, toxins, pesticides, infective agents. Global warming proposed as contributor. Tens of thousands of lives lost to ESRD in resource-poor settings.

Exam Traps & High-Yield Pearls

Trap 01Classic AIN Triad Is the Exception, Not the Rule
A question presents a patient on methicillin for 2 weeks with rising creatinine but NO fever, rash, or eosinophilia. Can this still be AIN? What is the most likely diagnosis?
YES — AIN is the most likely diagnosis. The classic triad of fever + rash + peripheral eosinophilia + oliguria is the EXCEPTION. Most patients present with only incidentally rising serum creatinine or nonspecific AKI symptoms.
Trap 02NSAID AIN — Atypical Presentation with Heavy Proteinuria
A patient on chronic NSAIDs presents with AKI. There is no fever, no rash, no eosinophilia — but urinalysis shows heavy proteinuria. What is the most likely diagnosis and distinguishing feature?
NSAID-induced AIN. The classic triad is RARE with NSAIDs. Instead, the distinguishing feature is AKI with heavy proteinuria — highly atypical for other drug-induced AIN subtypes.
Trap 03Rifampin Reintroduction — Severe and Rapid Onset AIN
A patient with TB was successfully treated and rifampin was stopped. Several months later, rifampin is restarted for a relapse. Within days, the patient develops AKI. Why?
Reintroduction of rifampin after a drug-free period can cause a particularly severe and rapid-onset AIN. This is a sensitization reaction — the drug-free period primes the immune system for an exaggerated response on re-exposure.
Trap 04Urinary Eosinophils — DO NOT Order
An attending asks you to order urinary eosinophils to confirm allergic AIN in a patient with AKI and drug exposure. What do you say?
Urinary eosinophils are NEITHER sensitive nor specific for AIN — testing is NOT recommended. Peripheral blood eosinophilia is also only present in a minority. Diagnosis relies on AKI + offending agent exposure; biopsy if needed.
Trap 05TINU — Ocular Symptoms Are Often ABSENT at Kidney Diagnosis
A 14-year-old girl presents with AKI, elevated ESR, sterile pyuria, Fanconi features, and elevated creatinine — but denies eye symptoms. Serologies are negative. What is the diagnosis?
TINU (Tubulointerstitial Nephritis with Uveitis). Ocular symptoms (painful anterior uveitis, blurred vision, photophobia) precede or accompany kidney disease in only 1/3 of cases. TINU is often a diagnosis of exclusion. Demographics: female, young (median 15 years), 3:1 F:M.
Trap 06Granulomatous AIN — Rule Out TB Before Steroids
A patient presents with relapsing AIN. Biopsy shows granulomas and giant cells. No sarcoidosis features. You plan to start prednisone. What must you do first?
Rule out Tuberculosis FIRST. TB is a rare cause of granulomatous interstitial nephritis. Starting glucocorticoids in undiagnosed active TB is dangerous and could lead to dissemination.
Trap 07IgG4 — Pseudotumors May Be Misdiagnosed as Malignancy
A patient has AIN with IgG4-expressing plasma cells on biopsy. Later, imaging shows mass lesions in the pancreas, kidneys, and retroperitoneum. What is the concern and what do you do?
These are fibrotic pseudotumors of IgG4-related systemic disease, NOT true malignancies. Failure to recognize IgG4-RD leads to unnecessary biopsies or excisions. Once correctly diagnosed, glucocorticoids are the first-line treatment.
Trap 08ICI AIN — Can Occur Up to 2 Months AFTER the Last Dose
A patient who completed a course of checkpoint inhibitor therapy 6 weeks ago (now off all therapy) presents with rising creatinine. Is ICI-induced AIN still possible?
YES — Checkpoint inhibitor AIN can occur up to 2 months after the final dose. Always inquire about recent ICI therapy even if the patient is no longer actively receiving treatment. Creatinine rise typically occurs within 15 weeks of starting but delayed presentation is well-documented.
Trap 09Myeloma Kidney — Dipstick Misses Bence-Jones Protein
An elderly patient with anemia, bone pain, hypercalcemia, and AKI has increased protein on laboratory measurement of a urine sample — but the urine dipstick is NEGATIVE for protein. What does this mean?
Urinary dipstick detects ALBUMIN but NOT immunoglobulin light chains. Increased protein on lab testing + negative dipstick = highly suggestive of Bence-Jones (light chain) protein. Order SPEP + UPEP + immunofixation + free light chains. This is myeloma kidney (LCCN) until proven otherwise.
Trap 10Allopurinol Has NO Benefit Once Tumor Lysis Has Occurred
A patient with lymphoma develops tumor lysis syndrome and oliguric AKI. The team wants to start allopurinol. Is this the right move?
Allopurinol is of NO benefit once tumor lysis has occurred. Prophylactic allopurinol reduces risk, but once oliguria develops, treatment requires emergent hemodialysis or rasburicase (recombinant urate oxidase) to rapidly lower uric acid and restore kidney function. Alkaline diuresis may provide some benefit.
Trap 11VUR Surgery — Do NOT Operate After Scarring Has Occurred
A 22-year-old woman is found to have bilateral reflux nephropathy with moderate CKD. She asks about surgery to fix the reflux. What do you advise?
Surgical reimplantation of ureters is NOT indicated in adolescents or adults after scarring has occurred — it is ineffective. Surgery is only appropriate for young children with persistent high-grade reflux. Treatment in adults: ACEI/ARB + SGLT2 inhibitors to control BP, reduce proteinuria, and slow progression.
Trap 12Lithium Biopsy — Small Cysts in Distal Tubule/Collecting Duct = Pathognomonic
A biopsy on a patient with bipolar disorder on lithium for 15 years shows interstitial fibrosis and tubular atrophy out of proportion to vascular disease, plus small microcysts in the distal tubules and collecting ducts. What is the diagnosis?
Lithium-associated nephropathy. Small cysts or dilation of the distal tubule and collecting duct are HIGHLY CHARACTERISTIC of lithium nephropathy. The degree of fibrosis correlates with duration and cumulative dose. Monitor levels; target <1 meq/L.
Trap 13Saturnine Gout Triad = Lead Exposure
A patient presents with the triad of gout, hypertension, and progressive kidney impairment. What is the specific diagnosis to consider and what additional history should you get?
Think lead nephropathy ("saturnine gout"). The triad of saturnine gout + hypertension + impaired kidney function should prompt specific inquiry about lead exposure (occupation, moonshine). Blood level is insufficient — measure urinary lead after chelating agent or bone fluoroscopy. Lead chelation therapy can slow CKD progression.
Trap 14Aristolochic Acid — Mandatory Surveillance for Urothelial Cancer
A patient from a Balkan region has aristolochic acid nephropathy and is now in ESRD on dialysis. What special surveillance/intervention should be considered?
Aristolochic acid nephropathy is associated with a very high incidence of upper urinary tract urothelial cancers (risk related to cumulative dose, due to mutagenic A:T-to-T:A transversions). Mandatory surveillance with CT + ureteroscopy + urine cytology. Consider bilateral nephroureterectomy once ESRD is reached.
