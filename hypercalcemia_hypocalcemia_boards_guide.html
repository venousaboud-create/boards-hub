Calcium Physiology & Feedback Mechanisms

Why Calcium Matters

Calcium is the most abundant mineral in the body. Its physiological roles span every major organ system — understanding WHY tight regulation is critical will explain every clinical manifestation you'll see on boards.

Skeleton

Structural strength as hydroxyapatite salt Ca₁₀(PO₄)₆(OH)₂

Cardiac

Pacemaker rhythmicity and contractility

Muscle

Skeletal muscle contractility

Signaling

Cellular signaling and hormone secretion

Immune

Immune function

Neural

Neural signaling

The Homeostatic Feedback Loop

Serum calcium is maintained within 8.5–10.5 mg/dL (2.1–2.6 mM) via a tight feedback system involving PTH and vitamin D metabolites — primarily 1,25(OH)₂D (active form) and 25(OH)D (storage form). The key signals occur on two timescales: intracellular (milliseconds) and extracellular (minutes–hours).

🔑 Key Concept
About 50% of total serum calcium is ionized (free, active); the remainder is bound principally to albumin. This is why you must always correct calcium for albumin before interpreting a result.
📐 Albumin Correction Formula
Corrected Ca = Total Ca + 0.8 × (4.0 − serum albumin)
Add 0.8 mg/dL for every 1.0 g/dL albumin drops below 4.0 g/dL
Subtract 0.8 mg/dL for every 1.0 g/dL albumin rises above 4.0 g/dL

Hypercalcemia — Etiology

The Two Most Common Causes

Most Common Overall

Primary Hyperparathyroidism (pHPT)

  • Usually a parathyroid adenoma (or hyperplasia) — non-malignant
  • Impaired feedback inhibition by calcium → PTH not suppressed even when Ca is high
  • Genetic causes found in ~30% even in sporadic (non-familial) cases — primarily affect cyclin D1 or menin expression
  • Reduced CaSR expression (via H3K27me3 and H3K9me3 hypermethylation) in ~50% of sporadic adenomas
  • Because of PTH's phosphaturic effect → pHPT often accompanied by low serum phosphate / hypophosphatemia
  • Typically chronic (months to years), fluctuating high-normal to mildly above normal
Second Most Common / More Acute

Hypercalcemia of Malignancy

  • PTHrP overproduction — many solid tumors; PTHrP shares homology with first 13 amino acids of PTH; binds PTH receptor → mimics PTH on bone and kidney
  • Lytic skeletal metastases — breast cancer, multiple myeloma
  • Excess 1,25(OH)₂D production — constitutive macrophage CYP27B1 activity in lymphomas (B cell, cutaneous T cell/Sézary syndrome)
  • Usually presents more acutely than pHPT
  • PTH is suppressed (normal parathyroids respond correctly to ↑Ca)

Full Etiology — Organized by Mechanism

A. Excessive PTH Production

  • Primary hyperparathyroidism — sporadic or familial (MEN), adenoma or hyperplasia; rarely parathyroid carcinoma
  • Tertiary hyperparathyroidism — long-term stimulation in renal insufficiency → autonomous PTH secretion
  • Ectopic PTH secretion — very rare; occurs in carcinomas with PTH gene rearrangements; does NOT fluctuate with therapeutic correction (not CaSR-controlled)
  • Familial Hypocalciuric Hypercalcemia (FHH) — autosomal dominant; inactivating mutations in CASR gene (FHH type 1, most common); rare mutations in GNA11, AP2S1; impairs extracellular calcium sensing in parathyroids AND kidneys → concomitant inappropriate PTH + decreased urinary calcium excretion
  • Nongenetic CaSR dysfunction — lithium therapy (↑ CaSR set point for negative feedback); rare CaSR inhibitory autoantibodies

B. Granulomatous Diseases & Lymphomas (Excess 1,25(OH)₂D)

  • Mechanism: Extrarenal conversion of 25(OH)D → 1,25(OH)₂D by macrophage CYP27B1 — this is a feature of local innate immune regulation by vitamin D that operates independent of normal negative feedback loops
  • This chronic 1,25(OH)₂D production enhances intestinal Ca absorption → hypercalcemia + suppressed PTH
  • Causes: sarcoidosis, tuberculosis, leprosy, fungal infections, foreign body reaction, B-cell lymphoma, cutaneous T-cell lymphoma / Sézary syndrome
  • Serum phosphate frequently elevated or high normal (increased GI absorption + reduced renal excretion)

C. Vitamin D Intoxication

  • Ingestion of excessive vitamin D (D₂/D₃ — cholecalciferol/ergocalciferol): Mechanism — acute vitamin D ingestion rapidly displaces 25(OH)D from plasma vitamin D binding protein (DBP). Although 25(OH)D potency is 500-fold less than 1,25(OH)₂D, the acute displacement exposes tissues to concentrations sufficient to directly activate vitamin D receptor in small intestine → ↑ transcellular Ca absorption. The long half-life of vitamin D and 25(OH)D can prolong hypercalcemia for several weeks
  • Ingestion of excessive 1,25(OH)₂D (calcitriol) or 1-alpha-hydroxylated vitamin D analogues — direct receptor activation
  • Reduced vitamin D catabolism — CYP24A1 (24-hydroxylase) deficiency; the inactivating enzyme is absent → 1,25(OH)₂D accumulates

D. Primary Increase in Bone Resorption (Suppressed PTH)

  • Hyperthyroidism — thyrotoxicosis directly increases bone resorption
  • Paget's disease of bone
  • Immobilization — reduced mechanical loading → ↑ osteocyte RANKL → ↑ osteoclast bone resorption; in older patients, consider occult pHPT, Paget's, or myeloma as a contributor
  • Ketogenic diet — in children with refractory epilepsy (uncommon); increases bone resorption

E. Excessive Calcium Intake

  • Milk-alkali syndrome — excessive oral calcium ingestion
  • Total parenteral nutrition — excessive calcium supplementation

F. Other Causes

  • Endocrine disorders: adrenal insufficiency, pheochromocytoma, acromegaly, VIPoma
  • Medications: thiazides, vitamin A, lithium, foscarnet, teriparatide, aromatase inhibitors
  • Denosumab discontinuation with rebound bone resorption
  • Recovery phase of rhabdomyolysis with renal failure
  • Williams-Beuren syndrome
  • Immune checkpoint inhibitors — rarely associated with hypercalcemia OR hypocalcemia depending on whether the immunoglobulin inhibits or activates the CaSR, respectively
  • Excessive mammary PTHrP production with pregnancy or lactation (rare)
  • Pseudohypercalcemia — calcium-binding IgM in Waldenström's macroglobulinemia

Hypercalcemia — Clinical Manifestations

The severity of symptoms correlates with both the degree and the rate of rise in serum calcium. Chronic, slowly rising hypercalcemia is often better tolerated than the same level achieved acutely.

Severity Spectrum

Mild — 10.5–11.9 mg/dL
  • Often asymptomatic
  • Vague neuropsychiatric symptoms — trouble concentrating, personality changes, depression
  • Fatigue
  • Proximal muscle weakness
  • Constipation
  • Hyporeflexia
  • Nephrolithiasis
  • Reduced bone mineral density
Moderate — ≥12–14 mg/dL
  • Progressive lethargy
  • GI symptoms — nausea, vomiting, anorexia, peptic ulcer, pancreatitis
  • Acute renal failure
  • Polyuria — hypercalcemia ↓ renal concentrating ability by downregulating collecting duct aquaporin 2
  • Volume depletion with frequent prerenal failure
Severe — ≥14 mg/dL
  • Stupor → coma
  • Severe GI symptoms
  • Acute renal failure
  • With long-standing disease: bone pain, pathologic fractures, hypertension

Cardiovascular & ECG Effects

⚠️ Danger — ECG Changes in Hypercalcemia
  • Bradycardia
  • Atrioventricular (AV) block
  • QRS widening
  • Short QTc interval ← Classic boards finding (HIGH Ca = SHORT QTc)
Memory trick: HyperCalcemia = HyperConcentrated QT = Short QTc

Hypercalcemia — Diagnostic Approach

Step 1 — Confirm True Hypercalcemia

Exclude pseudohypercalcemia (e.g., calcium-binding IgM in Waldenström's macroglobulinemia). Measure total serum calcium + albumin. Correct using the albumin correction formula. Direct ionized calcium measurements are available but easily influenced by collection methods (e.g., prolonged tourniquet application alters calcium binding to albumin) — corrected total calcium is generally preferable.

  • More acute course → most malignancy-associated hypercalcemia

History Clues to Etiology

  • Medications: thiazides, aromatase inhibitors, lithium, vitamin A, foscarnet, teriparatide
  • Over-the-counter supplements or special diets (excessive calcium, vitamin D)
  • Personal/family history of nephrolithiasis or endocrine/metabolic disorders
  • Previous neck surgery
  • Prior/current malignancy diagnosis
  • Systemic symptoms suggesting sarcoidosis, lymphoma, or hyperthyroidism

Hypercalcemia — Treatment

Mild, Asymptomatic Hypercalcemia

Does NOT require immediate therapy. Maintain adequate oral hydration. Close diagnostic follow-up. Management dictated by underlying cause.

Significant / Symptomatic Hypercalcemia — Acute Management

Requires acute intervention to restore normocalcemia regardless of etiology, in addition to treating the underlying cause. The sequence matters — follow this order:

Treatment Sequence — Significant Hypercalcemia
Step 1: IV Volume Expansion (FIRST)
↓
4–6 L IV saline over first 24h → ↑ renal perfusion → ↑ natriuresis → ↑ calciuresis
Step 2: IV Aminobisphosphonates
↓
Onset 1–2 days; normalize Ca in 60–90% of patients
Step 3: Calcitonin (rapid but transient)
↓
4–8 IU/kg IM/SC q6h for first 48h — fastest reduction but effect wanes
Step 4 (if refractory/renal disease): Denosumab

Vitamin D– or 1,25(OH)₂D–Mediated Hypercalcemia

💊 Glucocorticoids = Foundational Therapy Here
  • Mechanism: Directly reduce Ca absorption from small intestine AND decrease 1,25(OH)₂D production
  • IV hydrocortisone: 200–400 mg/day for 3–5 days
  • Oral prednisone: 40–60 mg/day for 7–10 days, with prolonged taper guided by serum calcium
  • For severe intoxication requiring hospitalization: IV hydration + aminobisphosphonates still often needed
  • Ketoconazole, chloroquine, hydroxychloroquine — decrease 1,25(OH)₂D production; used infrequently due to side effects
  • CYP24A1 deficiency specifically: Ketoconazole or fluconazole to inhibit 1,25(OH)₂D synthesis — helps avoid toxicity of chronic glucocorticoid use
  • Thyroid storm hypercalcemia: Aggressive management of thyrotoxicosis (IV glucocorticoids + thionamides) restores normocalcemia

Hypocalcemia — Etiology

The Organizing Framework — PTH Level

The most important organizational principle: Is PTH low (the problem is in the parathyroid gland) or high (the parathyroid is responding appropriately but something downstream is preventing calcium from rising)?

🔑 The Two Most Common Causes Overall
Impaired PTH production (hypoparathyroidism) — especially post-surgical | Severely impaired intestinal calcium absorption with or without profound vitamin D deficiency

Category A — Low PTH (Hypoparathyroidism)

PTH is the main minute-by-minute defense against hypocalcemia — deficient PTH can cause profound, debilitating, and life-threatening hypocalcemia.

1. Parathyroid Agenesis

  • Isolated (genetic: autosomal recessive GCM2 loss of function; X-linked mutations)
  • Syndromic: DiGeorge's syndrome, HDR (hypoparathyroidism, sensorineural deafness, renal disease), Kearns-Sayre syndrome

2. Parathyroid Destruction

  • Surgical — most common cause in adults; inadvertent damage to all four parathyroid glands during thyroid or parathyroid surgery
  • Autoimmune — accounts for ~25% of hypoparathyroidism; often part of APS-1 (anti-interferon and anti-NALP5 antibodies frequently present); includes immune checkpoint inhibitor–induced
  • Radiation
  • Iron or copper overload — hemochromatosis, transfusion-dependent thalassemia, Wilson's disease
  • Infiltration — metastases, sarcoidosis, IgG4-related disease, Riedel's struma
  • During COVID-19 pandemic: transient hypoparathyroidism and even spontaneous resolution of pHPT reported (mechanism unknown)

3. Reduced Bioactive PTH Secretion

  • Hypomagnesemia — impairs PTH secretion via intracellular actions; can arise from drug-induced renal loss (chronic alcohol, cisplatin, anti-EGF antibodies), proton pump inhibitors, or rare autoimmunity (claudin-16 tubulointerstitial nephritis)
  • Severe hypermagnesemia — e.g., obstetric magnesium infusions for preeclampsia/preterm labor; extracellular Mg binds CaSR with ~1/3 the affinity of calcium → suppresses PTH release
  • Autosomal dominant hypocalcemia (ADH) — CaSR activating mutations (CaSR is always "on" → always suppresses PTH) or GNA11 protein mutations
  • Autoimmune CaSR activating antibodies (including from immune checkpoint inhibitors)
  • Familial isolated hypoparathyroidism — rare PTH gene mutations

Category B — High PTH (Secondary Hyperparathyroidism)

The parathyroids are working correctly and trying to compensate — the problem lies downstream. Note: degree of hypocalcemia in these disorders is generally less severe than hypoparathyroidism because the parathyroids can mount a compensatory response (at the cost of bones).

1. Vitamin D Deficiency / Impaired 1,25(OH)₂D Production or Action

  • Nutritional vitamin D deficiency — poor intake or absorption
  • Renal insufficiency — phosphate retention + impaired 1,25(OH)₂D production by CYP27B1
  • Excessive IV phosphate infusion
  • Vitamin D resistance — including vitamin D receptor defects

2. Intrinsic Small Intestine Disorders (Chronic Calcium Malabsorption)

  • Roux-en-Y bariatric surgery / short bowel syndrome
  • Celiac disease
  • Crohn's disease (often with concomitant vitamin D deficiency)
  • ⚠️ These patients are at great risk for hypocalcemia with even modest hypoparathyroidism following neck surgery and can be very difficult to manage

3. PTH Resistance

  • Pseudohypoparathyroidism — GNAS mutations or imprinting defects; PTH is present but cells cannot respond
  • Congenital PTH receptor mutation syndromes
  • PTH receptor blocking autoantibodies

4. Drugs Causing Hypocalcemia

  • Alcohol — both acute and chronic ingestion
  • Calcium chelators — citrate from massive blood transfusion or FFP
  • Bone resorption inhibitors — bisphosphonates, denosumab, plicamycin
  • Increased vitamin D catabolism — phenytoin, phenobarbital
  • Phosphate-containing enemas — excessive use or with renal disease

5. Miscellaneous

  • Acute pancreatitis
  • Acute rhabdomyolysis
  • Hungry bone syndrome after parathyroidectomy — rapid uptake of Ca into demineralized bone
  • Osteoblastic metastases with marked stimulation of bone formation (prostate cancer)
  • Respiratory or metabolic alkalosis — increases Ca binding to albumin → ↓ ionized Ca; when tetany arises with alkalosis, another underlying Ca homeostasis problem is frequently present
  • Refeeding syndrome and its management
  • Pseudohypocalcemia — low serum albumin (malnutrition, nephrotic syndrome, cirrhosis); gadoversetamide MRI contrast
  • Conditions with severe tissue injury: burns, rhabdomyolysis, tumor lysis, pancreatitis — combination of hyperphosphatemia, tissue Ca deposition, and impaired PTH secretion

Hypocalcemia — Clinical Manifestations

Patients may be asymptomatic if hypocalcemia is mild and chronic. Moderate to severe hypocalcemia causes a spectrum of neuromuscular and cardiovascular findings driven by increased neuromuscular irritability.

Neuromuscular Irritability — The Core Manifestation

Chvostek's Sign

Twitching of the ipsilateral perioral levator and zygomaticus muscles in response to gentle tapping of the facial nerve (just anterior to the ear, or between corner of mouth and zygomatic arch).

⚠️ Present in ~10% of normal individuals — less specific than Trousseau's

Trousseau's Sign (More Definitive)

Carpal spasm induced by inflating a blood pressure cuff on the upper arm to 20 mmHg above systolic blood pressure for 3 minutes → ipsilateral thumb apposition with wrist flexion (carpopedal spasm).

✓ More specific sign of neuromuscular irritability and impending tetany

Additional Manifestations

  • Paresthesias — usually fingers, toes, and circumoral regions (increased neuromuscular irritability)
  • Proximal myopathy — with profound vitamin D insufficiency or hypomagnesemia; difficulty rising from a chair with arms crossed over chest

Life-Threatening Manifestations

🚨 Life-Threatening — Severe Hypocalcemia
  • Seizures
  • Carpopedal spasm
  • Bronchospasm
  • Laryngospasm
  • Prolonged QTc interval → risk of ventricular tachyarrhythmia (Torsades de Pointes) → sudden death
Memory: HypoCalcemia = Hypo-TENSE/short QT → LONG QTc (opposite of hypercalcemia)

Hypocalcemia — Treatment

Treatment approach depends on: (1) severity of hypocalcemia, (2) rapidity of onset, and (3) accompanying complications (tetany, seizures, QTc prolongation).

Acute, Symptomatic Hypocalcemia

🚨 Acute Management Protocol
  • Calcium gluconate — 10 mL ampule of 10% wt/vol = 90 mg (2.2 mmol) elemental calcium; dilute in 50 mL of 5% dextrose or 0.9% NaCl; give IV over 5–10 min with telemetry
  • After 1–2 additional ampules given at 10–60 min intervals as needed to initially resolve symptoms, while transitioning to oral medication
  • Often requires period of continuous IV calcium infusion — start at 1 mg/min elemental calcium for adults; titrate to symptoms, ECG, and blood calcium
  • Target: lower limit of normal serum calcium
  • Telemetry + serial exams + blood Ca every 4–6 hours
  • Treat accompanying hypomagnesemia with magnesium supplementation — hypocalcemia will be refractory until Mg is corrected
  • Initiate chronic-phase treatment once: tetany resolved + patient stable + able to take oral medication + QTc normalized

Chronic Hypocalcemia — Hypoparathyroidism

  • Oral calcium supplements: 1000–3000 mg/day elemental calcium in divided doses
  • Calcitriol [1,25(OH)₂D]: 0.25–1 μg/day — careful titration to albumin-corrected low-normal serum Ca levels
⚠️ Why Not Normalize Calcium to Normal-High?
Ca reabsorption in the distal convoluted tubule (~10% of filtered load) is PTH-dependent. Without PTH replacement, normalizing calcium above low-normal → hypercalciuria → risk of nephrolithiasis and nephrocalcinosis. Urine 24h calcium targets: <250 mg/day (women), <300 mg/day (men).
  • Vitamin D₃ nutrition: 1000–2000 IU daily for most adults; monitor serum 25(OH)D every 6–12 months

PTH Replacement Strategies

AgentStatus / DoseNotes
PTH (1-84) / NatparaFDA-approved but discontinued by manufacturerNo longer available
Teriparatide PTH (1-34)Off-label: 20 μg SC twice dailyFDA-approved for osteoporosis; successfully used off-label for refractory hypoparathyroidism; decrease dose in CKD to avoid hypercalcemia

Vitamin D Deficiency Hypocalcemia

  • Nutritional vitamin D deficiency: Moderate oral doses — e.g., ergocalciferol 50,000 IU, 2–3 times per week for several months
  • Severe enteric malabsorption (e.g., Roux-en-Y bariatric surgery): May require much higher doses — ergocalciferol carefully titrated up to 100,000 IU/day
  • Calcium form in intestinal disease: Use calcium citrate (preferred) — better absorbed; also increases urinary citrate to reduce nephrolithiasis risk from increased dietary oxalate absorption
  • Monitoring: Serum Ca, phosphate, PTH every 4 weeks initially when treating severe vitamin D deficiency or GI malabsorption — goal is normalizing serum biochemistries and 24h urine Ca to low normal

Exam Traps

Trap 01The Loop Diuretic Sequence
A patient presents with severe hypercalcemia (Ca 14.2 mg/dL). The question asks about the FIRST step in management. The choices include "give furosemide to increase calcium excretion" vs. "give IV normal saline." Which do you choose and why?
CORRECT: Start with IV normal saline (4–6 L over first 24h). Loop diuretics should NOT be initiated until volume status has been restored to normal. Hypercalcemia invariably leads to dehydration — giving furosemide first worsens volume depletion, reduces GFR, and paradoxically impairs calciuresis. Saline first restores renal perfusion and enables natriuresis → calciuresis.
Trap 02FHH vs pHPT — Don't Send Them to Surgery
A patient has mild asymptomatic hypercalcemia with slightly elevated PTH and strong family history of "failed parathyroid surgeries." The Ca/creatinine clearance ratio is 0.007. What is the diagnosis and what do you NOT do?
CORRECT: Familial Hypocalciuric Hypercalcemia (FHH). Do NOT refer for parathyroid surgery. Ca/Cr clearance ratio <0.01 is suggestive of FHH. FHH is due to inactivating CaSR mutations — the defect is in the calcium sensor in both the parathyroids AND kidneys. Surgery cannot fix a receptor mutation and will NOT resolve hypercalcemia. Confirm with CASR/GNA11/AP2S1 gene sequencing.
Trap 03ECG in Calcium Disorders — Short vs Long QTc
An ECG shows a short QTc interval. Is this hypercalcemia or hypocalcemia? A second patient has prolonged QTc with carpopedal spasm — which disorder?
Short QTc = HYPERcalcemia. The high extracellular calcium shortens phase 2 of the action potential. Prolonged QTc = HYPOcalcemia — the low calcium prolongs repolarization → risk of Torsades de Pointes and ventricular tachyarrhythmia → sudden death. Memory: HYPERcalcemia concentrates/shortens; HYPOcalcemia elongates/stretches (like low-Ca muscles — tetany).
Trap 04Bisphosphonates & Renal Function
A patient with metastatic cancer has severe hypercalcemia and a GFR of 22 mL/min/1.73m². You want to use a bisphosphonate. What is the issue and what is the alternative?
CORRECT: IV aminobisphosphonates (zoledronic acid, pamidronate) are CONTRAINDICATED when GFR <35 mL/min/1.73m² due to nephrotoxicity risk. The alternative is denosumab, which is cleared by the reticuloendothelial system (not renally cleared) — use 0.3 mg/kg for advanced kidney disease.
Trap 05PTHrP — Not the Same as PTH
A patient has hypercalcemia with SUPPRESSED PTH and a solid malignancy. Why is the PTH suppressed if the cancer is causing hypercalcemia? What does the tumor secrete?
CORRECT: The tumor secretes PTHrP (PTH-related peptide), NOT PTH itself. PTHrP shares homology only with the first 13 amino acids of PTH and binds the PTH receptor (mimicking PTH's effects on bone and kidney). However, the parathyroid glands are normal and appropriately detect the high serum calcium via CaSR → correctly suppress native PTH secretion. Standard PTH assay measures native PTH only, so it reads low/suppressed.
Trap 06Granulomatous Disease — Not the Normal Vitamin D Pathway
Why does sarcoidosis cause hypercalcemia even when the patient avoids sunlight and dietary vitamin D? What lab pattern would you expect?
CORRECT: Activated macrophages in granulomas constitutively express CYP27B1 (1-alpha-hydroxylase), converting 25(OH)D → 1,25(OH)₂D independently of normal negative feedback loops (unlike renal CYP27B1 which is regulated by PTH and calcium). Expect: ↑ or inappropriately-normal 1,25(OH)₂D, suppressed PTH, elevated or high-normal serum phosphate (increased GI absorption + reduced renal excretion). Treatment: glucocorticoids (inhibit macrophage CYP27B1 activity).
Trap 07The Trousseau's Sign Threshold
You need to perform Trousseau's sign. The patient's systolic BP is 130 mmHg. To what pressure do you inflate the cuff, and for how long?
CORRECT: Inflate to 20 mmHg ABOVE systolic (= 150 mmHg in this patient) for 3 minutes. A positive result is carpopedal spasm — ipsilateral thumb apposition with wrist flexion. This is more definitive for neuromuscular irritability and impending tetany than Chvostek's sign (which is present in ~10% of normal individuals).
Trap 08Hypomagnesemia — Refractory Hypocalcemia
A patient with hypocalcemia is treated with IV calcium gluconate but symptoms keep returning. A magnesium level is not yet checked. What is the likely explanation and critical step?
CORRECT: Check and treat hypomagnesemia. Hypomagnesemia impairs PTH secretion via intracellular actions. If magnesium is depleted, you cannot restore PTH function no matter how much calcium you give — the calcium will keep falling. This is a classic boards scenario especially in alcoholics, patients on cisplatin, PPIs, or anti-EGF antibodies. Always treat hypomagnesemia concomitantly with hypocalcemia.
Trap 09Vitamin D Intoxication Duration — Weeks, Not Days
A patient accidentally ingested massive doses of over-the-counter vitamin D₃ (cholecalciferol) 2 weeks ago. You treat the acute hypercalcemia and discharge the patient. The patient re-presents with hypercalcemia 3 weeks later. Why?
CORRECT: Vitamin D and 25(OH)D have long half-lives (unlike 1,25(OH)₂D which is metabolized within a day or two). Excessive vitamin D ingestion displaces 25(OH)D from vitamin D binding protein (DBP), exposing tissues to active concentrations. Because of the prolonged half-life, hypercalcemia from vitamin D intoxication can persist for several weeks. Patients require sustained treatment (hydration, glucocorticoids, possibly bisphosphonates) and extended monitoring.
Trap 10Calcium Citrate vs Calcium Carbonate in GI Disease
A patient with Crohn's disease and chronic calcium malabsorption needs supplemental calcium. Which form of calcium should you prescribe and why?
CORRECT: Calcium citrate (not calcium carbonate). In intestinal disease, calcium citrate is preferred because: (1) it is better absorbed (does not require acid for dissolution, unlike carbonate), and (2) this formulation increases urinary citrate, which mitigates the risk of nephrolithiasis in this setting — these patients have increased dietary oxalate absorption from their bowel disease, which raises oxalate levels in urine and stone risk.
Trap 11Why Target Low-Normal Calcium in Hypoparathyroidism?
You're managing a patient with post-surgical hypoparathyroidism on oral calcium + calcitriol. The patient's Ca is 8.6 mg/dL. A colleague suggests increasing the dose to achieve a mid-normal Ca of 9.5 mg/dL. Is this appropriate?
CORRECT: NO — target LOW-NORMAL serum calcium, not mid-normal. In the absence of PTH, the distal convoluted tubule cannot reabsorb calcium normally (~10% of filtered load is PTH-dependent). Normalizing calcium above low-normal → excessive filtered calcium load → hypercalciuria → nephrolithiasis and nephrocalcinosis. Urine 24h Ca targets: <250 mg/day (women), <300 mg/day (men).
Trap 12Multiple Myeloma — Don't Miss 3% of Cases
A patient with suppressed PTH and hypercalcemia has negative SPEP and UPEP with immunofixation. Can you exclude multiple myeloma?
CORRECT: No. ~3% of myeloma may be UNDETECTED by serum or urine protein electrophoresis with immunofixation. You must also measure serum free light chains to exclude myeloma in this setting. This is a classic boards pitfall — always include free light chains in the workup of hypercalcemia with suppressed PTH.
