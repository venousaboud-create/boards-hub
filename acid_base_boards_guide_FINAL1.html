Section 1

Normal Acid-Base Homeostasis & Diagnostic Approach

Systemic arterial pH is maintained between 7.35 and 7.45 by extracellular and intracellular buffering together with respiratory and renal regulatory mechanisms. Everything depends on a single ratio.

pH = pK′ + log10 [HCO₃⁻] / (αCO₂ × PaCO₂)  |  pK′ = 6.1  |  αCO₂ = 0.03
🔑
pH depends on the RATIO of HCO₃⁻ to PaCO₂, not either value alone. Normal values can coexist with serious mixed disorders — always check the anion gap.

🫁 Respiratory Arm

Controls: PaCO₂
Speed: Minutes
Regulated by: CNS + respiratory system
Normal: PaCO₂ ~40 mmHg
Hypercapnia = hypoventilation (not overproduction)
PaCO₂ regulated by neural factors, not CO₂ production rate

🫘 Metabolic/Renal Arm

Controls: HCO₃⁻
Speed: Hours to days
Regulated by: Kidneys
Normal: HCO₃⁻ 24–25 mmol/L
Renal compensation takes 24–72h for full effect
Kidneys respond to ↓ PaCO₂, not alkalosis per se

▶Anion Gap — Calculation, Correction & Delta-DeltaDeep Dive
AG = Na⁺ − (Cl⁻ + HCO₃⁻)  |  Normal: 6–12 mmol/L, average ~10

The AG represents unmeasured anions normally present: anionic albumin, phosphate, sulfate, organic anions. K⁺ is omitted from the standard US calculation.

AG Increases when…

  • Non-chloride acids accumulate (lactate, ketoacids, uremic acids)
  • Inorganic: phosphate, sulfate (renal failure)
  • Organic: ketoacids, lactate
  • Exogenous: salicylate, ingested toxins
  • ↑ Anionic albumin (severe dehydration)
  • An elevated AG is meaningful even if HCO₃⁻ or pH is normal

AG Decreases when…

  • ↑ Unmeasured cations (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, K⁺)
  • Abnormal cations: lithium intoxication
  • Cationic immunoglobulins (plasma cell dyscrasias)
  • ↓ Albumin (nephrotic syndrome, liver disease, malabsorption)
  • Hyperviscosity / severe hyperlipidemia → underestimates Na⁺ & Cl⁻
⚠️
Albumin Correction — CRITICAL: For every 1 g/dL of albumin BELOW 4.5 g/dL, ADD 2.5 mmol/L to the reported AG. Example: albumin 2.5 g/dL (2 below normal), uncorrected AG 15 → corrected AG = 15 + (2×2.5) = 20 — this is a HIGH-AG acidosis. Labs do NOT auto-correct.

💡
Classic trap: Alcoholic + vomiting → metabolic alkalosis (HCO₃⁻ 40). Then develops ketoacidosis. Result: pH 7.40, HCO₃⁻ 25, PaCO₂ 40 — ALL NORMAL. But AG = 30! ΔAG = 20, ΔHCO₃⁻ = 0 → 20 mmol/L unaccounted → mixed high-AG acidosis + metabolic alkalosis.

Section 2

Metabolic Acidosis

Metabolic acidosis arises by three mechanisms, produces profound multi-organ effects, and splits into two categories based on the anion gap.

① Endogenous Acid ↑

Lactate or ketoacids accumulate faster than excreted (e.g., shock, DKA)

② Loss of HCO₃⁻

Bicarb physically leaves the body (e.g., diarrhea — stool HCO₃⁻ > plasma)

③ Failure to Excrete Acid

Kidneys can't handle daily acid load (e.g., CKD — reduced NH₄⁺ excretion)

💥
Systemic effects: ↓pH → medullary chemoreceptor stimulation → Kussmaul breathing | Cardiac contractility depressed but catecholamines compensate | Peripheral vasodilation + central venoconstriction → pulmonary edema risk with minimal volume overload | CNS: headache → lethargy → stupor → coma | Glucose intolerance
HIGH Anion Gap
  • AG >10 mEq/L
  • Cl⁻ = normal
  • New unmeasured anion appeared
  • HCO₃⁻ replaced by organic/inorganic anion
vs
NON Anion Gap (Hyperchloremic)
  • AG = normal (6–12)
  • Cl⁻ = HIGH (↑1:1 with ↓HCO₃⁻)
  • HCO₃⁻ replaced by Cl⁻
  • Increase in Cl⁻ ≈ decrease in HCO₃⁻

▶Special Scenarios
  • Bowel ischemia/infarction in elderly + severe atherosclerosis or cardiac decompensation on vasopressors — common, often unrecognized
  • Pyroglutamic acidemia (5-oxoproline): acetaminophen depletes glutathione → 5-oxoproline accumulates → high-AG acidosis. No osmolar gap. Critically ill + acetaminophen
  • D-Lactic acidosis: jejunoileal bypass, short bowel, intestinal obstruction — gut bacteria produce D-lactate. Standard assays (L-lactate only) miss it
▶Treatment of L-Lactic Acidosis
  1. Correct the underlying condition — restore tissue perfusion
  2. Avoid vasoconstrictors if possible — worsen tissue perfusion
  3. Alkali (NaHCO₃) for pH <7.00 to improve cardiac function
    Paradox NaHCO₃ stimulates phosphofructokinase → ↑ lactate production. Never normalize pH with NaHCO₃.
  4. Target: raise pH to no more than 7.20, HCO₃⁻ to no more than 12 mmol/L
⚠️
NaHCO₃ complications: fluid overload, hypercapnia, hypertension. Poorly tolerated especially in the oliguric patient when central venoconstriction coexists. If underlying cause corrected → lactate → HCO₃⁻ → overshoot alkalosis if NaHCO₃ given excessively.
▶Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)Ketoacidosis
↑ Fatty acid metabolismAcetoacetate + β-OHBHigh-AG acidosis

Triggers: Insulin cessation in T1DM, or intercurrent illness (infection, gastroenteritis, pancreatitis, MI) → ↑ insulin requirements → relative insulin deficiency

▶Euglycemic DKA (eDKA) — CRITICAL MIMIC
⚠️
SGLT2 inhibitors (empagliflozin, dapagliflozin) cause glycosuria → osmotic diuresis → volume depletion → ↓ plasma glucose. DKA is present (ketonemia + high-AG acidosis) BUT glucose is normal. Also occurs in starvation ketoacidosis. Never dismiss DKA just because glucose is normal if the patient is on an SGLT2i.
▶Key Diagnostic Points
  • Dipstick underestimates ketosis — nitroprusside detects acetoacetate but NOT β-hydroxybutyrate. Measure plasma β-OHB directly.
  • Ketoacid excretion obligates Na⁺/K⁺ loss → volume depletion + Cl⁻ retention
  • Mixed non-AG + high-AG acidosis can occur; recognized when ΔHCO₃⁻ > ΔAG
▶DKA Treatment
  1. Isotonic saline for volume resuscitation — avoid overexpansion → hyperchloremic acidosis
  2. Regular insulin: IV bolus 0.1 U/kg → infusion 0.1 U/kg/h until AG returns to normal
  3. Bicarbonate: only for pH <7.00 or shock. Target HCO₃⁻ 10–12, pH ~7.20 only. Slow infusion 50 mEq NaHCO₃ in 300 mL saline over 30–45 min. Risk: cerebral edema. Monitor K⁺ carefully.
  4. Monitor electrolytes — K⁺ drops as urine output resumes
▶Alcoholic Ketoacidosis (AKA)Mixed Disorders Common

Context: Chronic alcoholism + binge + vomiting + abdominal pain + poor nutrition + volume depletion. Glucose is variable. β-hydroxybutyrate predominates.

▶Drug- & Toxin-Induced AcidosisOsmolar Gap Key
Osmolality = 2[Na⁺] + BUN/2.8 + Glucose/18  |  Osmolar Gap = Measured − Calculated  |  Normal: <10–15
⏱️
Temporal relationship: Osmolar gap elevated EARLIER than AG. As osmolar gap declines, AG rises. DO NOT wait for alcohol levels — treat on clinical suspicion when both gaps are present.
▶Salicylates — Dual Pattern
🔑
Salicylates cause respiratory alkalosis (direct medullary chemoreceptor stimulation) AND high-AG metabolic acidosis (salicylate + ↑ lactic acid from uncoupled OxPhos). Most common drug-induced respiratory alkalosis.

Treatment:

  1. Vigorous gastric lavage with isotonic saline (NOT NaHCO₃)
  2. Activated charcoal: 1 g/kg up to 50g via NG tube
  3. IV NaHCO₃ → alkalinize urine to pH >7.5 → maintain urine output. pH 6.5→7.5 = fivefold ↑ in salicylate clearance (ion trapping)
  4. Anticipate hypokalemia with bicarb therapy — treat aggressively
  5. Watch for hypernatremia from insensible fluid losses
  6. Acetazolamide when alkaline diuresis can't be achieved, with coexisting alkalemia, or to reduce volume overload from NaHCO₃. Caution: if HCO₃⁻ not replaced → systemic acidosis → acidifies urine → markedly reduces salicylate clearance
  7. Hemodialysis if AKI prevents clearance — use [HCO₃⁻] = 30–35 mEq/L dialysate

▶Pyroglutamic Acid (5-Oxoproline)

Acetaminophen depletes glutathione → 5-oxoproline accumulates → high-AG acidosis. Occurs with overdose OR standard doses in malnourished/critically ill. No osmolar gap — differentiates from toxic alcohols.

Treatment: Immediately discontinue acetaminophen + IV NaHCO₃. N-acetylcysteine suggested but not unequivocally proven to hasten 5-oxoproline metabolism.

🦴
Bone buffering trade-off: Despite retaining up to 20 mmol/d of acid, serum HCO₃⁻ doesn't keep falling — alkaline salts from bone buffer the retained acid. Trade-off: significant loss of bone mass (↓ bone calcium carbonate). Chronic acidosis → muscle catabolism + bone disease + accelerates CKD progression itself.

Treatment: Target HCO₃⁻ >22–24 mmol/L. NaHCO₃ tablets (650 mg = 7.8 mEq) or sodium citrate (Shohl's solution). Dose: 1.0–1.5 mmol/kg/day. Fruits and vegetables (citrate) safely increase HCO₃⁻ and slow CKD progression.

Non–Anion Gap (Hyperchloremic) Acidoses

🔑
In non-AG acidosis, HCO₃⁻ is lost and Cl⁻ rises 1:1. If the increase in Cl⁻ does NOT approximate the decrease in HCO₃⁻ — suspect a mixed disturbance.
▶Gastrointestinal Bicarbonate LossExtrarenal

Stool contains a higher concentration of HCO₃⁻ than plasma → diarrhea → HCO₃⁻ leaves body → non-AG acidosis.

😮
Confusing finding in diarrhea: Urine pH is usually >6 (ALKALINE), not acid. WHY: metabolic acidosis + hypokalemia → ↑ renal NH₄⁺ synthesis/excretion → NH₄⁺ buffers urine → raises urine pH. Can be mistaken for RTA — use the UAG to distinguish.
UAG = [Na⁺]ᵤ + [K⁺]ᵤ − [Cl⁻]ᵤ
UAG ResultNH₄⁺ ExcretionInterpretation
NEGATIVE (Cl⁻ > Na⁺+K⁺)HIGH ✓Kidneys responding normally → Extrarenal cause (diarrhea)
POSITIVE (Na⁺+K⁺ > Cl⁻)LOW ✗Kidneys NOT excreting acid → Renal tubular origin (RTA)
🔬
Recent studies show poor correlation between UAG and measured urine ammonium. Clinical labs should measure urine NH₄⁺ directly using the enzymatic method. Dilute urine 1:200 in normal saline before assay.

Treatment of GI HCO₃⁻ loss: NaHCO₃ IV or orally, guided by severity of acidosis AND accompanying volume depletion.

Other causes (Table 58-5 I): External pancreatic/small-bowel drainage fistula | Ureterosigmoidostomy, jejunal loop, ileal loop | Drugs: calcium chloride, magnesium sulfate (diarrhea), cholestyramine

▶Type 2 RTA — Proximal (Most Challenging)
  • Defect: Impaired HCO₃⁻ reabsorption in proximal tubule → HCO₃⁻ spills until serum level falls to new threshold
  • Often part of Fanconi syndrome: glycosuria + aminoaciduria + phosphaturia
  • Low plasma HCO₃⁻ → urine pH acid (<5.5). With exogenous alkali → urine pH >5.5 (proximal tubule overwhelmed again)
  • FE HCO₃⁻ may exceed 10–15% when serum HCO₃⁻ >20 mmol/L
  • Causes: acetazolamide, topiramate | Inherited: SLC4A mutation (NBCe1) + ocular abnormalities; NHE3 mutation + short stature
  • Alkali paradox: NaHCO₃ → delivers HCO₃⁻ distally → enhances K⁺ secretion → hypokalemia. Must always add K⁺ supplementation.
  • Formulation: Citric acid 334 mg + sodium citrate 500 mg + potassium citrate 550 mg per 5 mL → Virtrate or Cytra-3
▶Type 1 RTA — Classic Distal
  • Four classic features: (1) Hypokalemia (2) Non-AG metabolic acidosis (3) Low urinary NH₄⁺ (positive UAG) (4) Inappropriately high urine pH (>5.5) ALWAYS — even with systemic acidosis
  • Complications: Hypocitraturia → hypercalciuria → nephrolithiasis, nephrocalcinosis, bone disease
  • Causes: Amphotericin B, ifosfamide | Inherited: ATP6V1B1 defect (basolateral HCO₃⁻/Cl⁻ exchanger)
  • Treatment: Correct hypokalemia first → then alkali (sodium citrate or NaHCO₃). Target HCO₃⁻ 24–26 mEq/L. Type 1 responds readily. Long-term: ↓ stones, ↑ bone density, normal growth in children
▶Type 4 RTA — Generalized Distal (Hyperkalemia)
⚠️
Vicious cycle: Hyperkalemia → ↓ ammoniagenesis → ↓ net acid excretion → worsens acidosis. Fix K⁺ → independently improves acidosis.

Typical patient: Older adult + diabetes mellitus OR tubulointerstitial disease + CKD stage 3–4. K⁺ 5.2–6.0, hypertension, CHF. Acidosis AND hyperkalemia disproportionate to GFR reduction. Kidney function may be compromised by diabetic nephropathy, obstructive uropathy, or tubulointerstitial disease.

Causes — Table 58-5: Selective aldosterone deficiency | Mineralocorticoid resistance (PHA I autosomal dominant) | Voltage defect (PHA I autosomal recessive, PHA II) | Hyporeninemic hypoaldosteronism | Tubulointerstitial disease

Drugs (Table 58-5 III — all require CKD): K⁺-sparing diuretics | Trimethoprim | Pentamidine | ACE-I/ARBs | NSAIDs | Calcineurin inhibitors | Heparin in critically ill patients

Treatment: (1) Correct acidosis (same approach as Type 1) (2) Correct hyperkalemia — Patiromer preferred: 8.4 g PO twice daily, adjust weekly, max 25.2 g/d (3) Sodium polystyrene sulfonate historical: 15 g without sorbitol PO 2–3×/week — unpalatable, poor compliance (4) Diet low in K⁺; avoid salt substitute (5) Discontinue all K⁺-retaining medications (6) Fludrocortisone for isolated hypoaldosteronism — cautiously with furosemide in edema/HTN

Section 3

Metabolic Alkalosis

Elevated pH + elevated HCO₃⁻ + elevated PaCO₂. The key framework is understanding generation vs. maintenance — because treatment must address the maintenance mechanism, not just the generation.

↑ pH (>7.45)

Distinguishes from respiratory acidosis (both have high PaCO₂ + high HCO₃⁻ — the pH is the tiebreaker)

↑ HCO₃⁻

Primary driver. Often accompanied by hypochloremia and hypokalemia

↑ PaCO₂

Compensatory alveolar hypoventilation. Comp: PaCO₂ ↑ 0.75 per 1↑ HCO₃⁻

Stage 1 — GENERATION
  • Net gain of HCO₃⁻ OR removal of H⁺ (usually HCl)
  • Vomiting: stomach loses HCl → HCO₃⁻ retained in ECF
  • Each H⁺ secreted into stomach → one HCO₃⁻ enters ECF
  • Alkalosis is generated
→
Stage 2 — MAINTENANCE
  • Normal kidneys would excrete excess HCO₃⁻ — so why does it persist?
  • Path A: Volume + Cl⁻ + K⁺ depletion → urine Cl⁻ <20 → saline-responsive
  • Path B: Autonomous aldosterone → urine Cl⁻ >20 → saline-unresponsive
🔍
Differential Diagnosis Approach: Measure (1) BP recumbent + upright (orthostasis) (2) Serum K⁺ (3) Urine [Cl⁻]. Then if mineralocorticoid excess suspected → measure renin + aldosterone. Urine Cl⁻ <20 = saline-responsive. Urine Cl⁻ >20 = saline-unresponsive → check BP → hypertensive vs normotensive.
▶Table 58-6 Causes — Saline-Responsive (ECFV Depletion)Urine Cl⁻ <20
▶GI Origin: Vomiting / NG Suction (Prototype)
  1. Active vomiting → filtered HCO₃⁻ exceeds proximal reabsorption capacity → spills distally → alkaline urine → K⁺ secretion → hypokalemia begins
  2. Vomiting stops → volume + K⁺ + Cl⁻ depletion promote HCO₃⁻ reabsorption → maintenance phase
  3. Volume depletion → ↑ angiotensin II + aldosterone → Na⁺ reabsorption coupled to H⁺ secretion → retains HCO₃⁻ indirectly

Other GI (Table 58-6): Gastric aspiration | Congenital chloridorrhea | Gastrocystoplasty | Villous adenoma

▶Diuretics — Contraction + Direct Generation
  • Acute (contraction alkalosis): Remove NaCl-rich fluid without altering total body HCO₃⁻ → same HCO₃⁻ in smaller volume → serum [HCO₃⁻] rises
  • Chronic: ↑ distal Na⁺ delivery → stimulates K⁺ and H⁺ secretion → generates new HCO₃⁻
  • Maintained by: ECFV contraction + secondary hyperaldosteronism + K⁺ deficiency + direct diuretic effect
  • Treatment: Stop diuretic + isotonic saline to correct ECFV deficit
▶Posthypercapnic State

Chronic CO₂ retention → kidneys generate new HCO₃⁻ (appropriate). PaCO₂ abruptly corrected (e.g., mechanical ventilation) → HCO₃⁻ reabsorption mechanisms still revved up → metabolic alkalosis from residual stimulation. Urine Cl⁻ low → saline-responsive.

▶Hypercalcemia / Hypoparathyroidism (Table 58-6 IIB.3)

Both promote enhanced HCO₃⁻ reabsorption in the proximal tubule. Hypercalcemia directly stimulates proximal tubule HCO₃⁻ reabsorption. Hypoparathyroidism removes a normal inhibitor of proximal HCO₃⁻ reabsorption. Volume depletion accompanying these conditions contributes to maintenance.

▶Rebound Alkalosis / K⁺ Depletion / Non-Reabsorbable Anions / Mg²⁺
  • Rebound (Table 58-6 IIB.4): Lactate/ketones metabolized to HCO₃⁻ + exogenous HCO₃⁻ given during treatment → surfeit → overshoot alkalosis
  • K⁺ depletion (IIB.7): ↑ urinary net acid excretion + upregulates ammoniagenesis + upregulates H⁺,K⁺-ATPases → H⁺ secreted → generates alkalosis. Resistant to salt administration — must fix K⁺.
  • Non-reabsorbable anions (IIB.5): IV carbenicillin/ticarcillin → appear in distal tubule → ↑ transepithelial potential → drives H⁺ + K⁺ secretion → alkalosis + hypokalemia
  • Mg²⁺ deficiency (IIB.6): Thiazides, alcoholism, malnutrition. In Gitelman's → stimulates renin + aldosterone → distal acidification
  • Bartter's (IIB.8): Loss-of-function mutations of transporters/ion channels in TALH (thick ascending limb of Henle's loop)
  • Gitelman's (IIB.9): Loss-of-function mutation of Na⁺-Cl⁻ cotransporter in DCT and collecting duct. Distinguished by hypocalciuria.

▶SymptomsSimilar to Hypocalcemia

Alkalosis → ↑ albumin-Ca²⁺ binding → ↓ ionized free Ca²⁺ → excitable tissue effects identical to hypocalcemia.

  • Mental confusion, obtundation
  • Paresthesias, circumoral numbness
  • Muscular cramping, tetany
  • Seizures — predisposition
  • Cardiac arrhythmias (aggravated by hypokalemia)
  • Hypoxemia in COPD (left shift O₂-Hb curve)
  • Related: hypokalemia (K⁺ shifts intracellularly) + hypophosphatemia

Section 4

Respiratory Acidosis

Occurs when lungs fail to excrete CO₂ adequately. Almost always due to hypoventilation, not overproduction. The kidneys compensate — but the chronic response is 4× stronger than the acute.

Acute (<24h)

Cellular buffering only
HCO₃⁻ ↑ 0.1 mmol/L per mmHg ↑ PaCO₂

Chronic (>24h)

Full renal adaptation
HCO₃⁻ ↑ 0.4 mmol/L per mmHg ↑ PaCO₂
Max HCO₃⁻ = 38 — if higher → coexisting metabolic alkalosis

▶TreatmentCritical Caveats
⚠️
O₂ in COPD: Titrate O₂ carefully in severe COPD with chronic CO₂ retention. High-flow O₂ → removes hypoxic drive → hypoventilation → PaCO₂ rises → severe acidemia.
⚠️
Rapid correction: Aggressive rapid correction of hypercapnia → same complications as acute respiratory alkalosis: cardiac arrhythmias, reduced cerebral perfusion, seizures. Lower PaCO₂ gradually to patient's baseline (not necessarily 40 mmHg).
💡
Chronic respiratory acidosis correction: Provide sufficient Cl⁻ and K⁺ to allow renal excretion of accumulated HCO₃⁻. Without these, posthypercapnic metabolic alkalosis develops.

Permissive hypercapnia: Target pH ~7.20 with NaHCO₃ if needed — but do NOT normalize pH.

Section 5

Respiratory Alkalosis

The only acid-base disorder where compensation can fully normalize pH. Most common disturbance in critically ill patients. When severe, portends poor prognosis.

Acute

HCO₃⁻ ↓ 0.2 mmol/L per mmHg ↓ PaCO₂
Renal HCO₃⁻ excretion NOT yet increased — but within hours, net acid excretion begins to decrease

Chronic (>3–5 days)

HCO₃⁻ ↓ 0.4–0.5 mmol/L per mmHg ↓ PaCO₂
1 mmHg ↓ PaCO₂ → 0.3 mmol/L ↓ [H⁺] (0.003 pH unit ↑)
pH can fully normalize — only disorder where this happens

🌟
HCO₃⁻ floor: Rarely falls below 12 mmol/L from pure respiratory alkalosis. If HCO₃⁻ <12 → suspect coexisting metabolic acidosis. Kidneys respond to the lowered PaCO₂ itself, not to alkalosis per se.

Section 7 ★

Exam Traps

High-yield traps based exclusively on the PDF material — written from the perspective of an Internal Medicine boards examiner.

★ 01Trap: Normal Labs Hiding a Mixed DisorderLOOK-ALIKE
A 42-year-old man with chronic alcoholism is admitted after a binge. He has been vomiting for 3 days. Labs: pH 7.40, PaCO₂ 40 mmHg, HCO₃⁻ 25 mmol/L, Na⁺ 135, Cl⁻ 95, AG 15. What is the correct interpretation?
⚠️ Tempting Wrong Answer
Normal acid-base status — pH, HCO₃⁻, and PaCO₂ are all within normal range
✅ Correct Answer
Mixed metabolic alkalosis (from vomiting) + metabolic acidosis (alcoholic ketoacidosis). ΔAG = 15−10 = 5, but ΔAG = 20 if corrected. The AG is elevated, revealing a hidden high-AG acidosis despite normal pH and HCO₃⁻.
🔬 Mechanism
Vomiting → metabolic alkalosis (↑ HCO₃⁻). AKA → high-AG acidosis (↓ HCO₃⁻). The two cancel each other → normal values. Only the elevated AG reveals the hidden acidosis. ΔAG >> ΔHCO₃⁻ confirms the mixture.
🔑 Board Keyword
ELEVATED AG WITH NORMAL pH = MIXED DISORDER
★ 02Trap: Missing High-AG in Hypoalbuminemic PatientNUMBER
A 58-year-old with end-stage liver disease has albumin 2.0 g/dL. ABG shows pH 7.30, HCO₃⁻ 16, PaCO₂ 32. Electrolytes: Na⁺ 138, Cl⁻ 107, HCO₃⁻ 15, AG = 16. The laboratory reports the AG as 16 (normal). What are you missing?
⚠️ Tempting Wrong Answer
Normal AG — this is a pure non-gap (hyperchloremic) metabolic acidosis, likely RTA
✅ Correct Answer
Corrected AG = 16 + (2.5 × 2.5) = 22.25 — this is a HIGH-AG metabolic acidosis. The hypoalbuminemia masked the elevated AG.
The normal AG assumes albumin of 4.5 g/dL. Albumin is 2.0 g/dL — 2.5 g/dL below normal. Add 2.5 × 2.5 = 6.25 to uncorrected AG. Clinical labs never auto-correct. In any sick patient with low albumin, always correct the AG manually.
CORRECT AG FOR ALBUMIN IN ALL SICK PATIENTS
★ 03Trap: Euglycemic DKA on SGLT2 InhibitorPOPULATION
A 55-year-old with T2DM on empagliflozin presents with nausea and malaise. pH 7.21, HCO₃⁻ 12, AG 24, glucose 142 mg/dL (mildly elevated). Urine ketones positive. What is the diagnosis?
⚠️ Tempting Wrong Answer
Cannot be DKA — glucose is only mildly elevated, not in the classic hyperglycemic range
✅ Correct Answer
Euglycemic DKA (eDKA) from SGLT2 inhibitor. Diagnosis confirmed by high-AG metabolic acidosis + ketonemia despite near-normal glucose.
SGLT2 inhibitors cause glycosuria → osmotic diuresis → volume depletion → decreased plasma glucose. Ketone accumulation continues independently. Result: classical DKA biochemistry (high-AG + ketones) without hyperglycemia. Measurement of plasma β-hydroxybutyrate confirms degree of ketosis (urine dipstick underestimates).
SGLT2i + KETONEMIA + HIGH-AG = eDKA (REGARDLESS OF GLUCOSE)
★ 04Trap: Isopropyl Alcohol — High Osmolar Gap WITHOUT High-AGLOOK-ALIKE
A confused 28-year-old is brought in after ingesting rubbing alcohol. Measured osmolality 340, calculated 310 (osmolar gap 30). AG is 12 (normal). Toxicology is pending. What toxic alcohol does this pattern indicate, and why is there no AG acidosis?
⚠️ Tempting Wrong Answer
Ethylene glycol or methanol — both cause elevated osmolar gap
✅ Correct Answer
Isopropyl alcohol (rubbing alcohol). High osmolar gap WITHOUT high-AG acidosis is the hallmark. Metabolized to acetone (not an acid), so no AG rise.
Isopropyl alcohol → alcohol dehydrogenase → acetone. Acetone is rapidly excreted and does not generate H⁺. Both isopropyl alcohol AND acetone raise the osmolar gap, but neither causes metabolic acidosis. EG and methanol cause high-AG acidosis through toxic metabolite accumulation (glycolate/oxalate and formic acid respectively).
OSMOLAR GAP WITHOUT AG ACIDOSIS = ISOPROPYL ALCOHOL
★ 05Trap: Liddle's vs Primary AldosteronismMECHANISM
A 28-year-old with hypertension, hypokalemia, and metabolic alkalosis has LOW plasma aldosterone and LOW plasma renin. What is the diagnosis, and what drug should NOT be used?
⚠️ Tempting Wrong Answer
Primary aldosteronism — hypertension + hypokalemia + metabolic alkalosis → give spironolactone
✅ Correct Answer
Liddle's syndrome — rare monogenic form of hypertension (ENaC gain-of-function). Treat with amiloride or triamterene, NOT spironolactone.
Liddle's = constitutively active ENaC → continuous Na⁺ reabsorption → volume expansion → hypertension → suppresses BOTH renin and aldosterone (hyporeninemic-hypoaldosteronism). Spironolactone blocks the aldosterone receptor — which is not the problem here. The problem is the channel itself, so only ENaC blockers work.
LOW ALDOSTERONE + LOW RENIN + HTN + HYPOKALEMIA = LIDDLE'S
★ 06Trap: AKA Nitroprusside ParadoxTIMING
A patient with alcoholic ketoacidosis is being treated with IV saline and dextrose. The nurse reports that urine ketone dipsticks are showing increasingly strong positivity since IV fluids started, suggesting worsening ketosis. What is the correct interpretation?
⚠️ Tempting Wrong Answer
Worsening ketoacidosis — increase insulin dose and aggressive NaHCO₃ therapy
✅ Correct Answer
This is the nitroprusside paradox — a normal finding indicating improving ketoacidosis, not worsening. IV fluids restore circulation → β-hydroxybutyrate shifts to acetoacetate → dipstick becomes more positive. Always measure plasma β-hydroxybutyrate directly.
AKA: β-hydroxybutyrate predominates (due to high NADH from ethanol). Urine dipstick (nitroprusside reaction) detects only acetoacetate. As circulation improves with IV fluids → equilibrium shifts toward acetoacetate → dipstick lights up. The apparent increase in ketones is a sign of metabolic improvement, not deterioration.
INCREASINGLY POSITIVE DIPSTICK WITH TREATMENT = IMPROVEMENT IN AKA
★ 07Trap: Sepsis Presenting as Respiratory AlkalosisTIMING
A 65-year-old presents with confusion and mild tachycardia. Temperature 37.8°C, BP 118/72. ABG: pH 7.52, PaCO₂ 28, HCO₃⁻ 22. Chest X-ray clear. What is the most important next step and likely diagnosis?
⚠️ Tempting Wrong Answer
Hyperventilation syndrome or anxiety — reassure and observe
✅ Correct Answer
Occult gram-negative septicemia. Respiratory alkalosis may be an early finding occurring before fever, overt hypoxemia, or hypotension develops. Initiate sepsis workup (blood cultures, lactate) immediately.
Endotoxins and inflammatory mediators from gram-negative organisms directly stimulate the medullary respiratory center → hyperventilation → respiratory alkalosis → can precede all other classic sepsis findings. The diagnosis of hyperventilation syndrome is always one of exclusion.
RESPIRATORY ALKALOSIS BEFORE FEVER = THINK EARLY SEPSIS
★ 08Trap: Diarrhea with Alkaline Urine Mistaken for RTALOOK-ALIKE
A 34-year-old with chronic diarrhea has pH 7.28, HCO₃⁻ 15, normal AG. Urine pH is 6.8. A colleague suggests distal RTA. How do you differentiate?
⚠️ Tempting Wrong Answer
Distal (Type 1) RTA — urine pH >5.5 with systemic acidosis = distal tubule cannot acidify
✅ Correct Answer
GI bicarbonate loss from diarrhea. Calculate the UAG: if UAG is NEGATIVE (Cl⁻ > Na⁺+K⁺ in urine) → NH₄⁺ excretion is high → kidneys are working correctly → extrarenal cause. Alkaline urine in diarrhea is expected and normal.
Metabolic acidosis + hypokalemia (both present in diarrhea) → ↑ renal NH₄⁺ synthesis/excretion → NH₄⁺ is a urinary buffer → raises urine pH above 5.5. In Type 1 RTA: distal tubule cannot acidify → urine pH >5.5, BUT UAG is POSITIVE (NH₄⁺ excretion is low). The UAG is the discriminating test.
ALKALINE URINE IN DIARRHEA IS NORMAL — USE UAG TO DISTINGUISH FROM RTA
★ 09Trap: Overcorrecting Chronic Respiratory AcidosisTIMING
A COPD patient with chronic PaCO₂ of 65 mmHg and pH 7.37 is intubated for an exacerbation. The ICU team rapidly corrects PaCO₂ to 40 mmHg within 2 hours using aggressive ventilation settings. What complication is expected?
⚠️ Tempting Wrong Answer
Rapid PaCO₂ correction is always beneficial — normalizes pH and relieves the acidosis burden
✅ Correct Answer
Posthypercapnic metabolic alkalosis + risk of cardiac arrhythmias, reduced cerebral perfusion, and seizures. PaCO₂ must be lowered gradually back to the patient's baseline, not to 40 mmHg.
Chronic respiratory acidosis → kidneys retain large HCO₃⁻ buffer (HCO₃⁻ ↑ 0.4 per mmHg PaCO₂). When PaCO₂ drops rapidly, HCO₃⁻ cannot be excreted fast enough → posthypercapnic metabolic alkalosis. Additionally, the rapidly falling PaCO₂ causes cerebral vasoconstriction → ↓ cerebral blood flow → seizures. Must also provide Cl⁻ and K⁺ to allow renal HCO₃⁻ excretion.
CHRONIC RESP. ACIDOSIS: LOWER PaCO₂ SLOWLY TO PATIENT'S BASELINE
★ 10Trap: Salicylate Acetazolamide BackfireDRUG INTERACTION
A patient with salicylate toxicity is receiving IV NaHCO₃ to alkalinize urine. Volume overload develops. The team starts acetazolamide to reduce volume overload and continue the alkaline diuresis. HCO₃⁻ excretion increases but salicylate clearance worsens. Why?
⚠️ Tempting Wrong Answer
Acetazolamide always promotes urinary alkalinization and therefore always improves salicylate clearance
✅ Correct Answer
Acetazolamide causes systemic metabolic acidosis if the excreted HCO₃⁻ is not replaced. This acidifies the urine — the exact opposite of what is needed — and markedly reduces salicylate clearance by reverse ion trapping.
Ion trapping: ionized salicylate (salicylate anion in alkaline urine) cannot be reabsorbed → excreted. If urine acidifies (acetazolamide causes systemic acidosis → acidic urine) → salicylate becomes non-ionized → freely reabsorbed → accumulates. Acetazolamide must be used with replacement of excreted HCO₃⁻ to prevent this. It is appropriate only when coexisting alkalemia, unachievable alkaline diuresis, or volume overload is present.
ACETAZOLAMIDE WITHOUT HCO₃⁻ REPLACEMENT → ACIDIFIES URINE → REDUCES SALICYLATE CLEARANCE
