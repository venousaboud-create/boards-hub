PTH & Calcium Physiology

PTH Structure & Primary Function

  • 84-amino acid single-chain peptide; bioactive N-terminal PTH(1–34) is highly conserved
  • Fragments as small as PTH(1–11) can activate the PTH/PTHrP receptor at high concentrations
  • Synthesized as preproPTH (115 aa) → cleavage of "pre" (25 aa) then "pro" (6 aa) → mature 84-aa peptide
  • Primary function: maintain ionized [Ca²⁺] in extracellular fluid within narrow normal range

PTH Actions by Organ

Bone
Calcium Release
  • Increases osteoclast-mediated resorption (continuous exposure)
  • Intermittent low-dose PTH → net bone GAIN (anabolic)
  • Acts via osteoblasts → RANKL → activates RANK on osteoclasts
  • Up to 12 mmol (500 mg) Ca²⁺/day transferred bone↔ECF
Kidney
Multifocal Renal Actions
  • Proximal tubule: ↑ phosphate excretion, ↑ 1α-hydroxylase → ↑ 1,25(OH)₂D
  • Distal tubule: ↑ Ca²⁺ reabsorption
  • Net: hypophosphaturia + hypercalciuria (relative)
Intestine (Indirect)
Via 1,25(OH)₂D
  • PTH → ↑ renal 1α-hydroxylase → ↑ calcitriol → ↑ intestinal Ca²⁺ & phosphate absorption
  • Longer-term Ca²⁺ balance mechanism
Immediate Control
Speed of Effect
  • Bone effects: within minutes (rapid Ca²⁺ release from osteocyte matrix)
  • Chronic effects: hours (↑ osteoblast/osteoclast numbers)
  • Days: cellular replication to ↑ parathyroid mass

  • PTH secretion increases ~5× basal as Ca²⁺ falls from normal to 1.9–2.0 mmol/L (7.6–8.0 mg/dL)
  • Calcium-sensing receptor (CaSR): Class C GPCR, obligate homodimer; couples to Gαq, Gα11, Gαi in parathyroids
  • High Ca²⁺ → CaSR activated → ↓ PTH secretion, ↓ distal tubule Ca²⁺ reabsorption
  • 1,25(OH)₂D: strongly suppresses PTH gene transcription
  • FGF23 (phosphaturic hormone): can suppress PTH secretion
  • Severe hypomagnesemia impairs PTH secretion

PTH Metabolism & Assay Pitfalls

  • PTH degraded intraglandularly + in liver/kidney after secretion
  • Removing even the first amino acid → biologically inactive fragment, e.g., PTH(7–84)
  • 1st-gen assays: measured inactive C-terminal/mid-fragments — unreliable
  • 2nd-gen (intact PTH, double-antibody immunometric): standard of care; captures + detects N/C-terminal portions — still detects some PTH(7–84)
  • 3rd-gen assays: detect only full-length PTH(1–84); theoretically better for CKD management but not yet proven clinically superior
  • CKD pitfall: PTH(7–84) can accumulate and cross-react with 2nd-gen assays → overestimate biologically active PTH → risk of oversuppression → adynamic bone disease

PTHrP — Parathyroid Hormone–Related Peptide

  • Responsible for most cases of Humoral Hypercalcemia of Malignancy (HHM)
  • Produced by nearly all cell types: brain, pancreas, heart, lung, mammary, placenta, endothelial, smooth muscle
  • Structural homology: first 30 amino acids share close identity with PTH (10 identical aa, 20/30 homologous); PTHrP extends to 139–173 aa
  • Both PTH and PTHrP bind the same PTH1R (class B GPCR)
  • In adults, PTHrP has little influence on calcium homeostasis except in disease (large squamous, renal cell carcinomas)
  • Paracrine/autocrine roles: endochondral bone formation, breast branching morphogenesis, uterine contraction
  • HHM: PTHrP mimics PTH → ↑ bone resorption + hypercalcemia, but PTH is suppressed

Calcitonin

  • 32-aa peptide from thyroid parafollicular (C) cells; hypocalcemic properties
  • Acts via osteoclast receptors → ↓ bone resorption; also ↑ renal Ca²⁺ clearance
  • Limited physiologic role in humans — neither deficiency (total thyroidectomy) nor excess (medullary thyroid carcinoma) significantly changes Ca²⁺/phosphate
  • Pharmacologic use: Paget's disease, severe hypercalcemia, osteoporosis adjunct
  • Salmon calcitonin: 10–100× more potent than mammalian forms
  • Tumor marker: medullary thyroid carcinoma (sporadic and hereditary)
  • Levels elevated in pseudohypoparathyroidism (PHP)

Primary Hyperparathyroidism (pHPT)

Epidemiology & Pathophysiology

  • Typically a disease of postmenopausal women
  • Incidence: ~50 per 100,000 person-years in the US
  • Most common cause of asymptomatic hypercalcemia

Board Pearl — MEN 1 Genetics
In MEN 1: germline mutation in one MEN1 allele + somatic loss of the remaining allele (Knudson two-hit hypothesis). 15–20% of sporadic parathyroid adenomas also show biallelic somatic loss of MEN1 locus.

Genetic Defects in pHPT — Molecular Overview

  • Protooncogene activation: single mutant allele sufficient (gain-of-function)
    • CCND1 (cyclin D1/PRAD1): reciprocal translocation places CCND1 under PTH promoter → cyclin D1 overexpression in 20–40% of sporadic adenomas
    • RET: gain-of-function mutations in MEN 2
  • Tumor suppressor loss: biallelic loss required
    • MEN1 (MENIN) — MEN 1 and sporadic adenomas
    • CDC73/HRPT2 — HPT-JT and parathyroid carcinomas (mutations in 75–80% of carcinomas)
    • Rb gene (13q14) — first identified in parathyroid carcinoma

Pathology of Adenomas

  • Most common location: inferior parathyroid glands
  • Ectopic in 6–10%: thymus, thyroid, pericardium, behind esophagus, mediastinum
  • Size: usually 0.5–5 g; may reach 10–20 g (normal gland ~25 mg)
  • Chief cells predominant in both hyperplasia and adenoma

Signs & Symptoms

Key Point
Most patients with pHPT are now asymptomatic at diagnosis due to routine serum calcium screening.
Kidneys
Nephrolithiasis / Nephrocalcinosis
  • Occurred in 60–70% pre-1970; now <20%
  • Stones: calcium oxalate or calcium phosphate
  • Nephrocalcinosis → ↓ renal function + phosphate retention
Bone
Osteitis Fibrosa Cystica
  • Now very rare (earlier detection)
  • Histology: giant osteoclasts in Howship's lacunae + fibrous tissue replacing marrow
  • X-ray: subperiosteal resorption of phalangeal tufts, "salt and pepper skull"
  • Cortical bone ↓, cancellous (spine) relatively preserved
Neuromuscular
Proximal Muscle Weakness
  • Easy fatigability, atrophy
  • Complete regression after parathyroidectomy
  • May mimic primary neuromuscular disorder
GI / Other
Subtle Manifestations
  • Peptic ulcers (esp. with MEN 1 — Zollinger-Ellison)
  • Pancreatitis (mechanism unclear)
  • Neuropsychiatric: depression, irritability, cognitive dysfunction
  • Cardiovascular: LV hypertrophy, endothelial dysfunction

Diagnosis

  • Key finding: elevated or inappropriately normal PTH in a patient with hypercalcemia
  • Serum phosphate: usually low (but may be normal in renal failure)
  • Immunometric PTH assay is the principal lab test
  • PTH elevated in >90% of parathyroid-related hypercalcemia

Surgical Treatment

  • Definitive therapy: surgical excision of abnormal parathyroid tissue
  • Two approaches: (1) conventional neck exploration under general anesthesia; (2) minimally invasive parathyroidectomy (local anesthesia, outpatient)
  • Preoperative localization: neck ultrasound, ⁹⁹ᵐTc sestamibi SPECT, C-11 choline PET/CT, 4D-CT
  • Intraoperative PTH monitoring: rapid fall >50% to normal levels → successful removal
  • Multiple-gland hyperplasia (familial): more complex; consider forearm reimplantation
  • Postop hypocalcemia: falls within 24 h; low-normal for 3–5 days
  • Risk factors for postop hypocalcemia: "hungry bone" (undermineralized bone matrix), vitamin D deficiency, hypomagnesemia
  • Permanent hypoparathyroidism unlikely if PTH >15 pg/mL at 12–24 h postop

Medical Management

  • Bisphosphonates (alendronate): ↑ BMD significantly without changing serum calcium
  • Calcimimetics (cinacalcet): ↓ PTH → lower serum calcium; does NOT affect BMD
  • Scandinavian RCT: parathyroidectomy vs. observation — no difference in morbidity/mortality at 10 years
  • Growing concern about late bone deterioration (>8 years) without surgery

Parathyroid Carcinoma

  • Often not aggressive initially; long-term survival if entire gland removed without capsular rupture
  • Clue: calcium values of 3.5–3.7 mmol/L (14–15 mg/dL)
  • HRPT2/CDC73 mutations in 75–80% of all parathyroid cancers
  • Distinct from benign adenomas pathogenetically; germline mutations found in some sporadic cases → family screening indicated
  • Diagnosis often made in retrospect when metastases occur (lung, liver, bone)

Lithium-Induced Hypercalcemia

  • Occurs in ~10% of lithium-treated patients
  • Lithium shifts PTH secretion curve rightward (higher Ca²⁺ needed to suppress PTH) via CaSR effect
  • Reversible when lithium stopped in most patients
  • Cinacalcet effective; parathyroid surgery only if hypercalcemia + elevated PTH persist after lithium discontinued

Jansen's Disease (Activating PTH1R Mutations)

  • Rare autosomal dominant; constitutive PTH1R activation
  • Features: severe hypercalcemia + short-limbed dwarfism (abnormal chondrocyte maturation in growth plates)
  • Lab: hypercalcemia + hypophosphatemia with undetectable or low PTH
  • Multiple cystic bone lesions resembling severe hyperparathyroidism in adults

High-Yield Epidemiology
Hyperparathyroidism + cancer = 90% of all hypercalcemia cases. Disorders other than these two account for <10%.

Clinical Symptoms of Hypercalcemia

  • "Bones, groans, moans, stones, psychic overtones"
  • Fatigue, depression, mental confusion, anorexia, nausea, vomiting, constipation
  • Reversible renal tubular defects, polyuria
  • Short QT interval on ECG; cardiac arrhythmias
  • Symptoms more common at Ca²⁺ >2.9–3.0 mmol/L (11.6–12.0 mg/dL)
  • Ca²⁺ >3.2 mmol/L (12.8 mg/dL): calcification in kidneys, skin, vessels, lungs, heart, stomach; renal insufficiency
  • Severe hypercalcemia ≥3.7–4.5 mmol/L (14.8–18.0 mg/dL): coma, cardiac arrest — medical emergency

Differential Diagnosis Approach (Figure 422-6)

Step 1: Is hypercalcemia chronic (>1 year) or acute?
Chronic → pHPT most likely
  • Measure PTH
  • PTH high → pHPT (exclude FHH)
  • PTH low → other chronic causes: granulomatous disease, FHH, medications (lithium, thiazides), vitamin D/A intoxication
Acute/Unknown → Rule out malignancy
  • Measure PTH + PTHrP assay
  • PTH high → pHPT/MEN (consider FHH)
  • PTH low + clinical evidence → consider malignancy workup (CXR, CT chest/abdomen, bone scan)

PTH as a Diagnostic Tool

  • Elevated (or inappropriately normal) PTH: >90% of parathyroid-related causes
  • Suppressed/undetectable PTH: malignancy-related, vitamin D–related, high-bone-turnover causes
  • PTHrP immunoassays: diagnose HHM
  • 1,25(OH)₂D: low specificity overall; valuable specifically in sarcoidosis and lymphoma

Vitamin D–Related Hypercalcemia

Vitamin D Intoxication
  • Requires chronic ingestion >10,000 IU/day in healthy individuals
  • Mechanism: excessive 25(OH)D (not necessarily ↑ 1,25(OH)₂D) → ↑ intestinal absorption + ↑ bone Ca release
  • Diagnosis: 25(OH)D >100 ng/mL
  • Treatment: restrict dietary calcium + hydration + D discontinuation; glucocorticoids 40–100 mg/d prednisone if severe
  • Stores in fat → intoxication may persist weeks after stopping
Sarcoidosis & Granulomatous Disease
  • Macrophages in granulomas convert 25(OH)D → 1,25(OH)₂D without normal feedback regulation
  • Positive correlation between 25(OH)D intake and circulating 1,25(OH)₂D (unlike normal)
  • PTH low; 1,25(OH)₂D elevated
  • Management: avoid sunlight/vitamin D; glucocorticoids 100 mg/d hydrocortisone equivalent — block excess 1,25(OH)₂D production + blunt target organ response
Williams Syndrome
  • Autosomal dominant; microdeletions at elastin locus (chromosome 7)
  • Supravalvular aortic stenosis + intellectual disability + elfin facies + hypercalcemia
  • Abnormal sensitivity to vitamin D; 1,25(OH)₂D elevated (46–120 nmol/L)
CYP24A1 Mutations (24-hydroxylase deficiency)
  • Biallelic inactivating mutations → impaired 1,25(OH)₂D catabolism → hypercalcemia starting in infancy
  • Treatment: avoid sun + vitamin D + reduce calcium; fluconazole/ketoconazole (CYP27B1 inhibitors); rifampin (CYP3A4 inducer)

Other Causes

Tertiary Hyperparathyroidism (CKD)
  • Pathogenesis of secondary HPT in CKD: multifactorial — PTH resistance + ↑ FGF23 → ↓ 1α-hydroxylase → ↓ 1,25(OH)₂D → ↑ PTH
  • Tertiary = autonomous PTH hypersecretion no longer responsive to medical therapy → requires parathyroid surgery
  • Emerge as monoclonal outgrowth of previously hyperplastic gland(s)
  • Medical Rx: dietary phosphate restriction + non-absorbable phosphate binders (sevelamer preferred over calcium carbonate to avoid excess Ca loading) + calcitriol IV pulses
Milk-Alkali Syndrome
  • Excessive calcium + absorbable alkali intake
  • Cycle: mild hypercalcemia → bicarbonate retention → alkalosis → renal Ca retention → worsening hypercalcemia
  • Chronic form (Burnett's syndrome): irreversible renal damage

Bisphosphonate Mechanism
Analogues of pyrophosphate; concentrated in areas of high bone turnover → taken up by osteoclasts → inhibit prenylation of proteins → apoptosis (amino group–containing bisphosphonates). Stable in vivo (phosphatase can't hydrolyze C-P-C bond).

Hypoparathyroidism

Overview

  • Rare: ~25–35 patients/100,000 population (US + Danish estimates)
  • Most common cause: post-surgical (inadvertent removal/damage during thyroid or parathyroid surgery)
  • Surgical frequency ↓ with improved techniques and ↑ nonsurgical therapy for hyperthyroidism
  • Result: absent or inactive PTH → hypocalcemia + hyperphosphatemia

ADH1 Treatment Trap
In ADH1 (gain-of-function CaSR): treating hypocalcemia with vitamin D analogues + oral calcium exacerbates urinary calcium excretion → irreversible renal damage from stones + ectopic calcification. Novel therapy: encaleret (oral CaSR negative allosteric modulator) — normalized serum/urine Ca in phase 2 trial.

Acquired Causes

  • Post-surgical (most common): thyroid surgery, parathyroid surgery
  • Radiation-induced (radioiodine therapy for hyperthyroidism)
  • Hemochromatosis/hemosiderosis (repeated transfusions)
  • Autoimmune (APECED/APS-1)

Hypomagnesemia & Hypocalcemia

  • Severe hypomagnesemia (<0.4 mmol/L / <0.8 mEq/L) → impaired PTH secretion + impaired PTH responsiveness
  • PTH low despite hypocalcemia (maximum stimulus for PTH); corrects rapidly after Mg repletion
  • Phosphate often not elevated (unlike other hypoparathyroidism) because phosphate deficiency co-exists with hypomagnesemia
  • Treatment: parenteral magnesium repletion (must restore intracellular deficit; IV Mg then monitor — serum Mg normalizes transiently, may fall again); oral if renal Mg wasting is the cause (long-term)
  • Hypermagnesemia can also inhibit PTH secretion → hypocalcemia

Clinical Features of Chronic Hypocalcemia

  • Tingling, muscle spasms, carpopedal spasm, laryngeal spasm, convulsions
  • Chvostek's sign: tap facial nerve → twitching (less sensitive/specific)
  • Trousseau's sign: inflate BP cuff above systolic for 3 min → carpopedal spasm (more sensitive and specific)
  • Prolonged QT interval (opposite of hypercalcemia)
  • Arrhythmias; ↓ digitalis effectiveness
  • Increased intracranial pressure; papilledema
  • Mental changes: irritability, depression, psychosis
  • Basal ganglia calcification + extrapyramidal syndromes (especially hereditary forms)
  • Lenticular cataracts, papilledema
  • Chronic malabsorption, intestinal cramps

Treatment of Hypoparathyroidism

Conventional Therapy
  • Calcitriol (1,25(OH)₂D): preferred over high-dose plain vitamin D (cleared much faster); doses 0.5–1 μg/d
  • Oral calcium supplementation: 1.5–2 g/d elemental calcium
  • Maintain blood Ca at low-normal or just below normal to avoid hypercalciuria → nephrocalcinosis/stones/CKD
  • Thiazide diuretics: reduce urine Ca by ~100 mg/d (with low-sodium diet) — useful adjunct
  • Limitation: does not restore other PTH functions; exacerbates hypercalciuria; does not normalize hyperphosphatemia or low bone turnover
PTH Replacement Therapy
  • Palopegteriparatide: inactive SC prodrug releasing PTH(1–34) from inert carrier in sustained manner; once daily; normalized blood + urine Ca in phase 3 trial; approved in US + Europe
  • Eneboparatide: biased peptide agonist to PTH receptor with prolonged intracellular signaling; phase 2 complete → phase 3 ongoing
  • PTH(1–84): approved by FDA for hypoparathyroidism but no longer available in US
  • Effective half-life of PTH analogues: too short for full 24-h effect with single injection → driving development of long-acting molecules

Pseudohypoparathyroidism (PHP)

Overview

  • Group of inherited disorders resembling hypoparathyroidism functionally
  • Key distinction: PTH levels are ELEVATED (resistance, not deficiency) with hypocalcemia + hyperphosphatemia
  • Caused by PTH target organ resistance, primarily in proximal renal tubule

PHP1A — Albright's Hereditary Osteodystrophy (AHO)

  • Caused by inactivating Gsα mutations on the maternal GNAS allele
  • GNAS gene at chromosome 20q13.3
  • Gsα is paternally silenced in specific tissues (proximal renal tubule, thyroid, pituitary) → maternal allele defect → loss of Gsα → PTH resistance
  • AHO features: short stature, early-onset obesity, round face, brachydactyly (short 4th/5th metacarpals), intellectual impairment, heterotopic calcifications
  • Additional: TSH resistance (↑ TSH), basal ganglia calcification
  • Short metacarpals: premature epiphyseal closure
Imprinting Rule — GNAS
  • PHP1A: Maternal GNAS mutation → hypocalcemia + AHO (PTH resistance in kidney)
  • PPHP: Paternal GNAS mutation → AHO only (normal Ca/PTH; no kidney resistance because paternal Gsα is silenced in proximal tubule anyway)
  • Female with PHP1A/PPHP → offspring who inherits mutant allele: PHP1A
  • Male with PHP1A/PPHP → offspring who inherits mutant allele: PPHP

PHP1B

  • Caused by loss of methylation at maternal GNAS exon A/B → silences maternal Gsα in proximal renal tubule → PTH resistance
  • Autosomal dominant (AD-PHP1B): microdeletions/duplications/inversions within or upstream of GNAS locus
  • Sporadic PHP1B (sporPHP1B): most common form; some caused by paternal uniparental isodisomy of chromosome 20q
  • Less AHO features than PHP1A; may have osteitis fibrosa–like bone response (bone not resistant to PTH)

PHP2 / Acrodysostosis

  • Normal urinary cAMP response to PTH, but impaired phosphaturic response
  • Acrodysostosis with hormonal resistance: PRKAR1A mutations (regulatory subunit of PKA)
  • Acrodysostosis without hormonal resistance: PDE4D or PDE3A mutations
  • Must exclude vitamin D deficiency before diagnosing PHP2

Treatment of PHP

  • Similar to hypoparathyroidism: calcitriol + oral calcium
  • Doses often higher to maintain Ca in normal range and PTH in upper-normal/slightly elevated range
  • PHP1: NO PTH resistance in distal tubules → urinary Ca clearance NOT elevated → no risk of nephrocalcinosis (unlike true hypoparathyroidism) unless overtreated
  • Reduce doses after puberty (when bone maturation complete)

Hypocalcemia — Classification & Special Scenarios

Functional Classification

PTH-Related
  • Absent PTH: DiGeorge (22q11DS), isolated hypoparathyroidism, PTH gene mutations, GCM2/GATA3 mutations; post-surgical; APECED; infiltrative
  • Impaired secretion: ADH1/2 (gain-of-function CaSR or Gα11); hypomagnesemia/hypermagnesemia
  • Target organ resistance: PHP; hypomagnesemia
Vitamin D–Related
  • Deficiency: nutritional, ↓ cutaneous, malabsorption
  • Accelerated loss: anticonvulsants, rifampin
  • Impaired 25-hydroxylation: severe liver disease, CYP2R1 mutations
  • Impaired 1α-hydroxylation: renal insufficiency, azole antifungals (ketoconazole), genetic 1α-hydroxylase deficiency, FGF23-related disorders (TIO, XLH, CKD)
  • Target organ resistance: VDR mutations (vitamin D–dependent rickets type II)
Other Causes
  • Impaired bone resorption: denosumab, bisphosphonates
  • Excessive skeletal deposition: hungry bone syndrome, osteoblastic malignancies
  • Chelation: citrated blood, EDTA, phosphate infusion
  • Critical illness: pancreatitis, ICU

Vitamin D–Dependent Rickets

Type I (Pseudo-VD resistant)
  • CYP27B1 mutations (25(OH)D-1α-hydroxylase)
  • Impaired synthesis of 1,25(OH)₂D
  • 1,25(OH)₂D levels: LOW
  • Responds to physiologic doses of calcitriol
  • Features: hypocalcemia ± tetany, hypophosphatemia (secondary HPT), osteomalacia, ↑ alkaline phosphatase
vs
Type II (True VD resistant)
  • VDR mutations (end-organ resistance)
  • 1,25(OH)₂D levels: ELEVATED
  • Same metabolic features PLUS partial or total alopecia
  • Treatment: regular nocturnal calcium infusions (normalizes PTH → ↓ phosphaturia → improves rickets) — does NOT restore hair growth

CKD & Hypocalcemia

  • Key mediator: ↑ FGF23 → ↓ 1α-hydroxylase → ↓ 1,25(OH)₂D → ↓ intestinal Ca absorption → secondary HPT
  • FGF23 elevated even in early CKD → correlates with disease progression, mortality, LV hypertrophy
  • Treatment targets: PTH 100–300 pg/mL; use calcitriol/analogues carefully to avoid oversuppression → adynamic bone disease
  • Sevelamer (phosphate binder): preferred over calcium carbonate (avoids excess Ca loading → cardiovascular calcification)

Acute Symptomatic Hypocalcemia — Treatment

  • Mild-moderate: oral calcium + calcitriol (0.5–1 μg/d)
  • Severe/symptomatic: parenteral calcium
  • IV infusion: 0.5–2 mg/kg per hour or 30–100 mL/h of 1 mg/mL solution
  • Usually needed for only a few days; add calcitriol if symptoms persist or IV needed >2–3 days

Severe Acute Hyperphosphatemia → Hypocalcemia

  • Extensive tissue damage/cell destruction → massive phosphate release → Ca precipitation → hypocalcemia
  • Usually reverses with tissue repair + renal function restoration
  • Treatment: phosphate-binding antacids or dialysis; caution with calcium during hyperphosphatemic phase (↑ extraosseous deposition)
  • Mild hypercalcemia may follow in oliguric recovery phase (Ca redistribution from muscle)

Hungry Bone Syndrome (Post-parathyroidectomy)

  • Severe osteitis fibrosa cystica → large bone mineral deficit
  • After parathyroidectomy: hypocalcemia persists for days if Ca replacement inadequate
  • Treatment: parenteral Ca + calcitriol + oral calcium until bone mineral deficits filled; may need weeks to months
