01

What is a UTI?

  • An infection somewhere along the urinary tract that produces clinical symptoms
  • Caused by either:
    • Bladder invasion by a new urinary pathogen
    • A shift in the existing urinary bacteria's balance with the host
  • Both causes result in local tissue injury and inflammation
⚠ Key Distinction — UTI vs ASB
UTI = bacteria + symptoms
→ Almost always needs treatment
→ Both have bacteria + pyuria in urine
ASB = bacteria without symptoms
→ Usually does NOT need treatment
→ Treating ASB unnecessarily may increase future UTI risk (allows more virulent/resistant pathogens to colonize)

Two Exceptions Where ASB MUST Be Treated

Pregnant Patients

Untreated ASB → pyelonephritis, preterm delivery, low birth weight

Pre-Urologic Procedures

Procedures causing mucosal injury (e.g., lithotripsy, TURP) — bacteria can enter bloodstream

03

Epidemiology

Who Gets UTI? (by age)

Neonates

Slightly more common in males (congenital urinary tract anomalies)

Age 1–50

Predominantly females. Up to 80% of women have at least one UTI in their lifetime. Most male UTI only with catheterization or anatomic abnormality.

After Age 50

Men catch up — prostatic hypertrophy causes obstruction → UTI incidence nearly equals women.

ASB Prevalence

  • ~5% in women age 20–40
  • Up to 40–50% in elderly women and men

Recurrent UTI Numbers

  • 20–30% of women who've had one UTI will have recurrent episodes
  • Rate: 0.3–7.6 infections/patient/year, average ~2.6/year
  • Early recurrence (within 2 weeks) = likely relapse, not reinfection → may indicate a sequestered focus of ongoing infection
04

Risk Factors

Risk Factors for Recurrent UTI

🔑 Most Consistently Documented
  • Frequent sexual intercourse ← most consistently documented
  • Spermicide use ← most consistently documented
  • New sexual partner
  • First UTI before age 15
  • Maternal history of UTI

3 Risk Factor Categories

1. Impede Urine Outflow
  • Congenital urinary tract abnormalities
  • Cystoceles, pelvic organ prolapse
  • Bladder dysfunction → overflow incontinence
  • Benign prostatic hypertrophy
2. Introduce Bacteria
  • Sexual activity — risk up to 4.8× with intercourse 5×/week
  • For men: insertive rectal intercourse, lack of circumcision
  • Urinary instrumentation (catheter, nephrostomy tube)
  • Foreign bodies (stents, kidney stones) → niduses for bacteria
3. Disrupt Mucosal Defenses
  • Diaphragm or spermicide use (kills normal lactobacilli)
  • Menopause → lactobacilli replaced by Gram-negative bacteria
  • Topical estrogens help reduce recurrent UTIs in postmenopausal women
  • Oral estrogens should NOT be used for UTI prevention
  • Diabetes: 2–3× higher rates; risk increases with duration + insulin use
  • SGLT-2 inhibitors → increase urinary glucose → associated with UTI

Etiology

Key Pathogens by Syndrome

SyndromeKey PathogensNotes
Acute CystitisE. coli 75–90%; S. saprophyticus 5–15%; Klebsiella, Proteus, Enterobacter, CitrobacterS. saprophyticus more common in younger women
CAUTIE. coli still dominates; also Pseudomonas, enterococci, Staph aureus, CandidaMore diverse bacteriology — culture essential

Antimicrobial Resistance — Growing Problem

  • Resistance >20% to TMP-SMX, fluoroquinolones, nitrofurantoin in some US regions
  • ~6% of isolates resistant to 3+ antibiotic classes
  • ~9% produce extended-spectrum β-lactamase (ESBL)
🔑 Stewardship Principle
Local antibiogram data is essential for guiding empirical therapy — resistance rates vary significantly by region.

Clinical Syndromes

Asymptomatic Bacteriuria (ASB)

  • Positive culture + no localizing urinary symptoms and no unexplained systemic symptoms
  • Pyuria degree should NOT influence diagnosis — high pyuria can occur in urinary stasis
🚨 Challenging Scenario: Elderly Patient with Acute Altered Mental Status
  1. May not report symptoms reliably
  2. Must first rule out sepsis
  3. Seek collateral history (caregivers)
  4. Rule out all other causes of delirium first
  5. "History of recurrent UTIs" in elderly often reflects history of persistent ASB — not true UTI

Cystitis

Typical Symptoms
  • Dysuria (painful urination)
  • Urinary frequency
  • Urinary urgency
  • Suprapubic discomfort/tenderness
  • New nocturia, hesitancy, or gross hematuria
🚩 Red Flags → Look Beyond Bladder
  • Fever
  • Rigors
  • Unilateral back or flank pain

→ These are inconsistent with uncomplicated cystitis. Investigate for kidney, prostate, or bloodstream involvement.

In Women
  • Can treat on history alone if symptoms are clear
  • If unclear → urine dipstick or microscopy
  • Negative dipstick does NOT fully rule out UTI
  • Urine culture needed in: pregnancy, suspected resistant organism, recurrent UTI
In Men
  • Less common — approach cautiously
  • Must first exclude: ABP, CBP, pyelonephritis
  • Always obtain urine culture (differentiates bacterial prostatitis from pelvic pain syndrome)
  • True uncomplicated cystitis in afebrile men → 7 days of treatment

Prostatitis

Acute Bacterial Prostatitis (ABP)
  • Dysuria, frequency, prostatic/pelvic/perineal pain
  • Severe illness: may have fever, rigors, bladder outlet obstruction
  • Suspect in men with UTI + systemic symptoms without clear anatomic source (e.g., not clearly the kidney)
Chronic Bacterial Prostatitis (CBP)
  • Recurrent episodes of cystitis ± pelvic/perineal pain
  • No systemic signs typically
  • Managed in outpatient setting
  • Suspect when recurrent UTI is not catheter-related
🔑 Useful Signs in Prostatitis
  • Elevated PSA + enlarged prostate/seminal vesicles on ultrasound → suggests prostate involvement
  • Men with febrile UTI → should get imaging (CT or ultrasound) to evaluate for obstruction/abnormality

Pyelonephritis

  • Fever is the key distinguishing feature from cystitis
  • Mild: Low-grade fever ± lower back or costovertebral angle pain
  • Severe: High fever, rigors, nausea, vomiting, flank pain
  • Symptoms are usually acute in onset, may or may not be preceded by cystitis

Complications of Pyelonephritis

Acute Papillary Necrosis

Sloughed papillae obstruct ureter → urgent intervention needed. Also occurs in sickle cell disease and analgesic nephropathy.

Emphysematous Pyelonephritis

Gas production in renal/perinephric tissue. Almost exclusively in poorly controlled diabetes.

Xanthogranulomatous Pyelonephritis

Chronic obstruction (staghorn calculi) + infection → suppurative destruction of renal tissue. Residual tissue has yellow coloration (lipid-laden macrophages).

Intraparenchymal Abscess

Suspect if continued fever/bacteremia despite antibiotics.

CAUTI (Catheter-Associated UTI)

  • Bacteriuria is inevitable with catheterization → does NOT automatically mean UTI
  • Diagnosis requires all 3:
    • Symptoms (localizing OR systemic)
    • Pyuria + bacteriuria (threshold ≥10³ CFU/mL)
    • Other causes of symptoms ruled out
  • Diagnostic evaluation of a febrile catheterized patient should NOT end with an abnormal urinalysis alone
07

Diagnostic Tools

1. History

  • Patient-reported symptoms have high positive predictive value in uncomplicated cystitis in adult women
  • Woman with ≥1 localizing symptom + no complicating factors → 50% probability of cystitis or pyelonephritis
  • If vaginal discharge and complicating factors are absent AND risk factors present → probability close to 90%
  • No need for further workup in low-risk women unless resistant pathogens suspected

2. Urine Dipstick (Nitrite + Leukocyte Esterase)

Nitrite
  • Only Enterobacterales convert nitrate → nitrite
  • False positive: Enterobacterales ASB (bacteria but no UTI)
  • False negative: UTI due to non-Enterobacterales (Pseudomonas, enterococci, Staph)
  • False negative: Patient voiding frequently (not enough accumulation time)
Leukocyte Esterase
  • Detects enzyme in neutrophils (intact or lysed)
  • Less sensitive and specific surrogate for pyuria
  • Both negative → consider diagnoses other than UTI
  • Negative dipstick does NOT rule out bacteriuria in pregnant women — must use culture

3. Urine Microscopy

  • Pyuria in nearly all cases of cystitis
  • Hematuria in ~30% of cystitis cases
  • Traditional pyuria cutoff: >10 WBCs/HPF
  • Modern data: this cutoff has poor specificity (36%) for UTI
  • Cutoff closer to 250 WBCs/HPF may better correlate with urinary symptoms
  • But >10 WBCs/HPF remains the accepted standard for now

4. Urine Culture

  • Confirms bacteria present + gives susceptibility data
  • Results take ≥24h (susceptibilities add another 24–48h)
  • Positive culture = consistent with UTI and ASB → symptoms are required for diagnosis
  • Colony count threshold: >10² bacteria/mL is more sensitive (95%) than 10⁵/mL for acute cystitis
  • Mixed bacterial species → usually contamination
    • Exceptions: long-term catheterization, chronic urinary retention, urinary-GI/genital fistula
08

Treatment

General Principles

  • Antimicrobial therapy is warranted for any symptomatic UTI
  • Non-antibiotic treatment (NSAIDs alone) → delays response, risks more invasive infection
  • Key stewardship goals: avoid treating ASB, avoid unnecessary cultures, avoid overly long courses
⚠ Collateral Damage — Always Consider
  • Kills normal flora → predisposes to C. difficile
  • Selects drug-resistant organisms
  • Low collateral damage: pivmecillinam, fosfomycin, nitrofurantoin
  • High collateral damage: TMP-SMX, quinolones, late-generation cephalosporins

β-Lactams preferred over FQs as alternative (resistance + side effects). FDA advises against FQs for uncomplicated cystitis unless no alternatives.

Nitrofurantoin — Important Notes

  • Low resistance after >70 years of use
  • Do NOT use if creatinine clearance <30 mL/min
  • Does NOT reach tissue levels → cannot treat pyelonephritis or prostatitis
  • Avoid long-term (years) prophylaxis → risk of irreversible pulmonary toxicity

Fosfomycin — Important Notes

  • Active against E. coli (less reliable for other Enterobacterales)
  • Renal tissue levels low → do NOT use for pyelonephritis
  • Has been used for prostatitis in MDR pathogens

β-Lactams — Important Notes

  • Higher failure rates and relapse than TMP-SMX or fluoroquinolones
  • Fail to eradicate uropathogens from vaginal reservoir (specific to women)
  • Amoxicillin resistance widespread in E. coli
💜 Exception: Prostatic Involvement in Men
If prostatic involvement suspected → fluoroquinolones or TMP-SMX preferred over β-lactams (better prostate penetration)

Prostatitis Treatment

ABP
  • Fluoroquinolone or TMP-SMX for 2–4 weeks
  • Start after urine + blood cultures obtained
  • Prostatic abscess → urology consultation
CBP
  • 4–6 weeks of antibiotics
  • Recurrences may need up to 12 weeks
  • These durations based on older data — modern RCTs still needed

Pyelonephritis Treatment

  • Requires therapy that reaches renal parenchyma AND blood
Hospitalized Patients
  • IV β-lactam empirically (based on local antibiogram + patient history)
Outpatient Patients
  • TMP-SMX, ciprofloxacin, or levofloxacin
  • Consider initial dose of IV/IM β-lactam or aminoglycoside for safety
  • Hospitalize if: unable to take oral meds (nausea/vomiting) or unlikely to follow up

Duration

  • 5–7 days oral fluoroquinolone OR
  • 7–14 days TMP-SMX (depending on clinical improvement)
  • Oral β-lactams less effective → use with caution + close follow-up
🚫 Do NOT Use for Pyelonephritis
Nitrofurantoin, pivmecillinam, fosfomycin — inadequate tissue levels

If No Clinical Response Within 72 Hours → Evaluate for:

  • Papillary necrosis with obstruction → relieve obstruction
  • Perinephric/cortical abscess → percutaneous drainage
  • Emphysematous pyelonephritis → drainage ± elective nephrectomy
  • Xanthogranulomatous → often nephrectomy needed

CAUTI Treatment

  • Culture is essential (diverse bacteriology)
  • Exchange or remove catheter during treatment (removes biofilm nidus)
  • Ideally before sending culture → culture reflects bladder, not catheter
  • Duration: 7–14 days based on clinical response

CAUTI Prevention

  • Avoid unnecessary catheterization; remove catheters as soon as no longer needed
  • Antimicrobial-impregnated catheters (silver/nitrofurazone) — NOT shown to significantly help
  • Intermittent catheterization may be preferable to long-term indwelling catheterization (e.g., spinal cord injury)

  • ASB or cystitis: treat for 5–7 days
  • Pyelonephritis: parenteral β-lactam ± aminoglycoside

Candiduria

  • Increasingly common in: ICU, broad-spectrum antibiotic use, diabetes
  • 50% are non-C. albicans species
  • Asymptomatic: Remove catheter first → resolves in >1/3 of cases; treatment generally NOT recommended
When to Treat Candiduria
  • Symptomatic cystitis or pyelonephritis
  • Asymptomatic but high risk: neutropenia, upcoming urologic manipulation, low-birth-weight infants
Treatment
  • Fluconazole 200–400 mg/day for 7–14 days (first-line)
  • Bladder irrigation with amphotericin B → generally NOT recommended
10

Prevention of Recurrent UTI in Women

Intervene when ≥3 episodes/year OR when UTIs are interfering with lifestyle.

Initial evaluation: Confirm diagnosis, imaging for stones, assess for urinary retention.

Non-Antibiotic Approaches

Increased Fluid Intake

Reduces recurrent UTIs; generally well tolerated.

Methenamine Hippurate

Converted to formaldehyde in bladder. Well tolerated; may reduce UTIs as much as antibiotic prophylaxis.

Cranberry Products

Cochrane review found significant benefit (optimal product/dose unclear).

Lactobacillus Probiotics
  • Intravaginal → may reduce recurrent UTIs
  • Oral → does not appear effective
  • Caution in immunocompromised (risk of invasive infections)
⚠ Mannose Products
Mixed results — insufficient evidence to recommend routinely.

Antibiotic Prophylactic Strategies (3 Options)

1. Continuous

Daily or thrice-weekly low-dose TMP-SMX or nitrofurantoin. Typically prescribed for 6 months.

2. Postcoital

Single dose after intercourse. Ideal when UTI is clearly linked to sexual activity.

3. Patient-Initiated Therapy
  • Patient gets urine culture materials + antibiotic course
  • Self-medicates at first symptom onset
  • Culture refrigerated → sent to physician for confirmation
  • If established patient-provider relationship → culture can be omitted if episodes respond completely
11

Prognosis

  • Cystitis → risk factor for recurrent cystitis and pyelonephritis
  • ASB in elderly/catheterized → does NOT in itself increase risk of infection or death
  • Recurrent UTI without anatomic abnormalities → does NOT lead to chronic pyelonephritis or renal failure
  • Infection alone does NOT play a primary role in chronic interstitial nephritis
    • Primary causes: analgesic overuse, obstruction, reflux, toxin exposure
⚠ Exception
In the presence of underlying renal abnormalities (especially obstructing stones) → infection as a secondary factor can accelerate renal parenchymal damage.

Exam Traps

Stage A Internal Medicine board coaching. Each trap shows the vignette, the tempting wrong answer, and the correct reasoning.

Trap 01ASB in the Elderly ≠ UTI
An 82-year-old woman with dementia is confused. Staff report "she always gets UTIs." Urine culture: E. coli >10⁵ CFU/mL with pyuria. No fever, no dysuria, no urgency reported. You start antibiotics for UTI.
Correct Answer: WRONG — This is likely ASB, not UTI.
No localizing urinary symptoms = ASB until proven otherwise. "History of recurrent UTIs" in elderly often reflects persistent ASB. Must rule out sepsis and other causes of delirium first. Treating ASB increases risk of future resistant UTI.
⚠ Trap: Positive culture + confused patient = automatic UTI diagnosis. Keyword: no localizing symptoms = ASB first, not UTI.
Trap 02Nitrofurantoin for Pyelonephritis
A 25-year-old woman has dysuria, frequency, fever 39°C, and right flank pain. Urine culture grows susceptible E. coli. You prescribe nitrofurantoin 100 mg BID for 7 days.
Correct Answer: WRONG — Nitrofurantoin must NOT be used for pyelonephritis.
Fever + flank pain = pyelonephritis = renal tissue invasion. Nitrofurantoin does not reach adequate tissue levels. Use TMP-SMX, ciprofloxacin, or levofloxacin instead.
⚠ Trap: Student remembers nitrofurantoin is the preferred agent for UTI. Keyword: pyelonephritis needs tissue-level drug — nitrofurantoin, fosfomycin, pivmecillinam are bladder-only.
Trap 03Urine Dipstick Ruling Out UTI
A 30-year-old sexually active woman has classic dysuria and frequency. Urine dipstick is nitrite-negative and leukocyte esterase-negative. You conclude she does not have UTI.
Correct Answer: Negative dipstick doesn't fully rule out UTI — clinical judgment still required.
Both negative together should prompt consideration of other diagnoses, but cannot completely exclude UTI. S. saprophyticus (common in young women) is nitrite-negative. If high clinical suspicion, proceed with microscopy or culture.
⚠ Trap: Negative dipstick = UTI ruled out. Keyword: both negative = consider other diagnoses, not definitively rule out UTI.
Trap 04CAUTI: Culture Positive = Treat
A catheterized post-op patient has a urine culture growing E. coli 10⁵ CFU/mL. No fever. No urinary complaints. The nurse flags this for treatment. You start antibiotics.
Correct Answer: WRONG — Bacteriuria in catheterized patients is expected and does not require treatment without symptoms.
CAUTI diagnosis requires symptoms (localizing OR systemic) + bacteriuria + pyuria + exclusion of other causes. No symptoms = catheter-associated ASB → remove catheter if no longer needed, no antibiotics.
⚠ Trap: High colony count triggers treatment reflex. Keyword: catheter + bacteriuria alone ≠ CAUTI; symptoms are mandatory.
Trap 05Treat ASB in Pregnancy — Always
A 22-year-old at 16 weeks gestation has no urinary symptoms. Routine prenatal urine culture grows E. coli. She is feeling well. You decide not to treat since she has ASB and is asymptomatic.
Correct Answer: WRONG — Pregnancy is one of only TWO exceptions where ASB must be treated.
Untreated ASB in pregnancy → pyelonephritis, preterm delivery, low birth weight. Screen and treat with cephalosporin (preferred) or amoxicillin (if susceptible) for 5–7 days.
⚠ Trap: "ASB doesn't need treatment" applied universally. Keyword: pregnancy + positive culture = always treat, regardless of symptoms.
Trap 06Fluoroquinolone as First-Line for Uncomplicated Cystitis
A healthy 28-year-old woman presents with uncomplicated cystitis. Local susceptibility data shows >95% E. coli susceptibility to ciprofloxacin. She has no allergies. You prescribe ciprofloxacin 3-day course.
Correct Answer: Not preferred — FDA advises against fluoroquinolones for uncomplicated cystitis when alternatives exist.
Preferred agents are TMP-SMX (if susceptible), nitrofurantoin, fosfomycin, or pivmecillinam. FQs: high collateral damage + rare serious toxicities (tendon rupture, aortic dissection) + drives resistance. Reserve FQs for when no alternatives.
⚠ Trap: High susceptibility rate makes ciprofloxacin look ideal. Keyword: FDA advisory — FQs are last resort for uncomplicated cystitis in women.
Trap 07Early UTI Recurrence = Reinfection?
A woman treated for cystitis returns 10 days later with the same symptoms and same organism on culture. You treat her as a new UTI (reinfection) with the same regimen.
Correct Answer: Early recurrence (<2 weeks) = likely RELAPSE, not reinfection.
Relapse suggests a sequestered focus of ongoing infection (e.g., abscess, stone, prostate focus in men). Further evaluation is warranted, not just re-treatment with the same drug.
⚠ Trap: Second positive culture = new infection. Keyword: <2 weeks recurrence = relapse → evaluate for sequestered focus.
Trap 08Prostatitis Duration: Same as Cystitis
A 45-year-old man has perineal pain, dysuria, and fever. ABP is diagnosed. You prescribe ciprofloxacin for 7 days (same as cystitis in men).
Correct Answer: WRONG — ABP requires at least 2–4 weeks of treatment, not 7 days.
Prostate penetration requires longer courses. ABP: 2–4 weeks. CBP: 4–6 weeks (recurrences up to 12 weeks). First get urine + blood cultures, then start fluoroquinolone or TMP-SMX.
⚠ Trap: Student confuses cystitis duration (7 days for men) with prostatitis. Keyword: ABP = minimum 2–4 weeks; CBP = 4–12 weeks.
Trap 09Emphysematous Pyelonephritis — Who Gets It?
CT shows gas within the renal parenchyma. Which underlying condition is most strongly associated with this finding?
Correct Answer: Poorly controlled diabetes mellitus.
Emphysematous pyelonephritis occurs almost exclusively in poorly controlled diabetics. Gas-forming organisms (usually E. coli) destroy renal parenchyma. Treatment: drainage ± elective nephrectomy.
⚠ Trap: Gas in organ = emphysematous = could be anything. Keyword: gas in kidney = emphysematous pyelonephritis = poorly controlled diabetes.
Trap 10Recurrent UTI → Causes Renal Failure?
A patient with recurrent cystitis asks if she will eventually develop kidney damage or renal failure. You reassure her that recurrent UTI leads to chronic pyelonephritis and renal insufficiency over time.
Correct Answer: WRONG — Recurrent UTI without anatomic abnormalities does NOT lead to chronic pyelonephritis or renal failure.
Infection does not play a primary role in chronic interstitial nephritis. Primary causes: analgesic overuse, obstruction, reflux, toxin exposure. Exception: obstructing stones + infection can accelerate parenchymal damage.
⚠ Trap: Repeated infections = cumulative damage assumption. Keyword: recurrent UTI (no anomaly) ≠ chronic pyelonephritis ≠ renal failure — unless obstructing stones are present.
