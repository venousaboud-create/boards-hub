Cardiac Biomarkers

🔑 Board Anchor
After MI, troponin remains elevated for 7–10 days — this is comparable to CK elevation in duration but far more specific. Troponin is preferred because it has cardiac-specific sequences (cTnT and cTnI) and shows rises 20–50× the upper reference limit in STEMI.

⚠ Myocardial Injury ≠ MI
Troponin >99th percentile without clinical context (symptoms + ECG) = Myocardial Injury, NOT MI.
Common non-MI causes: sepsis, PE, CKD, myocarditis, Takotsubo, stroke/SAH, strenuous exercise, chemotherapy, CKD.
💜 1-Hour Rule-Out Algorithm
For NSTE-ACS: hsTn must be non-elevated at BOTH 0h AND 1h to safely rule out NSTEMI. A single negative troponin is NEVER sufficient.
✓ Earlier Troponin Peak = Reperfusion Success
In STEMI treated with PCI or fibrinolytics: an earlier-than-expected troponin peak (rapid washout from interstitium) suggests successful reperfusion of the infarct-related artery.

Exercise Stress Test (ETT)

🔑 Core Principle
ETT (Exercise Treadmill Test) is the best initial investigation for intermediate pre-test probability patients who can exercise and have an interpretable baseline ECG. When ECG is uninterpretable or patient cannot exercise → add imaging (nuclear, stress echo, CMR).
Criteria for POSITIVE ETT — Memorize These

A positive ETT requires ischemic ST changes, not just symptoms. The key criteria are:

  • Horizontal or downsloping ST depression ≥0.1 mV (1 mm), measured at J+0.08s (80ms after J-point) — this is the gold standard criterion
  • ST depression ≥0.2 mV (2 mm) in any lead = strongly positive
  • SBP drop >10 mmHg during exercise (hemodynamic compromise)
  • Development of ventricular tachyarrhythmia during exercise
  • Symptoms + any of the above = highest specificity
⚠ What Does NOT Make ETT Positive
  • Upsloping ST at 0.1 mV from baseline is NOT considered positive — it has very low specificity
  • Atrial flutter/fibrillation appearing during exercise = NOT a positive test for ischemia
  • Blood pressure drop (alone, without ST changes)
  • Non-specific ST changes in the absence of ≥1 mm horizontal/downsloping depression
False Negatives — When ETT Misses Ischemia
🚨 Circumflex Territory = Classic False Negative
The left circumflex (LCX) territory (posterolateral wall) is not well represented on the standard 12-lead ECG. Isolated LCX disease can cause significant ischemia with completely normal ST changes on ETT → false negative.

Solution: Add imaging modality (nuclear SPECT, stress echo, or cardiac MRI) when intermediate/high pretest probability despite negative ETT — especially in women and those with possible circumflex disease.
Causes of False Negative ETT
  • Isolated circumflex territory disease (most common)
  • Submaximal exercise (didn't achieve target HR)
  • Single-vessel disease with small territory
  • Anti-ischemic medication masking response
ECG Non-Interpretable → Can't Use ETT Alone
  • LBBB
  • WPW pattern
  • Paced rhythm
  • ≥1 mm resting ST depression
  • LV hypertrophy with repolarization abnormalities
  • Digitalis effect
Prognostic Significance — High vs Good Prognosis Findings
Poor Prognosis Features
  • Increased lung uptake on nuclear scan (↑ pulmonary congestion)
  • Large or multiple perfusion defects
  • ↓ LV EF during exercise on echo/radionuclide
  • Unable to complete Stage II Bruce (<6 min)
  • ST depression ≥1 mm before Stage II completion
  • ST depression ≥2 mm at any stage
  • ST depression persisting >5 min post-exercise
  • SBP drop >10 mmHg during exercise
  • VT during exercise
Good Prognosis Features
  • Completing Stage III Bruce without ischemia
  • Normal perfusion scan or negative stress echo
  • Normal LV function throughout test
  • No hemodynamic compromise
✓ Board Key
A 50-year-old symptomatic man with typical angina who has a positive ETT (≥1 mm downsloping ST depression) has approximately 98% probability of obstructive CAD — the test is confirming very high pre-test probability. In this setting, the test adds minimal diagnostic value (Bayes' theorem) but is important for prognosis and treatment planning.

Stable Angina — Drug Therapy

🚨 High-Yield Rule
Nitrates are NOT indicated for silent ischemia without symptoms (CAD present, no angina). They are purely symptomatic agents. Beta-blockers and statins address prognosis and risk reduction in asymptomatic CAD.
Nitrates — Mechanism, Doses, Tolerance, Contraindications

Mechanism

  • Metabolized → releases NO → activates guanylyl cyclase → ↑ cGMP → vascular smooth muscle relaxation
  • Venodilation → ↓ LVEDV/LVEDP → ↓ wall tension → ↓ MVO₂ (primary effect)
  • Dilation of epicardial coronary arteries and collaterals (↑ supply)
  • Antithrombotic: NO-mediated ↓ intraplatelet Ca²⁺ → ↓ platelet aggregation
PreparationDoseKey Notes
SL NTG tablet/spray0.3–0.6 mg PRNUp to 3 doses, 5 min apart; also prophylactic before exercise
NTG ointment 2%0.5–2 inches q8hNitrate-free interval overnight
NTG transdermal patch0.2–0.8 mg/hRemove at bedtime for 10–12h nitrate-free interval
Isosorbide dinitrate10–40 mg 2–3×/dEccentric schedule (not equal spacing)
Isosorbide 5-mononitrate SR30–240 mg once dailyMost convenient long-acting oral
⚠ Nitrate Tolerance
Maintain 10–12 hour nitrate-free interval daily. Continuous exposure depletes intracellular thiols → tolerance. Remove patch at bedtime. This is why continuous-wear patches fail.
⚠ Nitrate Contraindications — Know All
  • SBP <90 mmHg (hypotension)
  • RV infarction (depends on preload — nitrates → cardiovascular collapse)
  • PDE-5 inhibitors: Sildenafil/Vardenafil within 24h, Tadalafil within 48h
  • Severe aortic stenosis
Beta-Blockers — Selectivity, Doses, Dangers

Mechanism

Competitive antagonism of β adrenoceptors → ↓ HR, ↓ arterial pressure, ↓ contractility → ↓ MVO₂. Effect most prominent during exercise. ↓ Mortality and reinfarction post-MI.

🚨 NEVER Stop Abruptly
Sudden discontinuation → rebound adrenergic stimulation → ↑↑ HR, ↑ MVO₂ → intensified ischemia → MI. Taper over ≥2 weeks when discontinuing.

Calcium Channel Blockers — DHP vs Non-DHP, Combinations
Dihydropyridines (DHP)

Drugs: Amlodipine, felodipine, nicardipine, nifedipine SR

  • Potent peripheral vasodilators
  • Minimal negative inotropy
  • SAFE to combine with beta-blockers
  • Good for angina + hypertension
  • Vasospastic angina responds very well
  • Short-acting nifedipine IR: AVOID — reflex tachycardia → ↑ MVO₂ → may precipitate MI
Non-Dihydropyridines (Non-DHP)

Drugs: Verapamil, Diltiazem

  • Significant negative chronotropy and inotropy
  • Slow AV conduction
  • Verapamil + Beta-blocker = ABSOLUTELY AVOID → severe bradycardia, AV block, acute HF
  • Diltiazem + Beta-blocker: acceptable only if normal LV function and no conduction disturbances
  • Can worsen LV failure
💜 When to Choose CCB Over BB
  • Inadequate response to BB + nitrate combination
  • Asthma or COPD (use DHP CCB)
  • Significant AV conduction disturbances (use DHP CCB)
  • Vasospastic (Prinzmetal) angina — CCBs are first-line
  • Symptomatic peripheral arterial disease
  • Ischemic heart disease without structural disease + HF signs: Verapamil + metoprolol = most dangerous combination
Ranolazine — Late INa Inhibitor

Mechanism: Inhibits late inward Na⁺ current (late Iₙₐ) → limits Na⁺-mediated Ca²⁺ overload → ↓ diastolic wall stiffness → improves perfusion. Does NOT significantly lower HR or BP.

Dose: 500–1000 mg twice daily. Start at 500 mg BID.

⚠ Key Drug Interactions (CYP3A Inhibitors)
Diltiazem, verapamil, ketoconazole, macrolides, HIV protease inhibitors, grapefruit juice — all ↑ ranolazine plasma levels → QTc prolongation. If adding diltiazem, reduce ranolazine to 500 mg BID.

Contraindications: Hepatic impairment, significant QTc prolongation.

Vasospastic Angina (Prinzmetal's)

🔑 Classic Presentation
Young patient (fewer CV risk factors), rest pain especially at night or early morning, ST elevation (not depression!) during episodes, episodes self-terminate. ECG normalizes between attacks. Coronary angiography: focal spasm ± proximal plaque; spasm in ~50% without significant stenosis.
Key Distinguishing Features — vs NSTE-ACS
Vasospastic Angina
  • Younger patients, fewer risk factors
  • Cigarette smoking = main risk factor
  • Pain at rest, classic nocturnal pattern
  • ECG: ST ELEVATION (transient)
  • Associated with Raynaud's, migraine
  • Troponin: may be slightly elevated during prolonged spasm, but not typical pattern
  • Provocation: acetylcholine or hyperventilation
NSTE-ACS
  • Older patients, multiple CV risk factors
  • Pain with exertion or rest
  • ECG: ST depression or T-wave changes
  • Troponin elevated (NSTEMI)
  • No vasospastic associations
  • Plaque rupture/erosion mechanism
Treatment — CCBs First-Line, Aspirin Caution
  • Calcium Channel Blockers — FIRST-LINE: Prevent coronary smooth muscle contraction. Both DHP (amlodipine) and non-DHP (diltiazem, verapamil) effective. High doses often needed.
  • Nitrates: Effective for acute spasm relief (SL NTG). Long-term efficacy limited by nitrate tolerance — cannot rely as sole therapy.
  • Statins: Shown to reduce major adverse events in vasospastic angina.
  • Aspirin: USE WITH CAUTION — may increase severity of ischemic episodes by inhibiting prostacyclin synthesis (prostacyclin normally opposes vasoconstriction). Only use aspirin if significant concurrent atherosclerosis is present.
🚨 Beta-Blockers in Vasospastic Angina
Beta-blockers have variable response in vasospastic angina and are NOT first-line. Non-selective BB (propranolol) may worsen vasospasm by blocking β₂-mediated vasodilation. CCBs are always preferred.
💜 Beta-Blockers in Acute Decompensated HF
A separate but commonly tested concept: Beta-blockers (even β₁-selective) have negative inotropic and chronotropic effects. In acute decompensated heart failure (acute pulmonary edema), they can worsen cardiac output. Use with extreme caution; the acute setting is a relative contraindication. Beta-blockers are started at very low doses only when patient is stable/compensated.

Coronary Revascularization — PCI vs CABG

🔑 Guiding Principle
In stable IHD, revascularization is for symptom relief, NOT to reduce MI or mortality (ISCHEMIA trial). In ACS (especially STEMI), early PCI reduces mortality.
Prefer CABG Over PCI — The Indications
  • Left main coronary artery stenosis >50% — most critical; CABG improves survival
  • 3-vessel disease — especially if EF <35% (greatest survival benefit)
  • 2-vessel disease with proximal LAD involvement — especially with ↓ EF or diabetes
  • T2DM + multivessel CAD — CABG + optimal medical therapy > PCI for death, MI, and repeat revascularization
  • Lesions anatomically unsuitable for PCI (small diffuse disease, complex bifurcation)
  • Hibernating myocardium with LV dysfunction (viable tissue → revascularization restores function)
✓ Exception: When PCI is Preferred Over CABG
  • 1- or 2-vessel disease without proximal LAD, normal LV — PCI appropriate
  • Left main + proximal LAD disease → PCI is acceptable (not required to go CABG)
  • Poor surgical candidate (high operative risk) → PCI even if anatomy favors CABG
  • Patient preference for less invasive procedure (when anatomy is suitable for PCI)
CABG Outcomes — Numbers to Memorize
OutcomeNumber/Rate
Operative mortality (appropriate candidates)<1%
Angina abolished/greatly reduced~90% of patients
Saphenous vein graft (SVG) occlusion — First postoperative year10–20%
SVG occlusion rate — Years 5–7~2%/year
SVG occlusion rate — After year 7~4%/year
Return to work (patients)2–4 weeks (uncomplicated)
💜 Why IMA Graft is Superior to SVG
Internal mammary artery (IMA) → LAD anastomosis: much better long-term patency than saphenous vein graft. The IMA is a living conduit with anti-atherogenic properties. SVG progressively occludes due to atherosclerosis. IMA graft to LAD → better survival than SVG → LAD.
Post-DES Treatment — DAPT Protocol
🔑 DES = Drug-Eluting Stent
After DES implantation, patients require dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT) to prevent in-stent thrombosis:
Aspirin + P2Y₁₂ inhibitor (clopidogrel OR prasugrel OR ticagrelor) for ideally 12 months

The most commonly asked combination: Aspirin + Clopidogrel OR Aspirin + Prasugrel (prasugrel is more potent, preferred in ACS with planned PCI).
  • DES has delayed endothelialization → prolonged risk of subacute/late stent thrombosis
  • Restenosis rate with DES: <5% (vs 10–30% with bare metal stent BMS)
  • Fondaparinux alone during PCI = dangerous (catheter thrombosis) → always add UFH if doing PCI on fondaparinux

NSTE-ACS — Anti-Ischemic Treatment

Beta-Blocker Contraindications in Acute NSTE-ACS — THE LIST
🚨 Absolute Contraindications — Memorize All 7
PR >0.24 seconds 2nd/3rd AV Block HR <50 bpm SBP <90 mmHg Acute/Severe HF Cardiogenic Shock (Killip III/IV) Active Bronchospasm

The most commonly tested: Pulmonary edema (= acute heart failure) is a contraindication because the heart compensates by tachycardia, and blocking this → cardiovascular collapse.

Beta-blockers are the other mainstay alongside nitrates. Target HR: 50–60 bpm. Once heart failure is stabilized, beta-blockers are re-introduced at low doses. Do not withhold indefinitely once decompensation resolves.

Nitrates in NSTE-ACS — Protocol and Contraindications
  • SL NTG 0.3–0.6 mg (up to 3 doses, 5 min apart)
  • If symptoms persist → IV nitroglycerin: start 5–10 μg/min; increase by 10 μg/min q3–5 min
  • Stop IV NTG when: symptoms relieved, SBP <90 mmHg, or dose reaches 200 μg/min
  • Transition to oral/topical nitrates when pain-free for 12–24h
🚨 PDE-5 Inhibitor Windows — Board Favorite
  • Sildenafil (Viagra) / Vardenafil (Levitra): Nitrates contraindicated for 24 hours after use
  • Tadalafil (Cialis): Nitrates contraindicated for 48 hours (longer half-life)
  • Mechanism: synergistic vasodilation → profound, potentially irreversible hypotension
⚠ Nitrates CONTRAINDICATED with Aortic Stenosis
In NSTEMI with severe aortic stenosis + hypertension: nitrates are the treatment for ischemia relief and BP control. The contraindication is severe AS with fixed low output, not moderate AS. However, use with caution and monitor BP closely as afterload reduction can drop CO.
CCBs in NSTE-ACS — When to Use
  • Persistent ischemia despite full-dose nitrates AND beta-blockers
  • Contraindication to nitrates or beta-blockers
  • Vasospastic angina — CCBs are the primary treatment
  • Avoid short-acting nifedipine (reflex tachycardia)
  • Avoid non-DHPs (verapamil, diltiazem) when: SBP <90, pulmonary edema, or LV dysfunction
Morphine in NSTE-ACS — Critical Drug Interaction

Use for: Continued severe angina despite 3 SL NTG tablets, or recurrent ischemia despite adequate anti-ischemic therapy.

🚨 Morphine Blunts P2Y₁₂ Absorption
Morphine slows GI motility → delayed absorption and blunted antiplatelet effect of oral P2Y₁₂ inhibitors (ticagrelor, clopidogrel). This is critical when timing DAPT before PCI. Consider IV cangrelor if rapid platelet inhibition is needed.

NSTE-ACS — Anticoagulant Therapy

🔑 Four Parenteral Options for NSTE-ACS
UFH → Enoxaparin (LMWH) → Fondaparinux → Bivalirudin. Know their key differences.
Fondaparinux vs Enoxaparin — Most Tested Comparison
Fondaparinux (Indirect Factor Xa Inhibitor)
  • Dose: 2.5 mg SC once daily
  • Equivalent efficacy to enoxaparin for recurrent events
  • LOWER major bleeding than enoxaparin
  • Preferred for NSTE-ACS when NOT going urgently to PCI
  • CRITICAL LIMITATION: Cannot be used alone during PCI → causes catheter-related thrombosis
  • If PCI planned: must add supplemental UFH at time of procedure
Enoxaparin (LMWH — Anti-Xa)
  • Dose: 1 mg/kg SC q12h
  • Superior to UFH in conservative strategy (fewer recurrent events)
  • More bleeding than fondaparinux
  • Renally cleared: reduce to 1 mg/kg once daily if CrCl <30
  • Can be used directly at PCI (0.5 mg/kg IV bolus)

NSTE-ACS — Invasive Strategy Selection

Three-Tier Risk Classification → Timing of Catheterization
Very High Risk → Immediate Invasive (<2 hours)
  • Cardiogenic shock or unstable hemodynamics
  • Acute HF due to ongoing ischemia
  • Cardiac arrest post-presentation
  • Life-threatening arrhythmias
  • Mechanical complications of MI
  • Angina refractory to medical treatment
  • Recurrent dynamic ischemic ECG changes
High Risk → Early Invasive (<24 hours)
  • Confirmed NSTEMI (troponin +)
  • GRACE risk score >140
  • Transient ST elevation
  • Dynamic ST or T-wave changes
Non-High Risk → Selective Invasive
  • None of the above criteria
  • Clinical + non-invasive assessments first
  • Angiography if: rest pain recurs, troponin becomes +, severe ischemia on stress test, or CT angio shows stenosis
💜 Post-Angiography: Complete vs Staged Revascularization
  • Single-stage PCI (complete revascularization): Preferred if multivessel disease without cardiogenic shock, severe comorbidities, or complex anatomy
  • Staged PCI: If cardiogenic shock, severe comorbidities (AKI), or complex/unfavorable anatomy → culprit lesion first, then evaluate non-culprit lesions
⚠ The Conservative Strategy (Non-Invasive)
For non-high-risk patients, initial conservative management (medical therapy) is acceptable. If angina recurs, troponin turns positive, or stress test shows severe ischemia → proceed to angiography. Ticagrelor (and clopidogrel) are appropriate in conservative strategy. Prasugrel is NOT for conservative management.

STEMI — Acute Management

🔑 The Goal: Minimize Time to Reperfusion
Primary PCI is preferred when achievable within 90 min (PCI center) or 120 min (transfer). If PCI cannot be done in time → fibrinolysis (door-to-needle ≤30 min). NEVER give fibrinolytics for NSTE-ACS or without ST elevation — may be harmful.
Initial ED Management — The First Drugs
  • Aspirin 160–325 mg chewed — buccal absorption; first and most essential. Follow with 75–162 mg daily.
  • Heparin (UFH) — 60 U/kg bolus (max 4,000 U) + 12 U/kg/h (max 1,000 U/h); maintain aPTT 1.5–2× control. UFH combined with streptokinase saves ~5 lives per 1,000 patients.
  • P2Y₁₂ Inhibitor — add clopidogrel, prasugrel (post-angiography only), or ticagrelor to aspirin
  • SL Nitroglycerin — 0.4 mg, up to 3 doses, for ischemic pain; not if RV infarction, SBP <90, or PDE-5 inhibitor within 24–48h
  • IV Morphine — 2–4 mg IV q5 min PRN for severe pain (slows GI absorption of oral antiplatelets)
  • Beta-Blocker — IV metoprolol 5 mg q2–5 min × 3 doses IF: HR >60, SBP >100, PR <0.24s, no acute HF
Beta-Blockers in STEMI — Timing and Contraindications

Oral beta-blocker in first 24h: Start if no acute HF, no low-output state, no cardiogenic shock risk, no conduction disturbances. Regimen: 50 mg q6h × 48h → 100 mg q12h.

🚨 Beta-Blocker Contraindications in STEMI — Same as NSTE-ACS
PR >0.24s 2nd/3rd AV block HR <50 SBP <90 Acute HF / Pulmonary Edema Cardiogenic shock Reactive airway disease
💜 Beta-Blockers for VPBs in STEMI
VPBs (ventricular premature beats) occur in almost all STEMI patients and do NOT require specific antiarrhythmic therapy. Prophylactic lidocaine is CONTRAINDICATED — increases mortality. Beta-blockers are the appropriate preventive agent for VF.

  • Suspected aortic dissection

  • STEMI WITHOUT cardiogenic shock: PCI of non-culprit vessels (during index or within 45 days) → lower CV events (COMPLETE, PRAMI, CvLPRIT trials)

💜 AIVR — Benign Reperfusion Rhythm
Accelerated Idioventricular Rhythm (AIVR): rate 60–100 bpm, wide complex, occurs transiently during fibrinolysis at reperfusion. This is a benign reperfusion rhythm — does NOT presage VT. No treatment needed — monitor only. Do NOT give amiodarone for AIVR.

STEMI — Complications

Inferior STEMI + Bradycardia — Why Adenosine?
🔑 Inferior STEMI → AV Block Mechanism
The right coronary artery (RCA) supplies the inferior wall AND the AV node (in ~90% of patients). When the RCA is occluded in inferior STEMI, ischemic myocardium releases adenosine — which is a potent inhibitor of AV nodal conduction. This causes AV block (1st, 2nd, or even complete/3rd degree) and bradycardia in inferior MI.

Key: AV block in inferior STEMI is usually transient, nodal level, and responds to atropine. AV block in anterior STEMI (LAD occlusion) is usually infranodal and carries much worse prognosis.
Inferior STEMI + AV Block
  • RCA occlusion → adenosine release from ischemic tissue
  • AV block at AV NODE level
  • Junctional escape rhythm: narrow complex
  • Usually temporary, hemodynamically stable
  • Responds to atropine
  • Good prognosis
Anterior STEMI + AV Block
  • LAD occlusion → large infarct involving bundle branches
  • AV block at INFRANODAL level (His-Purkinje)
  • Wide complex escape rhythm
  • Does NOT respond to atropine
  • Requires temporary pacing
  • Very poor prognosis (large infarct)
RV Infarction — The Triad + Nitrate Danger
🚨 RV Infarction = Nitrates Absolutely Contraindicated
RV infarction patients depend entirely on adequate preload (high RV filling pressure) to maintain cardiac output. Nitrates cause venodilation → ↓ preload → precipitous cardiovascular collapse. Same applies to diuretics.
🔑 Classic Triad of RV Infarction
JVD + Clear Lungs + Hypotension (no pulmonary congestion despite low BP) in the setting of inferior MI (ST elevation in II, III, aVF).
Confirm with V₄R lead — ST elevation in right-sided leads (present in first 24h)
Treatment: Volume expansion (IV fluids) to maintain RV preload + improve LV performance

Trap 01BB Contraindication in NSTEMI — Pulmonary Edema
A 60-year-old man with NSTEMI is admitted. He has bilateral basal crackles, HR 112, SpO₂ 90% on room air, and BP 95/60. Which condition should prevent you from giving a beta-blocker right now?
Pulmonary edema (acute heart failure)
Beta-blockers are contraindicated in acute/severe HF (Killip Class III) because they remove the compensatory tachycardia and further decrease cardiac output. The crackles + tachycardia + low BP = acute decompensated HF. Once stabilized, beta-blockers can be carefully introduced.
⚠ Distractor: Students see NSTEMI and reflexively give BB. The key is READING THE CLINICAL SIGNS first. Board keyword: pulmonary edema = absolute BB contraindication in acute setting. PR >0.24, HR <50, SBP <90 are the other absolute contraindications.
Trap 02Vasospastic Angina — CCBs Are First-Line, Not BB
A 31-year-old woman with no CV risk factors has episodes of chest pain lasting 30 minutes, mainly at night and at rest. ECG during an episode shows ST elevation. Coronary angiography shows no stenosis but provocation test induces focal spasm. What is the most appropriate treatment?
Calcium channel blockers (CCBs)
This is vasospastic (Prinzmetal's) angina. CCBs are the mainstay — they prevent coronary smooth muscle spasm. Nitrates are second-line (limited by tolerance). Beta-blockers are NOT first-line and non-selective BB (propranolol) may worsen spasm.
⚠ Distractor: The young age + chest pain might trigger a reflex to give aspirin and beta-blockers. In vasospastic angina: (1) CCBs first-line, (2) aspirin may worsen spasm, (3) BB variable/not first-line. Board keyword: rest pain + ST elevation (transient) + young patient = vasospastic angina = CCBs.
Trap 03PCI vs CABG — When PCI is Acceptable for LAD Disease
In which of the following patients would you prefer PCI over CABG? (A) Left main disease. (B) 3-vessel disease + EF 30%. (C) T2DM with 3-vessel disease. (D) Proximal LAD disease with 2-vessel involvement.
Proximal LAD disease with 2-vessel involvement (Option D) — PCI is acceptable here, though CABG is often preferred. The absolute CABG indications are: left main, 3-vessel with reduced EF, T2DM with multivessel disease.
⚠ Distractor: Proximal LAD sounds very concerning (which it is), but it's NOT an absolute CABG indication like left main disease is. 2-vessel disease with proximal LAD ESPECIALLY with ↓EF or DM → lean toward CABG, but PCI remains an option in the absence of those factors. Board keyword: left main >50% → CABG; proximal LAD alone → PCI possible.
Trap 04Troponin Duration — 7–10 Days, Not 48–72h
A patient had an MI. How long after the event can troponin levels remain elevated with normal renal function?
7–10 days
Cardiac troponin (cTnI and cTnT) remains elevated for 7–10 days post-MI, far longer than CK-MB (which returns to normal in 48–72h). This makes CK-MB more useful for detecting re-infarction — a new rise after initial decline is detectable because CK-MB falls faster.
⚠ Distractor: Students confuse troponin with CK-MB duration. Board keyword: Troponin = 7–10 days; CK-MB = 48–72h → CK-MB is used to detect reinfarction.
Trap 05Acute Decompensated HF — Beta-Blockers Require Caution
A 70-year-old man with EF 50% presents to the ED with pulmonary edema, BP 120/80, HR 104, SpO₂ 88%. B-lines on POCUS, global LV hypokinesis on echo. Which drug is MOST likely to worsen his condition?
Beta-blockers
In acute decompensated heart failure, beta-blockers can worsen cardiac output by removing the compensatory tachycardia and reducing contractility. They are contraindicated in the acute phase. Treatment priorities: diuresis (furosemide IV), non-invasive ventilation (CPAP/BiPAP), and vasodilators (nitrates if not hypotensive).
⚠ Distractor: BB are standard long-term therapy in HFrEF. But in acute decompensated HF, they should be held or reduced. Board keyword: acute decompensated HF = hold/avoid BB acutely. Restart carefully at very low doses once compensation achieved.
Trap 06Nitrates Have No Indication in Silent Ischemia Without Symptoms
Which treatment has NO indication in patients with silent ischemia (asymptomatic ischemia) who have known coronary artery disease?
Nitrates
Nitrates are purely SYMPTOMATIC agents — they relieve anginal pain and ischemic discomfort. They have no proven benefit for asymptomatic (silent) ischemia in terms of reducing events or improving prognosis. Beta-blockers, aspirin, and statins all have evidence in asymptomatic CAD. Nitrates do not.
⚠ Distractor: Students associate nitrates with ischemia generally. But nitrates address symptoms, not pathophysiology. Board keyword: nitrates = symptomatic only; no indication for silent ischemia.
Trap 07Return to Sexual/Physical Activity After Uncomplicated STEMI
A 55-year-old man had an uncomplicated STEMI treated with PCI. He was discharged in good condition. He asks how soon he can return to mild physical activity. What is the answer?
After 1 week (approximately 7 days)
For uncomplicated STEMI: hospitalization is 3–5 days. In the first 1–2 weeks at home, light activity and normal sexual activity may resume. After 2 weeks, activity is regulated by exercise tolerance. Most patients return to work within 2–4 weeks.
⚠ Distractor: Students often choose 6 weeks (like post-cardiac surgery) or 1 month. The correct timeline for uncomplicated STEMI is much earlier. Board keyword: uncomplicated STEMI: mild activity after 1 week; work after 2–4 weeks.
Trap 08Increased Lung Uptake on Nuclear Stress = Poor Prognosis
Which finding on nuclear myocardial perfusion imaging indicates the WORST prognosis?
Increased pulmonary (lung) uptake of the tracer
Increased lung uptake on nuclear stress imaging indicates significantly elevated LV filling pressures (pulmonary congestion) during stress — reflecting severely impaired LV function during exercise. This is one of the most powerful indicators of poor prognosis on nuclear imaging. It is associated with large ischemic territory and compromised LV reserve.
⚠ Distractor: Students may choose multiple perfusion defects (also a bad sign) but increased lung uptake specifically signals the highest-risk patients requiring urgent intervention. Board keyword: ↑ lung uptake on nuclear scan = severe LV dysfunction during stress = very poor prognosis.
Trap 09Drug-Eluting Stent — DAPT Protocol
What is the most appropriate antiplatelet therapy after implantation of a Drug-Eluting Stent (DES)?
Aspirin + Prasugrel (or clopidogrel/ticagrelor) for 12 months
DES requires DAPT (dual antiplatelet therapy) for at least 12 months to prevent in-stent thrombosis, which is the main risk with drug-eluting stents due to delayed endothelialization. The drug coating (sirolimus, paclitaxel) reduces restenosis (<5%) but extends the period of bare metal exposed to blood.
⚠ Distractor: Aspirin alone or warfarin alone are not sufficient. Fondaparinux alone is never used post-stent. Board keyword: DES = DAPT (aspirin + P2Y12) for 12 months standard; fondaparinux requires supplemental UFH if used at time of PCI.
Trap 10CABG: Saphenous Vein Graft Occlusion Rates
What is the most accurate statement regarding CABG outcomes?
Saphenous vein graft (SVG) has a risk of occlusion of ~2%/year beginning in year 2 (the "second year" transition)
SVG patency rates: 10–20% occlusion in the first postoperative year. Then ~2%/year at years 5–7 (stable phase). Then ~4%/year after year 7 (late acceleration). The IMA graft has far superior long-term patency.
⚠ Distractor: The question uses "second year" rates. The key numbers: first year = 10–20%; years 5–7 = 2%/year. The SVG eventually fails due to accelerated atherosclerosis. IMA patency is significantly better. Board keyword: SVG: 10–20% fail first year; then 2%/yr for several years.
Trap 11ETT: 98% PPV in Symptomatic Man >50 — Bayes' Theorem
What does a positive ETT mean in a symptomatic man over 50 with typical chest pain?
~98% probability that obstructive CAD is present
Pre-test probability drives post-test probability (Bayes' theorem). A symptomatic man >50 with typical angina has HIGH pre-test probability. When the ETT is positive, the probability of obstructive CAD approaches 98%. The test mainly helps risk-stratify and plan management, not diagnose (which is almost certain from clinical features alone).
⚠ Distractor: Students think ETT alone "diagnoses" CAD. In reality, a positive ETT in a high-pretest-probability patient adds to confirmation, but the probability was already very high. Board keyword: positive ETT in man >50 with typical angina = ~98% CAD probability.
Trap 12Atrial Flutter During ETT ≠ Positive Test for Ischemia
A patient develops atrial flutter during exercise treadmill testing. Does this make the test positive for ischemia?
NO — atrial flutter/fibrillation during ETT is NOT a criterion for a positive ischemia test
A positive ETT requires ischemic ST changes (≥1 mm horizontal or downsloping ST depression) or hemodynamic compromise. Arrhythmias during exercise (including AF, flutter, SVT) may be clinically significant but do NOT constitute a positive ischemia test by ECG criteria.
⚠ Distractor: Any abnormality during exercise testing seems like a positive result. The specific definition requires horizontal/downsloping ST depression ≥0.1 mV. Upsloping ST is also NOT positive. Board keyword: ETT positive criteria = horizontal or downsloping ST ≥1 mm; NOT arrhythmias alone.

Trap 16Inferior STEMI + Bradycardia = Adenosine (Not Atropine-Resistant AV Block)
A 69-year-old man presents with acute MI and wide-complex bradycardia. Which statement best explains why LAD occlusion is less likely to cause this picture?
LAD occlusion causes an anterior STEMI and infranodal block — which manifests as wide-complex bradycardia that does NOT respond to atropine and requires pacing. In contrast, inferior STEMI (RCA occlusion) releases adenosine from ischemic myocardium, causing AV NODE-level block (narrow escape, responds to atropine, usually temporary).

The answer is that LAD (proximal) occlusion can cause wide complex bradycardia due to infranodal conduction system damage — which is mechanistically different from the adenosine-mediated AV block in inferior MI.

Board keyword: anterior STEMI + wide complex bradycardia = infranodal block = pacing needed, not atropine.
Trap 28NSTE-ACS Catheterization Timing — Conservative Approach for Low-Risk
A 66-year-old man with hypertension and diabetes presents with chest pain for 2 minutes, resolved on its own. ECG: no ST changes. Troponin: 0.1 mV elevation at presentation. What strategy is correct?
Early invasive catheterization within 24 hours (high-risk: confirmed NSTEMI = troponin elevated).
Even with brief pain that resolved, a positive troponin = NSTEMI = high risk → early invasive (<24h). If troponin were negative and symptoms resolved, selective conservative approach would be appropriate with further risk stratification.
⚠ Distractor: Pain resolved → conservative management/discharge. Wrong — troponin is elevated. This is NSTEMI, not just stable angina. Board keyword: troponin+ = NSTEMI = early invasive <24h regardless of symptom resolution.
Trap 29Acute Decompensated HF + Ischemia — Treat the HF FIRST with LNBB
A 70-year-old man with known EF 50%, treated with furosemide and amlodipine, presents with pulmonary edema and ischemia on ECG. POCUS shows B-lines and global hypokinesis. What is the BEST immediate drug to improve the hemodynamic picture?
Beta-blockers (specifically initiated after stabilization) — but the ACUTE treatment is: IV furosemide, IV nitrates (if not hypotensive), and CPAP/BiPAP. Beta-blockers are indicated chronically but contraindicated acutely in decompensated HF. Once compensation is achieved, beta-blockers are added at very low doses.

If the question asks what drug is MOST CONTRAINDICATED in acute decompensated HF with ischemia → beta-blockers.
⚠ This trap combines two concepts: (1) BB contraindicated acutely in decompensated HF, and (2) BB are beneficial chronically in HFrEF + ischemia. Board keyword: acute pulmonary edema = BB contraindicated NOW; treat with diuretics + nitrates + CPAP.
