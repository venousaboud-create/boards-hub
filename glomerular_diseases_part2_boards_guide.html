Minimal Change Disease (MCD)

Epidemiology

  • Nephrotic syndrome in children: MCD accounts for 70–90% of cases — the default diagnosis until proven otherwise
  • Nephrotic syndrome in adults: MCD accounts for only 10–15% — membranous GN and FSGS are more common in adults
  • Peak in children 2–6 years of age; males > females in children; equal sex distribution in adults
  • Second peak in older adults — often secondary

Pathogenesis

  • Thought to be caused by a circulating T cell–derived permeability factor → damages podocyte slit-pore membranes → foot process effacement → massive proteinuria
  • No identifiable antigen-antibody complex; glomeruli look completely normal on LM and IF
  • Secondary causes (MUST KNOW):
    • NSAIDs (most common drug cause in adults)
    • Hodgkin's lymphoma (classic paraneoplastic MCD — lymphokine production by Reed-Sternberg cells)
    • Non-Hodgkin's lymphoma (less classic)
    • Lithium, penicillamine, mercury, gold
    • Bee sting, poison ivy, vaccinations

Biopsy Findings

Light Microscopy

Normal — no hypercellularity, no immune deposits, no sclerosis visible. "Minimal change" name comes from here.

Immunofluorescence

Negative — no immune deposits. If IgM staining → may suggest FSGS rather than pure MCD.

Electron Microscopy

Diffuse podocyte foot process effacement — the ONLY abnormality. Foot processes are retracted and replaced by a flat continuous cytoplasmic layer. This is the diagnostic key.

Clinical Presentation

  • Abrupt onset of massive proteinuria (often >10 g/24hr) with classic nephrotic triad: hypoalbuminemia, edema, hyperlipidemia
  • Edema is often severe — periorbital edema (classic in children, especially in the morning), ascites, pleural effusions
  • Hematuria: rare — distinguishes from nephritic syndromes
  • Selective proteinuria (albumin >> globulins) — reflects intact size barrier; only charge barrier impaired
  • Complement: normal — no complement activation
  • HTN: variable; GFR usually preserved initially
  • Hypercholesterolemia + lipiduria (oval fat bodies, "Maltese cross" under polarized light) — result of ↓oncotic pressure → liver upregulates lipoprotein synthesis
Complications
Thrombotic events (venous > arterial) from hypoalbuminemia → ↓antithrombin III, ↓protein C and S. Infection (pneumococcal peritonitis, cellulitis) from loss of immunoglobulins in urine and immunosuppression. Steroid side effects with long-term treatment.

Treatment

  • High-dose prednisone = first-line. Response rate:
    • Children: 90–95% achieve complete remission within 8 weeks
    • Adults: 75–85% respond but more slowly (may take 16 weeks)
  • Frequent relapsers / steroid dependent: cyclophosphamide, mycophenolate mofetil, calcineurin inhibitors (cyclosporine, tacrolimus), or rituximab (CD20 B-cell depletion — increasingly preferred to avoid cytotoxic agents)
  • Supportive: ACEi/ARB for proteinuria reduction, salt restriction, loop diuretics for edema

Prognosis

  • Excellent — rarely progresses to ESKD
  • Majority achieve complete remission; up to 70% relapse at least once; steroid-dependent course in 30–40%
  • Adults have higher steroid toxicity burden and slower response → treatment-related morbidity is main concern

Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (FSGS)

Boards Importance
FSGS = #1 cause of primary GN leading to ESKD in the United States. Disproportionately affects African Americans (APOL1 risk alleles). One of the most clinically important nephrotic diseases on the boards.

Epidemiology

  • Male predominance; any age; peak incidence in 3rd–4th decade for primary FSGS
  • APOL1 high-risk genotypes (G1/G2 alleles): account for the majority of FSGS in African Americans and for much of the excess risk of kidney disease in this population
  • Rapidly increasing incidence in the USA — now the leading cause of primary nephrotic syndrome in adults

Pathogenesis — Podocyte Injury Model

  • Primary FSGS: circulating permeability factor (identity debated; some evidence for suPAR, anti-CD40 antibodies, cardiolipin-like factor) → podocyte injury → foot process effacement throughout ALL glomeruli → eventual segmental collapse and sclerosis
  • Secondary FSGS: podocyte stress from hyperfiltration, reduced nephron mass, or direct toxin → segmental sclerosis in some glomeruli (especially at corticomedullary junction where tubular oxygen demand is highest)
  • Podocytes are terminally differentiated — cannot regenerate after loss → scarring is permanent

Primary vs. Secondary FSGS — Critical Distinction

Primary FSGS

Circulating permeability factor. EM: foot process effacement throughout ALL glomeruli (diffuse, even in non-sclerotic segments). Rapid onset of heavy proteinuria. Recurs in transplant (30–40%).

Secondary FSGS

Hyperfiltration, toxin, infection, reduced nephron mass. EM: foot process effacement ONLY in sclerotic segments (focal). Slower onset, less proteinuria. Does NOT recur in transplant.

Secondary Causes of FSGS

  • HIV (HIVAN — collapsing variant; APOL1 amplifies risk)
  • Heroin nephropathy (perihilar or NOS)
  • Obesity / hyperfiltration (perihilar; adaptive; occurs even in morbid obesity without DM)
  • Reflux nephropathy, unilateral renal agenesis, surgical nephrectomy → reduced nephron mass → hyperfiltration
  • Sickle cell disease (collapsing or NOS)
  • Pamidronate (bisphosphonate) → collapsing FSGS
  • Fabry's disease → podocyte Gb3 accumulation → NOS pattern
  • Familial/genetic: COL4A mutations, APOL1 risk alleles

Clinical Presentation

  • Nephrotic-range proteinuria (often >5 g/24hr in primary); may be subnephrotic in secondary
  • Hypoalbuminemia, edema, hyperlipidemia — typical nephrotic syndrome features
  • Microscopic hematuria in ~50%
  • HTN common; GFR often ↓ at presentation (especially collapsing)
  • Often detected incidentally on routine urinalysis

Treatment

  • High-dose corticosteroids (prednisone 1 mg/kg/day) for 4–6 months — only 25–45% of adults achieve complete remission
  • Steroid-resistant primary FSGS: calcineurin inhibitors (cyclosporine, tacrolimus), MMF, rituximab
  • Sparsentan (dual ETA/AT1 receptor antagonist): FDA approved for primary FSGS — reduces proteinuria, slows GFR decline
  • Secondary FSGS: treat underlying cause (antiretrovirals for HIVAN, weight loss for obesity-related); ACEi/ARB for proteinuria
  • SGLT2 inhibitors: emerging nephroprotective role in FSGS
  • Recurrence post-transplant (primary FSGS): plasmapheresis; rituximab

Prognosis

  • Without treatment: 50% reach ESKD within 10–20 years
  • Proteinuria >3 g/24hr, HTN, ↑ creatinine at presentation = poor prognosis
  • Complete remission (proteinuria <300 mg/24hr) → excellent long-term outcomes
  • Collapsing variant → often ESKD within 1–3 years

Membranous Glomerulonephritis (Membranous Nephropathy)

Epidemiology Pearl
Most common cause of primary nephrotic syndrome in white adults over age 50. 80% = primary (PLA2R-associated). 20% = secondary. Highest thrombotic risk of all nephrotic syndromes.

Pathogenesis — In Situ Immune Complex Formation

  • Primary (80%): Circulating IgG4 autoantibodies against PLA2R (phospholipase A2 receptor, ~70–80%) or THSD7A (~5%) on podocytes → in situ immune complex formation on subepithelial surface of GBM
  • Also: NELL-1, Sema3B, EXT1/EXT2, NCAM1 (new antigens in non-PLA2R primary MGN)
  • Mechanism: antigen expressed on podocyte foot processes → IgG antibody binds → subepithelial immune complex → complement activation (C5b-9 membrane attack complex) → podocyte injury → proteinuria
  • WHY subepithelial? Podocytes face the outer GBM surface; their antigens are on the subepithelial side
  • Secondary (20%) causes — MUST KNOW ALL:
    • Malignancy (paraneoplastic): solid tumors — colon, lung, breast, prostate; associated antigen = NELL-1 (colorectal), Sema3B (pediatric)
    • Hepatitis B (HBsAg and anti-HBe in immune complexes)
    • Lupus (Class V lupus nephritis)
    • Drugs: gold, penicillamine, NSAIDs, captopril, mercury
    • Syphilis, malaria
    • Autoimmune: thyroiditis, rheumatoid arthritis, Sjögren's

Biopsy Findings

Light Microscopy

Diffuse thickening of GBM. "Spikes" on Jones silver stain = new GBM material projecting up between subepithelial deposits (encasing them). No significant hypercellularity.

Immunofluorescence

Diffuse granular IgG along GBM. Also C3, C4, C1q in secondary MGN. PLA2R staining: positive in primary MGN.

Electron Microscopy (Stages)

Stage I: small subepithelial deposits. Stage II: GBM spikes between deposits. Stage III: deposits incorporated into GBM. Stage IV: deposits resorb → lucent areas in GBM ("moth-eaten" appearance). Foot process effacement.

PLA2R Antibody — Clinical Use

  • PLA2R antibody titer correlates with disease activity: high titer = active disease
  • Falling titers precede clinical remission (often by months) → early predictor of treatment response
  • PLA2R positive biopsy AND negative serum antibody = early remission OR inactive burnt-out disease
  • Pre-transplant PLA2R positivity → risk of disease recurrence in the allograft
  • PLA2R NEGATIVE primary MGN → consider biopsy staining for THSD7A, NELL-1, Sema3B; and rule out secondary causes aggressively

Clinical Presentation

  • Heavy proteinuria — median ~8 g/24hr; often insidious onset
  • Microscopic hematuria in ~50%
  • HTN in ~50%
  • Complement levels: normal in primary MGN (C5b-9 formed locally at GBM, does not lower serum complement levels)
  • Edema typically less severe than MCD
Thrombosis Risk — Highest of All Nephrotic Syndromes
Membranous GN has the highest risk of DVT/PE of all nephrotic syndromes. Also: renal vein thrombosis (classic association). Mechanism: heavy proteinuria → massive urinary loss of antithrombin III, protein C, protein S → hypercoagulable state. Consider prophylactic anticoagulation when albumin <2.0 g/dL.

The Rule of Thirds

1/3 Spontaneous Remission

Complete or partial remission without treatment, typically within 2–5 years. Basis for "watchful waiting" in mild/moderate disease.

1/3 Stable, Non-Progressive

Persistent proteinuria but stable kidney function. May have chronic nephrotic complications but do not progress to ESKD over decades.

1/3 Progression to ESKD

Predictors: male sex, older age, persistent heavy proteinuria >8 g/24hr, HTN, ↑ creatinine, high PLA2R titer. These patients need aggressive treatment.

Treatment

  • Watchful waiting 6–12 months first in stable patients — to assess for spontaneous remission
  • ACEi/ARB: reduce proteinuria in all patients regardless of immunosuppression
  • Rituximab (anti-CD20): now considered first-line immunosuppression for primary MGN — targets B cells producing anti-PLA2R; superior safety vs. alkylating agents
  • Ponticelli regimen (alternating monthly cyclophosphamide + methylprednisolone × 6 months): traditional first-line, still used
  • Calcineurin inhibitors (cyclosporine, tacrolimus): alternative, but high relapse rate when stopped
  • Obinutuzumab, belimumab: emerging agents targeting B cells
  • Secondary MGN: treat underlying cause (antivirals for HBV, stop offending drug)

Diabetic Nephropathy

Epidemiology
#1 cause of CKD and ESKD worldwide. Complicates both Type 1 and Type 2 DM. Affects ~30–40% of all diabetics over their lifetime. Prevention and early detection are the most impactful interventions.

Pathogenesis

  • Hyperglycemia → advanced glycosylation end products (AGEs) + reactive oxygen species (ROS) + activation of PKC, TGF-β, RAAS → GBM thickening + mesangial expansion + podocyte injury
  • Glomerular hyperfiltration (early): diabetic kidneys initially hyperperfuse → large, hyperfunctioning kidneys → elevated GFR → damage over time
  • Proteinuria → amplifies tubular injury → fibrosis (same downstream mechanism as all GN)

Biopsy Findings

  • Light microscopy:
    • Diffuse mesangial expansion + GBM thickening
    • Kimmelstiel-Wilson (K-W) nodules — ovoid/ellipsoidal PAS-positive acellular nodules in the peripheral mesangium; pathognomonic of diabetic nodular glomerulosclerosis
    • Exudative lesions: "fibrin caps" (PAS-positive homogeneous material in capillary loops) and "capsular drops" (material between Bowman's capsule and parietal epithelium)
    • Afferent AND efferent arteriolar hyalinosis (efferent arteriolar hyalinosis = specific for DM; not seen in other diseases)
  • IF: Non-specific; linear IgG, albumin, fibrinogen along GBM (no immune complex pattern)
  • EM: GBM thickening (>450nm women, >430nm men); diffuse foot process effacement

When to Biopsy a Diabetic Patient

Clinical Rule
Diabetic nephropathy is usually diagnosed clinically (DM + microalbuminuria + retinopathy = sufficient). Biopsy when: (1) nephrotic syndrome without retinopathy, (2) gross hematuria, (3) RBC casts, (4) rapid loss of kidney function, (5) short duration of DM (<5 years Type 1 DM with nephrotic syndrome). These suggest a coexisting non-diabetic GN.
Board Trap
Hematuria in a diabetic patient = investigate for another cause. Diabetic nephropathy alone does NOT cause gross hematuria and only rarely causes significant microscopic hematuria. If hematuria is prominent → think IgA nephropathy, lupus, ANCA, or bladder malignancy.

Treatment — Evidence-Based Hierarchy

  • Glycemic control (HbA1c <7.0%): prevents/delays microalbuminuria; slows progression at early stages
  • Blood pressure control (<130/80 mmHg): ACEi or ARB = first-line (even without HTN if microalbuminuria present); prevent progression
  • SGLT2 inhibitors (dapagliflozin, empagliflozin, canagliflozin): independently nephroprotective; reduce adverse kidney outcomes by 30–40% regardless of glycemic effect; now recommended for all DKD regardless of HbA1c
  • Finerenone (non-steroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist): reduces proteinuria + CKD progression in DKD
  • GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide, liraglutide): reduce proteinuria and slow kidney decline; cardiovascular + renal benefits
  • Protein restriction: modest benefit; limit to 0.8 g/kg/day

Glomerular Deposition Diseases

Abnormal proteins deposit in the glomerulus → structural damage. Key diseases: AL amyloidosis, LCDD/HCDD, MGRS, fibrillary GN, immunotactoid GN. The unifying theme: always look for an underlying plasma cell or B-cell clone.

AL Amyloidosis (Light Chain Amyloid)

  • Monoclonal light chains (usually lambda > kappa) misfolded → amyloid fibrils → deposit in mesangium, GBM, arterioles, tubular basement membranes
  • Associated with: multiple myeloma, MGUS, Waldenström's macroglobulinemia

Biopsy

  • LM: pale, acellular amorphous deposits expanding the mesangium
  • Congo red stain → apple-green birefringence under polarized light = diagnostic for amyloid
  • IF: monoclonal lambda (or kappa) staining; also positive for SAP (serum amyloid P component)
  • EM: 8–12nm randomly arranged, non-branching, fibrillary deposits — the defining ultrastructure of amyloid

Clinical

  • Heavy proteinuria / nephrotic syndrome is the most common kidney manifestation
  • Multi-organ involvement: cardiac amyloidosis (most important; restrictive cardiomyopathy, arrhythmias), peripheral neuropathy (glove-and-stocking), autonomic dysfunction, macroglossia (tongue enlargement — pathognomonic), periorbital purpura ("raccoon eyes"), hepatomegaly, "shoulder pad sign" (amyloid in shoulder joint)
  • Low voltage on EKG + thick-walled heart on echo = cardiac amyloid

Treatment

  • Daratumumab + bortezomib + cyclophosphamide + dexamethasone (Dara-CyBorD): current standard first-line for AL amyloidosis
  • Autologous stem cell transplant (ASCT): for eligible patients (<70 years, good performance status, limited cardiac involvement)
  • Tafamidis, patisiran (TTR amyloid-specific, NOT AL): do not confuse with AL amyloidosis treatment

AA Amyloidosis (Secondary Amyloid)

  • Serum amyloid A protein (SAP) accumulates → amyloid fibrils
  • Caused by chronic inflammatory diseases: RA, IBD, FMF, osteomyelitis, bronchiectasis
  • Congo red positive; staining for SAA protein positive; NO monoclonal Ig
  • Treatment: control the underlying inflammatory disease

LCDD (Light Chain Deposition Disease)

  • Monoclonal light chains (usually kappa > lambda — opposite of AL amyloid) deposit as non-fibrillar material in glomeruli, tubules, and arterioles
  • Associated with multiple myeloma, MGUS, lymphoma
  • LM: nodular glomerulosclerosis — KW-like nodules (mimics diabetic nephropathy!)
  • IF: linear kappa light chain along GBM AND tubular basement membranes (unlike membranous GN which is granular IgG)
  • EM: granular, powdery, non-fibrillar deposits (contrast with amyloid fibrils)
  • Clinical: proteinuria, hematuria, progressive kidney failure, often with chronic renal failure
  • Treatment: treat the underlying plasma cell disorder (bortezomib-based regimen ± ASCT)
LCDD vs. Amyloid — Key Distinctions
Both produce nodular glomerulosclerosis and are monoclonal. LCDD: kappa, Congo red NEGATIVE, non-fibrillar on EM. AL amyloid: lambda, Congo red POSITIVE, 8–12nm fibrils on EM.

MGRS — Monoclonal Gammopathy of Renal Significance

  • Kidney damage from monoclonal Ig produced by a small B-cell or plasma cell clone — not yet meeting diagnostic criteria for overt myeloma, lymphoma, or CLL
  • The clone is "too small" for hematology treatment but "too damaging" for nephrology to ignore → must treat the clone to save the kidney
  • Spectrum of kidney manifestations: AL amyloid, LCDD, HCDD, fibrillary GN, immunotactoid GP, cryoglobulinemic GN, MPGN, C3 glomerulopathy (when driven by monoclonal complement inhibitor)
  • Diagnosis requires: paraprotein in serum/urine (SPEP, UPEP, serum free light chains) + biopsy showing monoclonal protein deposits + kidney injury
  • Treatment: clone-directed therapy (bortezomib, daratumumab, rituximab, ASCT depending on clone type) even though clone is small

Fibrillary GN & Immunotactoid GN

Fibrillary GN

12–22nm fibrils composed of polyclonal IgG. Congo red NEGATIVE. DNAJB9 on IF = pathognomonic. Associated with HCV, autoimmune disease, monoclonal gammopathy. Clinical: nephrotic range proteinuria, hematuria, progressive CKD. Treatment: based on underlying cause; rituximab for idiopathic.

Immunotactoid GN

Large organized microtubular structures (30–50nm) — much larger than amyloid (8–12nm) or fibrillary GN (12–22nm). Congo red NEGATIVE. Strongly associated with B-cell lymphoproliferative disorders, CLL, cryoglobulinemia. Treatment: treat underlying lymphoproliferative disorder.

EM Diameter Memory Aid
Amyloid = 8–12nm (smallest) → Fibrillary GN = 12–22nm (medium) → Immunotactoid GN = 30–50nm (largest, microtubular). All Congo red negative except amyloid.

Fabry's Disease

Overview

  • X-linked lysosomal storage disease caused by deficiency of α-galactosidase A (GLA)
  • Deficiency → accumulation of globotriaosylceramide (Gb3) in lysosomes of podocytes, endothelial cells, tubular cells, smooth muscle cells, neurons
  • Affected males: full syndrome. Carrier females: variable (can have significant kidney/cardiac disease)

Kidney Manifestations

  • Early: proteinuria (often subnephrotic), microscopic hematuria, concentrating defect
  • Progressive FSGS → CKD → ESKD in males in 3rd–4th decade
  • Kidney is the leading cause of death in untreated males

Biopsy Findings

  • LM: vacuolization of podocytes and tubular cells; eventual FSGS pattern
  • IF: non-specific
  • EM: Myelin figures / "zebra bodies" — lamellated, concentric membrane-bound inclusions in podocytes (from Gb3 accumulation). Pathognomonic. Also present in endothelial cells and tubular cells
  • Oil Red O: lipid in tubular cells

Systemic Manifestations

Early Onset (Childhood/Adolescence)

Neuropathic pain (burning in hands/feet — acroparesthesias); episodes triggered by heat, exercise, fever. Anhidrosis (decreased sweating → heat intolerance). Angiokeratomas (small dark-red skin lesions over trunk/genitalia).

Later Onset

Cornea verticillata (whorled corneal opacity on slit lamp — does NOT impair vision). Cardiac: LVH, arrhythmias, valve abnormalities. Stroke (young patients, white matter lesions). ESKD.

Diagnosis & Treatment

  • Diagnosis: Plasma α-galactosidase A activity (low in males; may be normal in females → gene sequencing needed); plasma lyso-Gb3 elevated; urine Gb3 elevated
  • Enzyme replacement therapy (ERT): agalsidase alfa or agalsidase beta (IV infusion every 2 weeks) — slows kidney/cardiac progression, reduces pain
  • Migalastat (oral pharmacological chaperone): only for patients with amenable (responsive) GLA mutations — stabilizes misfolded enzyme
  • ACEi/ARB for proteinuria; dialysis/transplant for ESKD (ERT should continue post-transplant)

Pulmonary-Renal Syndrome

Emergency
Pulmonary-renal syndrome = RPGN + diffuse alveolar hemorrhage. One of the most dangerous presentations in nephrology — high mortality without immediate treatment. Requires urgent serologies, urgent biopsy, and often empirical treatment before results are back.

Causes — Three Must-Know Categories

Anti-GBM Disease (Goodpasture's)

Linear IgG on IF. Anti-α3 NC1 antibodies. Target = alveolar + glomerular BM. Predominantly young men. Plasmapheresis + CYC + steroids.

ANCA Vasculitis

Pauci-immune on IF. Anti-PR3 (GPA) or Anti-MPO (MPA). Alveolar capillaritis. Steroids + rituximab/CYC ± plasmapheresis.

Lupus (SLE)

Granular IgG + C3 on IF. Anti-dsDNA, ↓complement. Diffuse alveolar hemorrhage + Class III/IV nephritis. Pulse steroids + CYC or rituximab.

Less Common Causes

  • Cryoglobulinemic vasculitis (usually HCV-related)
  • IgA vasculitis (Henoch-Schönlein purpura — rare pulmonary involvement)
  • Antiphospholipid syndrome (TMA + pulmonary capillaritis)
  • Rheumatoid vasculitis
  • Drug-induced ANCA vasculitis (hydralazine, propylthiouracil, minocycline → MPO-ANCA)

Approach to the Patient

  • Urgent serologies STAT: anti-GBM (α3 NC1), ANCA (PR3 + MPO), ANA, anti-dsDNA, complement C3/C4, cryoglobulins
  • Urgent kidney biopsy (when coagulation allows): IF pattern is the pivotal diagnostic step
  • Bronchoalveolar lavage: hemosiderin-laden macrophages confirm alveolar hemorrhage
  • Empirical treatment: pulse methylprednisolone + plasmapheresis before diagnosis confirmed if rapidly deteriorating
  • ICU admission often needed; mechanical ventilation for severe hemorrhage; dialysis for AKI

Hereditary Basement Membrane Diseases

Alport's Syndrome

  • Mutations in type IV collagen chains → structurally abnormal GBM → progressive nephritis
  • Inheritance patterns:
    • X-linked (85%): mutations in COL4A5 on X chromosome → absent α5 chain → destabilized α345 hexamer in GBM, alveoli, cochlea, lens
    • Autosomal recessive (10%): mutations in COL4A3 or COL4A4 → absent α3 or α4
    • Autosomal dominant: heterozygous COL4A3/A4 — generally milder

Biopsy Findings

  • LM: variable, often non-specific; glomerulosclerosis, tubular atrophy with progression
  • IF: absent α5 staining on GBM in X-linked males; patchwork pattern in carrier females
  • EM: GBM splitting, irregular thickening and thinning, "basketweave" lamination of lamina densa — pathognomonic; alternating dense/lucent lamellae

Clinical Features

  • Kidney: persistent microscopic hematuria from birth (often first clue); then progressive proteinuria; ESKD in males in 20s–40s (X-linked); carrier females can progress
  • Sensorineural hearing loss: predominantly high-frequency; often before ESKD; bilateral
  • Anterior lenticonus: protrusion of lens nucleus through anterior capsule → myopia, oil droplet appearance on ophthalmoscopy
  • Dot-and-fleck retinopathy: macular flecks; usually asymptomatic
  • Anti-GBM post-transplant: X-linked Alport males receive normal kidney with "foreign" α345 collagen IV → ~50% develop anti-GBM antibodies → only 3–5% develop overt Goodpasture's; most have no clinical disease; transplant outcomes generally good

Treatment

  • ACEi (gold standard): slows progression; best when started early (even before proteinuria)
  • Losartan ARB: also slows progression
  • SGLT2 inhibitors: emerging role in Alport's
  • No cure yet; transplant for ESKD

Thin Basement Membrane Disease (TBMD) / Benign Familial Hematuria

  • Most common cause of persistent isolated microscopic hematuria in young adults
  • Heterozygous mutations in COL4A3 or COL4A4 = carrier state of autosomal recessive Alport's
  • LM and IF: normal
  • EM: diffusely thinned GBM (<250nm; normal = 300–350nm in adults)
  • Clinical: persistent microscopic hematuria, normal kidney function, no proteinuria initially
  • Generally benign prognosis — most do NOT progress to ESKD; some may develop mild proteinuria and CKD over decades
  • No specific treatment; ACEi if proteinuria develops; reassurance is key
  • Family members should be screened (may reveal Alport's carriers)

Nail-Patella Syndrome

  • Mutations in LMX1B (transcription factor regulating COL4A3/A4 expression) → autosomal dominant
  • Kidney disease in ~30%: proteinuria, hematuria, progressive CKD
  • EM: "moth-eaten" GBM with irregular areas of lucency + fibrillar collagen cross-fibers within lamina densa (pathognomonic)
  • Extrarenal findings: absent or hypoplastic patellae, nail dysplasia (triangular lunulae), iliac horns (bony outgrowths from posterior iliac crests — visible on X-ray; pathognomonic), elbow dysplasia (limited extension/supination)

Glomerular-Vascular Syndromes

Hypertensive Nephrosclerosis

  • #2 cause of ESKD in USA (after DM); disproportionately affects African Americans
  • APOL1 high-risk genotype: G1/G2 alleles dramatically increase risk of hypertensive ESKD in African Americans; explains why Black Americans have 3–4× higher rates of ESKD from HTN vs. white Americans despite similar levels of blood pressure control
  • Pathology: afferent arteriolar hyalinosis → ischemia of glomerulus → global glomerulosclerosis → nephron loss → interstitial fibrosis
  • Clinical: mild proteinuria (<1 g/day), slowly progressive CKD, HTN for many years
  • Treatment: aggressive BP control (<130/80); ACEi/ARB; in APOL1 high-risk patients: consider aggressive treatment even at "lower" BP levels
Malignant Hypertension (Separate Entity)
Rapid, extreme BP elevation → fibrinoid necrosis of arterioles + "onion-skin" hyperplastic arteriolitis → TMA pattern → AKI. Presents with AKI, MAHA, ↑ LDH, schistocytes, retinal hemorrhages/papilledema, encephalopathy. Emergency BP reduction + supportive care.

Cholesterol Emboli Nephropathy

  • Cholesterol crystals shed from aortic/renal artery atherosclerotic plaques → lodge in intrarenal arteries → downstream ischemia
  • Triggers: aortic surgery, cardiac catheterization, arteriography, anticoagulation (dislodges plaques)
  • LM: "biconvex clefts" or "needle-shaped spaces" where cholesterol crystals dissolved during processing → foreign body giant cell reaction
  • Clinical:
    • Subacute or chronic progressive kidney failure (over weeks–months, unlike contrast nephropathy which peaks at 48–72hr)
    • Eosinophilia (40%) + eosinophiluria
    • Livedo reticularis (net-like skin discoloration on extremities)
    • "Blue toe" syndrome (digital ischemia)
    • Hollenhorst plaques in retinal arteries (cholesterol crystals visible on funduscopy)
    • Low complement (activation); eosinophilia may mimic ANCA vasculitis
  • No specific treatment; discontinue anticoagulation if possible; statins may stabilize plaques; supportive care

Sickle Cell Disease Nephropathy

  • Renal medulla = most susceptible: low oxygen tension + high osmolarity + sluggish flow → sickling in vasa recta
  • Earliest manifestation: loss of urinary concentrating ability → hyposthenuria / isosthenuria (urine specific gravity fixed at ~1.010) → polyuria + nocturia in young patients
  • Gross hematuria: common; from medullary ischemia + papillary necrosis
  • FSGS: most common glomerular lesion — from hyperfiltration (remaining nephrons work harder after medullary loss) + APOL1 alleles in many African Americans
  • Heavy proteinuria (nephrotic syndrome) → progressive CKD → ESKD in adulthood
  • Treatment: hydroxyurea (HbF induction), ACEi/ARB for proteinuria, transplant for ESKD

Thrombotic Microangiopathy (TMA)

Microvascular injury → thrombi in arterioles and glomerular capillaries → ischemia.

TMA Lab Triad
Microangiopathic hemolytic anemia (MAHA) — schistocytes on blood smear; thrombocytopenia; elevated creatinine (kidney injury). Also: ↑ LDH, ↓ haptoglobin, negative Coombs test.

Infectious Disease–Associated GN

The most common cause of GN worldwide — far more common than immune-mediated GN in developing countries. Mechanisms: direct infection of glomerular cells, immune complex deposition of microbial antigens, or complement activation.

HIV-Associated Nephropathy (HIVAN)

  • Most common and clinically significant kidney disease in HIV
  • Pathology: collapsing FSGS — visceral podocyte proliferation + collapse of glomerular capillary tuft; tubuloreticular inclusions (TRI) on EM
  • IF: pauci-immune (no deposits) — unlike other immune complex diseases
  • Predominantly in Black Americans with APOL1 high-risk alleles — APOL1 G1/G2 dramatically amplifies HIV-mediated podocyte injury
  • Clinical: heavy proteinuria (often nephrotic), rapid progression to ESKD (weeks–months untreated), large echogenic kidneys on ultrasound
  • Treatment: antiretroviral therapy (ART) → dramatic improvement in kidney function; decline in HIVAN since HAART era
  • Also: HIV immune complex kidney disease (HIVICK) — mesangioproliferative or MPGN pattern with immune deposits; distinct from HIVAN

Hepatitis B Virus (HBV)

  • Classic pattern: Membranous GN (especially in children with chronic HBV) — HBsAg and anti-HBe in subepithelial immune complexes
  • Also: MPGN, diffuse proliferative GN, polyarteritis nodosa (medium vessel vasculitis — HBV-associated)
  • Children: often spontaneous remission with HBeAg seroconversion (30–60% remit spontaneously)
  • Adults: less spontaneous remission; need antiviral therapy
  • Treatment: antivirals (tenofovir disoproxil, entecavir) → suppress viral replication → improve GN. Avoid steroids/immunosuppression (may reactivate HBV)

Hepatitis C Virus (HCV) — Cryoglobulinemia

  • Classic HCV kidney disease: type II mixed cryoglobulinemia → cryoglobulinemic vasculitis → MPGN pattern
  • Pathogenesis: HCV drives B-cell clonal expansion → monoclonal IgM (with rheumatoid factor activity) + polyclonal IgG → cryoprecipitate at cold temperatures → deposit in small vessels → complement activation → vasculitis
  • Clinical triad of cryoglobulinemia: purpura (palpable, lower extremities), arthralgia, peripheral neuropathy → + kidney = MPGN pattern
  • Key lab: Low C4 (cryoglobulins preferentially activate classical pathway via IgM) with relatively preserved C3; positive rheumatoid factor; positive cryocrit; positive HCV RNA
  • Treatment: DAAs (direct-acting antivirals — sofosbuvir-based regimens) → eradicate HCV → dramatic improvement or resolution of cryoglobulinemia and GN
  • Active vasculitis with organ-threatening disease: rituximab + plasmapheresis while awaiting antiviral response

SARS-CoV-2 / COVID-19 Nephropathy

  • Most common kidney manifestation: AKI from tubular injury (COVID-19 ATN) — not GN
  • GN pattern: collapsing FSGS — similar mechanism to HIVAN; predominantly in Black patients with APOL1 G1/G2 risk alleles; direct viral infection of podocytes + APOL1-mediated podocyte toxicity
  • Also described: TMA (endotheliitis), minimal change disease (cytokine storm theory), IgA nephropathy flares, pauci-immune GN
  • Treatment: treat COVID-19 (antivirals); RAAS inhibition + supportive care for kidney manifestations

Exam Traps — Board Exam Coach

Trap 01MCD vs. FSGS — How to Distinguish on EM
A biopsy shows normal LM, no deposits on IF, and diffuse foot process effacement on EM in ALL glomeruli. Another biopsy shows segmental sclerosis on LM and foot process effacement ONLY in the sclerotic segment on EM. What are the diagnoses?
Patient 1 = MCD (primary). Diffuse foot process effacement throughout ALL glomeruli without segmental sclerosis = primary podocyte permeability factor disorder. Responds to steroids. Does NOT recur in transplant as its own entity. Patient 2 = Secondary FSGS. Foot process effacement limited to sclerotic segments = adaptive/secondary mechanism, not a circulating permeability factor. Does NOT recur in transplant. Primary FSGS: diffuse FPE throughout all segments (even non-sclerotic) + recurs in transplant in 30–40%.
Trap 02MCD Secondary Causes — Hodgkin's and NSAIDs
A 62-year-old with nephrotic syndrome has normal biopsy LM, negative IF, diffuse podocyte effacement on EM. They have been taking ibuprofen for arthritis for years. A 45-year-old develops similar nephrotic syndrome and is found to have mediastinal lymphadenopathy. What are the diagnoses?
Both = secondary MCD. Patient 1: NSAID-induced MCD — NSAIDs are the most common drug cause of MCD in adults; stop NSAID → proteinuria often resolves. Patient 2: Hodgkin's lymphoma–associated MCD — classic paraneoplastic cause; Reed-Sternberg cells produce lymphokines acting as the circulating permeability factor. Also: penicillamine, lithium, gold, mercury. Always screen adults with new MCD for occult malignancy and review medications thoroughly.
Trap 03FSGS Columbia Classification — Best vs. Worst Prognosis
Match these FSGS variants to their prognosis: (a) collapsing, (b) tip lesion, (c) perihilar. What disease is collapsing FSGS most classically associated with?
Tip lesion = BEST prognosis (steroid-responsive, rarely ESKD). Perihilar = Intermediate (secondary/adaptive — obesity, hyperfiltration). Collapsing = WORST prognosis (rapid ESKD in 1–3 years). Collapsing FSGS classic associations: HIV (HIVAN) — most classic; also COVID-19 (SARS-CoV-2), APOL1 high-risk genotype, pamidronate (bisphosphonate), interferon therapy. Remember: APOL1 G1/G2 alleles dramatically amplify collapsing FSGS risk in both HIV and COVID-19.
Trap 04Membranous GN — Normal Complement Is Part of the Diagnosis
A patient with heavy proteinuria and the "rule of thirds" prognosis has serum complement levels checked. C3 is normal; C4 is normal. What is the diagnosis? What if both C3 and C4 were low?
Normal C3 + normal C4 + heavy proteinuria + subepithelial deposits = primary membranous GN. WHY normal complement? C5b-9 forms locally at the GBM surface — the complement is consumed locally without lowering serum levels. If C4 and C3 were BOTH low → think lupus Class V nephritis (lupus membranous GN, which activates the classical pathway systemically). The complement pattern in MGN is one of the highest-yield board distinctions. Also: PLA2R testing distinguishes primary (positive) from secondary (negative) MGN.
Trap 05Membranous GN — The Highest DVT/PE Risk
Among all nephrotic syndromes, which has the highest risk of DVT and pulmonary embolism? Which specific thrombotic complication is classically associated with membranous GN?
Membranous GN has the highest thrombotic risk of all nephrotic syndromes. All nephrotic syndromes cause hypercoagulability (urinary loss of antithrombin III, protein C, protein S + increased hepatic synthesis of clotting factors + platelet hyperaggregability). But membranous GN is specifically associated with renal vein thrombosis — clinically: sudden flank pain + worsening proteinuria + hematuria + CT showing filling defect in renal vein. DVT and PE are also elevated. Anticoagulate prophylactically when albumin <2.0 g/dL or in patients with prior thrombosis.
Trap 06Diabetic Nephropathy — Hematuria = Look for Another Cause
A Type 2 diabetic with 15 years of poorly controlled DM has nephrotic syndrome, retinopathy, and gross hematuria. Does this presentation fit diabetic nephropathy? What should you do?
NO — gross hematuria does NOT fit diabetic nephropathy. Diabetic nephropathy causes proteinuria (slowly progressive) but does NOT cause gross hematuria and only rarely causes significant microscopic hematuria. Gross hematuria in a diabetic demands a different explanation: IgA nephropathy, lupus, ANCA vasculitis, anti-GBM disease, papillary necrosis, bladder malignancy. Biopsy indications in diabetics: nephrotic syndrome without retinopathy, gross hematuria, RBC casts, rapid GFR loss, or DM duration <5 years with nephrotic syndrome.
Trap 07AL vs. LCDD — Congo Red and the Fibril Rule
Two patients have nodular glomerulosclerosis on LM resembling Kimmelstiel-Wilson nodules. Patient A has Congo red positive staining with apple-green birefringence. Patient B has Congo red negative staining but linear kappa light chain on IF. What are the diagnoses?
Patient A = AL Amyloidosis: Congo red POSITIVE + 8–12nm fibrils on EM = amyloid; monoclonal lambda (usually) light chain. Patient B = LCDD (Light Chain Deposition Disease): Congo red NEGATIVE + granular non-fibrillar deposits on EM; monoclonal KAPPA (not lambda) staining linearly along GBM and TBM. Both produce nodular glomerulosclerosis mimicking diabetes. Key memory rule: amyloid = fibrillary + Congo red+; LCDD = granular + Congo red negative. Amyloid = lambda dominant; LCDD = kappa dominant.
Trap 08Fibrillary GN vs. Immunotactoid GN — EM Fibril Size
A biopsy shows non-amyloid fibrillary deposits on EM. How do you distinguish fibrillary GN from immunotactoid GN, and which is more associated with lymphoproliferative disease?
Both are Congo red NEGATIVE. Distinguish by EM fibril diameter: Fibrillary GN = 12–22nm randomly arranged fibrils; DNAJB9 staining on IF is pathognomonic. Associated with HCV, autoimmune disease, gammopathy. Immunotactoid GN = 30–50nm organized parallel microtubules (much larger, organized differently — true microtubules, not random fibrils). Immunotactoid is more strongly associated with lymphoproliferative disorders (CLL, B-cell lymphoma). Memory: Immunotactoid = I for Immunoglobulin organization + biggest fibrils. Fibrillary = DNAJB9. Both need treatment of underlying cause.
Trap 09HIVAN — Who Gets It and Why APOL1 Matters
Two HIV-positive patients — one White, one Black — are biopsied and both show collapsing FSGS. Why is HIVAN so much more common in African Americans, and what is the treatment?
HIVAN (collapsing FSGS from HIV) occurs in ALL races but is dramatically more common in Black Americans because of APOL1 G1/G2 risk alleles. APOL1 normally protects against African trypanosomiasis but in the kidney, the G1/G2 variants cause podocyte toxicity — HIV further amplifies this injury through direct podocyte infection and inflammatory cytokines. The APOL1 risk alleles explain why Black patients have 60–70% of all HIVAN cases despite not having proportionally more HIV. Treatment: immediate antiretroviral therapy (ART) — since the HAART era, HIVAN has dramatically decreased. Without ART, ESKD occurs in weeks–months. ACEi also used for proteinuria reduction.
Trap 10HCV Cryoglobulinemia — C4 Is LOW, Not C3
A patient with known HCV has purpura, arthralgias, and nephrotic syndrome with MPGN on biopsy. Labs show low C4, normal-low C3, elevated rheumatoid factor. What is the mechanism of the complement abnormality?
This is HCV-associated type II mixed cryoglobulinemia. The monoclonal IgM rheumatoid factor activates the classical complement pathway via C1q → C4 is consumed first and is disproportionately low relative to C3. This pattern (low C4 >> low C3) is characteristic of cryoglobulinemia and is the opposite of poststrep GN (low C3, normal C4 — alternative pathway). HCV complement rule: ↓↓C4 + ↓C3 = cryoglobulinemia → think HCV. Cryocrit positive. Treatment: DAAs (direct-acting antivirals) → cure HCV → resolve cryoglobulinemia.
Trap 11Alport's and Anti-GBM Post-Transplant — Reassurance, Not Contraindication
A 28-year-old male with Alport's syndrome (X-linked, COL4A5 mutation) reaches ESKD. His mother asks whether kidney transplantation is safe given the risk of anti-GBM disease in the transplanted kidney. What do you tell her?
Transplantation is safe and recommended. X-linked Alport males lack the normal α3–α4–α5 hexamer of type IV collagen in their native kidneys → when they receive a normal kidney, they encounter "foreign" α345 collagen IV epitopes they were never tolerant to → ~50% develop anti-GBM antibodies post-transplant. However, only 3–5% develop overt Goodpasture's syndrome with clinical anti-GBM GN; most have antibodies without clinical disease. Allograft survival in Alport's is comparable to or better than other causes of ESKD. Living related donation: evaluate donors for TBMD or Alport's carriers (COL4A3/A4 mutations) before using as donors.
Trap 12TMA Differentiation — The ADAMTS13 Key
A patient has MAHA (schistocytes), thrombocytopenia, AKI, and neurologic symptoms. ADAMTS13 activity returns at 5%. Another similar patient has ADAMTS13 of 60% and a history of bloody diarrhea from a cookout. How do you manage each?
Patient 1 = TTP: ADAMTS13 <10% = diagnostic. Treatment: plasma exchange (PLEX) immediately (replaces ADAMTS13 + removes antibodies) + caplacizumab (anti-vWF) + if autoimmune (antibody-mediated): rituximab + steroids. Patient 2 = Shiga toxin–HUS (typical HUS): Bloody diarrhea + cookout = E. coli O157:H7; ADAMTS13 normal. Treatment: supportive only (fluids, dialysis). NO antibiotics (increase toxin release) and NO plasma exchange. aHUS: ADAMTS13 normal + no Shiga toxin + no obvious cause → eculizumab.
Trap 13Cholesterol Emboli — Not the Same as Contrast Nephropathy
Two patients undergo cardiac catheterization. Patient A develops AKI peaking at 48 hours with eosinophilia and livedo reticularis. Patient B develops AKI peaking at 72 hours that resolves in 1 week with hydration. What is the diagnosis for each?
Patient A = Cholesterol emboli nephropathy: eosinophilia (up to 40%), livedo reticularis, blue toes, subacute onset (days–weeks post-procedure), progressive (not self-limited), Hollenhorst plaques on funduscopy. AKI does NOT typically peak at 48–72 hours — it progresses slowly. Patient B = Contrast-induced nephropathy (AKI): peaks at 48–72 hours post-contrast, non-oliguric, self-limiting, no eosinophilia, no skin findings. Distinguishing feature: cholesterol emboli = eosinophilia + skin findings + progressive; contrast = rapid self-limited creatinine rise. No specific treatment for cholesterol emboli — discontinue anticoagulation if possible.
Trap 14Primary FSGS Recurrence in Transplant — The Circulating Factor
A 35-year-old with primary FSGS (heavy proteinuria, diffuse FPE on EM, steroid-resistant) receives a deceased donor kidney transplant. Within 24 hours, massive proteinuria appears. What happened and how is it managed?
Recurrence of primary FSGS in the transplant — occurs in 30–40% of recipients with primary FSGS. Recurs within hours to days of transplantation because the circulating permeability factor (unknown exact identity — suPAR, anti-CD40, others postulated) immediately attacks the new graft's podocytes. This is the most dramatic evidence that primary FSGS is a circulating factor disease. Treatment of recurrence: plasmapheresis (to remove the circulating factor) + high-dose steroids ± rituximab. If caught early and treated aggressively, the graft can be saved. Patients should be counseled pre-transplant about this risk; plasmapheresis sometimes used prophylactically.
