01

BP Physiology & Pathophysiology

Core Equation
BP = Cardiac Output × Total Peripheral Resistance
CO = Heart Rate × Stroke Volume. TPR set by small arteries and arterioles.

Regulatory Systems

Vasoconstrictors (raise BP)
  • Sympathetic nervous system — epinephrine/norepinephrine → vasoconstriction, ↑ HR
  • RAAS — Angiotensin II: vasoconstricts + stimulates aldosterone release → Na⁺/water retention
  • Endothelin — potent vasoconstrictor from endothelium
Vasodilators (lower BP)
  • Nitric oxide (NO) — endothelium-derived vasodilator
  • Bradykinin — promotes vasodilation (ACEi prevents its breakdown)
  • Atrial natriuretic peptide (ANP) — promotes Na⁺ excretion
  • Prostaglandins (PGE₂, PGI₂) — vasodilatory

Guyton's Pressure-Natriuresis Model

  • The kidney ultimately dictates long-term BP by controlling sodium excretion
  • ↑ Na intake → ↑ extracellular volume → ↑ CO → eventually ↑ PVR → sustained HTN
  • Salt-sensitive patients (Black adults, elderly, CKD, DM): greater BP rise per gram of Na ingested

White Coat vs. Masked Hypertension

White Coat HTN
  • Office BP: elevated (≥130/80)
  • Out-of-office BP: normal
  • Not fully benign — some CV risk remains
  • Avoid overtreatment → confirm with ABPM
BP
Masked HTN
  • Office BP: normal
  • Out-of-office BP: elevated
  • Higher CV risk than white coat HTN
  • Commonly missed → under-treated

Ambulatory BP Monitoring (ABPM) — Exam Tested (Q15)

Records BP every 15–30 min over 24 hours. The gold standard for true BP assessment.

What ABPM Provides
  • Confirms white coat vs masked HTN
  • Detects nocturnal dipping (normal ≥10% fall at night; non-dippers = higher CV risk)
  • Identifies morning BP surge (peak on waking — links to stroke/MI timing)
  • 24-hour BP burden — predicts CV outcomes better than office BP
  • Evaluates true treatment efficacy (vs trough-level effect)
  • Distinguishes true resistant HTN from white coat pseudo-resistance
HBPM (Home BP Monitoring)
  • Use validated upper-arm device (not wrist)
  • ≥2 readings AM and PM × 5–7 days
  • Normal HBPM: <135/85 mmHg (lower threshold than office)
  • Improves adherence; monitors treatment response
  • Cannot assess nocturnal dipping — ABPM needed for that
DIP Pattern (ABPM)
Non-dippers (fail to drop BP ≥10% at night) and reverse-dippers have significantly higher rates of LVH, stroke, CKD progression, and cardiovascular events. OSA is the classic cause of non-dipping.

Nonpharmacologic Interventions (Lifestyle)

General Principle
All lifestyle interventions show a linear dose-response. Benefit is greater in patients WITH hypertension. Combining ≥2 interventions provides additive BP reduction. Lifestyle also enhances medication effects.

Trap: Smoking Cessation vs BP Lowering (Q20)
Smoking cessation is the most impactful single lifestyle change for overall cardiovascular mortality — but it barely moves the BP number. If the question asks "what reduces CV risk most" → smoking cessation. If it asks "what lowers BP most" → DASH diet + sodium restriction.

Potassium — Key Details (Often Missed)

  • Dietary potassium preferred over pills — naturally lowers sodium too
  • Optimal supplemental dose: ~1200 mg/day additional (U-shaped dose-response)
  • Contraindications to K⁺ supplementation: hyperkalemia, advanced CKD, patients on K-sparing diuretics, ACEi, ARBs, or MRAs
  • Salt substitutes (25% KCl replacing NaCl) → ↓ stroke 14%, CVD 13%, all-cause mortality 12% (Chinese RCT)

Weight Loss Methods

  • Behavioral counseling: calorie reduction + physical activity → avg 10 lb loss in 6–12 months → ~5 mmHg SBP
  • GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide, liraglutide): FDA-approved for weight loss; also reduce CV events; modest direct BP effect
  • SGLT2 inhibitors: modest BP reduction ~5/2 mmHg; primarily for DM/CKD/HF; not FDA-approved as antihypertensives
  • Bariatric surgery: for BMI ≥40 (or ≥35 + comorbidity) — significant and sustained weight/BP reduction
04

First-Line Antihypertensive Drugs

Meta-Analysis of 123 RCTs — Active vs Placebo
Antihypertensive treatment: CVD ↓20% | CHD ↓17% | Stroke ↓27% | Heart Failure ↓28% | All-cause mortality ↓13%
Benefit extends to baseline SBP 130–139 mmHg. First-line: Thiazides, CCBs, ACEi, ARBs. Beta-blockers inferior in head-to-head trials.
1 · Thiazide & Thiazide-like Diuretics Best in Class
Mechanism & Drugs
  • Block Na⁺/Cl⁻ cotransporter (NCC) in the distal convoluted tubule
  • Long-term: also ↓ peripheral vascular resistance
  • Chlorthalidone 12.5–25 mg QD — preferred (longer half-life, used in ALLHAT)
  • HCTZ 12.5–50 mg QD — widely used but inferior to chlorthalidone
  • Indapamide 1.25–2.5 mg QD — fewer metabolic effects
  • Expected SBP reduction: ~12 mmHg
Side Effects
  • Hypokalemia ↓K⁺ — most common clinically important SE; treat with amiloride or Slow-K
  • Hyponatremia — especially elderly women; risk of seizures
  • Hyperuricemia / Gout — compete with urate for excretion
  • Hyperglycemia — impair insulin; worse with K⁺ depletion
  • Dyslipidemia — mild, transient ↑ TG and LDL
  • Hypercalcemia ↑Ca²⁺ — retain calcium in DCT → PREVENTS kidney stones; ↑ bone density
Trap: Thiazides vs Loop for Calcium
Thiazides → ↑Ca²⁺ (retain calcium) → prevent calcium nephrolithiasis, protect bone
Loop diuretics → ↓Ca²⁺ (waste calcium) → used to treat hypercalcemia crisis
Mnemonic: "Thiazides keep Ca in — Loops let Ca out"
2 · ACE Inhibitors (ACEi) First-Line
Mechanism & Drugs
  • Block ACE → ↓ Angiotensin II + ↑ Bradykinin (bradykinin not broken down)
  • Lisinopril 10–40 mg QD (preferred — longer-acting)
  • Enalapril 5–40 mg QD/BID
  • Ramipril 2.5–20 mg QD
  • Benazepril 10–40 mg QD
  • Trandolapril 1–4 mg QD
  • Expected SBP reduction: ~12 mmHg
Side Effects & CI
  • Dry cough — bradykinin-mediated; 5–15% (higher in Asian women) → switch to ARB
  • Angioedema — bradykinin-mediated; life-threatening; STOP drug
  • Hyperkalemia — ↓ aldosterone → ↑ K⁺; especially CKD
  • AKI in bilateral renal artery stenosis (remove efferent tone → lose GFR)
  • Hypotension — especially first dose with volume depletion
  • CI: Pregnancy (teratogenic), bilateral RAS, prior angioedema; NEVER with ARB
Compelling Indications for ACEi
DM + proteinuria, CKD with albuminuria, HFrEF, post-MI with reduced EF, high CV risk (HOPE trial)
In CKD: ACEi dilates efferent arteriole → ↓ intraglomerular pressure → slows progression.
3 · Angiotensin Receptor Blockers (ARBs) First-Line
Mechanism & Drugs
  • Block AT1 receptor → Ang II cannot bind; NO bradykinin effect
  • Losartan 50–100 mg QD/BID
  • Valsartan 80–320 mg QD
  • Candesartan 8–32 mg QD
  • Olmesartan 20–40 mg QD
  • Azilsartan 40–80 mg QD
  • Expected SBP reduction: ~12 mmHg
Key Advantage vs ACEi
  • No cough (no bradykinin effect)
  • Angioedema very rare
  • Same indications as ACEi
  • Use when ACEi causes intolerable cough
  • Same CI: pregnancy, bilateral RAS
  • NEVER combine with ACEi (ONTARGET: ↑ AKI, hyperkalemia, hypotension, no CV benefit)
4 · Calcium Channel Blockers (CCBs) First-Line
Dihydropyridines (DHP) — Preferred for HTN
  • Act primarily on vascular smooth muscle
  • Potent vasodilation, little cardiac effect
  • Amlodipine 2.5–10 mg QD — preferred (long t½ 35–50h)
  • Nifedipine LA 30–90 mg QD
  • Felodipine 2.5–10 mg QD
  • SEs: peripheral edema (~10%), gingival hyperplasia, flushing
  • CI: HFrEF
  • SAFE with beta-blockers
  • Expected SBP reduction: ~10 mmHg
CCB
Non-Dihydropyridines (Rate-Limiting)
  • Act on heart AND vessels → ↓ HR + vasodilation
  • Diltiazem ER 120–360 mg QD
  • Verapamil ER 100–300 mg QD (evening)
  • Used for: rate control AFib, angina
  • SEs: bradycardia, AV block, constipation
  • CI: HFrEF, high-grade AV/SA block
  • NEVER with beta-blockers — risk of severe bradycardia, cardiac arrest
  • Expected SBP reduction: ~9 mmHg
Amlodipine — Why It Wins (Q4)
ALLHAT: amlodipine = chlorthalidone for primary CV outcome (fatal CHD/MI). ACCOMPLISH: amlodipine + ACEi superior to HCTZ + ACEi for CV events. Best stroke prevention among all drug classes. No metabolic side effects. Preferred in elderly.

Second-Line & Add-On Drugs

1 · Beta-Blockers First-Step Only if IHD/HF
Role in Hypertension
Beta-blockers are NOT first-step for uncomplicated HTN. They are inferior to thiazides, CCBs, and RAAS blockers for stroke prevention and overall CV events in head-to-head trials. They are first-step ONLY when clinically indicated (IHD, HF, rate control).

Propranolol blocks β₁ AND β₂. It is NOT cardioselective. β₂-blockade → bronchoconstriction (CI in asthma/COPD); masks hypoglycemia symptoms. Cardioselective: atenolol, metoprolol, bisoprolol. Alpha+Beta: labetalol, carvedilol. Avoid atenolol for HTN: inferior per meta-analyses.

2 · Mineralocorticoid Receptor Antagonists (MRAs) Best 4th-Line for Resistant HTN
Mechanism
  • Block aldosterone receptor (mineralocorticoid receptor) in DCT, connecting tubule, and cortical collecting duct
  • ↑ Na⁺/water excretion + ↑ K⁺ retention (potassium-sparing)
  • Expected SBP reduction: ~9 mmHg
Indications
  • Primary aldosteronism — first-line treatment
  • Resistant HTN — best 4th-line agent; highly effective especially with subclinical aldosteronism
  • HFrEF (RALES: spironolactone; EMPHASIS-HF: eplerenone)
  • Post-MI with EF ≤40% + DM or HF (EPHESUS: eplerenone)
Spironolactone 25–100 mg QD — Non-Selective
  • Blocks mineralocorticoid receptor
  • Also blocks androgen and progesterone receptors
  • SE: gynecomastia, breast tenderness, menstrual irregularity, ↓ libido, erectile dysfunction (9–20%)
  • Older, cheaper, more potent
vs
Eplerenone 50–100 mg BID — Selective
  • Selective for mineralocorticoid receptor ONLY
  • No gynecomastia or sexual side effects
  • Shorter half-life, more expensive
  • Use when spironolactone causes intolerable SEs
Finerenone — New Non-Steroidal MRA
Finerenone 10–20 mg QD: newest MRA; non-steroidal structure → better cardiac/renal selectivity; fewer sex hormone side effects than spironolactone. Evidence in diabetic CKD (FIDELIO-DKD, FIGARO-DKD trials): reduced CKD progression and CV events. Better tolerated than spironolactone and eplerenone.
Key SE for All MRAs
Hyperkalemia — most dangerous. Avoid in CKD with K⁺ >5.0, or with ACEi/ARB without careful monitoring. Monitor K⁺ and creatinine regularly.
3 · Potassium-Sparing Diuretics Adjunct
  • Block ENaC (epithelial Na⁺ channel) in collecting duct — aldosterone-independent
  • Amiloride 5–10 mg QD/BID — drug of choice for thiazide-induced hypokalemia
  • Triamterene 50–100 mg QD/BID — often combined with HCTZ (Dyazide)
  • Minimal direct BP effect — always combined with another diuretic
  • Main SE: hyperkalemia — avoid in CKD, with ACEi/ARB
Amiloride vs Spironolactone — Different Mechanisms
Amiloride → blocks ENaC directly (aldosterone-independent)
Spironolactone → blocks mineralocorticoid receptor (competes with aldosterone)
Both are potassium-sparing but through completely different mechanisms.
4 · Loop Diuretics Volume / CKD
  • Block NKCC2 cotransporter in thick ascending loop of Henle — most powerful diuretic site
  • Furosemide 20–80 mg BID, Torsemide 5–10 mg QD, Bumetanide 1 mg ≈ 40 mg furosemide
  • NOT first-line for chronic HTN — short-acting; counterregulatory activation; poor sustained BP control
  • Preferred when GFR <30 (thiazides lose efficacy); symptomatic HF; using potent vasodilator (minoxidil)
  • Recent RCT: chlorthalidone effective even in eGFR <23 (↓ 10.5 mmHg + halved albumin/creatinine ratio)
  • SEs: hypokalemia, hypocalcemia (opposite of thiazides), hypomagnesemia, ototoxicity (high-dose IV)

Other Antihypertensive Drugs

1 · α₁-Receptor Blockers Not First-Line (ALLHAT)
  • Block α₁ adrenergic receptors on vascular smooth muscle → vasodilation → ↓ PVR
  • Doxazosin 1–16 mg QD | Prazosin | Terazosin
  • Expected SBP reduction: ~5 mmHg
  • Also block α₁ in prostate → relax bladder neck → improve urine flow → primary use in BPH
  • SE: first-dose orthostatic hypotension (~9% older adults) — take at bedtime, start low
ALLHAT — Doxazosin Arm Terminated Early (2000)
Doxazosin vs chlorthalidone: 2× more heart failure events with doxazosin. Trial stopped early. Alpha-blockers lower BP but do NOT provide adequate cardiovascular protection. Not first-line for HTN; use primarily for BPH or as 4th/5th-line in resistant HTN.
2 · Direct Vasodilators 4th–5th Line / Resistant HTN Only
Hydralazine 100–200 mg BID/TID
  • Direct arteriolar smooth muscle relaxation
  • Used in pregnancy (IV for severe pre-eclampsia)
  • SE: reflex tachycardia, fluid retention, palpitations
  • Rare: drug-induced lupus (hydralazine-induced SLE, + ANA)
  • Must combine with diuretic + beta-blocker to prevent reflex activation
vs
Minoxidil 5–100 mg QD/TID
  • Potent arteriolar vasodilation (opens K⁺ channels)
  • Reserved for truly refractory resistant HTN
  • SE: severe fluid retention, reflex tachycardia, hirsutism (especially women)
  • Up to 80% adverse event rate!
  • Must combine with loop diuretic (not thiazide) + beta-blocker
Why Combine Vasodilators with BB + Diuretic?
Direct vasodilators → reflex sympathetic activation → tachycardia + Na/water retention → negate BP effect and cause edema. Beta-blocker prevents tachycardia; diuretic (loop for minoxidil) prevents fluid retention. Without these, the vasodilator alone often fails.
3 · Central α₂-Agonists Infrequently Used
Drug & DoseKey FeaturesSpecial Indication
Clonidine oral 0.1–0.8 mg BID; patch 0.1–0.3 mg/wkSedation, dry mouth, bradycardia, orthostatic hypotension; rebound HTN crisis on abrupt withdrawalResistant HTN (add-on), ADHD, opioid withdrawal, alcohol withdrawal

Clonidine Rebound (Q21) — Hypertensive Emergency
Abrupt clonidine discontinuation → massive sympathetic surge → extreme BP elevation, sweating, anxiety — can resemble pheochromocytoma. Treatment: restart clonidine and taper slowly, or add alpha + beta-blocker. Doxazosin or ACEi withdrawal does NOT cause this syndrome.
4 · SGLT2 Inhibitors (Gliflozins) Emerging / CKD+DM+HF
  • Block SGLT2 in proximal tubule → ↑ glucose + Na⁺ excretion in urine
  • Modest BP reduction: ~5/2 mmHg (does not meet FDA threshold for antihypertensive classification)
  • Empagliflozin, Dapagliflozin, Canagliflozin
  • Major benefits: ↓ HF hospitalization, ↓ CKD progression, ↓ CV mortality in DM + CVD
  • SE: genital mycotic infections (UTI), DKA risk, polyuria; small ↑ fracture risk
  • Indicated primarily for: DM, CKD (eGFR ≥20), HFrEF — not as standalone antihypertensives
5 · Renal Denervation FDA Approved 2023
  • Catheter-based procedure: ultrasound or radiofrequency energy ablates renal sympathetic nerves
  • Approved as adjunctive treatment for resistant HTN
  • Indication: true resistant HTN + office SBP ≥160 mmHg despite ≥3 medications
  • Consider secondary causes and confirm adherence before referring
  • Evidence: SYMPLICITY HTN trials (mixed earlier results); RADIANCE TRIO (ultrasound-based: strong evidence)

Drug Combinations & Key Trials

Combination Principles
Starting 2-drug combination → better BP control + fewer side effects than one drug at high dose. Single-pill combinations → better adherence. Most patients need 2 drugs; average 3 drugs for <130/80 target.
  • Dual therapy: diuretic OR CCB + ACEi or ARB
  • Triple therapy: diuretic + CCB + ACEi or ARB

Three Key Trials — Detailed

ALLHAT — Antihypertensive and Lipid-Lowering Treatment to Prevent Heart Attack Trial (2002)

N = 33,357 | Age ≥55 | HTN + ≥1 CV risk factor | Chlorthalidone vs Amlodipine vs Lisinopril vs Doxazosin

Findings
  • Primary outcome (fatal CHD/MI): equivalent for all 3 active arms
  • Chlorthalidone SUPERIOR for HF prevention and combined CVD
  • Amlodipine superior to lisinopril for stroke
  • Doxazosin arm terminated early: 2× HF with doxazosin
Takeaway
  • Thiazide is the best single agent for overall CVD prevention
  • CCB excellent for stroke
  • ACEi comparable but inferior for stroke vs CCB
  • Alpha-blocker: NEVER first-line
ACCOMPLISH — Avoiding Cardiovascular Events in Combination Therapy in Patients Living with Systolic Hypertension (2008)

N = 11,506 | High-risk HTN on ACEi (benazepril) | Amlodipine + Benazepril vs HCTZ + Benazepril

Result
ACEi + CCB (amlodipine) SUPERIOR to ACEi + thiazide: ↓CV events by 20%.
→ When adding a 2nd drug to ACEi/ARB, prefer CCB over thiazide.
ONTARGET — ONgoing Telmisartan Alone and in combination with Ramipril Global Endpoint Trial (2008)

ACEi (ramipril) + ARB (telmisartan) vs either alone | High-risk CVD patients

Result
Combination ACEi + ARB: no additional CV benefit + more AKI, hyperkalemia, hypotension.
→ ACEi + ARB is contraindicated for general use.

BP Treatment Targets

Standard
  • Most patients with HTN: SBP/DBP <130/80 mmHg (ACC/AHA 2017)
  • Average 2 drugs needed for <140/90; average 3 drugs for <130/80
  • All 4 RCTs comparing 120 vs 140 target: fewer events in lower-goal group
  • Incremental benefit diminishes as achieved BP gets progressively lower
Special Populations
  • Elderly community-dwelling ≥65: SBP <130 mmHg
  • High comorbidity / limited life expectancy: individualize
  • CKD + proteinuria: <130/80
  • DM: <130/90 (most guidelines; some say <130/80)
  • Post-stroke: <130/80

Resistant HTN — 8-Step Investigation Algorithm

Definition
SBP/DBP ≥130/80 on 3 optimally dosed drugs including a diuretic — OR needing ≥4 drugs to achieve target.
  1. Exclude pseudo-resistance — check measurement technique; ensure correct cuff size
  2. Confirm with out-of-office BP (ABPM) — exclude white coat hypertension
  3. Evaluate lifestyle factors — high sodium intake, excessive alcohol, obesity, inactivity
  4. Exclude medications raising BP — NSAIDs, stimulants, OCP, decongestants, licorice, cocaine, sympathomimetics
  5. Confirm medication adherence — pill count, pharmacy refill records, supervised dosing
  6. Switch to long-acting diuretics — replace HCTZ with chlorthalidone or indapamide
  7. Add MRA (spironolactone) as 4th drug — most evidence-based 4th-line agent
  8. Add vasodilator (hydralazine or minoxidil); refer to specialist if uncontrolled after 6 months; consider renal denervation if SBP ≥160 mmHg
OSA — Most Common Cause of True Resistant HTN
Screen all resistant HTN patients for OSA: snoring, daytime sleepiness, witnessed apneas, non-dipping on ABPM. CPAP treatment → moderate additional BP lowering. Evaluate with sleep study (polysomnography).
09

Treatment in Special Populations

HTN + Cardiovascular Disease (CVD)
  • Target: SBP/DBP <130/80 mmHg (if well tolerated)
  • Preferred agents: ACEi, ARB, or beta-blockers (depending on type of CVD) + diuretics + CCB + MRA
  • Post-MI: ACEi + beta-blocker + MRA (if EF ≤40%) + aspirin + statin
  • Stable angina: beta-blocker or CCB (non-DHP) preferred
  • HFrEF: ACEi + beta-blocker + MRA + SGLT2i; avoid non-DHP CCB (negative inotropy)
  • SGLT2 inhibitors strongly indicated in HF + DM
  • Caution with non-DHP CCBs if bradycardia or AV block risk
HTN + Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)

HTN prevalence in advanced CKD: 80–85%. BP is both a cause AND consequence of CKD progression.

  • Target: <130/80 mmHg
  • ACEi or ARB central — dilate efferent arteriole → ↓ intraglomerular pressure → slow progression (especially with proteinuria)
  • NEVER combine ACEi + ARB in CKD — dangerous hyperkalemia + GFR decline
  • Diuretics: important for volume/resistance management
  • Loop diuretics traditionally for eGFR <30 — but recent RCT showed chlorthalidone effective even at eGFR <23
  • CCBs: good safety record when combined with ACEi/ARB (dilate afferent arteriole)
  • SGLT2 inhibitors (eGFR ≥20): ↓ CKD progression + CV events (CREDENCE, DAPA-CKD trials)
  • Monitor K⁺ and creatinine closely with any K-sparing or RAAS agent
  • Single-pill combinations strongly preferred — patients often on many medications
HTN + Diabetes Mellitus
  • Target: SBP/DBP <130/90 mmHg (most guidelines; some say <130/80)
  • Most require 3–4 agents for target
  • Include ACEi or ARB (especially with heavy proteinuria or kidney insufficiency)
  • Most also need a diuretic; CCBs commonly part of regimen
  • Resistant HTN with DM: MRA effective but monitor K⁺ carefully
  • SGLT2 inhibitors: ~5/2 mmHg modest direct BP reduction; major benefit on CV and renal outcomes
  • GLP-1 agonists: modest BP reduction + weight loss + CV event reduction (LEADER, SUSTAIN trials)
  • Watch for orthostatic hypotension in longstanding DM (autonomic neuropathy)
  • Single-pill triple therapy useful given polypharmacy burden
Older Adults (≥65 Years)
  • Vascular stiffening → isolated systolic HTN + wide pulse pressure common
  • High CV risk → disproportionately large treatment benefit from treating HTN
  • Target (community-dwelling ≥65): SBP <130 mmHg
  • High comorbidity + limited life expectancy: base decisions on clinical judgment + patient preference
  • Start at lower doses than younger patients (delayed renal/hepatic excretion)
  • Congestive HF common → prefer diuretic over CCB as second agent with ACEi/ARB
  • RCTs show no increase in falls, syncope, or infarctions with intensive treatment in older adults
  • Intensive treatment → reduced risk of cognitive impairment and dementia
  • Avoid atenolol: inferior per meta-analyses; prefer nebivolol, metoprolol, or carvedilol if BB needed
Non-Hispanic Black Adults
  • Highest HTN prevalence in US: 59%; onset at younger age; higher CVD and kidney disease risk
  • Control rate: <140/90 = 37% in Black vs 52% in White adults (NHANES 2017–2020)
  • Disparity driven primarily by social determinants of health, not genetics
  • Diuretics and CCBs especially effective for BP lowering in Black Americans (lower renin states)
  • ACEi and ARBs less effective as monotherapy (lower renin → less RAAS dependence) but still indicated for comorbidities
  • Most require combination therapy including agents for comorbidities
  • Requires team-based care + structural health system interventions
Sex Differences
  • Similar CV prevention benefit from antihypertensive therapy in both sexes
  • Women more likely to be aware, treated, and controlled vs men (especially at younger ages)
  • ACEi/ARBs contraindicated in pregnancy and women of child-bearing age contemplating pregnancy (teratogenic)
  • For pregnancy HTN: methyldopa, labetalol, or nifedipine preferred; hydralazine IV for acute severe HTN
  • Thiazides: reduce fracture risk in women (↑ calcium reabsorption + osteoblast formation)
  • Loop diuretics: ↑ Ca excretion; not associated with fractures per observational data
  • Women report more cough with ACEi and more ankle edema with CCBs than men

Hypertensive Crisis — Urgency vs. Emergency

Threshold
SBP/DBP ≥180/100 mmHg classifies as either urgency or emergency. The critical difference is the presence of acute end-organ damage.
Hypertensive Urgency
  • BP ≥180/100, no acute end-organ damage
  • FAR more common than emergency
  • Typically asymptomatic or mild headache
  • Tx: Restart/reinstitute/intensify oral antihypertensive agents
  • Setting: outpatient
  • Reduce BP gradually over 24–48 hours
  • Follow-up within 1–7 days
vs
Hypertensive Emergency
  • BP ≥180/100 + acute hypertensive end-organ damage
  • Requires immediate supervised IV antihypertensives
  • Setting: emergency room or ICU
  • Goal: ↓ SBP by 20–25% in first hour — NOT to normal
  • Then gradual further reduction with oral agents
  • Especially rapid care: acute aortic dissection, eclampsia, pheo crisis

Signs of End-Organ Damage (Q14)

Brain & Eyes
  • Severe headache (progressive, occipital) — most common presenting symptom
  • Papilledema — hallmark; flame hemorrhages, AV nicking on fundoscopy
  • Hypertensive encephalopathy — confusion, agitation, seizures
  • Acute stroke (ischemic or hemorrhagic)
  • Visual disturbances
Heart, Vessels & Kidneys
  • Acute left ventricular failure / pulmonary edema
  • Acute aortic dissection — requires SBP <120 immediately (different target!)
  • Acute coronary syndrome
  • Acute kidney injury (↑ Cr, oliguria)
  • Microangiopathic hemolytic anemia (MAHA)
  • Eclampsia / severe preeclampsia
  • Pheochromocytoma crisis
What Is NOT a Sign of HTN Emergency (Q14)
RBBB (right bundle branch block) is a conduction abnormality — not caused by hypertensive emergency. Fundoscopic papilledema, fresh hemorrhages, and neurological symptoms define the emergency. Normal fundoscopy + no organ damage = urgency, not emergency.

Critical Safety Rule: Never Lower BP Too Fast
Reduce SBP by 20–25% in the first hour only. The brain, kidney, and heart have adapted to high BP; rapid normalization → ischemic stroke, AKI, myocardial ischemia.
Exception: Aortic dissection → target SBP <120 mmHg within minutes (minimize shear force).

Pulse Pressure & Key Clinical Distinctions (Q3)

Definition
Pulse Pressure (PP) = SBP − DBP (normal ~40 mmHg)
PP reflects stroke volume and arterial compliance. Wide PP = large SV or stiff arteries. Narrow PP = reduced SV or outflow obstruction.
Wide Pulse Pressure (PP >60 mmHg)
  • Aortic Regurgitation (AR) — huge SV + backflow; DBP drops dramatically; water-hammer pulse
  • PVD / Arteriosclerosis — stiff aorta; SBP spikes; elderly
  • Thyrotoxicosis — high-output state
  • Fever — peripheral vasodilation → ↓ DBP
  • Severe anemia — high-output state
  • AV fistula / PDA — runoff into low-resistance circuit
  • Paget's disease — AV shunting in bone
PP
Narrow Pulse Pressure (PP <25 mmHg)
  • Severe Aortic Stenosis (AS) — reduced SV; pulsus parvus et tardus
  • HOCM — dynamic LVOT obstruction; murmur ↑ with Valsalva/standing
  • Cardiac tamponade — ↓ filling → ↓ SV; pulsus paradoxus
  • Cardiogenic shock — severely ↓ SV
  • Severe mitral stenosis — ↓ forward output
Classic Trap: AS vs AR
AS (Aortic Stenosis) = NARROW PP (reduced SV, pulsus parvus et tardus)
AR (Aortic Regurgitation) = WIDE PP (large SV + regurgitant drop in DBP, water-hammer pulse)
Mnemonic: "AR gives a WIDE PP; AS gives a NARROW PP."

Exam Traps — 12 Direct Question Insights

TRAP 01 — Q10Hypertensive Urgency: Oral Meds, Not IV
Vignette: BP 200/110 mmHg. No headache, no papilledema, no chest pain, ECG normal. Distractor: Give IV labetalol — the BP is dangerously high.
✓ Restart/adjust oral antihypertensive agents + close follow-up within 1–7 days.
No end-organ damage = Urgency. IV meds are not indicated and may cause harmful rapid BP drop. Keyword: no organ damage → oral meds only.
TRAP 02 — Q1MAHA: Cocaine Is the Trigger
Vignette: 25-year-old, BP 220/140, Hgb 10, PLT 125k, LDH 680, Cr 2.4, schistocytes. Options: cannabis, benzodiazepines, antihypertensives, cocaine, cannabinoids.
✓ Cocaine.
Cocaine → sympathomimetic HTN → necrotizing vasculitis → fibrin deposition → RBC shearing → MAHA. Cannabis doesn't cause this. Benzos TREAT cocaine toxicity. Keyword: young + extreme HTN + schistocytes = cocaine.
TRAP 03 — Q2Mineralocorticoid Antagonists: Selective vs Non-Selective
Vignette: Patient develops gynecomastia on spironolactone. What's the advantage of eplerenone/finerenone? Distractor: switch to amiloride (different mechanism entirely).
✓ Eplerenone (or finerenone) = selective MRA → no gynecomastia, no sexual side effects.
Spironolactone is non-selective → also blocks androgen/progesterone receptors → gynecomastia. Amiloride blocks ENaC directly — not an aldosterone antagonist. Keyword: gynecomastia on spironolactone → switch to eplerenone.
TRAP 04 — Q3Pulse Pressure: AR vs AS vs HOCM
Vignette: 72-year-old with PVD, hard peripheral arteries. What is expected to be elevated? Distractor: confuse AR (wide PP) with AS (narrow PP).
✓ Wide PP in PVD, AR, thyrotoxicosis. Narrow PP in AS, HOCM, tamponade.
AR → water-hammer pulse, wide PP. AS → pulsus parvus et tardus, narrow PP. HOCM → narrow PP (dynamic obstruction). PVD/arteriosclerosis → stiff aorta → wide PP. Keyword: AR = wide; AS + HOCM = narrow.
TRAP 05 — Q4Best CV Protection: CCB or Thiazide — Not Beta-Blocker
Vignette: Uncomplicated HTN. Best CV protection? Distractor: atenolol or doxazosin.
✓ Amlodipine (CCB) or Chlorthalidone (thiazide) — NOT beta-blocker or alpha-blocker.
ALLHAT: doxazosin → 2× HF events (terminated). Beta-blockers inferior to CCB/thiazides for stroke. ACCOMPLISH: amlodipine + ACEi best combination. Keyword: CV protection = CCB or thiazide; NOT beta-blocker.
TRAP 06 — Q5 & Q12Thiazide/Loop Hypokalemia → Add Amiloride
Vignette: Patient on thiazide or furosemide, K⁺ = 3.0. What to add? Distractor: add ARB (mildly raises K⁺ but not the right choice for this purpose), or add more diuretic.
✓ Add Amiloride (ENaC blocker) or Slow-K (oral KCl supplement).
Amiloride directly prevents K⁺ loss in collecting duct. Furosemide + amiloride: the amiloride corrects the K loss. ARBs raise K mildly but are chosen for other indications, not electrolyte correction. Keyword: diuretic hypokalemia → amiloride first.
TRAP 07 — Q6Resistant HTN: Rule Out White Coat + OSA First
Vignette: 50-year-old on 2 drugs, BP still elevated. What to do first? Distractor: add 3rd drug immediately.
✓ Confirm with ABPM (exclude white coat HTN), then screen for OSA, non-adherence, offending medications.
Before adding drugs: step 1 = exclude pseudo-resistance. OSA is the most common cause of true resistant HTN. If confirmed resistant, add MRA (spironolactone) as 4th drug. Keyword: apparent resistance → ABPM first, then OSA screen.
TRAP 08 — Q7Sodium Target — Exact Numbers
Vignette: What is the recommended sodium limit for HTN? Distractor: 10 mg/day or 10 g NaCl/day.
✓ <1500–2300 mg sodium per day (= <5–6 g NaCl/day).
Dose-response: ~5.5 mmHg SBP per 2300 mg Na reduction. Most patients consume 3400–4400 mg/day. 6g NaCl = 2400 mg Na (NaCl is 40% Na by weight). Keyword: Na target = 2000–2300 mg/day.
TRAP 09 — Q8ACEi + ARB = Contraindicated (ONTARGET)
Vignette: Not at target on ACEi alone. Add ARB? Distractor: yes — double RAAS blockade = better.
✓ NO. Add CCB (ACCOMPLISH principle) or thiazide — not ARB.
ONTARGET: ACEi+ARB → more AKI, hyperkalemia, hypotension, no CV benefit. ARB is only for patients who cannot tolerate ACEi (cough/angioedema). Keyword: ACEi+ARB = ONTARGET = harmful = contraindicated.
TRAP 10 — Q9Propranolol: β₁ + β₂ Non-Selective
Vignette: Which receptor does propranolol block? Distractor: β₁ only (like atenolol), or α₁+β₁ (like labetalol).
✓ Propranolol = β₁ + β₂ = non-selective.
β₂-blockade → bronchoconstriction (CI in asthma/COPD), masks hypoglycemia. Treats thyrotoxicosis (blocks T4→T3 conversion), essential tremor, portal HTN, migraine prophylaxis. Cardioselective = atenolol, metoprolol, bisoprolol. Alpha+beta = labetalol, carvedilol. Keyword: propranolol = non-selective β₁+β₂.
TRAP 11 — Q14RBBB Is NOT a Sign of Hypertensive Emergency
Vignette: BP 220/100, RBBB on ECG, fundoscopy unremarkable, normal labs. Emergency? Distractor: RBBB = cardiac involvement = emergency.
✓ NO. Normal fundoscopy + no organ damage = Urgency, not emergency. RBBB ≠ HTN emergency.
RBBB is often a pre-existing or benign finding. Signs of emergency: papilledema, fresh hemorrhages, rising Cr, AKI, neurological changes, acute HF. Keyword: RBBB ≠ emergency; papilledema = emergency.
TRAP 12 — Q21Clonidine Withdrawal = Rebound Hypertensive Crisis
Vignette: 78-year-old on multiple drugs. A medication was recently stopped. BP now 250/130. Which drug caused rebound? Distractor: stopping ACEi or thiazide causes rebound HTN.
✓ Clonidine (central α₂-agonist) withdrawal → massive sympathetic surge → rebound hypertensive emergency.
Mechanism: clonidine suppresses CNS sympathetic outflow. Abrupt stop → catecholamine surge → extreme BP. Mimics pheochromocytoma. Treatment: restart clonidine + slow taper, or add α+β-blocker. ACEi/thiazide withdrawal does not cause this. Keyword: abruptly stopped central agent → clonidine rebound crisis.
