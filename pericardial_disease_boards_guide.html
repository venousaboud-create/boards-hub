01

Normal Functions of the Pericardium

Anatomy

The pericardium is a double-layered sac consisting of two layers:

  • Visceral pericardium: The inner serous membrane directly touching the heart surface (epicardium).
  • Parietal pericardium: The outer fibrous layer that encloses the visceral layer.
  • Pericardial space: Contains 15–50 mL of fluid — an ultrafiltrate of plasma — that lubricates movement between layers.

Physiologic Functions

  • Restraining force: Prevents sudden dilation of cardiac chambers, especially the right atrium and right ventricle (e.g., during exercise when venous return surges).
  • Anatomic anchor: Restricts the anatomic position of the heart within the mediastinum.
  • Barrier function: Likely retards spread of infection from the lungs and pleural cavities to the heart.
🔑 Clinical Pearl — Total Absence
Total congenital or surgical absence of the pericardium does NOT produce obvious clinical disease. However, in partial left pericardial defects, the main pulmonary artery and left atrium may bulge through — very rarely causing herniation and strangulation of the left atrium, resulting in sudden death.
02

Acute Pericarditis

Acute pericarditis is the most common pathologic process involving the pericardium. It has four principal diagnostic features.

Classification by Time

Acute

<6 weeks
Fibrinous or Effusive (serous/sanguineous)

Subacute

6 weeks – 6 months
Effusive-constrictive or Constrictive

Chronic

>6 months
Constrictive or Adhesive (nonconstrictive)

Etiologic Classification

I. Infectious
  • Viral (most common): Coxsackievirus A & B, echovirus, herpesviruses, mumps, adenovirus, hepatitis, HIV, post-acute COVID, mpox
  • Pyogenic: Pneumococcus, Streptococcus, Staphylococcus, Neisseria, Legionella, Chlamydia
  • Tuberculous
  • Fungal: Histoplasmosis, coccidioidomycosis, Candida, blastomycosis
  • Other: Syphilitic, protozoal, parasitic
II. Non-Infectious
  • Acute idiopathic
  • Renal failure (uremic pericarditis)
  • Neoplasia: Primary tumors (mesothelioma); metastatic (lung, breast, lymphoma, leukemia)
  • Trauma: Penetrating or nonpenetrating chest wall
  • Aortic dissection with leakage into pericardial sac
  • Acute MI
  • Postirradiation
  • Familial Mediterranean fever and other autoinflammatory syndromes
  • Metabolic: Myxedema, cholesterol
III. Autoimmune / Drug-Induced
  • Rheumatic fever
  • Collagen vascular disease: SLE, rheumatoid arthritis, ankylosing spondylitis, scleroderma, granulomatosis with polyangiitis, IgG4 disease
  • Drug-induced: Procainamide, hydralazine, phenytoin, isoniazid, minoxidil, anticoagulants, methysergide
  • Postcardiac injury: Postpericardiotomy, posttraumatic, post-MI (Dressler's syndrome)

The 4 Diagnostic Features

  • Chest Pain
    • Often severe, retrosternal and/or left precordial, referred to neck, arms, or left shoulder.
    • Frequently pleuritic — sharp, worsened by inspiration and coughing (due to accompanying pleural inflammation).
    • Characteristically intensified by lying supine and relieved by sitting up and leaning forward — a key exam discriminator.
    • May radiate to the trapezius ridge (pathognomonic for pericardial irritation of the diaphragmatic pleura) or into either arm, mimicking ischemia.
    • Pain is often ABSENT in: slowly developing tuberculous, postirradiation, neoplastic, and uremic pericarditis.
    ⚠ Confusion with AMI
    Pericarditis can mimic AMI because serum biomarkers of myocardial damage may rise due to concomitant epi-myocarditis. However, elevations are modest compared to AMI, despite often extensive ECG ST-segment elevation — this dissociation is the distinguishing key. In AMI, chest pain precedes fever; in pericarditis, fever and chest pain develop simultaneously (often 10–12 days post-viral illness).
  • Pericardial Friction Rub
    • Audible at some point in ~85% of patients with acute pericarditis.
    • May have up to 3 components per cardiac cycle — described as rasping, scratching, or grating.
    • Heard most frequently at end expiration with the patient upright and leaning forward.
  • ECG Changes — 4 Stages
    🔑 ECG Stage Progression
    • Stage 1: Diffuse ST-segment elevation (upward concavity) in leads I, II, aVF, V2–V6 + PR-segment depression (reflects atrial injury current — earliest change). Reciprocal changes only in aVR and occasionally V1.
    • Stage 2: ST segments return to normal (after several days).
    • Stage 3: T waves become inverted.
    • Stage 4: ECG returns to normal (weeks to months later).
    🚨 Pericarditis vs AMI — ECG Key
    In pericarditis: ST elevations are upwardly concave (saddle-shaped) and diffuse. In AMI: ST elevations are upwardly convex and regional, with more prominent reciprocal depressions that normalize within 1–2 days.
  • Pericardial Effusion
    • Usually associated with pain and/or ECG changes above.
    • Large effusions cause electrical alternans (alternating QRS amplitude) due to swinging heart motion.
    • Heart sounds may be fainter; friction rub and apex impulse may disappear.
    • Ewart's sign: patch of dullness, increased fremitus, and egophony beneath the angle of the left scapula — due to lung compression by pericardial fluid.
    • Chest X-ray may show "water bottle" cardiac silhouette configuration but may be normal with small effusions.
    • Pericardial effusion is especially important when it develops rapidly — may lead to cardiac tamponade.

    • Diagnosis

    • Echocardiography (TTE): Most widely used — sensitive, specific, noninvasive, bedside-capable. Records pericardial fluid as echo-free space between posterior pericardium and LV epicardium, and/or between anterior RV and parietal pericardium under anterior chest wall.
    • CT / MRI: Superior for detecting loculated effusions, pericardial thickening, and pericardial masses. MRI is particularly useful for detecting pericardial inflammation (late gadolinium enhancement).

    Temperature >38°C

    Onset

    Effusion

    Host

    03

    Treatment — Acute Pericarditis

    First-Line: NSAIDs + Colchicine

    NSAIDs (First-Line)

    Mechanism

    Inhibit prostaglandin synthesis → reduce inflammation and pain in the pericardium.

    Drugs & Doses

    • Aspirin: 2–4 g/day
    • Ibuprofen: 600–800 mg three times daily
    • Indomethacin: 25–50 mg three times daily
    • Always add gastric protection (e.g., omeprazole 20 mg/day)

    Duration

    • Continue for 1–2 weeks then taper over several weeks in responsive patients.
    🚨 Contraindication
    Anticoagulants should be avoided — can cause bleeding into the pericardial cavity and tamponade.
    Colchicine (Co-First-Line)

    Mechanism

    Concentrated in and interferes with the migration of neutrophils → reduces pericardial inflammation and recurrence risk.

    Drugs & Doses

    • <70 kg: 0.5–0.6 mg once daily
    • >70 kg: 0.5–0.6 mg twice daily
    • Duration: 3 months

    Benefits

    • Enhances response to NSAIDs
    • Reduces risk of recurrent pericarditis

    Side Effects / Contraindications

    Diarrhea GI side effects Hepatic dysfunction (CI) Renal dysfunction (CI)

    Second-Line: Glucocorticoids

    Glucocorticoids (Second-Line / Rescue)

    Mechanism

    Broad anti-inflammatory suppression — usually suppress clinical manifestations of acute pericarditis effectively.

    Indication

    • Patients who have failed or do not tolerate NSAIDs and colchicine.

    Drugs & Doses

    • Prednisone: 1 mg/kg/day — give for only 2–4 days, then taper.
    🚨 Key Caveat
    Glucocorticoids increase the risk of subsequent recurrence. Full-dose corticosteroids should be given for only 2–4 days and then tapered. They are not first-line precisely because they worsen long-term outcomes regarding recurrence.

    Refractory / Recurrent Pericarditis

    For patients with multiple, frequent, disabling recurrences continuing >2 years, not prevented by colchicine and other NSAIDs, and not controlled by glucocorticoids:

    • Azathioprine — reported to be of benefit
    • Anakinra — an interleukin-1β receptor antagonist; reported benefit in refractory recurrent pericarditis
    • Pericardial stripping — rarely necessary; may not always terminate recurrences
    💜 Management Setting
    The majority of patients with acute pericarditis can be managed as outpatients with careful follow-up. Hospitalize only when specific causes are suspected or poor-prognosis predictors are present.
    04

    Cardiac Tamponade

    Accumulation of fluid in the pericardial space in a quantity sufficient to cause serious obstruction to ventricular inflow. May be fatal if not recognized and treated promptly.

    Common Causes

    • Idiopathic pericarditis
    • Neoplastic disease
    • Tuberculosis
    • Bleeding: aortic dissection, cardiac operation, trauma, anticoagulant therapy

    Beck's Triad — The 3 Principal Features

    Mnemonic — Beck's Triad
    HHS
    • Hypotension
    • Heart sounds soft or absent
    • Systemic venous distention (JVD) with prominent x descent but absent y descent
    🔑 Fluid Volume Threshold
    • Tamponade can occur with as little as 200 mL when fluid develops rapidly
    • With slowly developing effusions, the pericardium can stretch to accommodate >2000 mL before tamponade occurs

    Paradoxical Pulse

    An inspiratory decline in systolic arterial pressure of >10 mmHg. Normally, some fall occurs; paradoxical pulse is an exaggeration of this normal finding.

    Mechanism

    • Both ventricles share a tight, incompressible covering (pericardial sac).
    • During inspiration, right ventricular filling increases (↑ venous return) → RV enlarges → leftward bulging of the interventricular septum → reduces LV volume, stroke volume, and arterial systolic pressure.
    ⚠ Not Specific for Tamponade
    Paradoxical pulse also occurs in approximately one-third of patients with constrictive pericarditis, and in hypovolemic shock, acute and chronic obstructive airway disease, and pulmonary embolism.

    Diagnosis

    • Echocardiography (emergent): Prompt diagnosis — Doppler shows tricuspid and pulmonic flow velocities increase markedly during inspiration, while pulmonic vein, mitral, and aortic flow velocities decrease. Late diastolic inward motion (collapse) of the RV free wall and right atrium on 2D echo.
    • Transesophageal echo, CT, or cardiac MRI may be needed for loculated effusions.

    Treatment: Pericardiocentesis

    🚨 Emergency Treatment
    If manifestations of tamponade appear, pericardiocentesis must be carried out at once — if left untreated, tamponade may be rapidly fatal.
    • Approach: Apical, parasternal, or most commonly subxiphoid.
    • Perform under echocardiographic guidance whenever possible.
    • IV saline may be administered while preparing — but do not delay pericardiocentesis.
    • Measure intrapericardial pressure before withdrawing fluid; drain as completely as possible.
    • A small multiholed catheter may be left in place for recurrent fluid.
    • Surgical drainage (subxiphoid thoracotomy) may be required for recurrent tamponade, loculated effusions, or when tissue is needed for diagnosis.

    Pericardial Fluid Analysis

    • In developed nations: bloody fluid most commonly due to neoplasm, renal failure, or cardiac trauma.
    • In developing nations: tuberculosis is also a major cause of exudative/bloody effusion.
    • Analyze: RBC, WBC, cytology for neoplastic cells, cultures.
    • Elevated adenosine deaminase and/or M. tuberculosis DNA by PCR strongly supports tuberculous pericarditis — but pericardial tissue often needed to confirm.
    05

    Viral or Idiopathic Acute Pericarditis

    In many instances, acute pericarditis occurs in association with or following illnesses of known or presumed viral origin. When no viral cause can be established, the term idiopathic acute pericarditis is appropriate.

    Epidemiology & Causative Agents

    • Occurs at all ages but most common in young adult males.
    • Often associated with pleural effusion and pneumonitis.
    • Causative viruses: Coxsackievirus A or B, influenza, echovirus, mumps, herpes simplex, varicella-zoster, adenovirus, cytomegalovirus.
    • Viral isolation and serologic studies are usually negative.

    Clinical Features

    • Simultaneous fever and precordial pain, often 10–12 days after a presumed viral illness — key distinction from AMI (where chest pain precedes fever).
    • Constitutional symptoms usually mild to moderate.
    • Pericardial friction rub often audible.
    • Elevated C-reactive protein and WBC count are common.
    • Disease ordinarily runs its course in a few days to 4 weeks.
    • ST-segment alterations usually disappear after 1+ weeks; abnormal T waves may persist for years (source of confusion).

    Complications

    Most Frequent

    Recurrent (relapsing) pericarditis — occurs in approximately one-fourth (25%) of patients. A smaller number have multiple recurrences.

    Less Frequent

    Tamponade and constrictive pericarditis are possible but infrequent complications. Accumulation of some pericardial fluid is common.

    Postcardiac Injury Syndrome (Dressler's)

    Acute pericarditis appearing after previous injury to the myocardium with blood in the pericardial cavity. Mechanism: hypersensitivity (or autoimmune) reaction to antigens from injured myocardial tissue and/or pericardium.

    • Triggers: Cardiac surgery (postpericardiotomy syndrome), blunt or penetrating cardiac trauma, cardiac catheter perforation, rarely AMI.
    • Timing: Pain usually develops 1–4 weeks after cardiac injury.
    • Recurrences: Common — can occur up to 2 years or more post-injury.
    • Features: Fever, pleuritis, pneumonitis accompany pericarditis; illness usually subsides in 1–2 weeks.
    • Pericarditis type: May be fibrinous or pericardial effusion (often serosanguinous; rarely causes tamponade).
    • Treatment: Often only aspirin and analgesics needed; if severe/recurrent — NSAID, colchicine, or glucocorticoid.
    💜 Differentiation from Idiopathic
    Postcardiac injury pericarditis is differentiated from acute idiopathic pericarditis primarily by timing: occurrence within days to weeks of a chest blow, cardiac perforation, cardiac operation, or AMI.
    06

    Differential Diagnosis of Acute Pericarditis

    Because there is no specific test for acute idiopathic pericarditis, the diagnosis is one of exclusion. All other disorders that may be associated with acute fibrinous pericarditis must be considered.

    Key Diagnoses to Distinguish

    Acute MI (Most Common Error)

    A common diagnostic error is mistaking acute viral/idiopathic pericarditis for AMI and vice versa. Key distinctions:

    • In pericarditis: fever and pain occur simultaneously; ST elevations are diffuse and concave; biomarker elevation is modest
    • In AMI: pain precedes fever; ST elevations are regional and convex; biomarkers markedly elevated
    SLE / Drug-Induced Lupus

    Most important collagen vascular cause to distinguish. When pericarditis occurs without obvious underlying disorder, SLE may be suggested by:

    • Rise in antinuclear antibody (ANA) titers
    • Drugs: hydralazine, procainamide (drug-induced lupus)
    Pyogenic (Purulent) Pericarditis

    Usually secondary to:

    • Cardiothoracic operations
    • Extension from lungs/pleural cavities
    • Esophageal rupture into pericardial sac
    • Rupture of valvular ring abscess (infective endocarditis)
    • HIV-associated infections

    Generally accompanied by fever, chills, septicemia. Generally poor prognosis. Requires immediate drainage + vigorous antibiotics.

    Uremic Pericarditis

    Occurs in up to one-third of patients with severe renal dysfunction. Also seen in patients on chronic dialysis (dialysis-associated pericarditis).

    • Friction rub common; pain usually absent or mild
    • Fibrinous; associated with serosanguinous effusions; frank hemorrhage may occur before dialysis
    • Treatment: NSAID + intensification of dialysis
    • Occasionally requires pericardiocentesis
    • If recurrent/persistent: pericardial window or pericardiectomy
    Neoplastic Pericarditis

    Most common metastatic tumors:

    • Carcinoma of the lung and breast
    • Malignant melanoma
    • Lymphoma and leukemia

    Complications: pain, tamponade, atrial arrhythmias. Diagnosis by pericardial fluid cytology or pericardial biopsy. Mediastinal irradiation may cause acute or chronic constrictive pericarditis.

    Other Collagen Vascular Diseases

    Rheumatoid arthritis, scleroderma, and polyarteritis nodosa may cause acute pericarditis. Other evidence of these diseases is usually obvious at presentation.

    07

    Chronic Pericardial Effusions

    Sometimes encountered without an antecedent history of acute pericarditis. May cause few symptoms per se; often detected by finding an enlarged cardiac silhouette on chest roentgenogram.

    Common Causes

    • Tuberculosis
    • Myxedema (hypothyroidism)
    • Neoplasms, SLE, rheumatoid arthritis
    • Mycotic infections
    • Radiation therapy to the chest
    • Chylopericardium

    Pericardial Fluid Analysis

    • Grossly sanguineous pericardial fluid is most commonly due to neoplasm, tuberculosis, renal failure, or slow leakage from an aortic dissection.
    • Aspiration and analysis are often helpful in diagnosis.

    Treatment

    • Pericardiocentesis may resolve large effusions.
    • Pericardiectomy may be required for recurrence.
    • Intrapericardial instillation of sclerosing agents may prevent reaccumulation — most commonly used for recurrent neoplastic effusions.
    08

    Chronic Constrictive Pericarditis

    Results when healing of an acute fibrinous or serofibrinous pericarditis (or resorption of a chronic effusion) is followed by obliteration of the pericardial cavity with granulation tissue formation. The tissue contracts into a firm scar encasing the heart, which may calcify.

    Etiology

    • In developing nations: High percentage are of tuberculous origin.
    • In North America / Western Europe: Tuberculosis is now an uncommon cause.
    • Other causes: Acute/relapsing viral or idiopathic pericarditis, trauma with organized blood clot, cardiac surgery, mediastinal irradiation, purulent infection, histoplasmosis, neoplastic disease (breast cancer, lung cancer, lymphoma), rheumatoid arthritis, SLE, chronic renal failure on dialysis.
    • In many patients, the cause is undetermined — an asymptomatic or forgotten bout of viral pericarditis may have been the inciting event.

    Pathophysiology

    The core abnormality: the rigid, thickened pericardium limits ventricular filling.

    Constrictive Pericarditis
    • Ventricular filling is unimpeded during early diastole
    • Filling is reduced abruptly when the elastic limit of the pericardium is reached ("square root" sign)
    • y descent is the most prominent deflection in JVP — reflects rapid early ventricular filling that is then abruptly halted
    • Atrial pressure pulses display M-shaped contour with prominent x AND y descents
    Cardiac Tamponade
    • Ventricular filling is impeded throughout diastole
    • y descent is absent or diminished
    • Prominent x descent
    • Pericardial effusion present
    🔑 Shared Hemodynamics
    In both constrictive pericarditis and cardiac tamponade: ventricular end-diastolic and stroke volumes are reduced; end-diastolic pressures in both ventricles and mean pressures in atria, pulmonary veins, and systemic veins are all elevated to similar levels (within 5 mmHg of one another) — equalization of diastolic pressures. Despite this, systolic function may be normal or only slightly impaired at rest.

    In advanced cases, the fibrotic process may extend into the myocardium causing myocardial scarring and atrophy, leading to venous congestion from combined pericardial and myocardial lesions.

    Clinical & Laboratory Findings

    Symptoms

    • Weakness, fatigue, weight gain
    • Increased abdominal girth, abdominal discomfort, edema
    • Exertional dyspnea (common); orthopnea may occur but usually not severe
    • In advanced cases: anasarca, skeletal muscle wasting, cachexia

    Physical Examination

    • Neck veins: Distended even after intensive diuresis; may remain distended — Kussmaul's sign (venous pressure fails to decline or rises during inspiration). Characteristic M-shaped JVP waveform.
    • Pulse pressure: Normal or reduced. Paradoxical pulse in ~one-third of cases.
    • Congestive hepatomegaly: Pronounced, may impair hepatic function, cause jaundice.
    • Ascites: Common and usually more prominent than dependent edema — a key distinguishing feature.
    • Pleural effusions and splenomegaly may also be present.
    • Broadbent's sign: Apical pulse is reduced and may retract in systole.
    • Pericardial knock: Early third heart sound at cardiac apex — occurs with abrupt cessation of ventricular filling; often conspicuous.

    ECG / CXR

    • ECG: Low voltage QRS, diffuse flattening or inversion of T waves; atrial fibrillation in ~one-third of patients.
    • CXR: Normal or slightly enlarged heart; pericardial calcification most common in tuberculous pericarditis. Note: calcification may occur without constriction, and constriction may occur without calcification.
    ⚠ Classic Misdiagnosis
    Because murmurs and cardiac enlargement may be inconspicuous/absent, hepatic enlargement and intractable ascites may lead to a mistaken diagnosis of hepatic cirrhosis. This error can be avoided by inspecting the neck veins — they will be distended with characteristic M-shaped waveform.

    Imaging

    • Echocardiography: Pericardial thickening, dilated IVC and hepatic veins, sharp halt to rapid LV filling in early diastole with normal LV systolic function. Distinctive pattern of exaggerated respiratory variation in transvalvular flow (mitral and tricuspid) by Doppler. Cannot definitively establish or exclude diagnosis.
    • CT and MRI: More accurate than echo for pericardial thickening. MRI is superior for evaluating myocardial involvement.

    Differential: Constrictive Pericarditis vs Other Causes of Right Heart Failure

    • Cor pulmonale: Systemic venous hypertension, but advanced parenchymal pulmonary disease is usually apparent and Kussmaul's sign is negative (venous pressure falls during inspiration).
    • Tricuspid stenosis: Can simulate constriction (hepatomegaly, ascites, venous distention) — but characteristic murmur and accompanying mitral stenosis murmur usually present.
    • Restrictive cardiomyopathy: Similar pathophysiology — critical distinction because constrictive pericarditis is surgically correctable. Use Doppler echo (respiratory variation in transvalvular flow) and CT/MRI.

    Treatment: Pericardiectomy

    Surgical Treatment

    Indication

    Pericardial resection (pericardiectomy) is the only definitive treatment of constrictive pericarditis and should be as complete as possible.

    Preoperative workup

    • Coronary arteriography in patients aged >50 years to exclude concomitant coronary artery disease.

    Outcomes

    • Benefits from cardiac decortication are usually progressive over a period of months.
    • Operative mortality: 5–10% even in experienced centers.
    • Patients with most severe disease (especially secondary to radiation therapy) are at highest risk.
    • Surgical treatment should be carried out as early as possible.

    Subacute Effusive-Constrictive Pericarditis

    Combination of tense effusion in pericardial space AND constriction by thickened pericardium. Shares features with both tamponade and constrictive pericarditis.

    • Causes: Tuberculosis, multiple attacks of idiopathic pericarditis, radiation, traumatic pericarditis, renal failure, scleroderma, neoplasms.
    • After pericardiocentesis, physiologic findings may shift from tamponade to constriction.
    • Diagnosis: Pericardiocentesis followed by pericardial biopsy.
    • Treatment: Wide excision of both visceral and parietal pericardium.

    Tuberculous Pericardial Disease

    Common cause of chronic pericardial effusion, especially in the developing world where TB and HIV are endemic.

    • May present as: pericardial effusion, chronic constrictive pericarditis, or subacute effusive-constrictive pericarditis.
    • Clinical picture: chronic, systemic illness in a patient with pericardial effusion.
    • Consider if: Known TB, HIV, fever, chest pain, weight loss, enlarged cardiac silhouette of undetermined origin.
    • If diagnosis remains obscure despite detailed analysis: pericardial biopsy (preferably by limited thoracotomy). If specimen shows granulomas with caseation → antituberculous chemotherapy.
    • If biopsy shows thickened pericardium after 2–4 weeks of antituberculous therapy → pericardiectomy to prevent constriction. Tubercular cardiac constriction should be treated surgically while patient receives antituberculous chemotherapy.

    High-Yield Distinctions

    Pericarditis ECG vs AMI ECG
    • Pericarditis: Diffuse, concave-up ("saddle-shaped") ST elevation; PR depression; no reciprocal changes except aVR/V1
    • AMI: Regional, convex-up ST elevation; prominent reciprocal depressions; normalizes within 1–2 days
    • Pericarditis biomarkers rise modestly (epi-myocarditis) — dissociation from ST elevation extent is key
    Tamponade vs Constrictive
    • Tamponade: y descent absent; electrical alternans; pericardial effusion
    • Constrictive: y descent prominent; pericardial knock; Kussmaul's sign; pericardial thickening/calcification
    • Both: equalized diastolic pressures; pulsus paradoxus (more prominent in tamponade)
    Sitting Forward — Why It Works

    Sitting up and leaning forward moves the heart away from the parietal pericardium → reduces friction rub intensity and relieves pericardial chest pain. Lying supine increases contact → worsens pain. This positional change is pathognomonic for pericardial origin of chest pain.

    Ewart's Sign

    Patch of dullness, increased fremitus, and egophony beneath the angle of the left scapula — due to compression of the base of the left lung by pericardial fluid. Seen with large pericardial effusions.

    Exam Traps

    Expert board exam coaching — Stage A Internal Medicine Boards. Each trap shows exactly how the question is framed and the reasoning to the correct answer.

    Trap 01Pericarditis Mistaken for STEMI
    A 24-year-old male presents with 2 days of sharp chest pain that worsens when he lies flat and improves when he leans forward. He had a "cold" 10 days ago. ECG shows diffuse ST elevations in leads I, II, aVF, V2–V6 with PR-segment depression. Troponin is mildly elevated at 0.8 ng/mL. The next best step is:
    Correct Answer: Treat for acute pericarditis with aspirin/NSAIDs + colchicine (NOT emergent cath lab activation)
    The clinical picture is classic viral pericarditis: young male, post-viral illness, pleuritic positional chest pain, diffuse concave ST elevations, PR depression, and MILD troponin elevation (epi-myocarditis). Troponin elevation in pericarditis is modest despite extensive ECG changes — this dissociation distinguishes it from STEMI.
    ⚠ Trap: Students activate the cath lab because of ST elevations + troponin elevation. The key discriminators are: (1) diffuse vs regional ST changes, (2) concave vs convex morphology, (3) PR depression, (4) positional/pleuritic nature, (5) post-viral timing. Board keyword: "leaning forward relieves pain"
    Trap 02Anticoagulation in Pericarditis
    A 55-year-old with known atrial fibrillation is started on warfarin for stroke prophylaxis. Two weeks later he develops chest pain and a pericardial friction rub. Echo shows a small pericardial effusion. He is diagnosed with acute pericarditis. Should anticoagulation be continued?
    Correct Answer: Anticoagulants should be AVOIDED in acute pericarditis — they increase the risk of bleeding into the pericardial cavity and cardiac tamponade.
    The decision to anticoagulate in the setting of pericarditis must be carefully weighed, and the textbook states anticoagulants should be avoided due to risk of hemopericardium and tamponade.
    ⚠ Trap: Students continue warfarin because "he has AF and needs stroke prevention." The board answer prioritizes tamponade prevention. Board keyword: "anticoagulants → tamponade"
    Trap 03Corticosteroids Increase Recurrence
    A 30-year-old woman with acute pericarditis has failed ibuprofen due to intractable GI side effects. She cannot tolerate any NSAIDs. You begin prednisone 1 mg/kg/day. For how long should full-dose corticosteroids be maintained?
    Correct Answer: Full-dose corticosteroids should be given for only 2–4 days, then tapered — because they INCREASE the risk of subsequent recurrence.
    Glucocorticoids are second-line because while they effectively suppress acute inflammation, they paradoxically increase the likelihood of recurrent pericarditis. Rapid tapering limits this risk.
    ⚠ Trap: Students answer "2–4 weeks" extrapolating from other inflammatory conditions. Board keyword: "glucocorticoids increase recurrence"
    Trap 04Absent y Descent — Tamponade vs Constrictive
    A 67-year-old man with known lung cancer presents with progressive dyspnea, hypotension, muffled heart sounds, and marked jugular venous distension. JVP waveform inspection reveals a prominent x descent but an absent y descent. Echocardiography confirms a large pericardial effusion with right atrial collapse. The diagnosis is:
    Correct Answer: Cardiac Tamponade
    Beck's triad: hypotension, muffled heart sounds, elevated JVP. Absent y descent is the hallmark of tamponade — there is no early diastolic filling because the fluid compresses the ventricles throughout diastole. In constrictive pericarditis, the y descent is prominent (rapid early filling then abrupt halt).
    ⚠ Trap: Students confuse prominent x descent with tamponade's distinguishing feature. The absent y descent is the key. In constrictive pericarditis: prominent y descent. Board keyword: "absent y descent = tamponade"
    Trap 05Volume Threshold for Tamponade
    A trauma patient develops cardiac tamponade with 200 mL of pericardial blood after a stab wound. A chronic cancer patient has 2500 mL of pericardial fluid but no signs of tamponade. How do you explain this discrepancy?
    Correct Answer: The RATE of accumulation determines tamponade, not the absolute volume.
    When fluid accumulates rapidly (as in trauma), even 200 mL can cause tamponade because the pericardium has no time to stretch. In slowly developing effusions, the pericardium gradually adapts, allowing over 2000 mL to accumulate before hemodynamic compromise occurs.
    ⚠ Trap: Students focus on volume thresholds rather than rate of accumulation. Board keyword: "rate matters more than volume"
    Trap 06Constrictive Pericarditis Mimicking Cirrhosis
    A 58-year-old male with a history of tuberculosis treated 15 years ago presents with progressive ascites, hepatomegaly, jaundice, and anasarca. Liver function tests are mildly elevated. He appears chronically ill. Ultrasound shows a large amount of ascites with no liver cirrhosis. The most likely diagnosis is:
    Correct Answer: Chronic Constrictive Pericarditis
    The classic mimicry trap — hepatic congestion from elevated systemic venous pressure in constrictive pericarditis causes hepatomegaly, jaundice, and ascites that dominate the clinical picture. The key clue is the history of TB and the finding of distended neck veins with Kussmaul's sign (venous pressure doesn't fall with inspiration). Echo would show thickened pericardium.
    ⚠ Trap: Students diagnose cirrhosis given the ascites + hepatomegaly + jaundice triad. Always inspect the neck veins! Board keyword: "TB history + ascites + distended neck veins = constrictive pericarditis"
    Trap 07Colchicine Contraindication
    A 45-year-old with recurrent pericarditis and a serum creatinine of 3.8 mg/dL is started on colchicine 0.5 mg twice daily. Three days later, he develops severe diarrhea and hepatic transaminases are markedly elevated. What went wrong?
    Correct Answer: Colchicine is contraindicated in hepatic or renal dysfunction — it should not have been used in this patient with severe chronic kidney disease.
    Colchicine causes diarrhea and GI side effects even in normal patients; in renal/hepatic dysfunction, accumulation leads to severe toxicity. The drug must be avoided in these populations.
    ⚠ Trap: Students use colchicine because it's "first-line" without checking for contraindications. Board keyword: "colchicine contraindicated — hepatic or renal dysfunction"
    Trap 08Kussmaul's Sign — Where NOT Expected
    A 52-year-old presents with dyspnea, elevated JVP, and hepatomegaly. During inspiration, the jugular venous pressure rises further rather than falling. Which of the following diagnoses does this finding NOT support?
    Correct Answer: Cardiac Tamponade — Kussmaul's sign is ABSENT in tamponade.
    Kussmaul's sign (JVP rises or fails to decline during inspiration) is present in constrictive pericarditis, restrictive cardiomyopathy, and right ventricular infarction. It is characteristically ABSENT in cardiac tamponade. This is a high-yield distinction.
    ⚠ Trap: Students list tamponade as a cause of elevated JVP without remembering Kussmaul's sign is specifically absent there. Board keyword: "Kussmaul's sign absent in tamponade"
    Trap 09PR Depression — Earliest ECG Change
    A 19-year-old presents with 1 day of chest pain and low-grade fever after a URI 2 weeks ago. His ECG shows mild PR-segment depression in multiple leads but no clear ST-segment elevation yet. What does this ECG finding represent and what is the next step?
    Correct Answer: PR depression is the EARLIEST ECG change in acute pericarditis, reflecting atrial injury current — it may precede ST-segment elevation. Treat for acute pericarditis.
    Stage 1 ECG changes in pericarditis include both ST elevation AND PR depression (atrial injury). PR depression may occur before ST changes and is a highly specific sign of pericarditis when diffuse.
    ⚠ Trap: Students wait for ST elevation before diagnosing pericarditis. PR depression alone in the right clinical context is an early marker of pericarditis. Board keyword: "PR depression = earliest ECG change in pericarditis"
    Trap 10Pericardiectomy Timing
    A 60-year-old with chronic constrictive pericarditis from prior mediastinal radiation has progressive heart failure. His cardiologist says surgery is the only cure. The surgeon recommends waiting until the patient is "sicker" to justify the risk. What is the correct approach?
    Correct Answer: Surgical treatment (pericardiectomy) should be carried out as EARLY as possible — patients with the most severe disease have the highest operative mortality (5–10%).
    The risk of pericardiectomy depends on extent of myocardial involvement by the fibrotic process, severity of myocardial atrophy, and secondary hepatic/renal impairment. Waiting leads to worse outcomes. Radiation-induced constrictive pericarditis carries the highest surgical risk of all causes.
    ⚠ Trap: Students assume more severe disease = more justification = better timing. In constrictive pericarditis, the opposite is true — operate early before myocardial atrophy and organ damage occur. Board keyword: "pericardiectomy — earlier is better"
