01

Epidemiology

Definition of Unhealthy Drinking

Understanding what constitutes "unhealthy" drinking is essential because it defines the population at risk for ALD.

Unhealthy Drinking (Daily)
  • Women: ≥2 drinks per day
  • Men: ≥3 drinks per day
Heavy Episodic (Binge) Drinking
  • Women: ≥4 drinks on a single occasion
  • Men: ≥5 drinks on a single occasion
🔑 Standard Drink Definition
1 drink = ~14 g of ethanol = 1 beer = 4 oz of wine = 1 oz of 80% spirits

Prevalence and Disease Burden

US Unhealthy Drinking

Approximately 7% of US adults meet criteria for unhealthy drinking or heavy episodic drinking.

Fatty Liver Disease

Prevalence of alcohol-associated fatty liver disease is 4.7% of the general US population. Of these, 1.5% have stage ≥2 fibrosis.

Cirrhosis — Global Burden

Cirrhosis is the 11th leading cause of mortality worldwide, causing 1.16 million deaths annually. 48% of cirrhosis cases are attributed to alcohol.

Alcohol Misuse Outcomes
  • 18% had fibrosis
  • 26% had cirrhosis
  • 7% had acute alcohol-associated hepatitis without underlying cirrhosis
European Incidence — Acute AH
  • Women: 24–27 per million persons/year
  • Men: 46–65 per million persons/year
COVID-19 Impact

The COVID-19 pandemic increased excessive alcohol intake, resulting in a rise in morbidity and mortality linked to ALD.

⚠ Boards Point
ALD comprises a spectrum: alcohol-associated fatty liver disease → steatohepatitis → fibrosis → cirrhosis → acute alcohol-associated hepatitis (acute-on-chronic, high mortality).
02

Pathogenesis

Ethanol Metabolism Pathway

Alcohol is rapidly absorbed in the upper GI tract and predominantly metabolized in the liver via the portal vein. Understanding the metabolic cascade explains how each step leads to hepatocyte injury.

Ethanol (via portal vein → hepatocytes)
↓

↓
Induced by chronic use: CYP2E1
↓
Acetaldehyde
↓ via ALDH
Acetate → Acetyl-CoA
🧪 Acetaldehyde — The Key Hepatotoxin
  • Direct cellular toxicity to hepatocytes
  • Metabolism of ethanol → acetaldehyde generates reactive oxygen species (ROS)
  • ROS → lipid peroxidation and DNA damage
  • Further hepatocyte injury results

ALDH2 Deficiency — Asian Populations

⚠ High-Yield Ethnic Variant
Inherited deficiency of ALDH2 (acetaldehyde dehydrogenase 2) is common in Asian countries. This leads to acetaldehyde accumulation after alcohol consumption, causing nausea and cutaneous flushing — the classic "Asian flush" reaction.

Mechanisms of Hepatic Steatosis

Multiple converging mechanisms explain why alcohol causes fat to accumulate in the liver:

Pro-Steatosis Mechanisms
  • Acetate → Acetyl-CoA: feeds fatty acid and triglyceride synthesis
  • Epigenetic changes: increase expression of lipogenesis genes
  • NADH/NAD⁺ ratio ↑: reduces mitochondrial β-oxidation
  • Adipose tissue mobilization: ↑ fatty acid release into circulation
  • Intestinal fatty acid mobilization: leads to hepatic accumulation
Anti-Oxidation / Anti-Transport Mechanisms
  • Fatty acid transport genes: suppressed by alcohol (epigenetic)
  • Fatty acid oxidation genes: suppressed by alcohol
  • β-oxidation inhibition: reduced NAD⁺ availability impairs mitochondria
  • Net effect: lipid accumulation in hepatocytes

Progression to Advanced Disease

How does steatosis progress to fibrosis and cirrhosis?

  • Gut microbiome dysbiosis: a subset of patients with alcohol use disorder develop changes in gut microbiome + increased intestinal permeability
  • Bacterial LPS passage: microbial components (like lipopolysaccharide) traverse the portal vein → liver
  • Hepatic inflammation cascade: LPS → hepatic inflammation + hepatocyte death + fibrotic pathway activation
  • Ongoing fibrosis: from sustained alcohol consumption → cirrhosis + portal hypertension
  • Acute alcohol-associated hepatitis: impaired liver regeneration may contribute to acute onset in patients with underlying cirrhosis
💜 Mechanism Link to Clinical Finding
Portal hypertension from cirrhosis → ascites, splenomegaly, variceal formation, and portal-systemic collaterals seen on imaging.
03

Risk Factors for Progression

The Paradox: Why Do Only Some Progress?

Daily alcohol consumption or heavy drinking results in hepatic steatosis, but only 10–20% of such individuals will develop progressive liver disease and cirrhosis. This means behavioral, environmental, and genetic cofactors are essential determinants.

DOGMAS
  • D — Duration & Amount (dose-dependent)
  • O — Obesity / MASLD (MetALD subset)
  • G — Genetics (PNPLA3 chief · MBOAT7 · TM6SF2)
  • M — Microbiome (gut dysbiosis → LPS → inflammation)
  • A — Alcohol pattern (binge · outside meals)
  • S — Smoking + Sex (female develops ALD at lower intake)
  • + Other liver disease: viral hepatitis, hemochromatosis (synergistic)
🔑 Key Concept
Steatosis alone does NOT equal progressive disease. Progression requires additional cofactors beyond alcohol consumption alone.

Modifiable Risk Factors

High Risk — Behavioral
  • Alcohol amount and duration: dose-dependent increase in risk of cirrhosis
  • Binge drinking: particularly dangerous pattern
  • Drinking outside meals: increases risk of progressive ALD
  • Cigarette smoking: independent risk factor for alcohol-associated cirrhosis
Sex and Metabolic Cofactors
  • Female sex: women develop ALD at a lower daily alcohol intake than men
  • Obesity and MASLD: frequent cofactors contributing to ALD progression
  • MetALD: a distinct subset — patients with MASLD AND increased alcohol consumption
  • Viral hepatitis + hemochromatosis: synergistic effects on ALD progression

Genetic Risk Factors

Twin studies demonstrated a genetic predisposition to alcohol-associated liver cirrhosis that is independent from the genetic predisposition to alcohol use disorder itself.

💜 Clinical Pearl — PNPLA3
PNPLA3 polymorphism is the most boards-tested genetic factor in ALD. It also plays a role in MASLD progression. Boards love testing whether you know it is a risk factor for cirrhosis, not for alcohol use disorder itself.

Gut Microbiome

A subset of patients with alcohol use disorder develop dysbiosis and increased intestinal permeability. This enables passage of microbial components (especially bacterial LPS) through the portal vein into the liver, triggering hepatic inflammation, hepatocyte death, and fibrosis activation.

04

Clinical Features

The Silent Majority

The development of alcohol-associated steatosis, steatohepatitis, and even cirrhosis is most often clinically silent. Symptoms arise only once the patient decompensates or develops alcohol-associated hepatitis.

Alcohol-Associated Hepatitis — Diagnostic Criteria

🔴 Key Criteria to Memorize
  • Heavy drinking for >6 months prior to symptom onset:
  •   — Women: >40 g/day
  •   — Men: >50–60 g/day
  • <60 days of abstinence before onset of symptoms
  • Rapid onset of jaundice: serum bilirubin >3 mg/dL

Symptoms and Signs of Alcohol-Associated Hepatitis and Cirrhosis

Presenting Symptoms of Hepatitis
  • Rapid onset jaundice (bilirubin >3 mg/dL)
  • Fever
  • Malaise
  • Tender hepatomegaly
  • Signs of hepatic decompensation (ascites, variceal bleeding, hepatic encephalopathy)
  • Infections: occur in 12–26% of patients at admission
  • SIRS (systemic inflammatory response syndrome)
  • AKI secondary to hepatorenal syndrome
Physical Exam Findings — Cirrhosis
  • Tiredness, malnutrition, sarcopenia
  • Abdomen: hepatomegaly, splenomegaly, caput medusae, ascites
  • Skin: spider angiomata, palmar erythema, jaundice, ecchymoses
  • Eyes: icteric sclerae
  • Hands: Dupuytren contracture
  • Face: rhinophyma
  • Reproductive: gynecomastia, gonadal atrophy, loss of libido, amenorrhea

Neurological Complications

Peripheral Neuropathy

Chronic peripheral nerve damage from alcohol and nutritional deficiencies.

Alcohol Withdrawal Syndrome
  • Tachycardia
  • Agitation, tremor
  • Seizures
  • Delirium tremens
Hepatic Encephalopathy
  • Asterixis (flapping tremor)
  • Forgetfulness
    • DATS
    • D — Delirium (tremens)
    • A — Agitation
    • T — Tachycardia + Tremor
    • S — Seizures
  • Inverted sleep/wake pattern
  • Altered consciousness → confusion → lethargy → coma
    • FAICS
    • F — Forgetfulness (earliest)
    • A — Asterixis (flapping tremor)
    • I — Inverted sleep/wake

    ⚠ Wernicke-Korsakoff Syndrome
    A neuropsychiatric emergency in alcoholics due to thiamine (Vitamin B1) deficiency. Classic Wernicke triad: confusion + ophthalmoplegia + ataxia. Must give IV thiamine BEFORE glucose to avoid precipitating Wernicke's.

    Key Lab Rules — Must Memorize

    🔴 AST Never >300 IU/L in ALD (simple)
    In alcohol-associated liver disease (steatosis/steatohepatitis), serum AST is rarely >300 IU/L. If AST is extremely elevated, think other etiology or ischemic hepatitis.
    🔑 AH: AST <400 IU/L — Absolute Upper Limit
    In alcohol-associated hepatitis, AST and ALT elevations do NOT exceed 400 IU/L. If AST >400 IU/L, consider drug-induced liver injury, ischemic hepatitis, or another etiology — and perform liver biopsy.
    🔑 GGT Elevation
    Steatohepatitis is characterized by elevated AST and γ-glutamyl transferase (GGT). GGT is particularly sensitive for alcohol use — clinically useful as a screening marker.
    ⚠ Steatosis = Normal LFTs
    Patients with simple hepatic steatosis can present with completely normal liver function tests. Normal labs do NOT rule out alcohol-related liver damage.
    06

    Diagnosis

    Screening for Alcohol Use Disorder

    🔑 AUDIT Tool
    The Alcohol Use Disorders Inventory Test (AUDIT) is the validated tool for identifying patients with alcohol use disorder. Diagnosis of ALD requires exclusion of other liver diseases in heavy drinkers.

    Diagnosing Steatosis

    • Ultrasound: first-line, simple, non-invasive — detects hepatic steatosis
    • CT scan: also detects steatosis (lower sensitivity than MRI)
    • MRI: more sensitive than ultrasound
    • MR-PDFF (magnetic resonance proton density fat fraction): noninvasive quantification of hepatic fat — gold-standard non-invasive method
    • CAP (controlled attenuation parameter): ultrasound-based noninvasive fat quantification
    • Liver biopsy: rarely indicated for steatosis/steatohepatitis alone
    💜 Liver Biopsy Histology — Steatosis/Steatohepatitis
    • Hepatocytes with large lipid droplets (macrovesicular steatosis) around pericentral veins (Zone 3)
    • Hepatocyte injury + ballooning with Mallory-Denk bodies
    • Necrosis
    • Lobular inflammation with mononuclear and neutrophilic granulocytes

    Diagnosing and Staging Fibrosis — Non-Invasive

    TestMethodThresholds / Interpretation

    Normal: <6 kPa | Advanced fibrosis (≥F3): >8 kPa | Cirrhosis (F4): >12.5 kPa

    Diagnosing Cirrhosis

    • Imaging (US, MRI, CT): liver nodularity
    • Radiologic signs of portal hypertension: ascites, splenomegaly, portal-systemic collateral vessels
    • Histology: perivenular fibrosis → septal fibrosis → cirrhotic nodules
    ⚠ Prognosis Scores
    Prognosis and risk of mortality assessed using Child-Pugh-Turcotte (CPT) score or Model for End-Stage Liver Disease (MELD or sodium-MELD) score.

    Diagnosing Alcohol-Associated Hepatitis — When to Biopsy

    In most cases, the diagnosis of alcohol-associated hepatitis is clinical. However, a transjugular (TJ) liver biopsy is recommended when confounding factors are present:

    Indications for TJ Liver Biopsy
    • Possible ischemic hepatitis (hypotension, massive GI bleeding, cocaine use, septic shock)
    • Suspected drug-induced liver injury (DILI)
    • Suspected autoimmune liver disease
    • Uncertain alcohol use assessment
    • Atypical labs: AST <50 IU/L or >400 IU/L
    • AST/ALT ratio <1.5
    Histology of Alcohol-Associated Hepatitis
    • Macrovesicular steatosis
    • Hepatocyte ballooning with Mallory-Denk bodies
    • Megamitochondria
    • Neutrophil infiltration
    • Ductular reaction
    • Bilirubinostasis
    • Chicken wire fibrosis
    3M + DNBC
    • 3M: Macrovesicular · Mallory-Denk · Megamitochondria
    • D — Ductular reaction
    • N — Neutrophil infiltration
    • B — Bilirubinostasis
    • C — Chicken wire fibrosis
    🔴 Underlying Cirrhosis
    80% of patients with alcohol-associated hepatitis have underlying cirrhosis. Additionally, 10–20% of patients with a clinical diagnosis of AH will have another liver disease on biopsy.
    ⚠ Infection Workup — Always Do This
    In all patients presenting with alcohol-associated hepatitis: obtain chest X-ray + blood, urine, and ascites cultures routinely to assess for infection.
    07

    Treatment

    Cornerstone — Alcohol Abstinence

    ✅ Most Effective Therapy
    Prolonged alcohol abstinence is the most effective therapy to reduce progression and reverse ALD. Alcohol-associated hepatic steatosis and steatohepatitis are reversible with cessation. Treatment of the underlying alcohol use disorder is an integral part of ALD therapy.

    Currently no approved drugs exist for alcohol-associated steatosis or steatohepatitis with or without fibrosis.

    Management of Cirrhosis Complications

    • Upper GI endoscopy: screen for varices in all cirrhosis patients
    • HCC screening: liver ultrasonography + serum AFP every 6 months in cirrhosis
    • Management of variceal bleeding, ascites, hepatic encephalopathy, and HCC does not differ from cirrhosis of other etiology
    • Liver transplantation: for decompensated cirrhosis or HCC — currently the leading indication for liver transplantation in the United States

    Severity Assessment in Alcohol-Associated Hepatitis

    Short-term mortality in AH is predicted using three scoring systems:

    Maddrey Discriminant Function (MDF)

    Formula: 4.6 × [PT prolongation above control (seconds)] + serum bilirubin (mg/dL)

    • MDF <32: Moderate AH
    • MDF ≥32: Severe AH → high short-term mortality
    MELD Score
    • MELD ≤20: Moderate AH
    • MELD >20: Severe AH → high short-term mortality

    Also: ABIC score (age-bilirubin-INR-creatinine)

    Lille Score (Response Assessment)

    Assessed at 7 days of glucocorticoid treatment:

    • <0.45: Responder → continue prednisolone for 28 days total
    • ≥0.45: Non-responder → STOP prednisolone; consider early LT

    Glucocorticoid Therapy — Details

    Glucocorticoids for Severe AH (MDF ≥32 or MELD >20)

    Mechanism

    Reduces hepatic inflammation and cytokine storm in severe AH. Glucocorticoid use reduces risk of death within 28 days of treatment but NOT in the following 6 months.

    Drugs & Doses

    • Oral prednisolone: 40 mg/day for a total duration of 4 weeks (28 days) — preferred
    • IV methylprednisolone: 32 mg/day — for patients unable to take oral medications

    Adjunct Therapy

    • N-acetylcysteine infusion combined with glucocorticoids might add short-term survival benefit at 1 month

    Response Monitoring — Lille Score at Day 7

    • Lille score <0.45: Continue prednisolone for 28 days total
    • Lille score ≥0.45: Stop prednisolone (non-responder); consider early LT

    Contraindications for Glucocorticoids

    Uncontrolled infection/sepsis AKI / Hepatorenal syndrome Uncontrolled UGI bleeding Active tuberculosis HIV Active HCC Active viral hepatitis Pancreatitis DILI Multiorgan failure Shock
    BIOS
    • B — Bleeding (uncontrolled upper GI)
    • I — Infections (sepsis · active TB · HIV · viral hepatitis)
    • O — Organ failure (AKI / hepatorenal · multiorgan · shock)
    • S — Special liver disease (HCC · DILI · pancreatitis)

    ⚡ Once BIOS resolved → steroids can be started

    💜 Key Pearl
    Glucocorticoids CAN be used once infection, sepsis, and GI bleeding are adequately controlled. The contraindications are not absolute if the offending condition is treated first.

    Nutritional Support

    🔑 Nutritional Goals in AH
    • Enteral nutrition: goal of 35–40 kcal/kg/day
    • Zinc supplementation (micronutrient)
    • Vitamin B1 (thiamine) and Vitamin K supplementation
    • IV albumin: preferred for volume expansion

    Prognosis — Moderate vs. Severe AH

    Moderate AH (MDF <32 / MELD ≤20)
    • 20% risk of 1-year mortality
    • Multidisciplinary management: hepatologist + addiction specialist + dietitian
    • No glucocorticoids
    Severe AH (MDF ≥32 / MELD >20)
    • ~30% mortality at 3 months
    • Glucocorticoids if no contraindications
    • Non-responders (Lille ≥0.45): early LT or supportive/palliative care

    Liver Transplantation in AH

    Long-term prognosis (>1 year) largely depends on alcohol abstinence and underlying cirrhosis severity.

    • Standard: many centers require minimum 6 months of alcohol abstinence for LT evaluation
    • Patients with severe AH nonresponsive to medical therapy have high 30-day mortality and often cannot fulfill the 6-month abstinence requirement
    • Early liver transplantation can be successfully performed in highly selected patients with an excellent psychosocial profile
    • Ineligible non-responders → supportive or palliative care should be considered
    🔴 LT — Leading US Indication
    Liver transplantation for alcohol-associated decompensated cirrhosis or HCC is currently the leading indication for liver transplantation in the United States.

    High-Yield Distinctions

    ALD vs. Viral Hepatitis — AST Pattern

    In ALD: AST > ALT (ratio >1), AST rarely >300. In viral hepatitis: ALT often > AST, both can be in the thousands. This is a classic boards distinction.

    Steatosis vs. Hepatitis — Lab Difference

    Steatosis = normal LFTs. Steatohepatitis = elevated AST + GGT. Hepatitis = AST/ALT <400, bilirubin >3, coagulopathy.

    MetALD — New Category

    MetALD = patients who have BOTH metabolic risk factors (MASLD) AND increased alcohol consumption (20–50 g/day women; 30–60 g/day men). Different from pure ALD.

    Moderate vs. Severe AH — Management Fork

    The critical fork is MDF ≥32 or MELD >20 → glucocorticoids. Moderate AH gets supportive care only — no steroids.

    ★

    Exam Traps

    Expert board exam coaching — Stage A Internal Medicine Boards. Each trap shows exactly how the question is framed and the reasoning to the correct answer.

    Trap 01 The AST >400 IU/L Trap
    A 48-year-old man with a 15-year history of heavy alcohol use presents with jaundice, fever, and tender hepatomegaly. Labs show AST 650 IU/L, ALT 200 IU/L, bilirubin 8.2 mg/dL, INR 1.9. You consider starting prednisolone for alcohol-associated hepatitis. What is the most appropriate next step?
    Correct Answer: Perform transjugular liver biopsy BEFORE starting prednisolone.
    AST >400 IU/L is an atypical laboratory finding for alcohol-associated hepatitis. The ceiling for AH is AST <400 IU/L. When AST exceeds this threshold, alternative etiologies (ischemic hepatitis, drug-induced liver injury, other liver disease) must be excluded. A TJ biopsy is indicated to confirm the diagnosis.
    ⚠ Trap: Most students see jaundice + heavy alcohol use and immediately reach for prednisolone. The board keyword here is AST >400 — this single number should stop you and prompt biopsy first.
    Trap 02 The MDF Threshold — When NOT to Give Steroids
    A 55-year-old woman with AH has Maddrey DF of 28 and MELD score of 18. AST/ALT ratio is 2.1, bilirubin 5 mg/dL, INR 1.7. No infection or GI bleeding. Which is the correct management?
    Correct Answer: Supportive care — alcohol abstinence, nutritional support, multidisciplinary management. Do NOT give glucocorticoids.
    MDF <32 AND MELD ≤20 = Moderate AH. Glucocorticoids are only indicated for SEVERE AH (MDF ≥32 or MELD >20). Giving steroids in moderate AH is incorrect and potentially harmful.
    ⚠ Trap: Students see elevated bilirubin and coagulopathy and assume "severe." You must calculate MDF (4.6 × PT prolongation + bilirubin) and check MELD, not guess. Board keyword: MDF <32 = no steroids.
    Trap 03 Lille Score — When to STOP Steroids
    A 52-year-old man with severe AH (MDF 45, MELD 24) was started on prednisolone 40 mg/day. 7 days later, his Lille score is calculated as 0.52. What is the most appropriate next step?
    Correct Answer: Discontinue prednisolone. Evaluate for early liver transplantation.
    A Lille score ≥0.45 at day 7 identifies non-responders who will unlikely benefit from continued glucocorticoid treatment. Continuing steroids in a non-responder increases infection risk without survival benefit. Early liver transplantation should be considered in eligible patients.
    ⚠ Trap: Many students continue steroids for the planned 28 days regardless of response. The Lille score at day 7 is the mandatory reassessment point. Lille ≥0.45 = stop. Keyword: "day 7 Lille score."
    Trap 04 Glucocorticoids + Active Infection — Timing Matters
    A 44-year-old woman with severe AH (MDF 38) is admitted. She has fever, leukocytosis, and ascites cultures are positive for E. coli. She asks to start steroids. The correct action is:
    Correct Answer: Treat the infection first. Glucocorticoids are contraindicated in uncontrolled infection/sepsis. Once infection is adequately controlled, glucocorticoids may be initiated.
    Active uncontrolled infection is a contraindication to glucocorticoids. However, glucocorticoids are NOT permanently contraindicated — they can be started once the infection is controlled. This is a timing trap, not a permanent contraindication.
    ⚠ Trap: Students either start steroids immediately (wrong — active infection present) or say "steroids are contraindicated in this patient forever" (also wrong). The key is "adequately controlled" — treat, then re-evaluate for steroid eligibility.
    Trap 05 AST/ALT Ratio — ALD vs. Viral Hepatitis
    A 38-year-old man has the following labs: AST 280 IU/L, ALT 95 IU/L, GGT 420 IU/L, bilirubin 2.1 mg/dL. He denies alcohol use. The AST/ALT ratio is approximately 3.0. What is the most likely diagnosis?
    Correct Answer: Alcohol-associated liver disease (likely steatohepatitis) — despite the denial of alcohol use.
    An AST/ALT ratio >2 (especially >1 with elevated GGT) is highly characteristic of ALD. Viral hepatitis typically causes ALT > AST. The pattern here (AST much greater than ALT + elevated GGT) is a classic ALD fingerprint. Denial of drinking is common.
    ⚠ Trap: Boards will give you a patient denying alcohol. The lab pattern gives it away. Remember: ALD → AST > ALT (ratio >1 for ALD, >1.5 for AH). Viral hepatitis → ALT > AST. Keyword: AST/ALT >2 + elevated GGT = ALD.
    Trap 06 FibroScan Thresholds — Which Stage?
    A 50-year-old man with chronic alcohol use undergoes transient elastography (FibroScan). His liver stiffness is 10.2 kPa. What does this indicate?
    Correct Answer: Advanced fibrosis (≥F3). This does NOT yet meet the threshold for cirrhosis (F4).
    FibroScan thresholds in ALD: <6 kPa = normal; >8 kPa = ≥F3 advanced fibrosis; >12.5 kPa = F4 cirrhosis. At 10.2 kPa, this patient has advanced fibrosis (≥F3) but not yet confirmed cirrhosis by stiffness criteria.
    ⚠ Trap: Boards will give a value between 8 and 12.5 kPa and ask if this is "cirrhosis." The answer is no — it's advanced fibrosis. Cirrhosis by FibroScan requires >12.5 kPa. Memorize all three cut-points: 6, 8, 12.5.
    Trap 07 PNPLA3 — Risk for Cirrhosis, NOT for Alcohol Use Disorder
    A 40-year-old man with moderate alcohol use is found to have advanced cirrhosis. His twin brother drinks the same amount but has only fatty liver. Genetic testing reveals a PNPLA3 polymorphism in the patient. This variant is best described as:
    Correct Answer: A genetic risk factor for alcohol-associated liver cirrhosis that is INDEPENDENT of the genetic predisposition to alcohol use disorder.
    PNPLA3 polymorphism increases the risk of alcohol-associated liver cirrhosis through hepatic lipid metabolism pathways — it does not increase the likelihood of developing an alcohol use disorder itself. The twin study data confirm the independence of these two genetic predispositions.
    ⚠ Trap: Students conflate genetic risk for ALD with genetic risk for addiction. These are two separate and independent genetic traits. PNPLA3 = risk for liver disease progression, not for alcohol dependence. Keyword: "independent of alcohol use disorder genetics."
    Trap 08 Zone 3 Steatosis — Biopsy Location
    A liver biopsy from a patient with alcohol use shows large lipid droplets within hepatocytes. A histopathology question asks about the characteristic location of this finding in alcohol-associated steatosis. The answer is:
    Correct Answer: Pericentral veins — Zone 3 (centrilobular region).
    Alcohol-associated steatosis characteristically affects hepatocytes around pericentral veins (Zone 3 = centrilobular). This is distinct from Zone 1 (periportal) steatosis seen in other conditions like cardiac hepatopathy. The morphology is macrovesicular (large fat droplets, not small vesicles).
    ⚠ Trap: Boards love asking about liver zonation. Zone 3 = most susceptible to ALD (low oxygen tension, highest CYP2E1 activity). Zone 1 = affected in ischemia/congestive hepatopathy. Keyword: "Zone 3 + macrovesicular = ALD."
    Trap 09 Early Liver Transplant — The 6-Month Rule Exception
    A 39-year-old man with severe AH non-responsive to glucocorticoids (Lille score 0.71 at day 7) has multiorgan dysfunction. He does not meet the traditional 6-month abstinence criterion for liver transplantation. The best management is:
    Correct Answer: Evaluate for early liver transplantation if he has an excellent psychosocial profile; otherwise, supportive/palliative care.
    Early liver transplantation CAN be performed in highly selected patients with AH who fail medical therapy, even without 6 months of abstinence, provided they have an excellent psychosocial profile. The traditional 6-month rule is a guideline used in many centers, not an absolute rule.
    ⚠ Trap: Students assume "no LT without 6 months abstinence" is absolute. Boards will test whether you know the exception: early LT in highly selected patients with AH who fail medical therapy. Keyword: "excellent psychosocial profile" + "early LT."
    Trap 10 Normal LFTs — Does NOT Rule Out ALD
    A 45-year-old woman drinks 3 glasses of wine nightly (≥2 drinks/day = qualifies as unhealthy drinking). Her AST, ALT, bilirubin, and INR are all completely normal. Can ALD be ruled out?
    Correct Answer: No. Normal LFTs do NOT rule out alcohol-associated liver disease.
    Patients with simple alcohol-associated hepatic steatosis can present with completely normal liver function tests. The absence of elevated enzymes does not mean the liver is unaffected. Ultrasound, MRI, or MR-PDFF may reveal steatosis even with normal labs.
    ⚠ Trap: Boards will present a patient with clear excessive alcohol intake but normal LFTs and ask whether liver disease is present. The correct answer acknowledges that steatosis = clinically silent. Keyword: "normal LFTs + steatosis = still possible."
