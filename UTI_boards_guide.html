01

Definitions & Key Distinctions

UTI refers to infection anywhere along the urinary tract that produces clinical symptoms. This occurs either by bladder invasion with a new pathogen or a shift in the dynamic equilibrium of existing urinary bacteria — both causing local tissue injury and inflammation.

🚨 Critical Distinction — UTI vs ASB
Bacteria in the urine without symptoms = Asymptomatic Bacteriuria (ASB). This is NOT a UTI and usually does NOT require treatment. Both UTI and ASB show pyuria, but the clinical implications are vastly different.

Specific Syndrome Definitions

Cystitis

Symptomatic infection of the bladder. Dysuria, frequency, urgency, suprapubic discomfort, nocturia, hematuria.

Pyelonephritis

Symptomatic infection of the kidneys. Fever, rigors, flank/costovertebral angle pain, nausea/vomiting ± cystitis symptoms.

Prostatitis

Symptomatic infection of the prostate. ABP: dysuria, fever, perineal pain. CBP: recurrent cystitis episodes.

CAUTI

Catheter-associated UTI. Requires symptoms (localizing or systemic) + bacteriuria ≥10³ CFU/mL + pyuria. Bacteriuria alone ≠ CAUTI.

Complicated vs Uncomplicated UTI (Updated Approach)

Uncomplicated UTI
  • Infection confined to the bladder
  • In a man or woman without urinary catheter
  • Each episode can be treated as uncomplicated even in recurrent UTI
  • Key: think about bladder confinement, not just patient demographics
Complicated UTI
  • Extends beyond the bladder: prostatitis, pyelonephritis, bacteremia
  • UTI in the setting of intermittent or indwelling catheter
  • Requires antimicrobials reaching blood AND renal tissue levels
  • May require imaging to identify structural source
⚠ Updated Concept
The old approach automatically called UTI in diabetics, immunocompromised, or men "complicated." The new updated approach focuses on whether infection extends beyond the bladder, not on patient risk category alone. This more closely reflects actual clinical practice.

When ASB Should Be Treated (Only 2 Exceptions)

✓ Treat ASB In
  • Pregnant patients: Untreated ASB → pyelonephritis, preterm delivery, low birth weight. Screen via urine culture and treat.
  • Patients undergoing invasive urologic procedures causing mucosal injury (e.g., lithotripsy, transurethral resection of the prostate) — bacterial translocation risk.
🚫 Do NOT Treat ASB In

Everyone else — including elderly, diabetics, catheterized patients (without symptoms), renal transplant recipients. Treating ASB may actually increase future UTI risk by allowing more virulent/resistant pathogens to colonize.

02

Epidemiology & Risk Factors

Prevalence by Age and Sex

Neonates

Slightly higher in males — congenital urinary tract anomalies more common in male infants.

1–50 years

Predominantly a female condition. Most male UTI occurs in context of catheterization or anatomic abnormality.

After 50

Prostatic hypertrophy → obstruction → UTI incidence in men nearly equals women.

  • ASB prevalence: ~5% in women ages 20–40; up to 40–50% in elderly women and men
  • Up to 80% of women develop at least one UTI during their lifetime
  • Lifetime UTI prevalence in men: ~14%
  • Recurrence: 20–30% of women with one UTI will have recurrent episodes; rate 0.3–7.6 infections/patient/year, average 2.6/year
  • Early recurrence (<2 weeks): Usually relapse (not reinfection) — evaluate for sequestered focus of infection

Risk Factor Categories

Impaired Urine Outflow
  • Congenital anomalies
  • Cystoceles, pelvic organ prolapse
  • Bladder dysfunction / overflow incontinence
  • Benign prostatic hypertrophy
  • Neurogenic bladder
Bacterial Entry
  • Sexual activity (most common) — RR 1.4–4.8 based on frequency
  • Insertive rectal intercourse (men)
  • Lack of circumcision (E. coli colonizes glans/prepuce)
  • Urinary instrumentation / catheters
  • Foreign bodies: stents, kidney stones (biofilm)
Disrupted Mucosal Defense
  • Spermicide use (toxic to vaginal lactobacilli)
  • Diaphragm use
  • Menopause (loss of lactobacilli → Gram-negative colonization)
  • Diabetes (2–3× higher UTI rate)
  • SGLT-2 inhibitors (glucosuria)
🔑 Most Consistently Documented Risk Factors for Recurrent UTI
Frequent sexual intercourse + spermicide use. Also: new sexual partner, first UTI before age 15, maternal history of UTI (hereditary component).

Diabetes and UTI

Women with diabetes have 2–3× higher rates of ASB and UTI vs. those without. Risk increases with longer diabetes duration and need for insulin. Mechanisms: impaired cytokine secretion, bladder dysfunction from neuropathy. Glycosuria as a mechanism remains controversial. The FDA has issued a warning linking SGLT-2 inhibitors to UTI risk.

💜 Bladder Microbiome Pearl
Genetic sequencing consistently shows more bacterial species in the bladder than routine culture identifies — in both symptomatic and asymptomatic states. The healthy bladder is often not a sterile site.

Antimicrobial Resistance — The Growing Problem

  • Resistance >20% to TMP-SMX, fluoroquinolones, and nitrofurantoin among ambulatory US patients in many regions
  • ~6% of isolates resistant to three or more antibiotic classes
  • ~9% of isolates have extended-spectrum β-lactamase (ESBL) production
  • Similar rates in South America and Europe
⚠ Resistance Caveat
Antibiogram data may overestimate resistance in healthy ambulatory patients because cultures are typically only sent in complicated or recurrent UTI cases — which skew toward more resistant organisms.
04

Pathogenesis

The urinary tract is an anatomic unit linked by a continuous urine column from kidneys to urethra. Three factors interact to determine whether infection results: Host · Pathogen · Environment.

Routes of Infection

Ascending (Most Common — >98%)
  1. Periurethral/vaginal colonization with intestinal flora (usually E. coli)
  2. Bacteria ascend urethra → bladder
  3. Continue up ureter → renal parenchyma (pyelonephritis)

Colonization of vaginal introitus + periurethral area is the critical initial step. Sexual intercourse increases vaginal E. coli colonization. Nonoxynol-9 in spermicide kills normal lactobacilli → promotes colonization.

Hematogenous (<2%)
  • Bacteremia precedes UTI
  • Pathogens: relatively virulent organisms — S. aureus, Salmonella
  • Isolation of either → consider concomitant bacteremia
  • Can cause focal renal abscesses or pyelonephritis
  • Candida: hematogenous route MORE common in candiduria

Host Factors

  • Familial/hereditary susceptibility: Women with recurrent UTI more likely to have had first UTI before age 15 and maternal history of UTI
  • Blood group antigens: Not expressing certain antigens facilitates E. coli adherence to urothelium (threefold more binding in recurrent UTI patients)
  • Innate immune mutations: Toll-like receptor and IL-8 receptor gene mutations linked to recurrent UTI and pyelonephritis
  • Anatomy: Short female urethra is the primary reason UTI predominantly affects young women (vs. young men)

Microbial Virulence Factors (E. coli)

P Fimbriae

Hairlike protein structures binding blood-group antigen P (d-galactose-d-galactose). Critical for pyelonephritis pathogenesis and subsequent bloodstream invasion from kidney. "P" = ability to bind P antigen.

Type 1 Pili

Present on all E. coli but not always expressed. Key role in initiating bladder infection. Mediate binding to mannose on the luminal surface of bladder uroepithelial cells.

  • Additional virulence factors: Toxins, iron-acquisition systems (siderophores), biofilm formation, bacterial capsules
  • Intracellular bacterial communities: Demonstrated in animal models and exfoliated human urothelial cells — clinical impact in humans not yet clear

Environmental Factors

  • Postmenopausal women: Loss of lactobacilli → Gram-negative colonization due to vaginal atrophy. Topical estrogens reduce recurrent UTI frequency without altering systemic hormone levels. Oral estrogens should NOT be used for UTI prevention due to systemic side effects.
  • Anatomic/functional abnormalities: Vesicoureteral reflux, ureteral obstruction (BPH), neurogenic bladder, urinary diversion all create environments predisposing to UTI
  • Foreign bodies: Catheters and stones provide inert surfaces for biofilm formation → nidus for recurrent infection
  • Pregnancy: Inhibition of ureteral peristalsis + decreased ureteral tone → vesicoureteral reflux → important in pyelonephritis pathogenesis during pregnancy
05

Clinical Syndromes

Asymptomatic Bacteriuria (ASB)

Diagnosed when a patient has a positive urine culture but lacks localizing urinary symptoms or unexplained systemic symptoms. Typically detected incidentally during screening urine culture.

🚨 The Altered Mental Status Trap

Most challenging scenario: elderly patient with isolated altered mentation. Key approach:

  1. Establish whether there is evidence of sepsis first
  2. If no sepsis: can empirical antibiotics be safely withheld while workup is ongoing?
  3. Seek collateral history from caregivers
  4. Rule out other causes of delirium (including time-sensitive ones)
  5. A history of "recurrent UTIs" may actually reflect persistent ASB being overtreated

Degree of pyuria should NOT influence the ASB vs UTI decision — very high pyuria is common in urinary stasis (ESRD, obstructed catheters).

Cystitis

Typical Symptoms
  • Dysuria
  • Urinary frequency
  • Urinary urgency
  • Suprapubic discomfort/tenderness
  • New nocturia, hesitancy
  • Gross hematuria
Red Flags (NOT simple cystitis)
  • Fever
  • Rigors
  • Unilateral back or flank pain
  • → Investigate for kidney/prostate/bloodstream involvement

Women: Cystitis can be treated based on history alone if symptoms are typical. Probability of cystitis/pyelonephritis >90% if: ≥1 urinary symptom + no vaginal discharge + no complicating factors + risk factors present.

Men: Cystitis is uncommon except with catheterization/obstructive uropathy. Always obtain urine culture in men to differentiate cystitis from ABP/CBP (documented bacteriuria distinguishes them from chronic pelvic pain syndrome).

Prostatitis

Acute Bacterial Prostatitis (ABP)
  • Dysuria, frequency, pain in prostatic/pelvic/perineal area
  • Severe illness — fever, rigors, bladder outlet obstruction
  • Suspect in men with UTI + systemic symptoms without clear anatomic location (e.g., not clearly pyelonephritis)
  • Treat: ≥2 weeks, potentially up to 4 weeks; fluoroquinolone or TMP-SMX preferred (prostate penetration)
  • If prostatic abscess: urology consult
Chronic Bacterial Prostatitis (CBP)
  • Recurrent cystitis episodes (± pelvic/perineal pain)
  • Systemic signs typically absent
  • Often ambulatory evaluation
  • Treat: 4–6 weeks antibiotics; recurrences may warrant up to 12 weeks
  • Far less common than chronic pelvic pain syndrome (which is not bacterial and does NOT improve with antibiotics)
🔑 Febrile UTI in Men — Important Points
  • Elevated PSA + enlarged prostate/seminal vesicles on US → suggestive of prostate involvement
  • Urinary retention, early recurrence, hematuria at follow-up, voiding difficulties → predictive of surgically correctable disorders
  • Men with febrile UTI (even first episode) should have imaging (CT or US) to evaluate for anatomic abnormalities or obstruction

Pyelonephritis

Fever is a main feature distinguishing pyelonephritis from cystitis. "Febrile UTI" often = pyelonephritis or infection extending beyond the bladder.

Mild

Low-grade fever ± lower back/costovertebral angle pain.

Severe

High fever, rigors, nausea, vomiting, flank pain. Acute onset; may or may not follow cystitis symptoms.

Complications
  • Acute papillary necrosis: sloughed papillae → ureteral obstruction
  • Emphysematous pyelonephritis: gas in renal/perinephric tissue; almost exclusively in poorly controlled diabetes
  • Xanthogranulomatous pyelonephritis: chronic obstruction (often staghorn calculi) + chronic infection → suppurative destruction; yellow-colored residual tissue (lipid-laden macrophages)
  • Intraparenchymal abscess: continued fever/bacteremia despite antibiotics

CAUTI

Definition: symptoms (localizing urinary OR systemic such as fever) + bacteriuria ≥10³ CFU/mL + pyuria. Key challenge: bacteriuria is inevitable with catheterization, so pyuria + bacteriuria alone in catheterized patients do NOT indicate CAUTI. Must systematically rule out other infectious and noninfectious causes.

06

Diagnostic Approach

Key Diagnostic Questions

  1. Does the patient actually have UTI (vs. ASB or non-infectious urinary symptoms)?
  2. If UTI: confined to bladder (cystitis), or extending beyond (pyelonephritis, prostatitis)?
  3. If uncertain: can empirical antibiotics safely be delayed while pursuing more diagnostic certainty?

History as Diagnostic Tool

  • Probability of cystitis/pyelonephritis: 50% with ≥1 urinary symptom + no complicating factors
  • Probability ~90%: ≥1 urinary symptom + NO vaginal discharge + NO complicating factors + UTI risk factors present → no further lab evaluation needed before treating
  • Urine culture before treatment if: concern for resistant pathogens (recurrent UTI, prior treatment failure, known resistant organism colonization, high local resistance prevalence)
⚠ STI Differential
Women with multiple sexual partners + inconsistent condom use: at risk for both UTI and STIs. Dysuria may be due to cervicitis (C. trachomatis, N. gonorrhoeae), vaginitis (Candida, Trichomonas), herpetic urethritis, or interstitial cystitis. Vaginal discharge → STI evaluation. Consider STI screening at time of empirical UTI treatment.

Urine Dipstick (Nitrite + Leukocyte Esterase)

Nitrite
  • Only Enterobacterales convert nitrate → nitrite
  • False positive: Enterobacterales ASB (bacteria present, no symptoms)
  • False negative: UTI due to non-Enterobacterales organisms (Pseudomonas, enterococci, Staph) OR high oral fluid intake → frequent voiding prevents nitrite accumulation
Leukocyte Esterase
  • Detects enzyme in neutrophils (intact OR lysed)
  • Less sensitive/specific surrogate for pyuria vs microscopy
  • Negative dipstick (both nitrite AND LE negative) → consider diagnoses other than UTI
  • NOT sufficient to rule out bacteriuria in pregnant women → urine culture required

Urine Microscopy

  • Pyuria present in nearly all cases of cystitis
  • Hematuria in ~30% of cystitis cases
  • Classic threshold: >10 WBCs/HPF — but this has poor specificity (36%) for UTI in older women
  • Modern data: threshold closer to 250 WBCs/HPF may better correlate with urinary symptoms in older women
  • Despite this, >10 WBCs/HPF remains the generally accepted standard

Urine Culture

  • Results available ≥24h after presentation; susceptibilities require additional 24–48h
  • Colony count threshold: >10² bacteria/mL is more sensitive (95%) than 10⁵/mL for diagnosing acute cystitis in women
  • Mixed cultures = contamination in most cases (except: long-term catheterization, chronic urinary retention, GI/genital fistula)
  • Positive culture = consistent with both UTI AND ASB → clinical symptoms required to distinguish
🔑 Diagnostic Stewardship
Do NOT send urine cultures in patients lacking urinary or systemic symptoms suggesting UTI. This is the key intervention to reduce misdiagnosis and inappropriate ASB treatment. A urine culture does NOT need to be obtained at catheter placement/removal in asymptomatic patients.
07

Treatment

General Principles of Antimicrobial Therapy

  • Antimicrobial therapy is warranted for any true (symptomatic) UTI
  • Non-antibiotic treatment (e.g., NSAIDs alone) delays clinical response and predisposes to more invasive infections — not adequate substitute
  • Collateral damage: adverse ecologic effects — killing normal flora (→ C. diff risk), selecting resistant organisms. Drugs with minimal fecal flora effect: pivmecillinam, fosfomycin, nitrofurantoin. High collateral damage: TMP-SMX, quinolones, late-generation cephalosporins
⚠ Risk Factors for Antimicrobial Resistance
  • Prior culture with resistant organism to that antimicrobial
  • Recent exposure to that antimicrobial (greatest risk: fluoroquinolones)
  • Travel to area with high resistance to that drug
  • Urinary catheterization, prior antibiotics of any class, residence in healthcare facility

Drug-by-Drug Analysis for Cystitis

Nitrofurantoin — Preferred Agent

Mechanism

Reduced by bacterial enzymes to reactive intermediates that damage DNA, ribosomes, and cell-wall synthesis — multiple simultaneous targets.

Dose & Duration

  • 100 mg twice daily for 5–7 days (women and men)
  • 5-day course as effective as 3-day TMP-SMX for uncomplicated cystitis

Resistance

Remains low despite >70 years of use — several mutational steps required. Highly active against E. coli.

Intrinsically Resistant Organisms

Contraindications / Cautions

⚠ Long-Term Use Warning
Advise AGAINST multi-year prophylactic nitrofurantoin due to risk of sometimes irreversible pulmonary toxicities.
TMP-SMX — Preferred (Where Susceptible)

Mechanism

Trimethoprim inhibits dihydrofolate reductase; sulfamethoxazole inhibits dihydropteroate synthase — sequential blockade of folate synthesis.

Dose & Duration

  • 1 double-strength (DS) tablet twice daily
  • Women: 3 days | Men: 7 days

Use When

  • Local antibiogram doesn't clearly favor other preferred agents
  • Recent microbiology shows TMP-SMX–susceptible uropathogen
  • Patient has established relationship with provider and can seek care if symptoms don't resolve
💜 Clinical Pearl
Affects fecal flora significantly — contributes to resistance selection. High collateral damage agent.
Fosfomycin — Preferred (Low Collateral Damage)

Mechanism

Inhibits bacterial cell-wall formation (MurA enzyme). Selective concentration in urine.

Dose & Duration

  • Women: 3-g sachet powder dissolved in water — single dose (1 day)
  • Men: 3-g sachet every other day × 3 doses

Advantages

  • Easy dosing; low collateral damage; retains efficacy against MDR E. coli including ESBL producers
  • Used off-label in prostatitis for organisms resistant to other oral agents
⚠ Limitations
Activity against non-E. coli Enterobacterales is less reliable. Susceptibility testing difficult (not typically in automated reports). Renal tissue levels low — do NOT use for pyelonephritis.
Pivmecillinam — Preferred (US approval 2024)

Mechanism

Beta-lactam prodrug; inhibits penicillin-binding protein 2 (PBP2). Active against many MDR E. coli including some ESBL producers.

Dose & Duration

  • 400 mg twice daily
  • Women: 3–7 days
💜 Pearl
Low collateral damage. One of the preferred agents for uncomplicated cystitis. Limited by being relatively new to US practice.
Fluoroquinolones (Ciprofloxacin, Levofloxacin) — Alternative; Use Cautiously

Mechanism

Inhibit DNA gyrase (GyrA) and topoisomerase IV → block DNA replication and transcription.

Dose & Duration

  • Dose varies by agent
  • Women: 3 days | Men: 7 days
  • Moxifloxacin: may NOT reach adequate urinary levels — avoid for UTI

Why They're Alternatives (Not First-Line) for Cystitis

  • High collateral damage — drives resistance not just in uropathogens but in organisms causing other serious infections
  • Rare but serious toxicities: tendon rupture (Achilles), retinal detachment, aortic aneurysm, neuropathy, aortic dissection
  • FDA advisory: avoid for uncomplicated cystitis in women when alternatives available

When Preferred

Men with concern for prostate involvement — superior prostate penetration (preferred over β-lactams in this setting). Also preferred for pyelonephritis (oral step-down).

β-Lactams — Alternative; Less Effective

Duration

  • 5–7 days even in uncomplicated cystitis
  • Higher clinical failure rates vs. fluoroquinolones or TMP-SMX

Why Less Effective in Women

Fail to eradicate uropathogens from the vaginal reservoir — rates of pathogen eradication lower, relapse rates higher. In contrast, treatment success with cephalexin may be as good in male UTI (no vaginal reservoir).

⚠ Amoxicillin
Widespread resistance in E. coli; many TMP-SMX–resistant strains also resist oral beta-lactams. Preferred agents if susceptibility confirmed: cefpodoxime, cefixime.

Nitrofurantoin, pivmecillinam, and fosfomycin do NOT reach adequate renal tissue levels → NEVER use for pyelonephritis. They are bladder-only drugs.

Urinary Analgesics

Phenazopyridine: widely used for bladder discomfort; can cause significant nausea. Combination analgesics (methenamine + methylene blue + sodium phosphate + hyoscyamine) also available. Important: symptomatic treatment alone is NOT a substitute for antibiotics in true UTI.

Special Populations

UTI in Pregnant Patients

🚨 Pregnancy — Critical Points

Untreated ASB in pregnancy → pyelonephritis, preterm delivery, low birth weight. Screen and treat!

DrugSafety in PregnancyNotes
Aminopenicillins (ampicillin, amoxicillin) + Cephalosporins✅ Drugs of choiceWidespread amoxicillin resistance → avoid empirically without culture results
Nitrofurantoin⚠ Use cautiouslyACOG: can use during all trimesters if β-lactams not an option. One retrospective study suggested 1st trimester birth defect association — not confirmed.
TMP-SMX (sulfonamides)⚠ Avoid if possibleAvoid 1st trimester (possible teratogenicity) and near term (neonatal kernicterus risk). ACOG: can use as potential option if no alternatives.
Fluoroquinolones❌ AvoidPossible adverse effects on fetal cartilage development.
  • ASB or cystitis in pregnancy: treat 5–7 days (shorter durations inadequately studied)
  • Pyelonephritis in pregnancy: parenteral β-lactam ± aminoglycosides (guidelines recommend; based on limited observational data + historic norms)

Candiduria

  • Increasingly common with indwelling catheterization, ICU, broad-spectrum antibiotics, diabetes
  • >50% of urinary Candida isolates are non-albicans species
  • Asymptomatic candiduria: Remove catheter (resolves in >1/3 of cases). Do NOT treat routinely — no reduction in recurrence with treatment
  • Treat candiduria if: symptomatic cystitis/pyelonephritis, OR asymptomatic but neutropenic, pre-urologic procedure, or low-birth-weight infant
  • Non-instrumented immunocompetent patient with Candida in urine: Genital contamination vs. widespread visceral dissemination — evaluate carefully
  • Fluconazole 200–400 mg/day × 7–14 days: first-line. Bladder irrigation with amphotericin B: generally NOT recommended

Prevention of Recurrent UTI in Women

A preventive strategy is indicated when recurrent UTIs interfere with the patient's lifestyle. The threshold of ≥3 symptomatic episodes/year is not absolute — shared decision-making with each patient guides the approach.

Initial Evaluation of Recurrent Cystitis

  1. Confirm diagnosis of recurrent UTI
  2. Imaging to identify renal calculi or another potential nidus
  3. Assess for urinary retention
  4. Consider referral: urology, urogynecology, and/or pelvic physical therapy if anatomic abnormality suspected

Non-Antimicrobial Prophylactic Approaches

Increased Fluid Intake

Observational data suggests reducing recurrent UTIs. Generally well tolerated in healthy adults.

Methenamine Hippurate

Converted to antiseptic formaldehyde in the bladder. Two recent clinical trials: well tolerated, may reduce recurrent UTIs as much as antibiotic prophylaxis.

Cranberry Products

Recent Cochrane review of RCTs: significant benefit found, though optimal product and dosing unknown.

Topical Estrogens (Postmenopausal)

Demonstrated to reduce recurrent UTI frequency without altering systemic hormone levels. Oral estrogens should NOT be used for UTI prevention.

⚠ Lactobacillus Probiotics
One factorial trial: intravaginal (not oral) lactobacilli products reduce recurrent UTI. Studies of oral probiotics: mixed results. Caution in immunocompromised patients — probiotic-associated invasive infections described. D-mannose products: mixed results.

Antibiotic Prophylactic Strategies

Continuous Prophylaxis

Daily or thrice-weekly low-dose TMP-SMX or nitrofurantoin. Typically prescribed for 6 months, then discontinued. Highly effective during active intake. Can reinstitute if infections recur.

Postcoital Prophylaxis

One dose after intercourse. Low-dose TMP-SMX or nitrofurantoin. Ideal when UTI is clearly associated with sexual activity.

Patient-Initiated Therapy

Patient supplied with materials for urine culture + antibiotic course. Self-medicates at first symptoms. Culture refrigerated, sent to office for confirmation. Urine culture may be omitted when established, reliable patient–provider relationship exists and episodes respond completely to short-course therapy.

💜 Resistance Consideration
Selection of resistant strains in fecal flora documented with prophylactic antibiotics. Consider alternating antibiotic prophylaxis with periods of non-antimicrobial prevention — allows intermittent gut microbiome recovery. Choice between methenamine and cranberry: shared decision-making + individual response.

Exam Traps

Expert board exam coaching — Stage A Internal Medicine. Each trap shows exactly how the question is framed, the tempting wrong answer, and the reasoning to the correct answer.

Trap 01ASB vs UTI — The Demented Elder
An 82-year-old woman in a nursing home is brought in by staff for "confusion worse than usual." She has a history of dementia and "recurrent UTIs." Urine dipstick shows 3+ leukocytes. Urine culture grows E. coli. The most appropriate next step is to start antibiotics for a UTI.
Correct Answer: Do NOT start antibiotics for UTI — diagnose and treat ASB appropriately.
Without localizing urinary symptoms (dysuria, frequency, urgency), pyuria + bacteriuria in an elderly patient most likely represents ASB, not UTI. The altered mentation has many other causes. The "recurrent UTI" history likely reflects persistent ASB being overtreated.
⚠ Trap: The positive culture + pyuria + worsening confusion makes students jump to UTI. The keyword is absence of localizing urinary symptoms. Workup should rule out other causes of delirium first. Treat ASB → increases future UTI risk by selecting more resistant pathogens.
Trap 02Nitrofurantoin — Wrong Drug for Wrong Syndrome
A 28-year-old woman presents with 2 days of dysuria, urinary frequency, and right flank pain with fever of 38.9°C. Urine culture grows E. coli. She is otherwise healthy and tolerating oral fluids. You prescribe nitrofurantoin 100 mg BID for 5 days.
Correct Answer: This is WRONG — nitrofurantoin should NOT be used for pyelonephritis.
Nitrofurantoin does not reach significant levels in renal tissue — it is concentrated in urine only. Flank pain + fever indicates pyelonephritis (beyond the bladder). Use TMP-SMX, ciprofloxacin, or levofloxacin for outpatient pyelonephritis.
⚠ Trap: Nitrofurantoin is excellent for cystitis and the student remembers it's a preferred agent. The keyword is flank pain + fever = pyelonephritis = renal tissue invasion = needs tissue-level antibiotic. Also avoid fosfomycin and pivmecillinam for the same reason.
Trap 03CAUTI — Culture Without Symptoms
A 70-year-old post-op man has an indwelling urinary catheter. His nurse notices turbid urine and sends a urine culture "just to check." It grows Candida albicans 10⁵ CFU/mL with pyuria. He has no fever, no change in vitals, and no urinary complaints. What is the correct management?
Correct Answer: Do NOT treat — this is catheter-associated ASB (CA-ASB). Remove the catheter if no longer needed.
Bacteriuria and candiduria are expected in catheterized patients. Without symptoms, this is CA-ASB. No additional workup or antimicrobial treatment is needed. Removing the catheter resolves candiduria in >1/3 of asymptomatic cases.
⚠ Trap: High colony count + fungal organism alarms students. The keyword is no symptoms. Pyuria alone in catheterized patients has no diagnostic value. Do NOT send urine cultures in asymptomatic catheterized patients — diagnostic stewardship principle.
Trap 04Fluoroquinolone for Uncomplicated Cystitis — FDA Warning
A 24-year-old healthy woman with no prior UTI history presents with classic acute cystitis symptoms. She has no drug allergies. Local antibiogram shows E. coli susceptibility to ciprofloxacin >95%. Which antibiotic is most appropriate?
Correct Answer: Nitrofurantoin, TMP-SMX, fosfomycin, or pivmecillinam — NOT ciprofloxacin.
Despite high local susceptibility, the FDA advises against fluoroquinolones for uncomplicated cystitis in women when alternatives exist. Preferred agents: nitrofurantoin, TMP-SMX (if susceptibility confirmed), fosfomycin, or pivmecillinam.
⚠ Trap: High susceptibility makes ciprofloxacin look like a logical first choice. The keyword is "alternatives available" — FDA explicitly advises against FQs for uncomplicated cystitis. Reasons: collateral damage driving resistance + rare serious toxicities (tendon rupture, aortic aneurysm).
Trap 05ASB in Pregnancy — Always Screen and Treat
A 26-year-old at 14 weeks gestation has no urinary complaints. Routine prenatal urine culture grows E. coli 10⁵ CFU/mL. She feels well. What is the correct management?
Correct Answer: Treat the ASB with antibiotics (cephalosporin or amoxicillin based on susceptibility) for 5–7 days.
Pregnancy is one of only TWO exceptions to the general rule that ASB should not be treated. Untreated ASB in pregnancy risks pyelonephritis, preterm delivery, and low birth weight.
⚠ Trap: Students remember "ASB doesn't need treatment" as a general rule and apply it here. The keyword is pregnancy — automatic exception. The other exception: patients undergoing invasive urologic procedures. In ALL other populations, do not treat ASB.
Trap 06S. aureus in Urine = Look for Bacteremia
A 55-year-old man with no urinary catheter has a urine culture that grows Staphylococcus aureus. He has mild dysuria. Is this simply a UTI to be treated with standard oral antibiotics?
Correct Answer: NO — Staphylococcus aureus in urine should prompt consideration of concomitant bacteremia and hematogenous seeding.
S. aureus (and Salmonella) isolated from urine culture warrant blood cultures and evaluation for bacteremia. UTI due to these organisms usually results from hematogenous spread, not ascending infection.
⚠ Trap: Students treat S. aureus UTI like any other UTI. The keyword is S. aureus = hematogenous = check blood cultures. The hematogenous route is uncommon (<2% of UTIs overall) but these two specific pathogens should always raise this concern.
Trap 07Emphysematous Pyelonephritis — Who Gets It?
Imaging shows gas within the renal parenchyma and perinephric space of a 58-year-old patient. Which underlying condition is most strongly associated with this finding?
Correct Answer: Poorly controlled diabetes mellitus.
Emphysematous pyelonephritis occurs almost exclusively in patients with poorly controlled diabetes. Gas-forming facultative anaerobes (most often E. coli) destroy renal parenchyma and track gas through the retroperitoneal space.
⚠ Trap: Students may think of emphysematous conditions broadly (emphysematous cholecystitis, etc.) without recognizing the near-exclusive diabetes link. The keyword is gas in kidney = emphysematous pyelonephritis = poorly controlled diabetes. Treatment may include percutaneous drainage followed by elective nephrectomy.
Trap 08Prostatitis vs. Cystitis in Men — Duration Trap
A 42-year-old man presents with perineal pain, dysuria, fever of 39°C, and difficulty urinating. Urine culture grows E. coli susceptible to ciprofloxacin. You plan to treat him the same as uncomplicated cystitis with 7 days of ciprofloxacin.
Correct Answer: This presentation suggests Acute Bacterial Prostatitis (ABP) — requires at least 2–4 weeks of ciprofloxacin or TMP-SMX, not 7 days.
Perineal/prostatic pain + fever + bladder outlet obstruction = ABP. Fluoroquinolones or TMP-SMX are preferred for prostatic penetration. Duration ≥2 weeks, potentially up to 4 weeks.
⚠ Trap: 7 days is correct for uncomplicated cystitis in men, but prostatitis requires much longer courses. The keyword is perineal pain + fever + obstructive symptoms = ABP = minimum 2–4 weeks. CBP may need 4–12 weeks. These durations need modern RCTs to clarify.
Trap 09β-Lactams in Women — Vaginal Reservoir
A 30-year-old woman is given cephalexin for uncomplicated cystitis due to concerns about TMP-SMX resistance. She returns 1 week later with recurrent symptoms. Repeat culture grows the same E. coli. Why did treatment fail?
Correct Answer: β-Lactams fail to eradicate uropathogens from the vaginal reservoir in women, leading to higher relapse rates.
Unlike TMP-SMX or fluoroquinolones, beta-lactams do not effectively clear E. coli from the vaginal/periurethral reservoir. The bacteria re-colonize the bladder. In male UTI, cephalexin success rates may be comparable to other agents (no vaginal reservoir).
⚠ Trap: β-Lactams seem intuitive as antibiotics and are prescribed broadly. The keyword is vaginal reservoir → β-lactams fail → higher relapse in women. This explains why β-lactams require longer courses even in uncomplicated cystitis and are considered alternative agents, not preferred.
Trap 10Nitrite-Negative Dipstick — Does NOT Rule Out UTI
A 35-year-old woman presents with dysuria and urinary frequency. Urine dipstick is negative for nitrites. Is UTI ruled out?
Correct Answer: No — a negative nitrite ALONE does not rule out UTI.
Nitrites are only produced by Enterobacterales. A negative nitrite result could indicate UTI due to non-Enterobacterales organisms (S. saprophyticus, enterococci, Pseudomonas). However, a dipstick negative for BOTH nitrite AND leukocyte esterase should prompt consideration of diagnoses other than UTI.
⚠ Trap: Students use nitrite-negative to exclude UTI. The keyword is both negative (nitrite + LE) = consider other diagnoses. Single negative nitrite ≠ rule-out. S. saprophyticus (common in young women) is nitrite-negative. Context matters: if symptoms are highly specific, urine microscopy or culture may still be needed.
