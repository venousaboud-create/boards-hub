Aortic Stenosis & Aortic Regurgitation

A mechanism-first teaching guide. Every murmur, peripheral sign, echo criterion, and surgical threshold traced back to the underlying physics of the lesion. Built strictly from Harrison's Ch. 272 and Ch. 273.

01

Framing: pressure vs volume overload

Before any detail — fix the two diseases in your mind by the type of load they place on the left ventricle. Everything else follows.

AORTIC STENOSIS

Pressure overload

A fixed obstruction at the valve. The LV must generate a much higher systolic pressure to push blood through a narrowed orifice.

Response: concentric hypertrophy — wall thickens, chamber size stays normal. Laplace's law: thicker wall normalizes wall stress.

Late failure: diastolic dysfunction → systolic dysfunction → afterload mismatch → drop in EF and CO.

AORTIC REGURGITATION

Volume overload (chronic) — also pressure

Each beat, the LV ejects a normal forward stroke volume PLUS the regurgitant volume that leaked back. The chamber gets repeatedly stretched.

Response: eccentric hypertrophy + dilation — the LV becomes one of the largest hearts encountered at autopsy (sometimes >1000 g).

Late failure: end-diastolic volume rises, forward stroke volume falls, EF drops.

AS and AR are both "left-sided regurgitant/obstructive" diseases, but they are mechanically opposite.
  • AS = thick wall, normal cavity → preserves EF for years, then decompensates suddenly.
  • AR = dilated cavity, eccentric hypertrophy → preserves EF for years through Frank-Starling, then deteriorates often before symptoms appear.
This is why AS is followed by symptoms (wait for them), but AR is often followed by serial echo measurements (intervene before symptoms).
02

AS — etiology & pathogenesis

AS is the most common valve lesion in adults with chronic valvular disease, and the majority of symptomatic adult patients are male. Three major causes:

EtiologyTypical ageKey features
Degenerative calcific (trileaflet)70–80sMost common in high-income countries. Active fibrocalcific process, not passive wear.
Bicuspid aortic valve (BAV)50–60s (one to two decades earlier)Congenital. ~53% of surgical AS specimens are bicuspid; 4% unicuspid. Associated aortopathy in 30–40%.
RheumaticYounger in endemic areasCommissural fusion → bicuspid appearance. Almost always with mitral involvement and some AR.
RadiationVariableLate scarring/fibrosis/calcification — also affects mitral annulus.

The pathogenesis is active, not passive

Calcific AS shares many features with vascular atherosclerosis:

  • Endothelial dysfunction → lipid (LDL, Lp(a)) and inflammatory cell infiltration of leaflets
  • Cytokine release → cellular activation
  • Valvular myofibroblasts differentiate into osteoblasts → actively produce bone matrix proteins
  • Deposition of calcium hydroxyapatite crystals → fibrocalcific stenosis

Risk factors (same as atherosclerosis)

  • Hypertension, LDL cholesterol, Lp(a)
  • Diabetes mellitus, smoking, chronic kidney disease, metabolic syndrome
  • Hypertension, DM, and dyslipidemia account for ~1/3 of attributable risk for severe AS
Aortic sclerosis = leaflet thickening/calcification without obstruction. Jet velocity <2.5 m/s, peak gradient <25 mmHg. Affects ~30% of people >65. Carries an excess risk of CV death and MI — it's a marker of systemic atherosclerotic burden, not a benign finding.
Despite the shared pathogenesis with atherosclerosis, statins do NOT slow progression of established AS. Neither do ACE inhibitors or ARBs. Statins are used for the standard atherosclerotic CV prevention indications, not to treat the valve. The lesson: once the osteoblast-driven mineralization process is underway, the calcium machinery operates somewhat independently of LDL — and trials have repeatedly failed to show benefit.
03

Bicuspid aortic valve (BAV)

The most common congenital heart valve defect. Affects 0.5–1.4% of the population, with a 2–4:1 male predominance.

  • Inheritance: autosomal dominant with incomplete penetrance. ~10% prevalence in first-degree relatives. Genes include NOTCH1, GATA5, GATA4.
  • Presentation: AS, AR, or both — usually one to two decades earlier than degenerative AS.
  • Associated aortopathy in 30–40%: root or ascending aortic aneurysm; less commonly aortic coarctation. Develops independently of valve severity — directional shear forces from the abnormal valve geometry drive the aortic wall changes.
  • Risk: aneurysm formation and dissection.
  • Auscultation: systolic ejection sound (click) — usually disappears when the valve becomes calcified and rigid.
  • Surgical threshold for the aorta:
    • Aneurysm diameter >5.5 cm → surgery indicated
    • 4.5–5.0 cm if family history of aortic catastrophe or rapid growth (>0.5 cm/year)
When a BAV patient is referred for AVR, the surgeon must also assess the aortic root and ascending aorta. Concomitant aortic replacement may be needed. This is why echocardiography in BAV must always measure root and ascending aortic dimensions, and CT angiography is often used pre-op.
04

AS — pathophysiology

The pressure gradient and the LV response

Obstruction at the valve creates a systolic pressure gradient between the LV and aorta. The LV compensates with concentric hypertrophy, normalizing wall stress (Laplace: σ = Pr/h — increase h to offset increase P).

This compensation is so effective that a large gradient may exist for years with normal cardiac output and no LV dilation. But eventually:

  • Hypertrophy becomes maladaptive
  • Diastolic function abnormalities progress (stiff ventricle → ↑ LVEDP)
  • Afterload mismatch → systolic dysfunction (↓ EF)
  • Irreversible myocardial fibrosis develops

Definition of severe AS

Severe AS hemodynamics
  • Mean systolic gradient >40 mmHg with normal cardiac output, OR
  • Aortic valve area <1 cm² (~<0.6 cm²/m² BSA) — less than a third of normal
  • Peak jet velocity (Vmax) ≥4 m/s on Doppler

Three reasons cardiac output falls late in AS

  1. Afterload mismatch: the hypertrophied LV can no longer generate enough pressure → contractility declines.
  2. Loss of atrial kick: a stiff hypertrophied LV depends heavily on the atrial contribution to filling. Onset of atrial fibrillation or AV dissociation can cause rapid clinical deterioration.
  3. Small LV cavity with preserved EF (paradoxical low-flow AS): stroke volume can be reduced despite normal EF because the cavity is small.

Why AS causes ischemia even without CAD

The hypertrophied LV has:
  • Increased oxygen demand — more muscle to perfuse
  • Reduced capillary density relative to wall thickness
  • Increased compressive forces on intramural vessels
  • Elevated LVEDP → reduces coronary driving pressure (which is aortic diastolic − LVEDP)
The subendocardium is especially vulnerable. This is why angina occurs in severe AS even with completely normal coronary arteries.

Low-flow, low-gradient AS — the diagnostic challenge

Definitions:

  • Low-flow: stroke volume index <35 mL/m²
  • Low-gradient: mean gradient <40 mmHg

Two flavors:

  • With reduced EF (stage D2): the failing LV can't generate the gradient. Dobutamine stress echo distinguishes true severe AS (gradient rises with augmented flow) from pseudo-severe AS. Flow reserve = ≥20% increase in stroke volume.
  • With preserved EF (stage D3, "paradoxical"): small, hypertrophied LV with low stroke volume despite normal EF. Don't use dobutamine. Control hypertension first, recheck Doppler. CT calcium scoring of the valve can confirm severity when uncertain.
05

AS — the three cardinal symptoms

AS is asymptomatic for years, sometimes decades. Once symptoms appear, the prognosis worsens dramatically — and valve replacement is indicated.

The three classic symptoms and survival without surgery
Angina pectorisMean survival: ~3 years
Syncope (exertional)Mean survival: ~3 years
Dyspnea / heart failureMean survival: ~1.5–2 years

Mechanisms

  • Exertional dyspnea: ↑ pulmonary capillary pressure from elevated LVEDP (impaired LV relaxation, reduced compliance). Often the earliest symptom.
  • Angina: mismatch between elevated O₂ demand (hypertrophy) and reduced supply (compression of intramural vessels, reduced coronary driving pressure). May or may not have concurrent CAD. CAD is common in AS patients >65.
  • Exertional syncope:
    • Exercise → vasodilation in working muscles + inadequate compensatory vasoconstriction elsewhere
    • Cardiac output is fixed by the stenosis → can't increase to maintain BP
    • OR sudden CO drop from arrhythmia
In >80% of patients who die with AS, symptoms had existed for <4 years. Sudden death occurs in 10–20% but almost exclusively in previously symptomatic patients. Sudden death as the FIRST manifestation of severe AS is rare (~1% per year). This is the basis for the watchful-waiting approach in asymptomatic patients.
06

AS — physical exam & murmur

The classic peripheral signs

CAROTID PULSE
Pulsus parvus et tardus

Rises slowly to a delayed, low-amplitude peak. A palpable thrill or "anacrotic shudder" may be felt over the carotids — more often on the left.

In the elderly: stiff arterial walls can mask this finding.

BLOOD PRESSURE
Narrow pulse pressure (late)

Hypertension is common with mild-moderate AS. Late in severe AS, stroke volume falls → systolic pressure drops → narrow pulse pressure.

JVP
Prominent a wave

From hypertrophied septum bulging into the RV → reduced RV compliance.

PRECORDIUM
Sustained / double apical impulse

Double impulse = palpable S₄ + LV systole. LV impulse may displace laterally late. Systolic thrill at upper right sternal border or suprasternal notch.

Auscultation

Murmur of AS
  • Type: ejection (mid) systolic, crescendo-decrescendo ("diamond")
  • Timing: starts shortly after S₁, peaks toward mid-systole, ends just before A₂
  • Quality: low-pitched, rough, rasping
  • Location: loudest at the base, 2nd right intercostal space
  • Radiation: upward to the carotids
  • Gallavardin phenomenon: can also radiate to the apex with a more musical quality → may be confused with mitral regurgitation
  • Severity correlate: the later the peak, the more severe the stenosis. In severe AS with preserved CO, the murmur is at least grade III/VI.
  • Counter-intuitive: in very severe AS with low CO/HF, the murmur may be soft and brief — because there isn't enough flow to generate it.

Heart sounds

  • Ejection click — heard in young patients with mobile BAV. Disappears when valve calcifies.
  • A₂ becomes soft or absent as the valve calcifies and stiffens.
  • Paradoxical splitting of S₂: prolonged LV systole delays A₂ so it follows P₂ (instead of preceding it).
  • S₄ — common; reflects LVH and elevated LVEDP.
  • S₃ — late finding; LV dilation and systolic failure.
Why does AS cause paradoxical splitting of S₂? Normally A₂ precedes P₂, and inspiration delays P₂ further (normal splitting widens with inspiration). In severe AS, the LV takes longer to eject through the narrow orifice — so A₂ is delayed and falls after P₂. Now inspiration (which still delays P₂) brings A₂ and P₂ closer together — the split narrows on inspiration. That's "paradoxical."

AS — echo criteria & severity

Echo findings

  • Leaflet thickening, calcification, reduced systolic opening
  • LV hypertrophy (concentric)
  • Eccentric leaflet closure = characteristic of bicuspid valve
  • Doppler measures jet velocity → calculates gradient and valve area
  • Longitudinal strain imaging can detect early LV dysfunction before EF falls

Severity criteria

SeverityValve areaPeak velocity (Vmax)Mean gradient
Aortic sclerosis—<2.5 m/s<25 mmHg
Mild AS1.6–2.0 cm²——
Moderate AS1.0–1.5 cm²3.0–3.9 m/s—
Severe AS<1.0 cm²≥4.0 m/s≥40 mmHg
Very severe AS—≥5.0 m/s≥60 mmHg

Echo follow-up frequency (asymptomatic)

  • Mild (Vmax 2.0–2.9 m/s): every 3–5 years
  • Moderate (Vmax 3.0–3.9 m/s): every 1–2 years
  • Severe asymptomatic (Vmax ≥4 m/s): every 6–12 months
When jet velocity / mean gradient disagree with valve area (low-flow scenarios), turn to dobutamine stress echo (only if EF is low) or CT calcium scoring of the valve. Men have more calcium, women more fibrosis — sex-specific calcium-score cutoffs apply.
08

AS — natural history

  • Annual progression rates in calcific AS:
    • Valve area: ↓ ~0.1 cm²/year
    • Peak jet velocity: ↑ ~0.3 m/s/year
    • Mean gradient: ↑ ~7 mmHg/year
  • BAV patients: develop significant dysfunction one to two decades sooner than degenerative AS.
  • AS rarely matters clinically until valve area is around 1 cm².
  • Severe AS may be silent for years — once symptoms appear or EF falls, AVR is indicated.
09

AS — medical, SAVR, TAVI

Medical management

  • Avoid dehydration / hypovolemia — the stenotic LV is preload-dependent.
  • Avoid strenuous exercise / competitive sports if severe AS.
  • Beta-blockers and ACE-Is are generally safe in asymptomatic patients with preserved LV function. Control BP carefully — two resistance circuits in series (valve + arterial) amplify each other.
  • Nitroglycerin can be used cautiously for angina (with CAD).
  • Statins do NOT slow AS progression — use them per ASCVD prevention guidelines, not for the valve.
  • Endocarditis prophylaxis only for AS patients with prior history of endocarditis.
Old teaching said "avoid nitrates and ACE-Is in AS." Current Harrison's teaching: they are generally safe in asymptomatic patients with preserved LV function and adequate BP. The principle is still real — anything that drops preload too much can crash these patients — but careful use is acceptable. The dangerous combinations are: severe AS + IV nitrates in a hypovolemic patient + tachycardia → catastrophic drop in CO.

Indications for AVR (SAVR or TAVI)

Class I (definitely operate)
  • Symptomatic severe AS (stage D)
  • Asymptomatic severe AS + LV EF <50% (stage C2)
  • Asymptomatic severe AS + another cardiac surgery (e.g., CABG, aortic aneurysm repair)
Class IIa (reasonable to operate)
  • Very severe AS: Vmax ≥5 m/s or mean gradient ≥60 mmHg, with low surgical risk
  • Abnormal exercise treadmill test (↓ BP, ↓ exercise capacity, symptoms)
  • Rapid progression
  • Low-flow, low-gradient severe AS with reduced EF and demonstrable flow reserve on dobutamine echo
  • Low-flow, low-gradient severe AS with normal EF (stage D3) when symptomatic
Class IIb (may consider)
  • EF falling <60% on serial studies (asymptomatic)
  • BNP >3× upper limit in low-risk surgical patient

SAVR vs TAVI — outcomes

  • TAVI advantages: fewer periprocedural deaths, lower risk of stroke, less bleeding, less AF, shorter hospital stay, faster recovery
  • SAVR advantages: lower rates of permanent pacemaker, less perivalvular AR, less bioprosthetic leaflet thrombosis, fewer vascular complications
  • Transfemoral TAVI is preferred — non-femoral access has higher complication rates

Prosthesis choice — mechanical vs bioprosthetic

  • Bioprosthesis: generally favored for patients >65 years. ~10–20% primary valve failure by 15 years → may need re-replacement (or valve-in-valve TAVI).
  • Mechanical: very durable but requires lifelong anticoagulation. ~10–20% develop hemorrhagic complications.
  • Increasing numbers of patients <65 opt for bioprosthesis (with future valve-in-valve TAVI as a fallback).

Percutaneous aortic balloon valvuloplasty (PABV)

  • NOT definitive therapy in adult calcific AS — 80% restenosis within 1 year.
  • Useful as a bridge to SAVR/TAVI in patients with severe LV dysfunction and shock.
  • Routinely performed as part of the TAVI procedure.
  • Reasonable definitive therapy only in children/young adults with congenital non-calcific AS.

AR — etiology (valve vs root)

AR results from primary valve disease, aortic root disease, or both. The distinction matters because the treatment of root-driven AR may involve sparing the native valve.

PRIMARY VALVE
  • Congenital bicuspid — ~20% of BAV patients need surgery between 10–40 yrs
  • Infective endocarditis — perforation/destruction; can be acute
  • Rheumatic fever — almost always with mitral involvement
  • Myxomatous prolapse
  • Radiation
  • Trauma — most frequent serious lesion in nonpenetrating cardiac injury survivors
  • Syphilis, ankylosing spondylitis — leaflet scarring/retraction
  • Membranous subaortic stenosis — high-velocity jet damages leaflets
AORTIC ROOT
  • Aortic dissection (type A) — retrograde involvement of annulus
  • Medial degeneration
  • Marfan syndrome
  • Bicuspid aortic valve aortopathy
  • Nonsyndromic familial aneurysm
  • Aortitis (syphilis, Takayasu, giant cell)
  • Severe chronic hypertension
  • Osteogenesis imperfecta, annuloaortic ectasia
If the AR murmur is louder on the right sternal border than the left, it suggests the regurgitation is driven by aortic root dilation rather than primary leaflet disease. Useful exam clue.
Hemodynamically significant AS + AR coexistence essentially excludes the rarer causes of AR — because both lesions in the same valve usually mean rheumatic or congenital (bicuspid) disease. This is a practical exam shortcut.
11

AR — pathophysiology (acute vs chronic)

The same lesion (regurgitation back into the LV) produces two completely different clinical pictures depending on whether the LV has had time to adapt.

CHRONIC AR — the slow adapter
  • Total stroke volume = forward SV + regurgitant volume → increased
  • LV dilates (eccentric hypertrophy) → can eject larger SV without ↑ shortening of each myofibril
  • EF preserved for years — even with severe AR
  • Both preload AND afterload are increased (Laplace: dilated chamber → higher wall tension)
  • Long latent period of 10–15 years often asymptomatic
  • Eventually: end-diastolic volume rises further → forward SV and EF fall
  • LV dysfunction often precedes symptoms — major reason for serial echo follow-up
ACUTE SEVERE AR — the unprepared LV
  • LV has had no time to dilate — normal-sized, non-compliant chamber
  • Regurgitant volume into a small, stiff cavity → LVEDP rises rapidly (sometimes >40 mmHg)
  • LV pressure may exceed LA pressure in late diastole → premature mitral valve closure
  • Tachycardia develops to try to maintain CO
  • Pulse pressure not particularly wide (only mildly), absent peripheral signs
  • Pulmonary edema / cardiogenic shock develops rapidly
  • Diastolic murmur is short and soft, not the classic long blowing decrescendo
  • Soft S₁ from premature mitral closure

Why myocardial ischemia occurs in AR

Three reasons coronary perfusion is impaired in severe AR — and they all matter for understanding the nocturnal angina classically described:
  • Increased O₂ demand — LV is dilated, hypertrophied, and ejecting against high wall tension
  • Reduced O₂ supply — most coronary flow occurs in diastole, when aortic pressure is at its lowest (driving the regurgitation back into the LV instead of forward through the coronaries)
  • The subendocardium is particularly vulnerable
That's why severe AR can cause angina even without coronary disease — and why the angina may be worse at night (slower HR → longer diastole → more regurgitation, lower aortic diastolic pressure, worse coronary perfusion).
12

AR — symptoms & the latent period

  • ~3/4 of patients with pure valvular AR are male.
  • Chronic severe AR has a long latent period — patients may be asymptomatic for 10–15 years.
  • Earliest complaint: uncomfortable awareness of the heartbeat, especially lying down ("head pounding"); palpitations from the large stroke volume.
  • First true symptom of decreased reserve: exertional dyspnea.
  • Progression: orthopnea → PND → diaphoresis → frank LV failure.
  • Angina — can occur even in younger patients without CAD. Nocturnal angina is classic, often unrelieved by sublingual nitroglycerin, with marked diaphoresis.
  • Late: hepatomegaly, ankle edema.

Acute severe AR — the contrast

Acute severe AR (endocarditis, type A dissection, trauma) presents with:

  • Sudden pulmonary edema
  • Often cardiogenic shock
  • Tachycardia
  • None of the chronic peripheral signs
  • Surgical emergency — operate within 24 hours
13

AR — peripheral signs & murmurs

Chronic severe AR is the disease with the longest list of named physical signs in cardiology. They all reflect wide pulse pressure from a high stroke volume hitting a relatively empty diastolic aorta.

The eponymous signs

ARTERIAL PULSE
Corrigan's pulse ("water-hammer")

Rapidly rising, bounding pulse that collapses suddenly as diastolic pressure falls.

NAIL BED
Quincke's pulse

Capillary pulsations — alternate flushing and paling of the skin at the nail root with light pressure on the tip.

FEMORAL ARTERY
Traube's sign ("pistol shot")

Booming systolic sound audible over the femoral artery.

FEMORAL ARTERY (compressed)
Duroziez's sign

To-and-fro murmur over the femoral when lightly compressed with the stethoscope.

HEAD
De Musset's sign

Head bobbing with each heartbeat — from the violent arterial pulsation.

CAROTID
Pulsus bisferiens

Two systolic peaks — seen in AR or mixed AS+AR. Apex shows systolic expansion and diastolic retraction.

Blood pressure findings

  • Wide pulse pressure — systolic hypertension + low diastolic. Hallmark of chronic severe AR.
  • Korotkoff sounds may be heard with the cuff fully deflated — use phase IV (muffling) as the diastolic.
  • As the disease progresses and LVEDP rises, diastolic BP may paradoxically rise — because aortic diastolic pressure can't fall below LVEDP.
  • Acute severe AR: pulse pressure only mildly widened; tachycardia dominates.

Auscultation

Murmurs of AR
  • Primary murmur: high-pitched, blowing, decrescendo, early-diastolic murmur. Heard best at the third left intercostal space, patient leaning forward, breath held in expiration, diaphragm of stethoscope.
  • Severity → length: mild AR is brief; severe AR is holodiastolic.
  • Right vs left sternal border: louder on left → valvular cause. Louder on right → root cause (aneurysmal dilation).
  • Cooing / musical diastolic murmur → suggests an everted aortic cusp vibrating in the regurgitant stream.
  • Systolic ejection murmur: commonly present even in pure AR — from the large stroke volume crossing the valve. Does NOT mean coexisting AS.
  • Austin Flint murmur: soft, low-pitched, rumbling, mid-to-late diastolic murmur at the apex. Caused by the AR jet hitting the anterior mitral leaflet → functional mitral inflow obstruction. NOT from real MS.

Heart sounds

  • A₂ usually absent in severe AR (leaflets can't coapt to make the sound).
  • Ejection sound in BAV.
  • Possible S₄.
  • Acute severe AR: soft S₁ (premature mitral closure), short diastolic murmur.

Maneuvers

Handgrip (sustained, strenuous) → ↑ systemic vascular resistance → ↑ LV afterload → louder AR murmur. Useful to bring out a faint diastolic murmur.

14

AR — echo & severity

Key echo findings

  • Increased LV size (chronic) with preserved or supernormal systolic function until late
  • Rapid high-frequency diastolic fluttering of the anterior mitral leaflet — from the AR jet hitting it
  • Identification of cause: dilated annulus/root, dissection, primary leaflet pathology

Severe AR — quantitative criteria

Echo definition of severe AR
  • Central jet width >65% of LVOT width on color Doppler
  • Vena contracta >0.6 cm
  • Regurgitant volume ≥60 mL/beat
  • Regurgitant fraction ≥50%
  • Effective regurgitant orifice (ERO) ≥0.3 cm²
  • Holodiastolic flow reversal in the proximal descending thoracic aorta

Acute severe AR on echo

  • Rapid deceleration time of the AR jet on continuous-wave Doppler — because LV diastolic pressure rises so quickly that aorta-LV gradient equalizes fast.
  • Premature mitral closure — pathognomonic for hemodynamically significant acute AR.

Tools beyond echo

  • CMR: accurate LV volumes, extracellular volume (interstitial fibrosis), late gadolinium enhancement (replacement fibrosis). Useful when echo is limited.
  • TEE: detailed valve/root anatomy.
  • CT angiography: root and ascending aorta dimensions.
15

AR — medical & surgical management

Acute severe AR

Surgical emergency
  • IV diuretics + vasodilators (sodium nitroprusside) — only as a temporizing measure
  • Intra-aortic balloon counterpulsation is CONTRAINDICATED — IABP inflates in diastole and would worsen AR
  • Beta-blockers should be AVOIDED — slowing the heart rate gives more time for regurgitation; the patient depends on tachycardia to maintain CO
  • Surgery is the treatment of choice, usually within 24 hours
Two counterintuitive rules in acute severe AR:
  1. No IABP. In other cardiogenic shock, IABP helps by inflating in diastole and augmenting coronary flow. But in AR, diastolic balloon inflation pushes blood backward into the already-overwhelmed LV. Worsens hemodynamics.
  2. No beta-blockers. The compensatory tachycardia shortens diastole, which limits regurgitant filling and preserves CO. Slowing the heart removes that protection and may precipitate collapse.

Chronic AR — medical management

  • Diuretics and vasodilators (ACE-I, ARB, dihydropyridine CCBs, hydralazine) — useful as a temporizing measure in symptomatic patients awaiting surgery.
  • Vasodilators do NOT extend the compensated phase of asymptomatic chronic severe AR.
  • Treat hypertension aggressively (SBP >140 → treat). Hard to control because of the high stroke volume.
  • Beta-blockers were once considered relatively contraindicated but observational data now suggest functional benefit in some patients; useful for BP control and reducing forceful heartbeat sensation.
  • Marfan syndrome with root dilation: beta-blockers OR losartan retard root growth (no difference between them in RCT).
  • Nitrates — worth a trial for angina, less effective than in CAD.
  • Avoid isometric exercise, especially with aortopathy.

Chronic AR — indications for AVR

When to operate
  • Symptomatic severe AR — regardless of LV function (Class I)
  • Asymptomatic severe AR + EF ≤55% on serial studies (stage C2) (Class I)
  • Asymptomatic severe AR + LVESD >50 mm (or >25 mm/m²) (Class I)
  • Asymptomatic severe AR undergoing other cardiac surgery (Class I)
  • Asymptomatic severe AR + EF 55–60% with progressive changes on ≥3 serial studies, or LVEDD >65 mm in low-surgical-risk patients (Class IIb)
  • Moderate AR + other cardiac surgery (Class IIa)
The clinical principle to remember: in AR, LV dysfunction often precedes symptoms. If you wait for symptoms, you may be too late — surgery delayed >1 year after onset of LV dysfunction often fails to restore normal LV size and function. Serial echo every 6–12 months is the surveillance strategy.

Surgical options

  • AVR with mechanical or bioprosthetic valve — most patients.
  • Valve-sparing aortic root reconstruction (David procedure) — when AR is driven by root dilation with normal leaflets. Valve is reimplanted inside a contoured graft.
  • Primary leaflet repair — possible after IE perforation or trauma.
  • Resuspension of native leaflets — possible in ~50% of acute type A aortic dissection.
  • Composite valve-graft conduit — when valve AND root both require replacement.
  • TAVI is NOT recommended for chronic severe AR in surgical candidates — technical success limited by lack of leaflet/annular calcium for anchoring and degree of annular dilation.

Operative mortality

  • Isolated AVR for pure AR: ~1–2%
  • AVR + aortic surgery: ~2–4% (doubles)
  • AVR with established LV dysfunction + marked cardiac enlargement: ~5–10% operative mortality, ~3–5%/year late mortality

Exam traps — board coach mode

Each trap: what the question wants you to think, the truth, why it matters, and how it shows up in a case.

TRAP 01 Late-peaking AS murmur is reassuring (it sounds milder).
The trap A loud early-peaking AS murmur sounds severe; a softer murmur peaking late sounds mild.
The truth The later the peak, the more severe the stenosis — because higher gradients require more of systole to drive flow through the narrow orifice. Severe AS with low CO can have a soft, brief murmur because there isn't enough flow to generate volume.
Why it matters A "soft" murmur in a sick AS patient doesn't mean mild disease — it may mean critical AS with failing LV.
In a case "Soft, late-peaking systolic murmur + signs of heart failure" → critically severe AS with low cardiac output, not mild AS.
TRAP 02 Statins slow AS progression.
The trap AS pathogenesis resembles atherosclerosis → statins must slow valve disease.
The truth Statins do NOT slow established AS. Neither do ACE-Is or ARBs. The osteoblast-driven calcification operates somewhat independently of LDL once initiated.
Why it matters Use statins for the patient's ASCVD risk, not to treat the valve. Don't promise the patient the statin will slow their AS.
In a case "Asymptomatic moderate AS — does adding a statin slow progression?" → No — statins do not slow AS progression. Use per usual ASCVD prevention indications.
TRAP 03 Aortic sclerosis is benign because it doesn't obstruct.
The trap Sclerosis ≠ stenosis → just a calcified valve, no obstruction, no problem.
The truth Aortic sclerosis carries an excess risk of CV death and MI in patients >65 — it's a marker of systemic atherosclerotic burden.
Why it matters Sclerosis should trigger comprehensive ASCVD risk assessment, not reassurance.
In a case "70yo with aortic sclerosis (Vmax 2.0 m/s) — significance?" → Marker of increased CV risk; treat ASCVD risk factors. Not "benign."
TRAP 04 Sudden cardiac death is a common first presentation of severe AS.
The trap Severe AS = sudden death risk → operate everyone immediately.
The truth Sudden death as the first manifestation of severe AS is uncommon (~1%/year). The 10–20% sudden death rate occurs in patients who were already symptomatic. This justifies watchful waiting in truly asymptomatic patients.
Why it matters Guides timing of intervention — symptoms drive surgery, not asymptomatic severe disease alone (unless EF falls or other criteria are met).
In a case "Asymptomatic severe AS with normal EF — best management?" → Echocardiographic surveillance every 6–12 months, not immediate AVR.
TRAP 05 AVR is indicated for any severe AS, regardless of symptoms.
The trap Severe AS (Vmax ≥4, gradient ≥40, AVA <1) = automatic AVR.
The truth AVR is Class I for: symptomatic severe AS, asymptomatic severe AS with EF <50%, or asymptomatic severe AS undergoing other cardiac surgery. Otherwise, surveillance.
Why it matters Operating early on truly asymptomatic patients with normal LV function may not improve survival.
In a case "Asymptomatic severe AS (Vmax 4.2 m/s), EF 60% — next step?" → Echocardiography every 6–12 months, exercise testing to confirm asymptomatic status. Not immediate AVR.
TRAP 06 TAVI is for all comers in 2024.
The trap TAVI surpassed SAVR in volume → TAVI for everyone with severe AS.
The truth TAVI is preferred in patients >80. Patients <65 should generally have SAVR due to scant long-term TAVI data. 65–80 is shared decision. TAVI is NOT recommended for surgical candidates with chronic severe AR.
Why it matters Age, anatomy (BAV, calcium pattern, root size, vascular access), and concurrent surgical needs all matter.
In a case "58yo with symptomatic severe AS, no comorbidities — TAVI or SAVR?" → SAVR (age <65, limited TAVI durability data in this group).
TRAP 07 All blowing diastolic murmurs are valvular AR.
The trap Decrescendo diastolic murmur at LLSB = leaflet disease.
The truth If the murmur is louder along the right sternal border, the AR is likely due to aortic root dilation, not primary valve disease. Root etiology mandates imaging the ascending aorta (CT/MR) and may permit a valve-sparing repair.
Why it matters Drives the imaging workup AND the surgical strategy (David procedure for root disease).
In a case "AR murmur louder on right sternal border" → Suspect aortic root dilation; image the ascending aorta. May be amenable to valve-sparing root reconstruction.
TRAP 08 Wait for symptoms in chronic severe AR.
The trap Like AS — watch and wait until the patient is dyspneic.
The truth LV dysfunction in AR often precedes symptoms. Waiting for symptoms can mean irreversible LV dysfunction. Operate when EF ≤55%, LVESD >50 mm, or symptoms appear — whichever comes first.
Why it matters Surgery delayed >1 year after onset of LV dysfunction often fails to restore normal LV.
In a case "Asymptomatic severe AR, EF 52%, LVESD 48 mm" → AVR indicated (EF ≤55%), not surveillance.
TRAP 09 Acute severe AR — give beta-blockers and IABP, just like cardiogenic shock.
The trap Pulmonary edema and shock → start beta-blocker + IABP, standard care.
The truth Both are contraindicated in acute severe AR. Beta-blockers slow HR → longer diastole → more regurgitation → worse CO. IABP inflates in diastole → pushes blood backward through the incompetent valve. The patient depends on tachycardia and needs emergency surgery.
Why it matters Reflexive shock management can kill these patients. They need vasodilators, diuretics, and the OR within 24 hours.
In a case "Endocarditis + new severe AR + pulmonary edema — initial management?" → IV diuretics + nitroprusside; emergency surgery within 24h. NOT beta-blocker, NOT IABP.
TRAP 10 Austin Flint = mitral stenosis.
The trap A diastolic rumble at the apex → mitral stenosis.
The truth The Austin Flint murmur is a soft, low-pitched mid-to-late diastolic apical rumble in severe AR. It results from the AR jet hitting the anterior mitral leaflet → functional mitral inflow disturbance. The mitral valve itself is normal.
Why it matters Distinguishing it from true MS changes management entirely — no MS-directed therapy needed.
In a case "Severe AR + apical diastolic rumble" → Austin Flint murmur — not mitral stenosis.
TRAP 11 Systolic murmur + diastolic murmur = mixed AS + AR.
The trap Hearing a systolic and diastolic murmur means combined AS + AR.
The truth Pure severe AR commonly has a systolic ejection murmur from the large stroke volume crossing the aortic valve — without coexisting AS. A systolic thrill and murmur in AR do not mean AS is also present.
Why it matters Avoid over-diagnosing mixed disease — echo will sort it out.
In a case "Severe AR with a loud systolic ejection murmur" → Common finding from increased stroke volume, not necessarily coexisting AS.
TRAP 12 Bicuspid valve = just a valve problem.
The trap BAV → calcifies earlier, treat the valve, end of story.
The truth 30–40% of BAV patients have aortopathy — root or ascending aortic aneurysm — independent of valve severity. Aortic surgery thresholds: >5.5 cm, or 4.5–5.0 cm with family history or rapid growth. Always image the aorta.
Why it matters Missed aortopathy → risk of dissection. Pre-op planning must consider the ascending aorta.
In a case "BAV referred for AVR — additional pre-op imaging?" → Assess root and ascending aortic dimensions; consider concomitant aortic replacement.
TRAP 13 Marfan + root dilation — use beta-blocker; losartan is inferior.
The trap Beta-blockers are the standard for Marfan; ARBs are newer/less proven.
The truth A randomized controlled trial showed no difference in efficacy between atenolol and losartan for retarding aortic root enlargement in Marfan syndrome. Either is acceptable.
Why it matters Treatment choice can be individualized to tolerability.
In a case "Young Marfan patient with progressive root dilation — best medical therapy?" → Beta-blocker OR losartan — equivalent.
TRAP 14 Low-gradient AS = mild AS.
The trap Mean gradient <40 mmHg → not severe AS.
The truth Low-flow, low-gradient severe AS exists in two forms: with low EF (D2) and with normal EF + small ventricle ("paradoxical," D3). Both can be truly severe AS — the gradient is low because flow is low.
Why it matters Missing these patients means denying them an intervention that improves outcomes. Use dobutamine echo (if low EF) or CT calcium scoring to confirm severity.
In a case "Symptomatic patient, AVA 0.8 cm², mean gradient 30 mmHg, EF 30%" → Low-flow, low-gradient severe AS with reduced EF — dobutamine stress echo to confirm true severity and flow reserve.
