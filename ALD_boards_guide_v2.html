The ALD Spectrum

Alcohol-associated liver diseases (ALD) are a spectrum ranging from reversible early disease to life-threatening acute decompensation. Understanding the spectrum is the foundation for all board questions on this topic.

💡 Key Concept
Acute alcohol-associated hepatitis (AH) is an acute-on-chronic form of ALD — it typically occurs superimposed on underlying cirrhosis (80% of cases). It is associated with liver failure and high mortality, and it is the entity most heavily tested on boards. Simple steatosis and steatohepatitis are clinically silent; symptoms arise only at decompensation or when AH develops.

Epidemiology

Alcohol Use Definitions

DefinitionWomenMen
Unhealthy drinking≥2 drinks/day≥3 drinks/day
Heavy episodic drinking (binge)≥4 drinks on a single occasion≥5 drinks on a single occasion

1 drink = ~14 g of ethanol = 1 beer = 4 oz wine = 1 oz of 80-proof spirits.

Approximately 7% of U.S. adults meet criteria for unhealthy drinking. The COVID-19 pandemic increased excessive alcohol intake, resulting in a rise in ALD morbidity and mortality.

Prevalence & Burden

  • Alcohol-associated fatty liver disease prevalence: 4.7% of the general U.S. population; 1.5% has stage 2 or greater fibrosis
  • Liver cirrhosis is the 11th leading cause of mortality worldwide — 1.16 million deaths annually; 48% of cirrhosis cases attributable to alcohol
  • Among patients with alcohol misuse: 18% fibrosis, 26% cirrhosis, 7% acute AH without underlying cirrhosis
  • European annual incidence of acute AH: 24–27 per million women; 46–65 per million men
  • Alcohol-associated decompensated cirrhosis is the #1 indication for liver transplantation in the United States
  • Only 10–20% of heavy daily drinkers will develop progressive liver disease and cirrhosis — cofactors determine who progresses

⚠ Asian Population — ALDH2 Deficiency
Inherited deficiency of ALDH2 is common in Asian countries. It leads to acetaldehyde accumulation after alcohol consumption → nausea and cutaneous flushing (the "Asian flush"). These individuals cannot safely metabolize alcohol and are at high risk for organ injury.

Mechanisms of Hepatic Steatosis

Multiple parallel mechanisms converge to cause fat accumulation in the liver:

  • Acetyl-CoA from acetate → direct substrate for fatty acid and triglyceride synthesis
  • Epigenetic changes: alcohol ↑ expression of lipogenesis genes; ↓ expression of fatty acid transport and oxidation genes
  • ↑ NADH/NAD+ ratio: alcohol metabolism oxidizes NAD+ to NADH → high NADH → impairs mitochondrial β-oxidation of fatty acids
  • ↑ Fatty acid mobilization from adipose tissue and intestine → hepatic fatty acid accumulation

From Steatosis to Fibrosis and Cirrhosis

  • Gut microbiome & intestinal permeability: a subset of patients with alcohol use disorder develop gut dysbiosis and increased intestinal permeability → passage of microbial components (especially bacterial LPS) through portal vein into liver → hepatic inflammation, hepatocyte death, activation of fibrotic pathways
  • Ongoing fibrosis from sustained alcohol consumption → cirrhosis + portal hypertension
  • Impaired liver regeneration may contribute to acute onset of AH in patients with underlying cirrhosis
04

Risk Factors for Progression

Most heavy drinkers do NOT progress to cirrhosis — only 10–20% do. Cofactors determine who progresses:

Behavioral Factors
  • Alcohol amount and duration — dose-dependent increase in cirrhosis risk
  • Drinking pattern — binge drinking and drinking outside of meals ↑ progressive ALD risk more than the same total amount spread over meals
  • Cigarette smoking — independent risk factor for alcohol-associated cirrhosis
Host Factors
  • Female sex — ALD develops at a lower daily alcohol intake in women
  • Obesity and MASLD — frequent cofactors; a distinct entity called MetALD describes patients with MASLD AND increased alcohol consumption
  • Chronic liver disease — viral hepatitis and hemochromatosis have synergistic effects
  • Intestinal microbiota — gut dysbiosis ↑ LPS translocation
Genetic Factors
  • Twin studies show genetic predisposition to cirrhosis independent of genetic predisposition to alcohol use disorder
  • PNPLA3 (patatin-like phospholipase domain-containing 3) — most studied; polymorphisms ↑ risk
  • MBOAT7 (membrane bound O-acyltransferase domain-containing 7) — ↑ risk
  • TM6SF2 (transmembrane 6 superfamily member 2) — ↑ risk
  • HSD17B13 and FAF2 variants — protective; associated with reduced risk of ALD
Environmental / Mixed
  • COVID-19 pandemic increased excessive drinking → rise in ALD morbidity/mortality
  • Combination of multiple cofactors is common in clinical practice (e.g., alcohol + obesity + HCV)
05

Clinical Features

⚠ Silent Disease
The development of alcohol-associated steatosis, steatohepatitis, and even cirrhosis is most often clinically silent. Symptoms arise only when the patient decompensates or develops alcohol-associated hepatitis.

Diagnostic Criteria for Alcohol-Associated Hepatitis (Clinical)

  • Heavy drinking: >40 g/day for women and >50–60 g/day for men
  • Duration: >6 months of heavy drinking
  • Abstinence: <60 days of abstinence before onset of symptoms
  • Rapid onset of jaundice (serum bilirubin >3 mg/dL)
  • Accompanied by fever, malaise, tender hepatomegaly, and signs of hepatic decompensation (ascites, bacterial infection, variceal bleeding, hepatic encephalopathy)

Complications of AH at Presentation

  • Infections in 12–26% of patients with severe AH at time of admission
  • SIRS (systemic inflammatory response syndrome) — commonly accompanies AH
  • AKI — secondary to hepatorenal syndrome, a common complication of severe AH

🎯 The ALD Lab Signature — Boards Essential
  • AST/ALT ratio >2 is the classic clue for ALD (vs other hepatitis where ALT usually > AST)
  • In ALD/AH: AST rarely exceeds 300 IU/L and NEVER exceeds 400 IU/L
  • If AST >400 IU/L in a "suspected ALD" patient → think DILI, ischemic hepatitis, or another diagnosis → biopsy to confirm
  • In alcohol-associated hepatitis: AST/ALT ratio >1.5 is characteristic

Diagnosing the ALD Spectrum — Tools & Thresholds

Steatosis

  • Ultrasound — simple, first-line; detects steatosis
  • MRI or CT — also diagnose steatosis
  • CAP (controlled attenuation parameter) — ultrasound-based noninvasive fat quantification
  • MR-PDFF (magnetic resonance proton density fat fraction) — noninvasive quantification of hepatic fat
  • Liver biopsy rarely indicated for steatosis alone

Fibrosis Assessment

  • Transient elastography (FibroScan) — liver stiffness measurement:
    • <6 kPa → normal liver
    • >8 kPa → ≥F3 advanced fibrosis
    • >12.5 kPa → F4 cirrhosis
  • FIB-4 score — serum marker based on age, AST, ALT, and platelets; threshold <3.25 excludes advanced fibrosis/cirrhosis
  • Histology: perivenular fibrosis initially → extension of collagen into hepatic lobules (septal fibrosis) → cirrhosis

Cirrhosis

  • Imaging: liver nodularity on ultrasound/MRI/CT
  • Radiologic signs of portal hypertension: ascites, splenomegaly, portal-systemic collateral vessels (varices, caput medusae)
  • Prognosis scored by Child-Pugh-Turcotte (CPT) or MELD/MELD-Na

Histology of ALD — Board-Tested Features

Alcohol-Associated Steatosis

  • Hepatocytes with large lipid droplets (macrovesicular steatosis)
  • Distribution: around pericentral veins (zone 3)

Alcohol-Associated Steatohepatitis

  • Hepatocyte injury and ballooning
  • Mallory-Denk bodies (intracytoplasmic protein aggregates)
  • Necrosis
  • Lobular inflammation with mononuclear and neutrophilic granulocytes

Alcohol-Associated Hepatitis (AH)

  • Macrovesicular steatosis
  • Hepatocyte ballooning with Mallory-Denk bodies
  • Megamitochondria
  • Neutrophil infiltration
  • Ductular reaction
  • Bilirubinostasis
  • Chicken wire fibrosis (perisinusoidal/pericellular)

When to Do Transjugular Liver Biopsy in AH

Clinical diagnosis of AH is usually sufficient. A transjugular liver biopsy is recommended when confounding factors are present, including:

  • Possible ischemic hepatitis (hypotension, massive GI bleeding, recent cocaine use, septic shock)
  • Drug-induced liver injury (DILI)
  • Autoimmune liver disease
  • Uncertain alcohol use history
  • Atypical lab values: AST <50 IU/L or >400 IU/L
  • AST/ALT ratio <1.5

Additionally: 10–20% of patients with a clinical diagnosis of AH will have another liver disease on biopsy — biopsy confirms diagnosis when uncertainty exists. 80% of AH patients have underlying cirrhosis.

Workup at Presentation of AH

  • Imaging to exclude biliary obstruction and HCC
  • Exclude other causes: viral hepatitis, Wilson's disease, severe autoimmune liver disease
  • Infections assessment: chest X-ray, blood cultures, urine cultures, ascites cultures (infections in 12–26% at admission)
  • AUDIT questionnaire for alcohol use disorder assessment

Treatment

✓ Cornerstone of All ALD Treatment
The most effective therapy to reduce progression of and reverse ALD is prolonged alcohol abstinence. Alcohol-associated steatosis and steatohepatitis are reversible with cessation. Treatment of the underlying alcohol use disorder is integral. There are currently no approved drugs for treatment of alcohol-associated steatosis/steatohepatitis with or without fibrosis.

Glucocorticoid Treatment — Details

  • Indication: severe AH (MDF ≥32 or MELD >20) without contraindications
  • Drug: oral prednisolone 40 mg/day for a total of 4 weeks (28 days)
  • If unable to take oral medications: methylprednisolone 32 mg/day IV
  • Effect: reduces risk of death within 28 days — but does NOT confer benefit in the following 6 months
  • Combination with N-acetylcysteine infusion may add short-term survival benefit at 1 month
⛔ Contraindications to Glucocorticoids
  • Uncontrolled infections or sepsis
  • AKI and hepatorenal syndrome
  • Uncontrolled upper GI bleeding
  • Concomitant diseases: viral hepatitis, HCC, pancreatitis, DILI, active tuberculosis, HIV
  • Multi-organ failure or shock

Note: Glucocorticoids can be used once infection, sepsis, and GI bleeding are adequately controlled.

Nutritional Support in AH

  • Enteral nutrition goal: 35–40 kcal/kg
  • Micronutrient supplementation — especially zinc
  • Vitamin supplementation — especially vitamin B1 (thiamine) and vitamin K
  • Volume expansion: IV albumin preferred

Long-Term Prognosis

  • Short-term prognosis depends on liver disease severity at presentation
  • Long-term prognosis (>1 year) largely depends on alcohol abstinence and presence of underlying cirrhosis
  • Moderate AH: 20% 1-year mortality
  • Severe AH: ~30% 3-month mortality

Liver Transplantation in ALD

  • ALD is currently the #1 indication for liver transplantation in the United States
  • Alcohol-associated decompensated cirrhosis or HCC → definitive therapy
  • Traditional requirement: minimum 6 months of alcohol abstinence before transplant evaluation (many centers)
  • Early liver transplantation in severe AH non-responders: can be performed in highly selected patients with excellent psychosocial profile — does not require the 6-month rule in these cases
  • Patients with severe AH non-responsive to medical therapy have high 30-day mortality and cannot fulfill the 6-month abstinence rule → early LT may be the only option
  • If ineligible for early LT: supportive or palliative care for multi-organ failure

Cirrhosis Management (ALD-Specific)

  • Management of complications (variceal bleeding, ascites, HE, HCC) does not differ from cirrhosis of other etiologies
  • Upper GI endoscopy to screen for varices in all cirrhotic patients
  • HCC surveillance: liver ultrasound + serum AFP every 6 months

Exam Traps

Trap 01AST >400 in "alcoholic hepatitis" — reconsider
A 48-year-old alcoholic presents with jaundice, AST 650 IU/L, ALT 300 IU/L, bilirubin 8 mg/dL. You suspect alcoholic hepatitis. What is the most important next step?
Question the diagnosis and consider transjugular liver biopsy. In true alcohol-associated hepatitis, AST virtually never exceeds 400 IU/L. An AST >400 IU/L should raise strong suspicion for an alternative or concurrent diagnosis: ischemic hepatitis, DILI, acetaminophen toxicity (even covert), or autoimmune hepatitis. The atypical labs (AST >400) are listed as a specific indication for transjugular biopsy to confirm or exclude alternative diagnoses.
Trap 02The Lille score — what happens if it's ≥0.45 at day 7?
A patient with severe AH (MDF = 42) is started on prednisolone 40 mg/day. At day 7, the Lille score is 0.52. What do you do?
Stop prednisolone and consider early liver transplantation. The Lille score ≥0.45 identifies non-responders who will unlikely benefit from continued glucocorticoid treatment. Continuing steroids in a non-responder exposes the patient to the risks of glucocorticoids without benefit. The next step is evaluation for early liver transplantation. If the patient is ineligible for transplant, supportive or palliative care is appropriate. Do NOT simply continue steroids or escalate the dose.
Trap 03Steroids in AH — do they work long-term?
A patient with severe AH is treated with prednisolone. Does this treatment reduce mortality at 6 months?
No — glucocorticoids reduce mortality only within the first 28 days, not beyond. This is a classic boards distinction. Steroids reduce short-term death (28-day benefit), but this survival advantage does NOT persist at 6 months or beyond. Long-term prognosis >1 year is determined by alcohol abstinence and the severity of underlying cirrhosis — not by whether steroids were given.
Trap 04Active infection and steroids — absolute contraindication?
A patient with severe AH (MDF 38) is found to have a urinary tract infection and mild sepsis on admission. Can steroids be started?
Not yet — but not necessarily never. Uncontrolled infection and sepsis are contraindications to glucocorticoid therapy. However, glucocorticoids CAN be started once infection, sepsis, and gastrointestinal bleeding are adequately controlled. The mistake to avoid is both: (1) starting steroids in the face of active uncontrolled infection, and (2) permanently withholding steroids from an eligible patient just because an infection existed at admission. Treat the infection first, then reassess.
Trap 05Early liver transplantation — the 6-month rule exception
A 35-year-old with severe AH non-responsive to steroids (Lille ≥0.45) has 30-day mortality risk of ~80%. He has never had formal treatment for alcohol use disorder and cannot complete 6 months of abstinence. Is liver transplantation absolutely contraindicated?
Not absolutely — early LT can be performed in highly selected patients with an excellent psychosocial profile. The 6-month abstinence rule exists in many centers as a standard, but steroid-nonresponsive severe AH with high 30-day mortality represents a special case where the 6-month rule has been successfully circumvented. Early liver transplantation is an established option for these patients, provided careful psychosocial selection. If early LT is not available or the patient is ineligible, supportive/palliative care is the alternative — not continued futile medical therapy.
Trap 06The biopsy trap — 80% with AH have cirrhosis underneath
A patient is diagnosed with acute alcohol-associated hepatitis. An abdominal ultrasound appears "normal" with no obvious nodularity. Does this exclude underlying cirrhosis?
No — 80% of patients with alcohol-associated hepatitis have underlying cirrhosis. Imaging may not always reveal early or compensated cirrhosis. Furthermore, 10–20% of patients with a clinical diagnosis of AH will have another liver disease identified on biopsy. Neither a normal ultrasound nor a clean clinical picture excludes cirrhosis in the AH setting. When confounding factors exist, a transjugular liver biopsy is the right answer to confirm the true diagnosis.
Trap 07PNPLA3 — genetic risk that is independent of drinking behavior
Two patients drink the same amount of alcohol. One develops cirrhosis, the other does not. What explains this?
Genetic predisposition — particularly PNPLA3 polymorphisms. Twin studies have shown that genetic predisposition to alcohol-associated cirrhosis is independent from genetic predisposition to alcohol use disorder. PNPLA3 (most important), MBOAT7, and TM6SF2 are the three key genes conferring increased risk. Protective variants in HSD17B13 and FAF2 reduce risk. This explains why identical amounts of alcohol cause severe liver disease in some and minimal disease in others — the genetics of hepatic fat handling and inflammation matter as much as the amount consumed.
Trap 08HCC surveillance — who gets it and how often?
A patient with alcohol-associated cirrhosis, currently abstinent, asks whether he needs cancer screening. What is the correct answer?
Yes — liver ultrasound + serum AFP every 6 months. HCC surveillance is recommended for all patients with cirrhosis regardless of etiology or abstinence status. The alcohol etiology does not change the surveillance protocol. Note: management of ALL cirrhosis complications (HCC, varices, ascites, HE) in ALD does NOT differ from cirrhosis of other etiologies.
Trap 09The MDF formula — don't confuse PT prolongation with absolute PT
A patient with AH has a PT of 22 seconds (control = 13 seconds) and a serum bilirubin of 12 mg/dL. Calculate the MDF and determine severity.
MDF = 4.6 × (22 − 13) + 12 = 4.6 × 9 + 12 = 41.4 + 12 = 53.4 → Severe AH (MDF ≥32). The critical trap: MDF uses the PROLONGATION of PT (PT patient minus PT control), NOT the absolute PT value. Using the absolute value of 22 would give the wrong answer. This patient has severe AH and is a steroid candidate (if no contraindications exist).
Trap 10Asian flush — ALDH2 deficiency and acetaldehyde
A 30-year-old patient of East Asian descent reports flushing and nausea after even small amounts of alcohol. What is the mechanism and what is the implication?
Inherited ALDH2 (acetaldehyde dehydrogenase 2) deficiency — common in Asian countries. Without functional ALDH2, acetaldehyde cannot be converted to acetate and accumulates after alcohol ingestion → nausea and cutaneous flushing ("Asian flush" or "Asian glow"). Acetaldehyde is directly toxic to cells and generates reactive oxygen species, causing lipid peroxidation and DNA damage. These individuals face elevated organ injury with any alcohol consumption. This is a direct-toxic pathway to hepatocyte damage.
