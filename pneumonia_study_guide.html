Definition & Classificationbackground only
What is pneumonia, and how do we categorize it?
Core Definition
  • Pneumonia = infection of the pulmonary parenchyma (alveoli and surrounding tissue)
  • Despite significant morbidity and mortality, it is often misdiagnosed, mistreated, and underestimated
CAP Community-Acquired
  • Acquired outside hospital or healthcare settings
  • Most common type encountered in clinical practice
  • ~7 million+ cases/year in the US
  • Aspiration pneumonia accounts for 5–15% of CAP cases
HAP Hospital-Acquired
  • Onset ≥48 h after hospital admission
  • ~70% of HAP acquired outside the ICU
  • ~30% in the ICU; 35% of all HAP patients need mechanical ventilation
  • Ventilated HAP = distinct entity with unique challenges
VAP Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia
  • Onset ≥48 h after initiation of mechanical ventilation
  • Most-studied subset of HAP — forms the evidence base for hospital pneumonia principles
  • Prevalence: 6–52 cases per 100 ventilated patients
🚫 Healthcare-Associated Pneumonia (HCAP) — Discontinued
  • HCAP category has been abandoned
  • WHY? It did NOT reliably predict infection with resistant pathogens
  • It was associated with increased use of broad-spectrum antibiotics without benefit
  • Better approach: Assess patients individually based on risk factors for resistant organisms:
    • Certain comorbid illnesses
    • Recent hospitalization
    • Recent antibiotic therapy
💡 Pearl: Don't just ask "where did they get sick?" — ask "what risk factors do they have for resistant organisms?" This is the modern paradigm.
Aspiration Pneumonia — A Continuum
  • Caused by macroaspiration of oropharyngeal or gastric contents
  • Best thought of as a point on the continuum that includes CAP and HAP (not a separate category)
  • Airways or pulmonary parenchyma may be involved
  • Typical patient: risk factors for macroaspiration + gravity-dependent pulmonary involvement
  • Pathogens = those usually associated with CAP or HAP (anaerobes still play a role in poor dentition, lung abscess, necrotizing pneumonia, empyema)

Pathophysiologybackground only
Why does pneumonia happen, and how does it cause disease?
Old vs. New Paradigm
❌ Old View (Abandoned)
  • Lungs were thought to be sterile
  • Pneumonia = invasion of a sterile space by a pathogen
  • Microaspiration of oropharyngeal organisms → infection
✅ Current View
  • Lungs have a complex lung microbiota
  • Pneumonia = emergent phenomenon dependent on multiple mechanisms
  • Includes self-accelerating positive feedback loops
Lung Microbiota — Determinants

Three factors govern the composition of the lung microbiota:

  • Microbial entry: via inhalation, microaspiration, direct mucosal dispersion
  • Microbial elimination: hairs, turbinates, mucociliary clearance, cough/gag reflexes, innate/adaptive immunity
  • Regional growth conditions: pH, oxygen tension, temperature
🔬 Mechanical barriers (hairs, turbinates, mucociliary clearance, cough/gag reflexes) are necessary but insufficient to block all bacterial access to lower airways.

Respiratory Failure — Why It Happens (Mechanistic Chain)
  • Capillary leak → alveolar flooding → ↓ compliance
  • Hypoxemia → ↑ respiratory drive
  • ↑ secretions + bronchospasm → worsening dyspnea
  • ↓ Lung volume + intrapulmonary shunting → respiratory failure

Pathology — The Four Stagesbackground only
Classic lobar pneumococcal pneumonia staging
1 Edema Proteinaceous exudate in alveoli. Bacteria present. Initial inflammatory response.
→
2 Red Hepatization Erythrocytes flood intraalveolar exudate → red appearance. Named for liver-like consistency.
→
3 Gray Hepatization RBCs lysed + degraded. Neutrophils predominate. Fibrin deposition abundant. Bacteria disappear. Infection being contained — gas exchange improving.
→
4 Resolution Macrophages re-emerge as dominant cell. Neutrophil debris, bacteria, and fibrin cleared. Inflammatory response resolved.
Key Pathologic Distinctions
  • Lobar pattern: More common in bacterial CAP
  • Bronchopneumonia pattern: Most common in nosocomial (HAP/VAP) pneumonias
  • VAP: Respiratory bronchiolitis may precede a radiologically apparent infiltrate
  • Viral and Pneumocystis pneumonias: Represent alveolar (not interstitial) processes — despite appearance on imaging

CAP — Etiology & Microbiology★ 6 Qs · pg 1024
Know your bugs by setting and risk factors
Typical vs. Atypical Pathogens
Typical Bacteria
  • Streptococcus pneumoniae — #1 bacterial CAP
  • Haemophilus influenzae
  • Staphylococcus aureus (selected patients)
  • Klebsiella pneumoniae (gram-negative)
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa (gram-negative)
Atypical Organisms Cannot Gram stain / culture standard
  • Mycoplasma pneumoniae — more in ambulatory cases
  • Chlamydia pneumoniae — now <1% of CAP
  • Legionella spp. — severe cases; outbreaks; hotel/cruise
  • Respiratory viruses: influenza, parainfluenza, RSV, coronaviruses, metapneumovirus, adenovirus
💡 Atypical organisms are intrinsically resistant to all β-lactams. They require treatment with a macrolide, fluoroquinolone, or tetracycline — this is why empiric CAP coverage routinely includes atypicals.

High Yield CA-MRSA — Key Points
  • Can cause primary CAP — important to recognize
  • Two sources: spread from hospital setting + genetically distinct community strains
  • CA-MRSA strains have type IV SCCmec (vs. type II/III in hospital strains)
  • CA-MRSA strains carry genes for Panton-Valentine leukocidin (PVL):
    • Membrane-tropic toxin creating cytolytic pores in neutrophils, monocytes, macrophages
    • Associated with necrotizing pneumonia, cavitary lesions, gross hemoptysis
  • CA-MRSA often susceptible to: TMP-SMX, clindamycin, tetracycline, vancomycin, linezolid
  • Risk factors: MRSA colonization at other sites, post-viral (influenza), cavitary/necrotizing pneumonia, empyema
🩸 Gross hemoptysis in a pneumonia patient → think CA-MRSA necrotizing pneumonia until proven otherwise.
Viral Pathogens — Growing Importance
  • Viruses detected in respiratory tract of 20–30% of healthy adults AND 20–30% of pneumonia patients
  • Most common: influenza, parainfluenza, RSV
  • PCR of nasopharyngeal swabs = standard for viral diagnosis (including influenza, coronaviruses)
  • SARS-CoV-2 / COVID-19: changed etiologic profile of CAP; ultimate role still evolving
  • Whether viruses are true etiologic pathogens, co-pathogens, or colonizers often cannot be determined

Epidemiology★ pg 1024
Numbers matter on boards
CAP Burden (US)
  • 7 million+ cases/year
  • Incidence: 16–23 per 1,000 adults/year
  • Hospitalization: 650/100,000/year
  • Elderly: rises to 2,000/100,000/year
  • ~30% of patients hospitalized → 1.5 million admissions
  • 8th leading cause of death (with influenza)
  • >60,000 deaths/year
Mortality Rates
  • Outpatients: <5%
  • Hospitalized: 12–40%
  • Depends on whether ICU care needed
  • 18% of hospitalized CAP patients readmitted within 1 month
  • #1 cause of death from infection in patients >65 years
  • 1-year mortality following CAP exceeds 30-day mortality (especially elderly)
Cost
  • Overall yearly CAP cost: ~$17 billion
  • Inpatient cost exceeds outpatient by factor of 20
  • Hospitalization accounts for most CAP expenditures
Risk Factors for CAP & Pneumococcal Pneumonia
  • General CAP risks: alcoholism, asthma, immunosuppression, institutionalization, age >70
  • Elderly: ↓ cough/gag reflexes, ↓ antibody and Toll-like receptor responses
  • Pneumococcal CAP: dementia, seizures, heart failure, CVD, alcoholism, tobacco, COPD, HIV
  • CA-MRSA: skin colonization/infection with CA-MRSA; post-viral infection
  • Enterobacteriaceae: recent hospitalization, antibiotics, comorbidities (alcoholism, HF, renal failure)
  • P. aeruginosa: severe structural lung disease (bronchiectasis, CF, severe COPD)
  • Legionella: diabetes, hematologic malignancy, cancer, severe renal disease, HIV, smoking, male gender, recent hotel/cruise

Clinical Manifestations3 Qs · pg 1025
What does pneumonia look like? — Link to mechanism
Constitutional Symptoms
  • Fever and/or tachycardia (IL-6, TNF-mediated)
  • Chills / sweats
  • Fatigue, headache, myalgias, arthralgias
  • GI symptoms: nausea, vomiting, diarrhea (especially Legionella)
Respiratory Symptoms
  • Cough: nonproductive OR productive (mucoid → purulent → blood-tinged)
  • Gross hemoptysis → think CA-MRSA necrotizing pneumonia
  • Dyspnea (severity-dependent; capillary leak → ↓ compliance → ↑ drive)
  • Chest pain (pleuritic, if pleural involvement)
Physical Examination Findings
  • Vital signs: ↑ RR (tachypnea), ↑ HR, fever
  • Use of accessory muscles (↓ compliance → ↑ work of breathing)
  • Palpation: ↑ or ↓ tactile fremitus
  • Percussion: dull → flat (consolidated lung → pleural fluid, respectively)
  • Auscultation: crackles, bronchial breath sounds, ± pleural friction rub
  • Elderly patients: may present atypically — new-onset or worsening confusion, or worsening of chronic illness with FEW respiratory complaints
  • Severe: septic shock and organ failure
👴 Pearl: In the elderly, altered mental status may be the ONLY presenting sign. Always consider pneumonia in an elderly patient with new confusion.
Sensitivity and Specificity of Clinical Findings
  • Physical exam: sensitivity 58%, specificity 67% — both low
  • Chest radiography often unable to differentiate CAP from other conditions
  • Etiologic determination in only 7.6% of >17,000 emergency department CAP cases
  • In >50% of cases, specific etiology NOT determined

Diagnosis3 Qs · pg 1026
Two questions: Is this pneumonia? What is the pathogen?
The Two Diagnostic Questions
Q1: Is this pneumonia?
  • Answered by clinical + radiographic methods
  • Requires: cough + sputum production + fever + dyspnea + new infiltrate on CXR
  • Differential: acute bronchitis, COPD exacerbation, HF, PE
Q2: What is the pathogen?
  • Answered by laboratory techniques
  • Often NOT known at onset → empiric therapy
  • Identifying specific/unexpected pathogens → de-escalation, resistance tracking, public safety
Radiographic Findings & Their Implications
  • Cavitation / multilobar involvement → increased severity
  • Pneumatoceles → S. aureus infection
  • Upper-lobe cavitating lesion → tuberculosis
  • Gravity-dependent location → aspiration pneumonia
  • CT scan: useful for suspected loculated effusion, cavitary disease, or postobstructive pneumonia (tumor/foreign body)
Diagnostic Tools — Detail
🔬 Sputum Gram Stain & Culture
  • Main purpose: Ensure specimen suitability for culture
  • Suitable specimen criteria: >25 neutrophils AND <10 squamous epithelial cells per low-power field
  • Can identify: S. pneumoniae, S. aureus, gram-negative bacteria
  • Sensitivity and specificity are highly variable
  • Even in proven pneumococcal bacteremia, positive sputum cultures ≤50%
  • Recommended only for hospitalized patients — especially severe cases, MRSA/Pseudomonas risk
🩸 Blood Cultures
  • Yield disappointingly low: only 5–14% positive (even before antibiotics)
  • Most common: S. pneumoniae, then S. aureus, then P. aeruginosa
  • Not required for all hospitalized CAP patients
  • Recommended for: neutropenia, asplenia, complement deficiencies, chronic liver disease, severe CAP, MRSA/P. aeruginosa risk
  • Value: susceptibility data → narrow therapy + track resistance patterns nationally
🧪 Urinary Antigen Tests
  • Two tests available: Pneumococcal and Legionella urinary antigen
  • Legionella: Detects serogroup 1 only (accounts for most community-acquired Legionnaires'); sensitivity 70%, specificity 99%
  • Pneumococcal: Sensitivity 70%, specificity >90%; can give false-positive in colonized children
  • Both detect antigen even after antibiotic therapy started
  • Pneumococcal antigen: reserve for severe cases; Legionella antigen: severe cases + relevant epidemiologic factors
🧬 PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction)
  • Multiplex PCR panels test for multiple viral AND bacterial pathogens
  • Dramatically improve response times vs. standard cultures
  • Nasopharyngeal swab PCR = standard for influenza, coronaviruses
  • Can detect: Legionella, M. pneumoniae, C. pneumoniae, mycobacteria
  • Limitation: upper-airway flora contamination may require quantitative assays
  • Cost-effectiveness NOT definitively established
📊 Biomarkers — CRP & Procalcitonin (PCT)
  • Acute-phase reactants elevated in bacterial infections
  • PCT: Insufficiently accurate for diagnosing bacterial CAP; should NOT be used as basis for withholding antibiotics
  • CRP: Even less sensitive than PCT for detecting bacterial pathogens
  • Appropriate use: In conjunction with history, exam, imaging, labs → facilitate antibiotic stewardship
  • PCT can guide duration of therapy in ICU/VAP patients
🧫 Serology
  • 4-fold rise in specific IgM between acute and convalescent samples = diagnostic of infection
  • Largely fallen out of favor: delays in getting convalescent results, interpretation difficulties

ICU Criteria for Severe CAP (Table 131-3)
Major Criteria (Either alone → ICU)
  • Respiratory failure requiring invasive mechanical ventilation
  • Septic shock requiring vasopressors
Minor Criteria (≥3 of 9 → ICU or high-level monitoring)
  • RR ≥30 breaths/min
  • PaO₂/FiO₂ ratio ≤250

  • Hypotension requiring aggressive fluids

Other Indications for Hospitalization
  • Unable to maintain oral intake
  • Compliance concerns (cognitive impairment, homelessness)
  • O₂ saturation on room air <92%

Antibiotic Resistance★★ 8 Qs · pg 1027
The threats that shape our antibiotic choices
S. pneumoniae Resistance Mechanisms
Antibiotic ClassMechanism of ResistanceKey Numbers
β-Lactams / Penicillins (1) Direct DNA incorporation → remodeling of penicillin-binding proteins (via viridans streptococci contact)
(2) Natural transformation
(3) Gene mutations 		Only 0.6% of US pneumococci resist ceftriaxone/cefotaxime
Macrolides ermB gene: ribosomal methylation in 23S rRNA → high-level resistance (MIC ≥64 μg/mL)
mef gene (M phenotype): efflux pump → low-level resistance (MIC <16 μg/mL) 		ermB: ~40%; mef: ~60% of US resistant isolates. US prevalence: ~40% macrolide-resistant
Fluoroquinolones Mutations in gyrA (topoisomerase II) and parC (topoisomerase IV) genes; ± efflux pump Resistance usually <2%
Doxycycline — 87.5% of pneumococci susceptible in US and Canada
💊 #1 risk factor for antibiotic-resistant pneumococcal infection: Use of a specific antibiotic within the previous 3 months. Always take antibiotic history — use a drug from a DIFFERENT class.
MDR (Multidrug-Resistant) Strains
  • Defined as: resistant to drugs from ≥3 antimicrobial classes with different mechanisms
  • Pneumococcal penicillin resistance often co-occurs with macrolide, tetracycline, TMP-SMX resistance
  • In US, 58.9% of penicillin-resistant pneumococcal blood isolates also resist macrolides
  • High-level macrolide resistance more common in Europe; lower-level predominates in North America
  • If macrolide resistance >25% locally → do NOT use as empiric monotherapy
CA-MRSA Resistance Details
  • Methicillin resistance determined by mecA gene → encodes resistance to ALL β-lactam drugs
  • ≥5 SCCmec types: hospital strains = types II/III; CA-MRSA = type IV
  • CA-MRSA: less resistant overall; often susceptible to TMP-SMX, clindamycin, tetracycline, vancomycin, linezolid

CAP Treatment★★★ 18 Qs · pg 1027–1028
Outpatient, inpatient, and special situations
Treatment Principles
  • Initial therapy almost always empirical (etiology rarely known at outset)
  • Initiate treatment as expeditiously as possible
  • ATS/IDSA guidelines consider: likely pathogens, resistance risk, severity, site of care, MRSA/P. aeruginosa risk
Outpatients Initial Treatment Strategy (Table 131-4)

Treatment Duration, Switch, and Adjunctive Measures
  • Duration: 5 days usually sufficient for uncomplicated CAP
  • Longer courses needed: not clinically stabilized, bacteremia, metastatic infection, virulent pathogen (MRSA, P. aeruginosa)
  • Select patients doing well and clinically stable: may discontinue after 3 days
  • IV→PO switch: When patient can ingest/absorb drugs + hemodynamically stable + clinically improving
  • Fluoroquinolones: very well absorbed → may be given orally from outset to selected patients
Adjunctive Measures:
  • Adequate hydration
  • Oxygen therapy for hypoxemia
  • Vasopressor treatment + assisted ventilation when necessary
  • Glucocorticoids in severe CAP requiring mechanical ventilation or with shock:
    • Mortality benefit shown especially with high systemic inflammation (CRP >15 mg/dL)
    • Therapy continued for 8–14 days by intermittent or continuous infusion
    • COVID-19 severe: Dexamethasone + JAK inhibitor or IL-6 inhibitor for rapidly increasing O₂ needs
Special Situations
  • Proven influenza: Oseltamivir + appropriate antibacterial therapy; be vigilant for MRSA superinfection
  • Viral pathogen only (no bacterial pathogen identified): Antibacterial treatment can be discontinued
  • Severe illness: Always consider bacterial-viral coinfection
  • Aspiration pneumonia: Routine anaerobe coverage unnecessary UNLESS poor dentition, lung abscess, or necrotizing pneumonia
Failure to Improve — Approach at Day 3
  • Noninfectious mimics: pulmonary edema, PE, lung carcinoma, radiation/hypersensitivity pneumonitis, connective tissue disease
  • If truly CAP:
    • Pathogen resistant to drug
    • Sequestered focus (lung abscess, empyema) preventing antibiotic access
    • Wrong drug or wrong dose/frequency
    • Unexpected pathogen (CA-MRSA, M. tuberculosis, fungus)
    • Nosocomial superinfection (pulmonary or extrapulmonary)
  • Reassessment: may include CT or bronchoscopy
Complications of Severe CAP
  • Respiratory failure, shock, multiorgan failure, exacerbation of comorbidities
  • Metastatic infection (brain abscess, endocarditis): unusual; requires high suspicion
  • Lung abscess: CA-MRSA, P. aeruginosa, oral anaerobes, rarely S. pneumoniae
  • Complicated pleural effusion: Drain if pH <7.2, glucose <2.2 mmol/L, LDH >1000 U/L, or bacteria seen/cultured
  • Cardiovascular events (especially elderly, pneumococcal/influenza):
    • CHF, arrhythmia, MI, stroke
    • Can be acute OR delayed up to 1 year
    • Mechanisms: ↑ myocardial load, destabilization of atherosclerotic plaques by inflammation
Follow-up & Prognosis
  • Fever and leukocytosis resolve in 2–4 days in otherwise healthy patients
  • Physical findings may persist longer
  • Chest radiograph clearance: 4–12 weeks (depends on age, lung disease, pathogen)
  • Hospitalized patients: follow-up CXR ~4–6 weeks after discharge
  • Relapse/recurrence in same segment → consider underlying neoplasm or focal bronchiectasis
  • Outpatient nonsmokers with resolved symptoms (5–7 days): follow-up CXR not necessary
  • Prevention: Vaccination (per National Advisory Committees on Immunization Practices)

Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia (VAP)2 Qs · pg 1032
The most studied — and most deadly — hospital pneumonia
Epidemiology Key Numbers
  • Prevalence: 6–52 cases per 100 patients (depends on population)
  • On any given ICU day: 10% of patients have pneumonia (VAP in overwhelming majority)
  • Highest hazard ratio in first 5 days of ventilation; then ~1%/day
  • Cumulative rate in patients ventilated ≥30 days: as high as 70%
  • COVID-19 increased VAP frequency after years of decline
Microbiology — Core vs. MDR Pathogens (Table 131-6)
Non-MDR (Core Pathogens)
  • S. pneumoniae, other Streptococcus spp.
  • H. influenzae
  • Methicillin-sensitive S. aureus
  • Antibiotic-sensitive Enterobacteriaceae: E. coli, K. pneumoniae, Proteus, Enterobacter, Serratia
MDR Pathogens
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa
  • Methicillin-resistant S. aureus (MRSA)
  • Acinetobacter spp.
  • Antibiotic-resistant Enterobacteriaceae (ESBL-positive, carbapenem-resistant)
  • Legionella pneumophila
  • Burkholderia cepacia, Aspergillus spp.
📅 If VAP develops in the first 5–7 days, core pathogens predominate. Prior antibiotic treatment → MDR pathogens even early in hospital course.
Pathogenesis — Three Critical Factors
  • 1. Colonization of oropharynx with pathogenic microorganisms
  • 2. Aspiration of these organisms from oropharynx into lower respiratory tract
  • 3. Compromise of normal host defense mechanisms
The endotracheal tube — the most important risk factor:
  • Bypasses normal mechanical barriers preventing aspiration
  • Microaspiration exacerbated by secretions pooling above the cuff
  • Damages tracheal mucosa → facilitates tracheal colonization
  • Bacteria form glycocalyx biofilm on tube surface → protect from antibiotics + host defenses
  • Bacteria dislodged during suctioning → reinoculate trachea
  • Glycocalyx fragments embolize to distal airways, carrying bacteria
🦠 Antibiotic exposure is the greatest risk factor for P. aeruginosa VAP. Pseudomonal VAP is rare in patients without prior antibiotic exposure.

VAP Diagnosis — The Dilemma
  • No single set of criteria is reliably diagnostic
  • Clinical CAP criteria → overdiagnosis of VAP because:
    • Frequent tracheal colonization in intubated patients
    • Multiple alternative causes of radiographic infiltrates (pulmonary edema, ARDS, contusion, hemorrhage, infarction)
    • High frequency of other fever/leukocytosis causes (C. diff, central line infection, sinusitis, UTI, drug fever)
Two Diagnostic Approaches:
Quantitative Culture:
  • Endotracheal aspirate threshold: 10⁶ cfu/mL
  • Protected specimen brush: 10³ cfu/mL
  • More distal = more specific but less sensitive
  • Less antibiotic use; ideally withhold antibiotics until results (unless critically ill)
  • Limitation: New antibiotic in 24–48 h → false-negative results
Clinical Approach (IDSA/ATS Guideline Recommendation):
  • Semiquantitative cultures + clinical criteria
  • Absence of bacteria in Gram-stained ETT aspirates → pneumonia unlikely
  • Absence of MDR pathogen → can de-escalate empiric therapy
  • Individualized per institution based on colonization rates, expertise, antibiotic history

VAP Treatment Duration & Failure
  • Duration: 7–8 day course = as effective as 2 weeks + less emergence of resistant strains
  • Exceptions: inappropriate initial therapy, resistant organisms (e.g., carbapenemase-producing Acinetobacter)
  • Procalcitonin can guide duration in these exceptions
VAP Failure Rates (strikingly high):
  • MRSA VAP treated with standard-dose vancomycin: 40% clinical failure rate
  • Linezolid: ~15% more efficacious than adjusted-dose vancomycin
  • VAP due to Pseudomonas: 40–50% failure rate regardless of regimen
  • Biofilm on ETT → persistence + reintroduction of organism → recurrent VAP by same pathogen
  • ~50% of recurrent Pseudomonas VAP caused by a new strain
VAP Prognosis
  • Crude mortality: 50–70%
  • Attributable mortality: exceeded 25% in matched-cohort study; more recent studies: 5–10%
  • VAP complicating COVID-19: higher attributable mortality than other VAP
  • VAP in trauma patients: NOT associated with attributable mortality (patients otherwise healthy pre-injury)
  • MDR pathogens → significantly greater attributable mortality than non-MDR
  • S. maltophilia VAP: marker for severely immunocompromised host
