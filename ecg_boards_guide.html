Electrophysiologic Background

An electrocardiogram (ECG/EKG) is a graphical representation of electrical activity generated by the heart. Signals are detected by metallic electrodes on the extremities and chest wall, amplified, and recorded. ECG leads display the instantaneous differences in electrical potentials between specific electrode sets.

Why the ECG matters
The ECG is noninvasive, inexpensive, and immediately available. It detects arrhythmias, conduction disturbances, myocardial ischemia/infarction, life-threatening metabolic disturbances, drug toxicities, and increased susceptibility to sudden cardiac arrest.

The Three Sources of Electrical Current

  • Cardiac pacemaker cells — generate spontaneous electrical impulses
  • Specialized conduction tissue — rapidly distributes the impulse
  • Working myocardium — what the ECG actually records (depolarization + repolarization)
Critical Concept
The ECG records only the depolarization and repolarization of the working atrial and ventricular myocardium — not the activity of pacemaker cells or specialized conduction fibers themselves.

Cardiac Conduction Sequence

SA Node
→

→

Ventricles (endo→epi)
  • SA node: Initiates normal heartbeat; possesses spontaneous automaticity
  • AV junction: Composed of AV node + His-bundle region; imposes a physiologic delay
  • His bundle: Bifurcates into right bundle branch (RBB) and left bundle branch (LBB)
  • LBB: Fans out into left anterior fascicle and left posterior fascicle
  • Purkinje fibers: Rapidly and synchronously deliver the impulse to ventricular myocardium
  • Ventricular spread: Endocardium to epicardium, triggering coordinated contraction

Since cardiac depolarization and repolarization wavefronts have both direction and magnitude, they can be represented as vectors — the foundation of ECG interpretation.

Drugs & Electrolytes Affecting Phases
  • Decrease phase 0 slope (impair Na⁺ influx) — hyperkalemia, flecainide → widens QRS
  • Prolong phase 2 or 3 — amiodarone, hypocalcemia → increases QT interval
  • Shorten repolarization — hypercalcemia, digoxin → abbreviates QT interval

Basic ECG Waveforms & Intervals

Basic ECG waveform. The RR interval (not shown) spans two consecutive R waves.

Individual Waveforms

P Wave

Represents atrial depolarization. Initiated by SA node. Normal orientation: downward and leftward.

QRS Complex

Represents ventricular depolarization. Rapid, synchronous activation via bundle branches and Purkinje system.

ST–T–U Complex

Represents ventricular repolarization. ST segment = plateau phase; T wave = active repolarization; U wave = small, follows T.

J Point

Junction between the end of the QRS and the beginning of the ST segment. Key reference point for ST deviation measurement.

Atrial Repolarization
Atrial repolarization waveforms (ST-Ta) are usually too low in amplitude to detect. They may become apparent in: acute pericarditis, atrial infarction, and AV heart block syndromes.

U Wave

  • Small, rounded deflection (≤1 mm), follows T wave, same polarity as T wave
  • Abnormally prominent U waves most commonly due to: hypokalemia or drugs (dofetilide, amiodarone, sotalol, quinidine)
  • Very prominent U waves as part of prolonged repolarization syndromes → marker of increased susceptibility to torsades de pointes

ECG Paper and Calibration

Time (Horizontal)
  • Recording speed: 25 mm/s
  • Small box (1mm) = 40 ms (0.04 s)
  • Large box (5mm) = 200 ms (0.20 s)
  • Heart rate = 300 ÷ (# large boxes between R waves)
Amplitude (Vertical)
  • Standard calibration: 1 mV = 10 mm
  • Voltage criteria for hypertrophy given in millimeters
  • Low voltage defined as <5mm limb or <10mm chest leads

The Four Major ECG Intervals

QTc Formulas — Know Both
  • Bazett (classic "square root"): QTc = QT / √RR (computed in seconds) — criticized for errors at extremes of HR
  • Framingham (preferred): QTc = QT + 0.154 × (1000 – RR) — units in milliseconds
  • Upper limits: QTc >460 ms in women; >450 ms in men (based on longest QT visually)
  • Lower limits are less well defined
03

ECG Leads

The 12 conventional ECG leads are divided into two groups: 6 limb (extremity) leads recording potentials on the frontal plane, and 6 chest (precordial) leads recording potentials on the horizontal plane. Each lead is like a different camera angle viewing the same electrical events from different spatial orientations.

The Two Planes

Frontal Plane — Limb Leads
  • I, II, III — bipolar leads
  • aVR, aVL, aVF — augmented unipolar leads
  • Represented on a hexaxial diagram
  • Used to determine QRS axis
  • Lead II: positive pole points toward feet; P wave upright in sinus rhythm
  • aVR: positive pole points toward right shoulder; P normally negative
Horizontal Plane — Chest Leads
  • V1–V6 — explore from right sternum to left lateral chest
  • V1: right sternal border (4th ICS)
  • V2: left sternal border (4th ICS)
  • V3: between V2 and V4
  • V4: 5th ICS, midclavicular line
  • V5: anterior axillary line
  • V6: midaxillary line

Lead Polarity Principle

  • Positive (upright) deflection: depolarization wave spreads toward the positive pole of that lead
  • Negative deflection: depolarization wave spreads toward the negative pole
  • Biphasic deflection: mean depolarization vector is at right angles to the lead axis

Supplementary Leads

  • Right precordial leads (V3R–V6R): detect acute right ventricular ischemia/infarction (especially in inferior STEMI)
  • Posterior leads (V7–V9): same horizontal plane as V4; facilitate detection of acute posterolateral infarction
  • Bedside monitors and Holter monitors typically use only 1–2 modified leads
04

Genesis of the Normal ECG

P Wave

  • Normal atrial depolarization vector: downward and to the left (SA node → right atrium → left atrium)
  • Lead II: positive P wave (vector toward positive pole) ✓ Normal sinus rhythm
  • aVR: negative P wave (vector toward negative pole) ✓ Normal sinus rhythm
  • V1: may be biphasic — positive component (right atrial depolarization) + small (<1 mm²) negative component (left atrial depolarization)
Ectopic / Retrograde P Waves
Activation from an ectopic pacemaker in the lower atrium or AV junction produces retrograde P waves: negative in lead II, positive in aVR — the opposite of normal.

QRS Complex — Two Phases

Phase 1 — Septal Depolarization

Interventricular septum depolarizes from left to right and anteriorly (this is because the left bundle branch delivers impulse to LV side of septum first).

  • V1 (right side): small positive deflection → septal r wave
  • V6 (left side): small negative deflection → septal q wave
Phase 2 — Ventricular Depolarization

Simultaneous depolarization of LV and RV. LV dominates due to greater muscle mass → vector points leftward and posteriorly.

  • V1: larger negative deflection → S wave
  • V6: relatively tall positive deflection → R wave
R-Wave Progression
From V1 to V6, R-wave amplitude progressively increases and S-wave amplitude decreases. The transition zone (where R ≈ S) is normally at V3 or V4. Deviation from this pattern is an important diagnostic clue.

QRS Axis

The electrical axis describes the mean orientation of the QRS vector relative to the six frontal plane leads.

AxisDegreesCommon Causes
Normal−30° to +100°Normal finding
Left Axis Deviation (LAD)< −30°LVH, left anterior fascicular block, inferior MI
Right Axis Deviation (RAD)> +90° to +100°RV overload, lateral infarction, dextrocardia, left pneumothorax, LPFB, normal in children/young adults
Extreme Axis Deviation+180° to −90°Ventricular tachycardia, severe RVH, lead reversal
Spurious Right Axis Deviation
Reversal of the left and right arm electrodes causes a spurious right axis deviation — always check lead placement artifacts before diagnosing RAD.

T Wave and U Wave

  • Mean T-wave vector is roughly concordant with the mean QRS vector (within ~45° in the frontal plane)
  • Depolarization and repolarization are electrically opposite processes → T-QRS concordance indicates repolarization proceeds in the reverse direction from depolarization (epicardium to endocardium)
  • U wave: small (≤1mm), rounded, follows T, same polarity as T wave
  • Abnormally prominent U waves → hypokalemia or drugs (dofetilide, amiodarone, sotalol, quinidine)
  • Prominent U waves as part of prolonged repolarization → increased susceptibility to torsades de pointes
05

Cardiac Enlargement & Hypertrophy

Atrial Abnormalities

Right Atrial Overload ("P-pulmonale")
  • P wave amplitude ≥2.5 mm in limb or precordial leads
  • Tall, peaked P waves
  • Causes: pulmonary hypertension, pulmonary stenosis, cor pulmonale, tricuspid stenosis
Left Atrial Abnormality ("P-mitrale")
  • Broad (≥120 ms), often notched P wave in limb leads
  • Biphasic P in V1 with prominent negative component (represents delayed LA depolarization)
  • May occur with interatrial conduction delays without true atrial enlargement → hence "abnormality" not "overload"

Right Ventricular Hypertrophy (RVH)

  • Tall R wave in V1 (R ≥ S wave) — the most characteristic finding
  • Right axis deviation — usually present
  • Alternative: qR pattern in V1 or V3R
  • ST depression + T-wave inversion in right to mid-precordial leads (right ventricular "strain")
  • Prominent S waves in left lateral precordial leads (I, V5, V6)
  • This pattern requires sustained, severe pressure overload (e.g., pulmonic stenosis, pulmonary arterial hypertension)

Special RVH Patterns:

  • ASD (ostium secundum): right ventricular volume overload → incomplete or complete RBBB pattern + rightward QRS axis
  • Acute cor pulmonale (PE, COVID-19 ARDS): sinus tachycardia; QRS shift rightward; S1Q3T3 pattern; slow R-wave progression; ST-T abnormalities V1–V4 (can simulate anterior MI)
  • Chronic cor pulmonale (emphysema): slow R-wave progression; low-voltage complexes (hyperaeration); does NOT show classic tall R in V1
S1Q3T3 Pattern
Prominence of the S wave in lead I + Q wave in lead III + T-wave inversion in lead III. Classic ECG pattern of acute cor pulmonale (most commonly from pulmonary embolism).

Left Ventricular Hypertrophy (LVH)

Voltage Criteria (memorize these):

Cornell Criteria
RaVL + SV3 > 20 mm (women)
RaVL + SV3 > 28 mm (men)
Sokolow-Lyon Criteria
SV1 + (RV5 or RV6) > 35 mm
  • Repolarization abnormalities: ST depression + T-wave inversion ("LV strain pattern") in leads with prominent R waves
  • Presence of left atrial abnormality increases likelihood of LVH in borderline voltage cases
  • LVH often progresses to incomplete or complete left bundle branch block
  • LVH is a major noninvasive marker of increased cardiovascular morbidity and mortality (including sudden cardiac death)
Limitations of ECG for LVH Diagnosis
Sensitivity of voltage criteria is LOW in:
  • Middle-aged to older adults
  • Obese persons and smokers
  • Patients with right bundle branch block
Prominent precordial voltages are a common normal variant, especially in young or athletic individuals. Echocardiography and cardiac MRI provide more definitive anatomic information.

Bundle Branch Blocks & Related Patterns

QRS Duration Rules
Complete BBB: QRS ≥ 120 ms  |  Incomplete BBB: QRS 110–120 ms
The QRS vector points toward the myocardial region where depolarization is delayed.

Right Bundle Branch Block (RBBB)

  • Terminal QRS vector: right and anterior
  • rSR' in V1 ("rabbit ears") — the classic pattern
  • qRS in V6 (slurred S wave)
  • Secondary T-wave inversion in V1 (T opposite to last QRS deflection)
  • Without structural heart disease: more common than LBBB as an incidental finding

RBBB with Structural Disease:

  • Congenital: atrial septal defect, Ebstein anomaly
  • Acquired: valvular, ischemic heart disease
Brugada Pattern — Critical Board Topic
A distinctive pattern simulating RBBB: RBBB-like morphology with ST-segment elevations in the right chest leads (V1–V2). Associated with increased risk of ventricular fibrillation and sudden cardiac death. May be unmasked by sodium channel blockers, fever, or vagotonic states.

Left Bundle Branch Block (LBBB)

  • Alters both early and late phases of ventricular depolarization
  • Major QRS vector: left and posterior
  • Septal depolarization proceeds right to left (opposite of normal — reversed because impulse arrives from right side first)
  • Wide QS complex in V1 (predominantly negative)
  • Entirely positive (R) complex in V6
  • Secondary T-wave inversions in leads with wide R wave (V5, V6, I, aVL)

LBBB is a Marker of Four Serious Conditions:

1. Coronary Artery Disease

Frequently with impaired LV function — highest morbidity/mortality among the four

2. Hypertensive Heart Disease

Long-standing hypertension causing LVH → LBBB progression

3. Aortic Valve Disease

Including after transcatheter aortic valve replacement (TAVR)

4. Cardiomyopathy

Dilated cardiomyopathy most commonly; may indicate candidacy for CRT

RV Pacing vs LBBB
Electronic right ventricular pacing produces waveform patterns identical to LBBB, preceded by a sharp pacemaker spike (sometimes very low amplitude).

Primary vs. Secondary Repolarization Abnormalities

Secondary Repolarization

Caused by altered depolarization sequence (e.g., BBB). T wave is opposite in polarity to the last deflection of the QRS. This is an expected/predictable consequence of the conduction abnormality itself — not a sign of ischemia on its own.

Primary Repolarization

Independent of QRS changes. Due to actual alterations in the electrical properties of myocardial fibers (resting membrane potential or action potential duration). Caused by: ischemia, electrolyte imbalance, digoxin.

Clinical Implication
Primary and secondary T-wave changes may coexist. For example, T-wave inversions in the right precordial leads with LBBB, or in the left precordial leads with RBBB, may be important markers of underlying ischemia.

Fascicular Blocks (Hemiblocks)

  • Do not substantially prolong QRS duration (usually <120ms)
  • Manifest as shifts in the frontal plane QRS axis
Left Anterior Fascicular Block (LAFB)
  • QRS axis more negative than −45°
  • Most common cause of marked left axis deviation in adults
  • Left axis deviation without other explanation → think LAFB first
Left Posterior Fascicular Block (LPFB)
  • QRS axis more rightward than +110° to +120°
  • Extremely rare as an isolated finding
  • Requires exclusion of other causes of right axis deviation (RVH, lateral MI, dextrocardia)

Other Intraventricular Conduction Delays

  • Rate-related BBB: bundle branch block that appears when heart rate exceeds a critical value; resolves at lower rates
  • Extrinsic (toxic) causes: hyperkalemia, class 1 antiarrhythmic agents, tricyclic antidepressants, phenothiazines — all slow ventricular conduction
  • Pre-excitation (WPW): QRS prolongation due to early activation via bypass tract, NOT due to conduction delay — associated with delta wave and short PR interval

Myocardial Ischemia & Infarction

Mechanism of ST Deviation

Severe, acute ischemia lowers the resting membrane potential and shortens the action potential duration. This creates a voltage gradient between normal and ischemic zones → current of injury → ST-segment deviation.

Transmural (Epicardial) Ischemia
  • ST vector shifts toward outer (epicardial) layers
  • Overlying leads: ST elevation
  • Earliest change: tall, positive hyperacute T waves
  • Classic STEMI pattern
Subendocardial Ischemia
  • ST vector shifts toward subendocardium/ventricular cavity
  • Overlying leads: ST depression
  • Lead aVR: ST elevation (mirror image)
  • NSTEMI / unstable angina pattern

  • ST elevations typically followed within hours to days by T-wave inversions and often Q waves in the same lead distribution
  • Persistent ST elevation ≥ several weeks post-MI usually correlates with severe wall motion disorder (not necessarily frank ventricular aneurysm)
  • Complete ECG normalization after Q-wave MI is uncommon but may occur with smaller infarcts

Wellens T-Wave Sign — High-Yield

Wellens Sign — Do NOT Stress Test
Presentation: Patient with ischemic chest pain that has resolved. ECG shows deep, symmetric T-wave inversions in V1–V4 (sometimes I and aVL), with or without cardiac enzyme elevation.

Significance: High-grade stenosis of the left anterior descending (LAD) coronary artery — pre-infarction warning. The patient is at high risk for massive anterior STEMI.

Action: Admit, do NOT perform stress test, proceed to coronary angiography.

Q Waves — Updated Classification

  • Old teaching: Q-wave MI = transmural; non-Q-wave MI = subendocardial — this is unreliable
  • Current teaching:
    • Transmural MI can occur without Q waves
    • Subendocardial (nontransmural) MI can have Q waves
    • Classify as "Q-wave" or "non-Q-wave" MI
  • Posterior/lateral infarction: loss of depolarization forces → reciprocal increase in R-wave amplitude in V1–V2, without diagnostic Q waves in standard leads → add V7–V9

Prinzmetal's (Variant) Angina

  • Mechanism: coronary vasospasm
  • ECG: transient ST elevation without development of Q waves
  • ST elevations may resolve completely in minutes OR be followed by T-wave inversions that persist for hours or days

ECG Limitations in Ischemia

False Negatives — Don't Miss Ischemia
  • A single normal ECG does not exclude ischemia or acute MI
  • A normal ECG throughout the course of an acute infarct is distinctly uncommon
  • Diagnostic changes often masked by: LBBB, electronic ventricular pacing, WPW pre-excitation
  • Prolonged chest pain without ECG changes → always search for non-coronary causes
False Positives — Don't Over-diagnose Ischemia
ST elevation or depression, T-wave inversions, tall T waves, and Q waves can all have non-ischemic causes (pseudoinfarct patterns):
  • ST elevation: acute pericarditis/myocarditis, early repolarization, LVH/LBBB, Brugada, Takotsubo, hypothermia
  • T-wave inversion: RVH, cardiomyopathies, myocarditis, Takotsubo, subarachnoid hemorrhage, "cardiac memory"
  • ST depression: LVH, hypokalemia, digoxin, many other factors
  • Q waves: LVH, positional variants, HCM, myocarditis, LBBB

Metabolic Factors & Drug Effects

Metabolic abnormalities and drugs primarily affect repolarization (ST-T-U) and sometimes cause QRS prolongation. Several can be life-threatening and the ECG is crucial for initial diagnosis and monitoring.

Hyperkalemia — Progressive ECG Changes (Most Tested)

Peaked "Tented" T Waves ← EARLIEST
→
PR prolongation
→
Wide QRS
→
P waves disappear
→
Sine-Wave Pattern
→
Asystole ← FATAL
Board Key
Earliest change = peaked ("tented") T waves. If you see wide QRS + absent P waves → severe hyperkalemia requiring emergency treatment. Mechanism: impaired Na⁺ influx decreases phase 0 slope → widened QRS.

Hypokalemia

  • Prolonged ventricular repolarization
  • Prominent U waves — most characteristic finding
  • Prolonged QT interval
  • Risk for torsades de pointes

Calcium Disorders

Hypocalcemia
  • Prolongs QT interval — specifically the ST-segment portion
  • QTc >500ms possible
Hypercalcemia
  • Abbreviates the ST segment → shorter QT interval
  • Can cause ST elevation in V1–V3 (DDx for Brugada pattern)

Digitalis Effect

  • Shortened QT interval (shortens ventricular repolarization)
  • Characteristic "scooping" of the ST–T-wave complex ("Salvador Dali mustache" appearance)
  • This is a pharmacologic effect, distinct from digitalis toxicity

Hypothermia

  • Osborn wave (J wave): distinctive convex elevation at the J point — pathognomonic of hypothermia
  • Bradycardia
  • Prolonged QRS and QT
  • Atrial fibrillation is common

Intracranial Bleeds (Subarachnoid Hemorrhage)

  • "CVA T-wave pattern": marked QT prolongation with deep, wide T-wave inversions
  • Can mimic ischemic T-wave changes very closely
  • Mechanism: sympathetic surge and catecholamine-mediated myocardial injury

Drugs Prolonging QT Interval (→ Torsades Risk)

Drug ClassExamplesMechanism
Class IA antiarrhythmicsQuinidine, procainamide, disopyramideBlock IKr + INa → prolong phase 2 and 3
Class III antiarrhythmicsAmiodarone, dofetilide, sotalol, ibutilideBlock IKr → prolong phase 3
Tricyclic antidepressantsAmitriptyline, imipramineINa + IKr blockade
PhenothiazinesThioridazine, chlorpromazineIKr blockade
Torsades de Pointes
Very prominent U waves and/or prolonged QT — especially from hypokalemia, inherited channelopathies, or pharmacologic agents — indicate increased susceptibility to torsades de pointes ventricular tachycardia (a polymorphic VT that can degenerate to VF).

Tricyclic Antidepressant Overdose — ECG Pattern

  • Sinus tachycardia
  • QRS prolongation (due to Na⁺ channel blockade)
  • QT prolongation
  • Right axis deviation (terminal R wave in aVR >3mm — highly specific)
  • Risk of ventricular arrhythmias and sudden death

Early Repolarization vs. STEMI — Key Differentiators
  • Early repolarization: concave ("smiley face") upward ST elevation; stable over time; common in young athletes; no wall motion abnormality on echo
  • STEMI: convex ("frowning" or tombstone) ST elevation; evolves with time; territorial distribution; reciprocal changes in opposite leads
Acute Pericarditis Pattern
  • Stage 1: Diffuse STE in all leads except aVR and V1 (where there is ST depression); PR depression (very helpful finding)
  • No reciprocal changes (except in aVR/V1)
  • No evolution to Q waves
  • No territorial pattern — affects all coronary territories simultaneously

Low QRS Voltage & Electrical Alternans

Low QRS Voltage

Definition
Peak-to-trough QRS amplitude ≤5 mm in all six limb leads AND/OR ≤10 mm in the chest leads.

Causes (Think: Fluid, Air, Scar, Systemic):

  • Pericardial effusion — fluid insulates the heart; most dangerous (can have tamponade)
  • Pleural effusion — similar insulating mechanism
  • COPD / Emphysema — hyperaeration increases impedance; also displaces heart
  • Cardiac amyloid — infiltrative disease replaces myocardium with non-conducting amyloid protein; low voltage + LVH on echo ("voltage-mass discordance")
  • Anasarca — massive body fluid
  • Also: myocardial fibrosis, hypothyroidism (myxedema)
Cardiac Amyloid — Boards Pearl
Low voltage + pseudoinfarct Q waves on ECG + concentric LVH on echo + preserved or reduced EF = cardiac amyloidosis until proven otherwise. The "voltage-mass discordance" (LVH on echo but low voltage on ECG) is nearly pathognomonic.

Electrical Alternans Syndromes

Beat-to-beat alternation in one or more components of the ECG signal — a type of nonlinear cardiovascular response to hemodynamic/electrophysiologic perturbations.

Total Electrical Alternans (P-QRS-T)
  • Alternation in all components (P wave + QRS + T wave)
  • Combined with sinus tachycardia
  • = Relatively specific sign of pericardial effusion with cardiac tamponade
  • Mechanism: heart "swings" back and forth in the pericardial fluid
  • Classic triad of tamponade: sinus tachycardia + low voltage + total electrical alternans
Pure Repolarization Alternans (T-wave)
  • Alternation only in T-wave (repolarization)
  • Sign of electrical instability
  • May precede life-threatening ventricular tachyarrhythmias
  • T-wave alternans testing is used to stratify VT/VF risk
Sensitivity Limitation
The classic triad of tamponade (sinus tachycardia + low voltage + electrical alternans) is highly suggestive but of limited sensitivity — not all tamponades show all three features. Echocardiography is the gold standard for diagnosis.

Systematic ECG Interpretation — 14 Parameters

Accurate ECG analysis requires thoroughness and care. Consider the patient's age, biological sex, and clinical status always. Most ECG interpretation errors are errors of omission. Use this systematic approach for every ECG.

Always Compare with Previous ECGs
Comparison with prior ECGs is invaluable. A "new" finding has very different clinical significance than a longstanding one. Never interpret an ECG in isolation if priors are available.

The 14-Step Checklist

Technical Quality

1Standardization & calibration — Is the calibration mark 10mm? Any artifact? Correct lead placement?

Rhythm

2Rhythm — Sinus? Regular? P before every QRS? P-QRS relationship?

Rate

3Heart rate — 300 ÷ large boxes between R waves. Normal 60–100 bpm.

Conduction

4PR interval / AV conduction — Normal 120–200ms. Short = pre-excitation. Long = AV block.

QRS Duration

5QRS interval — ≤110ms normal; 110–120ms incomplete BBB; ≥120ms complete BBB or other cause.

QT Interval

6QT/QTc intervals — Rate-correct. QTc >460ms (♀) or >450ms (♂) = prolonged.

Axis

7Mean QRS electrical axis — Normal −30° to +100°. Check leads I and aVF first.

P Waves

8P waves — Morphology (peaked? broad? biphasic?). Sinus vs. ectopic. Atrial enlargement pattern?

Voltage

9QRS voltages — Check for LVH, RVH. Check for low voltage (<5mm limb; <10mm chest).

R Progression

10Precordial R-wave progression — Should increase from V1 to V5–V6. Transition zone V3 or V4. Poor progression = anterior MI or RVH.

Q Waves

11Abnormal Q waves — ≥40ms wide or ≥25% of QRS amplitude in any lead. Territorial distribution suggests MI.

ST Segments

12ST segments — Elevation or depression? Measure at J point. Reciprocal changes? Diffuse vs. territorial?

T Waves

13T waves — Inversion, hyperacuity, flattening. Concordant with QRS? Primary vs secondary change?

U Waves

14U waves — Prominent? Same direction as T? Think hypokalemia, drugs, channelopathy.

Computerized ECG Analysis

Critical Limitation
Fully automated computerized ECG analyses still have major limitations and should not be accepted without careful clinician review. Always read the raw tracing yourself.

Exam Traps

Board Exam Coach Format
Each trap below shows how the question is typically presented, the common mistake, and the correct answer.
Trap 01Negative P in aVR = Normal
A 35-year-old healthy male has a routine ECG. The P wave is negative in lead aVR. The USMLE-style question asks: "What does this finding indicate?"

The trap: Students think negative P in aVR is always abnormal.
CORRECT ANSWER: This is completely normal for sinus rhythm. The normal atrial depolarization vector is directed toward the negative pole of aVR. P wave is normally negative in aVR and positive in lead II. A positive P in aVR + negative P in II = retrograde activation (ectopic or junctional).
Trap 02Hyperkalemia: Earliest ECG Change
A dialysis patient misses 3 sessions. ECG shows wide QRS complexes with absent P waves. The question asks: "What was the EARLIEST ECG change in this patient?"

The trap: The current ECG shows the end-stage — students pick wide QRS or absent P waves.
CORRECT ANSWER: The earliest change is peaked ("tented") T waves. The progression is: peaked T → wide QRS → absent P → sine wave → asystole. Always identify earliest, not current, finding when asked.
Trap 03Wellens Sign — Don't Stress Test
A patient had chest pain that resolved 2 hours ago. ECG shows deep symmetric T-wave inversions in V1–V4. Troponin is borderline. The question asks: "What is the next best step?"

The trap: Pain resolved → students think the patient is stable → stress test next.
CORRECT ANSWER: This is Wellens T-wave sign — indicates critical LAD stenosis. The patient is at imminent risk for massive anterior STEMI. DO NOT perform stress testing (can precipitate occlusion). Admit and proceed directly to coronary angiography.
Trap 04Posterior MI — Read V1–V3 as a Mirror
Patient presents with inferior STEMI (ST elevation in II, III, aVF). Leads V1–V3 show ST depression with tall R waves. The question asks: "What additional territory is involved?"

The trap: Students see ST depression in V1–V3 and think anterior subendocardial ischemia.
CORRECT ANSWER: The ST depression in V1–V3 represents reciprocal changes from posterior wall involvement. The posterior MI causes ST elevation posteriorly, which appears as ST depression in V1–V3 (facing the opposite direction). Add posterior leads V7–V9. Posterior MI always accompanies inferior or lateral territory.
Trap 05Tamponade Triad — Electrical Alternans
An ICU patient with malignancy develops hypotension, sinus tachycardia, muffled heart sounds. ECG shows low voltage and beat-to-beat alternation of QRS amplitude. The question asks: "What does the ECG finding indicate?"

The trap: Students miss electrical alternans or confuse it with a different arrhythmia.
CORRECT ANSWER: Total electrical alternans (P-QRS-T) + sinus tachycardia = classic pattern of pericardial effusion with cardiac tamponade. The heart physically swings in pericardial fluid, producing alternating QRS amplitude. The classic triad = sinus tachycardia + low voltage + electrical alternans. Order emergency echocardiography and prepare for pericardiocentesis.
Trap 06LBBB Masks Ischemia
A patient with known LBBB presents with chest pain. ECG shows secondary T-wave inversions in V5–V6. Cardiology is called. The question asks: "Can you diagnose STEMI in this patient using standard ST criteria?"

The trap: Students try to apply standard ST elevation criteria to a LBBB ECG.
CORRECT ANSWER: No. LBBB causes expected (secondary) ST-T changes that mask ischemic changes. Standard ST criteria cannot be applied. Use the Sgarbossa criteria for detecting MI in LBBB (concordant ST elevation ≥1mm in any lead, ST depression ≥1mm in V1–V3, or discordant ST elevation ≥5mm). New or presumed new LBBB in the context of chest pain should be treated as STEMI equivalent until proven otherwise.
Trap 07Left Axis Deviation — Most Common Cause
A 65-year-old has a QRS axis of −65°. No prior history of MI. Normal echocardiogram. The question asks: "What is the most likely cause?"

The trap: Students jump to inferior MI or LVH without considering LAFB.
CORRECT ANSWER: Left anterior fascicular block (LAFB) — defined as QRS axis more negative than −45° — is the most common cause of marked left axis deviation in adults. Other causes: LVH, inferior MI, LBBB. LAFB does not substantially prolong QRS duration. Look for the axis shift without QRS widening as the key distinguishing feature.
Trap 08Brugada Pattern — High Risk in Young Males
An asymptomatic 28-year-old male has a pre-employment ECG showing RBBB-like morphology with coved ST elevation in V1–V2. He had a brother who died suddenly at age 32. The question asks: "What is the most important diagnosis to consider?"

The trap: Students attribute the pattern to normal RBBB variant.
CORRECT ANSWER: Brugada syndrome. The Brugada pattern (RBBB-like + coved STE in V1–V2) is associated with risk of ventricular fibrillation and sudden cardiac death. This is not normal RBBB — RBBB does not cause ST elevation. Family history of sudden death is a major risk factor. Refer to electrophysiology. ICD placement may be indicated.
Trap 09QTc Upper Limits — Know the Sex Difference
A drug study reports: "QTc of 455ms in a female patient taking the study drug." The question asks: "Is this QTc prolonged?"

The trap: Students apply the same QTc cutoff to all patients regardless of sex.
CORRECT ANSWER: Yes — QTc of 455ms is prolonged in a woman. Upper limits: QTc >460ms in women; QTc >450ms in men. Women have an intrinsically longer QTc than men at baseline and are at higher risk for drug-induced torsades de pointes. This sex difference is clinically important for drug prescribing and safety monitoring.
Trap 10Early Repolarization vs. STEMI
A healthy 22-year-old athlete has a routine ECG showing ST elevation in V2–V4 of 2mm. No symptoms. The question asks: "What is the correct interpretation?"

The trap: Students activate the cath lab for a normal variant.
CORRECT ANSWER: This is most consistent with early (benign) repolarization — a normal variant common in young athletic men. Key features: concave ("smiley face") upward ST elevation, J-wave notching, no reciprocal changes, no evolution over time. STEMI: convex or tombstone STE, evolves, territorial distribution, reciprocal changes, symptoms. Context and serial ECGs are essential.
Trap 11Low Voltage + LVH on Echo = Amyloid
A 72-year-old presents with progressive dyspnea. Echo shows marked concentric LVH with a "sparkling" appearance. ECG shows low voltage. The question asks: "What unifying diagnosis explains both findings?"

The trap: Students see LVH and expect HIGH voltage on ECG, not low voltage.
CORRECT ANSWER: Cardiac amyloidosis. The "voltage-mass discordance" — low voltage on ECG with LVH on echo — is near-pathognomonic for amyloid infiltration. Amyloid protein replaces conducting myocardium with non-conducting material, reducing voltage despite increased wall thickness. Pseudoinfarct Q waves may also be present. Confirm with cardiac MRI or nuclear scintigraphy (PYP/DPD scan for ATTR).
Trap 12Acute Pericarditis — PR Depression Is Key
A 24-year-old has pleuritic chest pain worsened by lying flat and relieved by leaning forward. ECG shows ST elevation in multiple leads including V2–V5, I, II, III, aVF. The question asks: "Which additional ECG finding best supports pericarditis over STEMI?"

The trap: Students see diffuse ST elevation and think massive STEMI.
CORRECT ANSWER: PR-segment depression (in most leads) with PR elevation in aVR. This reflects atrial injury — atrial repolarization changes become visible because the atrial pericardium is inflamed. Additional supporting features: diffuse (non-territorial) STE; saddle-shaped concave elevation; no reciprocal changes; no Q waves. In STEMI: territorial + reciprocal changes + evolving pattern.
Trap 13S1Q3T3 = Think PE
A post-operative patient develops sudden dyspnea. ECG shows sinus tachycardia, prominent S wave in lead I, new Q wave in lead III, and T-wave inversion in lead III. The question asks: "What is the most likely diagnosis?"

The trap: Students see Q wave in III and T-wave inversion and think inferior MI.
CORRECT ANSWER: The S1Q3T3 pattern (S wave in lead I + Q wave + T-wave inversion in lead III) + sinus tachycardia in a post-operative patient = acute cor pulmonale from pulmonary embolism. This is not an inferior MI (which would show ST elevation in II, III, aVF). Obtain CT pulmonary angiogram urgently. Remember: the ECG in PE is most commonly just sinus tachycardia — S1Q3T3 is specific but not sensitive.
Trap 14Subarachnoid Hemorrhage Mimics Ischemia
A 50-year-old presents with sudden "thunderclap headache" and is found unconscious. ECG shows deep wide T-wave inversions in V2–V5 with a very long QT interval. Troponin is mildly elevated. The question asks: "Is this presentation explained by acute myocardial infarction?"

The trap: Deep T-wave inversions + mild troponin elevation → students say NSTEMI.
CORRECT ANSWER: No — this is the "CVA T-wave pattern" seen with intracranial bleeds, particularly subarachnoid hemorrhage. Deep, wide T-wave inversions with marked QT prolongation result from catecholamine-mediated myocardial injury, not coronary occlusion. The thunderclap headache is the key clinical clue. Order CT head first. These patients can have troponin elevation and wall motion abnormalities (neurogenic stunned myocardium) without obstructive CAD.
