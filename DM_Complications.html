01

Overview of DM Complications

Core Concept
DM complications affect virtually every organ system and account for most of the morbidity and mortality of the disease. Prevention through aggressive glycemic, lipid, and blood pressure control alongside early detection is the cornerstone of management.

Classification

Microvascular (DM-Specific)
  • Retinopathy — nonproliferative / proliferative; macular edema
  • Neuropathy — sensory, motor (polyneuropathy); autonomic
  • Nephropathy — albuminuria; declining GFR → ESRD

Caused by chronic hyperglycemia. Usually appear after ~10 years of diabetes.

Macrovascular (Shared Pathophysiology)
  • Coronary heart disease (CHD)
  • Peripheral arterial disease (PAD)
  • Cerebrovascular disease
  • Heart failure — 2× more common in DM

Insulin resistance, dyslipidemia, hypertension all contribute. ASCVD risk may develop before overt hyperglycemia.

Nonvascular Complications
  • Infections (greater frequency and severity in DM)
  • Skin changes: xerosis, diabetic dermopathy, necrobiosis lipoidica diabeticorum
  • Cheiroarthropathy (thickened skin, reduced joint mobility), hearing loss, cataracts, glaucoma, periodontal disease
  • Comorbid conditions (uncertain link to hyperglycemia): depression, OSA, fatty liver disease, osteoporosis, hip fracture, cognitive impairment/dementia, low testosterone in men

Key Epidemiology

  • T2DM has a long asymptomatic hyperglycemic period — so both glucose-related AND insulin resistance–related complications may already be present at diagnosis
  • Diagnosis of T2DM at younger age dramatically increases complications: estimated 3–4 years of reduced life expectancy per decade of earlier diagnosis
  • ASCVD risk may develop before hyperglycemia is established (driven by insulin resistance and dyslipidemia)
  • Microvascular complications usually do not appear until the second decade of hyperglycemia
02

Glycemic Control & Key Clinical Trials

Principle
Chronic hyperglycemia is the primary driver of microvascular complications. For macrovascular disease, dyslipidemia, hypertension, and insulin resistance are additional key drivers. Improved glycemic control reduces risk of both, though blood pressure control may have an even greater impact.

DCCT — Diabetes Control and Complications Trial (T1DM)

Trial Design
>1,400 T1DM patients randomized to intensive vs conventional diabetes management. Mean follow-up 6.5 years. After DCCT ended, all participants offered intensive therapy and followed in the EDIC trial (>40 years combined follow-up).

HbA1c Achieved

  • Intensive group: 7.3%
  • Conventional group: 9.1%

Complication Reductions (Intensive Group)

  • Retinopathy (nonproliferative + proliferative): 47% ↓
  • Albuminuria: 39% ↓
  • Clinical nephropathy: 54% ↓
  • Neuropathy: 60% ↓

Costs of Intensive Therapy

  • Weight gain: +4.6 kg
  • Severe hypoglycemia (requiring another person to treat) was more common

Predicted Benefits from DCCT Data

  • 7.7 additional years of vision
  • 5.8 additional years free from ESRD
  • 5.6 years free from lower extremity amputation
  • >15.3 more years without significant microvascular complications
  • Additional 5.1 years of life expectancy
Metabolic Memory / Legacy Effect — EDIC Follow-up
The 30-year EDIC follow-up showed continued reduction in retinopathy, nephropathy, and CVD even after glycemic control equalized between groups. The intensive therapy group had a 57% reduction in cardiovascular events (nonfatal MI, stroke, or cardiovascular death) and a 33% reduction in mortality rate. This sustained benefit from a period of improved control is termed "metabolic memory." Estimated legacy effect: 10+ years. Other complications reduced in EDIC: autonomic neuropathy, bladder/sexual dysfunction, cardiac autonomic neuropathy, cheiroarthropathy, hearing loss.

UKPDS — UK Prospective Diabetes Study (T2DM)

Trial Design
>5,000 T2DM patients followed for >10 years. HbA1c: 7.0% (intensive) vs 7.9% (conventional).
  • Each 1% reduction in HbA1c → 35% reduction in microvascular complications
  • Continuous relationship between glycemic control and complications (same as DCCT)
  • Improved glycemic control also reduced cardiovascular event rate at >10 year follow-up
Major UKPDS Finding — BP Control > Glycemic Control
Strict blood pressure control (to 144/82 mmHg) reduced DM-related death, stroke, microvascular endpoints, retinopathy, and heart failure by 32–56%. The beneficial effects of blood pressure control were greater than those of glycemic control. ADA goal: <130/80 mmHg.

Genetic Susceptibility Note

Despite long-standing DM, some individuals never develop retinopathy or nephropathy — suggesting genetic susceptibility determines which complications occur. However, glycemic control exerts the strongest modifiable influence. Other trials (ACCORD, ADVANCE) also confirmed improved glycemic control reduces microvascular complications.

03

Mechanisms of Complications

Microvascular Mechanisms (Hyperglycemia-Driven)

  • Advanced Glycosylation End Products (AGEs) — e.g., pentosidine, glucosepane, carboxymethyllysine:
    • Bind specific cell surface receptors (RAGEs)
    • Nonenzymatic glycosylation of intra- and extracellular proteins → cross-linking
    • Leads to: glomerular dysfunction, endothelial dysfunction, altered extracellular matrix, accelerated atherosclerosis
  • Epigenetic changes: hyperglycemia alters gene expression in affected cells — this mechanism underlies metabolic memory
  • Reactive oxygen species / superoxide: increased mitochondrial production → activates multiple damaging pathways
  • VEGF-A (vascular endothelial growth factor A): increased locally in proliferative retinopathy; decreases after laser photocoagulation; target of anti-VEGF (intravitreous injection) therapy

Macrovascular Mechanisms

  • Traditional CV risk factors: dyslipidemia, hypertension
  • Insulin resistance: present years before T2DM diagnosis; associated with obesity and ectopic lipid accumulation in liver and muscle
  • Insulin fails to suppress lipolysis → ↑ fatty acid delivery to liver, muscle, endothelial cells, cardiac tissue → accumulation of triglycerides, diacylglycerol, ceramides
  • Inflammation — additional driver beyond hyperglycemia alone

Ophthalmologic Complications: Diabetic Retinopathy

Epidemiology
DM is the leading cause of new blindness in adults aged 20–74 in the United States. Glaucoma and cataracts occur earlier and more frequently in DM. Severe vision loss is primarily from progressive diabetic retinopathy causing significant macular edema and neovascularization.

Two Stages of Diabetic Retinopathy

Stage 1: Nonproliferative (NPDR)
  • Appears late in first decade or early second decade of hyperglycemia
  • Findings: retinal vascular microaneurysms, blot hemorrhages, cotton-wool spots
  • Mild NPDR can progress → venous caliber changes, intraretinal microvascular abnormalities, more hemorrhages
  • Pathophysiology: loss of retinal pericytes, ↑ retinal vascular permeability, abnormal retinal blood flow → retinal ischemia
Stage 2: Proliferative (PDR)
  • Hallmark: neovascularization in response to retinal hypoxemia
  • New vessels appear near optic nerve and/or macula
  • Rupture easily → vitreous hemorrhage → fibrosis → retinal detachment
  • Severe NPDR: high risk of progression to PDR within 5 years — creates opportunity for early detection and treatment
Clinically Significant Macular Edema
Can occur in either NPDR or PDR context. Associated with an increased chance of moderate visual loss over the next 3 years. Detected by fluorescein angiography or optical coherence tomography.

Risk Factors for Retinopathy

  • Duration of DM and degree of glycemic control — the best predictors
  • Hypertension, nephropathy, dyslipidemia also risk factors
  • Genetic susceptibility exists but confers less influence than duration or glycemic control

Screening Guidelines

  • T1DM: begin screening 5 years after diagnosis, then annually
  • T2DM: screen at the time of diagnosis, then annually
  • Requires dilated eye exam by optometrist/ophthalmologist OR retinal photography with remote reading — routine nondilated exam by PCP is inadequate
  • Subsequent management: by a retinal specialist
  • Pregnant patients with T1DM or T2DM: screen prior to and during pregnancy

Treatment of Diabetic Retinopathy

Best Treatment: Prevention
Intensive glycemic + blood pressure control delays development and slows progression of retinopathy in T1DM and T2DM. Once advanced retinopathy is present, improved glycemic control imparts less benefit. Appropriate ophthalmologic care can prevent most blindness.
  • Transient paradox: During the first 6–12 months of improved glycemic control, established retinopathy may transiently worsen. This is temporary; long-term improvement is associated with less retinopathy
  • GLP-1 receptor agonists: when associated with marked glycemic improvement → increased risk of worsening diabetic retinopathy. Consider this when choosing agents to improve glycemic control
  • Patients with retinopathy may need prophylactic laser photocoagulation before initiating intensive therapy, especially prior to pancreas or islet transplantation that can rapidly normalize glycemia
  • Fenofibrate: lowering elevated triglycerides may reduce progression of retinopathy

Interventional Treatments

  • Panretinal laser photocoagulation — severe NPDR or PDR
  • Anti-VEGF therapy (intravitreous injection) — PDR or macular edema; antiplatelet agents and anticoagulation can be continued during anti-VEGF injections
  • Surgical vitrectomy — severe PDR with vitreous hemorrhage and/or traction involving the macula
  • Aspirin does NOT influence the natural history of diabetic retinopathy

Renal Complications: Diabetic Nephropathy

Epidemiology
Diabetic nephropathy is the leading cause of CKD and Stage 5 CKD (ESRD) requiring renal replacement therapy. ~20–40% of DM patients develop diabetic nephropathy. More common in Black, Native American, and Hispanic individuals. CKD in DM → increased cardiovascular risk; prognosis on dialysis is poor.

Reversibility Pearl
10 years of normoglycemia from pancreas transplantation may lead to regression of mesangial glomerular lesions — but requires sustained normoglycemia for a decade.

Pathogenesis

  • Soluble factors: growth factors, angiotensin II, endothelin, AGEs
  • Epigenetic changes
  • Hemodynamic: glomerular hyperfiltration/hyperperfusion, ↑ glomerular capillary pressure
  • Structural: ↑ extracellular matrix, BM thickening, mesangial expansion, fibrosis
  • Tubular dysfunction: tubulointerstitial damage, fibrosis

Screening and Diagnosis

  • Assessment requires BOTH UACR and eGFR — some patients have declining GFR without albuminuria
  • Routine urinalysis does NOT detect low levels of albumin excretion
  • T1DM: screen 5 years after diagnosis, then annually
  • T2DM: screen at time of diagnosis, then annually

In T1DM, CKD and diabetic retinopathy almost always co-exist. If T1DM has CKD but NO retinopathy → investigate for alternative, non-diabetic causes of kidney disease.

Special Renal Considerations

  • Type IV Renal Tubular Acidosis (hyporeninemic hypoaldosteronism): may occur in T1 or T2DM → propensity to hyperkalemia and acidemia. Exacerbated by ACE inhibitors, ARBs, and mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists — monitor potassium closely
  • Radiocontrast-induced nephrotoxicity: risk factors = preexisting nephropathy + volume depletion. Hydrate well before and after; monitor creatinine 24–48h post-procedure; hold metformin until confirmed preserved renal function

Treatment of Diabetic Nephropathy — 5 Pillars

  • 1. Improved glycemic control — reduces rate of albuminuria appearance and progression; less effective once moderate albuminuria is established
  • 2. Strict blood pressure control — goal <130/80 mmHg; possibly lower if high ASCVD or CKD risk. First-line: ACE-i or ARB. Alternatives: thiazide-like diuretics, dihydropyridine CCBs. Beta blockers with caution if hypoglycemia unawareness. Do NOT combine ACE-i + ARB
  • 3. ACE inhibitor or ARB — for ALL patients with albuminuria (T1 or T2). Both classes considered equivalent. Increase to maximum tolerated dose. Acceptable creatinine rise: up to 30%. Goal for UACR >300 mg/g: reduce UACR by 30%
  • 4. SGLT-2 inhibitor (T2DM, eGFR >20): add while continuing ACE-i/ARB with any level of albuminuria → reduces CKD progression AND cardiovascular events. NOT effective if eGFR <20
  • 5. Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist — finerenone: reduces CKD progression and CV risk. Combining finerenone + SGLT2 inhibitor reduces risk of hyperkalemia

Additional Agents

  • GLP-1 agonist (semaglutide): improves kidney outcomes and reduces cardiovascular death in T2DM + CKD
  • Treat dyslipidemia aggressively (ASCVD is the leading cause of death in DM nephropathy)
  • Protein intake: ADA recommends 0.8 g/kg/day in diabetic kidney disease

Nephrology Referral Indications

  • eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73 m²
  • Albuminuria >300 mg/g creatinine
  • Atypical features: hematuria, rapidly declining renal function
Medication Adjustments with Declining GFR
  • Sulfonylureas + metformin: contraindicated in advanced renal insufficiency
  • Glinides, DPP-4 inhibitors: require dose adjustment
  • SGLT2 inhibitors: not effective if eGFR <20
  • Late nephropathy: insulin requirements ↓ (kidney is a site of insulin degradation)
Dialysis and Transplantation
Hemodialysis in DM → more complications: hypotension (autonomic neuropathy or loss of reflex tachycardia), difficult vascular access, accelerated retinopathy progression, greater mortality. Preemptive kidney transplantation from a living donor preferred for those nearing ESRD. For T1DM or insulin-deficient T2DM: simultaneous pancreas-kidney (SPK) transplantation from a deceased donor may be an option.

Neuropathy and Diabetes Mellitus

Prevalence and Key Rule
Diabetic neuropathy occurs in ~50% of individuals with long-standing T1DM and T2DM. Both myelinated and unmyelinated nerve fibers are lost. Because features are similar to other neuropathies, diagnose only after other etiologies are excluded.

Risk Factors for Diabetic Neuropathy

  • Duration of diabetes and degree of glycemic control
  • Greater BMI → greater neuropathy risk
  • Smoking
  • Presence of ASCVD, elevated triglycerides, hypertension

1. Distal Symmetric Polyneuropathy (DSPN) — Most Common Form

  • Most frequent presentation: distal sensory loss and pain
  • Up to 50% of patients have NO symptoms of neuropathy!
  • Symptoms: numbness, tingling, sharpness, or burning beginning in the feet, spreading proximally
  • Hyperesthesia, paresthesia, and dysesthesia also occur
  • Pain: usually present at rest, worsens at night
  • Acute painful form (<12 months): sometimes treatment-related (occurs with improved glycemic control)
  • Chronic form: pain eventually subsides but sensory deficit persists; motor defects may develop
Physical Exam Findings
Sensory loss (10-g monofilament and/or vibration) · Loss of ankle deep-tendon reflexes · Abnormal position sense · Muscular atrophy or foot drop
Loss of Protective Sensation (LOPS)
LOPS and DSPN are major risk factors for foot ulceration and falls (small and large nerve fiber dysfunction) and predispose to lower extremity amputation. Annual screening: T1DM = 5 years after diagnosis; T2DM = at diagnosis.

2. Autonomic Neuropathy

Affects parasympathetic (cholinergic) AND sympathetic (adrenergic) systems. Can involve cardiovascular, GI, genitourinary, sudomotor, and metabolic systems.

  • Cardiovascular autonomic neuropathy: decreased heart rate variability, resting tachycardia, orthostatic hypotension → associated with ↑ ASCVD. May prolong QTc interval → sudden death risk
  • Hypoglycemia unawareness: autonomic neuropathy reduces counterregulatory hormone release (especially epinephrine) → inability to sense hypoglycemia → ↑ risk of severe hypoglycemia
  • Gastroparesis and bladder-emptying abnormalities: caused by autonomic neuropathy in DM
  • Hyperhidrosis of upper extremities + anhidrosis of lower extremities (sympathetic dysfunction)
  • Anhidrosis of feet → dry skin with cracking → ↑ risk of foot ulceration

3. Mononeuropathy / Radiculopathy / Polyradiculopathy

  • Less common than polyneuropathy
  • Mononeuropathy: pain + motor weakness in single nerve distribution; can be at entrapment sites (carpal tunnel) or noncompressive
  • Most common cranial nerve: CN III → diplopia, ptosis, ophthalmoplegia with NORMAL pupillary constriction — distinguishes from posterior communicating artery aneurysm (which affects the pupil)
  • Other cranial nerves: IV, VI, VII (Bell's palsy)
  • Diabetic amyotrophy: lumbar plexus/femoral nerve → severe pain in thigh/hip + weakness in hip flexors/extensors
  • Diabetic polyradiculopathy: usually self-limited; resolves over 6–12 months

Treatment of Diabetic Neuropathy

Prevention is Critical
Improved glycemic control is essential for prevention. Treatment of established diabetic neuropathy is less than satisfactory; only symptomatic treatment is available.

General Measures

  • Lifestyle modifications (exercise, diet) — some efficacy
  • Treat dyslipidemia and hypertension in T2DM + hypertriglyceridemia
  • Avoid neurotoxins (including alcohol) and smoking
  • Check vitamin B12: metformin may reduce B12 absorption in T2DM; pernicious anemia more common in T1DM (anti–parietal cell autoantibodies) — may require sublingual or parenteral B12 replacement
  • Patient education: daily foot inspection, protective footwear, podiatrist if deformities present

  • T1DM + orthostatic hypotension: evaluate for primary adrenal insufficiency (Addison's disease) — associated with autoimmune polyendocrine syndrome

Gastrointestinal / Genitourinary Dysfunction

GI Manifestations

Gastroparesis

  • Symptoms: anorexia, nausea, vomiting, early satiety, abdominal bloating
  • Microvascular complications (retinopathy and neuropathy) usually present
  • Diagnosis: nuclear medicine scintigraphy after radiolabeled meal — may not correlate with symptoms
  • Pathophysiology: parasympathetic dysfunction from chronic hyperglycemia; hyperglycemia itself also directly impairs gastric emptying

Other GI Manifestations

  • Nocturnal diarrhea alternating with constipation — GI autonomic neuropathy
  • T1DM + GI symptoms → evaluate for celiac disease (anti-tissue transglutaminase autoantibodies; increased frequency in T1DM)

Treatment — GI

  • Small, frequent meals — liquid, low fat and fiber
  • Avoid medications that slow gastric emptying: opioids, GLP-1 receptor agonists
  • Metoclopramide: for severe symptoms; short-term only in both US and Europe (tardive dyskinesia risk with long-term use)
  • GERD symptoms: H2 blocker or PPI
  • Gastric electrical stimulatory devices available
  • Diabetic diarrhea without bacterial overgrowth: treat symptomatically

Genitourinary Manifestations

Diabetic Cystopathy

  • Progression: inability to sense full bladder → failure to void completely → ↑ postvoid residual → urinary hesitancy, decreased voiding frequency, incontinence, recurrent UTIs
  • Treatment: scheduled voiding or self-catheterization

Sexual Dysfunction

  • Female: reduced sexual desire, dyspareunia, reduced vaginal lubrication
  • Erectile dysfunction: very common in DM; may be one of the earliest signs of diabetic neuropathy; increases with age and diabetes duration; may occur WITHOUT other signs of autonomic neuropathy
  • Retrograde ejaculation also very common
  • Treatment: phosphodiesterase type 5 inhibitors — effective but slightly less efficacious in DM than in nondiabetic population

Cardiovascular Morbidity and Mortality

Major Cause of Mortality in DM
ASCVD (PAD, CHD, heart failure, cerebrovascular disease) occurs more frequently in T1 and T2DM and is the major cause of mortality for individuals with diabetes. ASCVD events are 2–4× greater in T2DM. CHD more likely to involve multiple vessels in DM. American Heart Association considers DM a controllable risk factor for CVD.

Silent Ischemia and Screening

  • Absence of chest pain ("silent ischemia") is common in DM — thorough cardiac evaluation before major surgical procedures
  • Seek evidence of ASCVD in symptomatic patients (even if atypical symptoms)
  • Screening asymptomatic DM patients for CHD is NOT recommended or cost-effective

Heart Failure in DM

  • Twice as common in individuals with DM (T1 or T2)
  • Related to diabetes duration and hypertension
  • Can present as HFpEF, HFmEF, or HFrEF
  • Diabetic cardiomyopathy: reduced left ventricular function without CHD or hypertension (pathogenesis unclear)

Treatment — Cardiovascular Disease

Four Integrated Targets
Glycemia · Blood Pressure · Lipids · CV/Kidney outcome–beneficial therapies — these must be addressed concurrently.

Revascularization for CHD in DM

  • PCI: initial success similar to nondiabetics, but higher rates of restenosis and lower long-term patency and survival
  • CABG + optimal medical management likely has better outcomes than PCI for DM with multivessel disease
  • Very strict glucose control has limited benefit on CV outcomes in established CVD — other factors (insulin resistance, dyslipidemia, inflammation) are more important

T2DM + ASCVD — Medication Strategy

  • Comprehensive effort (lifestyle, BP, lipids) PLUS either SGLT-2 inhibitor or GLP-1 receptor agonist
  • If diabetic CKD or heart failure is present/likely: prefer SGLT-2 inhibitor
  • T2DM + ASCVD or ASCVD risk factors: GLP-1 receptor agonist reduces cardiovascular events
  • Combination SGLT-2 inhibitor + GLP-1 agonist: likely provides additive risk reduction
  • T2DM + CKD + albuminuria on max ACE-i/ARB: add either SGLT2 inhibitor OR finerenone → reduces CKD progression + improves CV outcomes
  • HF or CVD: involve cardiovascular specialist; treat with ACE-i/ARB + beta blocker
SGLT-2 Inhibitors — Euglycemic DKA Risk
Counsel patients about risk and symptoms of DKA. Educate about measuring ketones if clinical scenario suggests this. Caution with SGLT-2 in T1DM and insulin-deficient T2DM.

Antiplatelet Therapy

  • Aspirin 75–162 mg/day: secondary prevention in DM + established ASCVD
  • Clopidogrel: aspirin allergy/intolerance
  • Primary prevention: consider aspirin if DM + age >50 + ≥1 additional risk factor (HTN, dyslipidemia, smoking, family Hx, albuminuria)
  • Aspirin NOT recommended for primary prevention in low CV risk (<50 years old, no risk factors)

Dyslipidemia Management in DM

Most common pattern: hypertriglyceridemia + reduced HDL. LDL is not increased, but small dense LDL particles in T2DM are more atherogenic (more easily glycated and susceptible to oxidation).

Lower Extremity Complications

Epidemiology
DM is the leading cause of nontraumatic lower extremity amputation in the United States. Foot ulcers and infections are also a major source of morbidity. 14–24% risk of amputation with each foot ulcer or subsequent ulceration.

Pathogenesis — Multifactorial

Neuropathy Contributions
  • Sensory: impaired protective mechanisms → major/repeated trauma without awareness
  • Proprioception: abnormal weight bearing → callus/ulceration
  • Motor: structural changes — hammer toe, claw toe, prominent metatarsal heads, Charcot joint
  • Autonomic: anhidrosis → dry skin → fissure formation
Vascular and Healing
  • PAD: impedes wound healing
  • Poor wound healing: allows minor breaks to enlarge and become infected
  • Altered superficial blood flow in foot (autonomic neuropathy)

Risk Factors for Foot Ulcer / Amputation

  • Male sex, DM >10 years, peripheral neuropathy
  • Abnormal foot structure (bony abnormalities, callus, thickened nails), PAD, smoking
  • History of previous ulcer or amputation
  • Visual impairment, poor glycemic control, diabetic nephropathy (especially dialysis)
  • Large calluses — often precursors to or overlie ulcerations

Most common site of ulceration: plantar surface (great toe and metatarsophalangeal areas)

Prevention

  • Annual foot examination for all DM patients (routine, comprehensive)
  • 10-g monofilament or vibration test → if abnormal → diagnose LOPS
  • Screen for asymptomatic PAD in patients >50 with DM + other risk factors using ankle-brachial index (ABI)
  • Podiatrist involvement for high-risk individuals (history of ulcers/amputation, dialysis patients, PAD, foot deformities)

Patient Education — Foot Care 5 Points

  • Careful selection of footwear
  • Daily inspection of feet (early signs of trauma or poor fit)
  • Daily foot hygiene (keep skin clean and moist)
  • Avoid self-treatment of foot abnormalities and high-risk behavior (e.g., walking barefoot)
  • Prompt consultation with healthcare provider if any abnormality arises

Treatment of Foot Ulcers — Multidisciplinary

Management requires expertise across orthopedics, vascular surgery, endocrinology, podiatry, and infectious diseases.

5 Interventions with Demonstrated Efficacy

  • 1. Off-loading — complete avoidance of weight bearing on the ulcer; removes mechanical trauma that retards wound healing
  • 2. Surgical debridement of nonviable tissue
  • 3. Physiologic topical wound dressings
  • 4. Revascularization — peripheral arterial bypass often effective in promoting wound healing and decreasing need for amputation
  • 5. Treatment of infections with appropriate antibiotics

If wound fails to improve after 4 weeks → consider advanced wound therapy: topical growth factors, acellular matrix tissues, bioengineered cellular therapies, negative-pressure wound therapy, electrical stimulation, pulsed radiofrequency, extracorporeal shockwave, hyperbaric oxygen therapy, topical oxygen therapy. Antiseptic agents: avoid. Topical antibiotics: limited value.

Infection Management

Infected Ulcer = Clinical Diagnosis
Superficial culture will find multiple organisms of unknown significance. Obtain cultures from debrided ulcer base, purulent drainage, or aspiration. Probe to bone — if probes to bone → highly likely osteomyelitis.
  • Organisms: aerobic gram-positive cocci (staph including MRSA, strep A/B) most common; gram-negative aerobes and anaerobes as co-pathogens
  • Gas gangrene can develop without clostridial infection
  • MRI: most specific modality for osteomyelitis. Nuclear medicine scans (PET, CT/SPECT) also used
  • Osteomyelitis: prolonged antibiotics + debridement of infected bone

Infections and Dermatologic Manifestations

Infections in DM

DM patients have greater frequency AND severity of infection. Mechanisms: incompletely defined abnormalities in cell-mediated immunity and phagocyte function from hyperglycemia + diminished vascularization. Hyperglycemia directly aids colonization and growth of organisms (Candida and other fungi). Poor glycemic control is the common denominator.

DM-Specific Rare Infections (seen almost exclusively in DM)

  • Rhinocerebral mucormycosis
  • Emphysematous infections of gallbladder and urinary tract
  • Malignant/invasive otitis externa: Pseudomonas aeruginosa in soft tissue surrounding external auditory canal; begins with pain and discharge; may rapidly progress to osteomyelitis and meningitis. Consider especially in severe hyperglycemia

Common Infections (More Frequent/Severe in DM)

  • Pulmonary: similar organisms to nondiabetics but gram-negative, S. aureus, and M. tuberculosis more frequent. Vaccinate against: pneumococcus, RSV, influenza (annually), SARS-CoV-2
  • UTI: E. coli most common; yeast (C. albicans, C. glabrata) also. Complications: emphysematous pyelonephritis, emphysematous cystitis. Bacteriuria in diabetic cystopathy does NOT require antibiotics except in pregnancy or before urologic procedures
  • Furunculosis, superficial candidal infections, vulvovaginitis: increased susceptibility
  • S. aureus: ↑ colonization in skinfolds and nares
  • Postoperative wound infections: ↑ risk; mitigated by perioperative insulin protocols for glycemic control

Exam Traps — Board Coach Format

Trap 01Retinopathy Transiently Worsening After Tight Control
A patient with T2DM starts intensive insulin therapy with tight glycemic control. At 3-month follow-up, ophthalmology notes worsening retinopathy. Should you stop the intensive regimen?
NO. Transient worsening of established diabetic retinopathy during the first 6–12 months of improved glycemic control is well known. It is temporary. In the long term, improved glycemic control is associated with less retinopathy. Continue intensive therapy.
Trap 02GLP-1 Agonist and Diabetic Retinopathy Worsening
A T2DM patient starts semaglutide and achieves marked HbA1c improvement. Ophthalmologic follow-up shows worsening retinopathy. Is this expected?
Yes — GLP-1 receptor agonists have been associated with increased risk of worsening diabetic retinopathy when marked glycemic improvement occurs. This should be factored into agent selection in patients with pre-existing retinopathy. The mechanism relates to rapid glucose lowering.
Trap 03CKD Without Retinopathy in T1DM
A T1DM patient has CKD with proteinuria but no diabetic retinopathy on dilated ophthalmologic exam. How do you interpret this?
Investigate for alternative (non-diabetic) causes of kidney disease. In T1DM, diabetic nephropathy and retinopathy almost always co-exist. CKD without retinopathy in T1DM strongly suggests a different etiology (e.g., IgA nephropathy, lupus nephritis) and warrants workup.
Trap 04Diabetic CN III Palsy — Pupil Sparing is the Key
A 65-year-old diabetic presents with diplopia, ptosis, and ophthalmoplegia. The pupil reacts normally to light. What is the most likely cause?
Diabetic mononeuropathy (CN III palsy) — pupil-sparing. Diabetic (ischemic) CN III palsy characteristically spares the pupil. Pupillary fibers run on the outside of CN III and are typically affected in compressive lesions (like a PCOM aneurysm). In ischemic/diabetic CN III palsy, the pupil is spared. Pupil-sparing CN III in a diabetic = microvascular disease until proven otherwise.
Trap 05Metabolic Memory / Legacy Effect
DCCT intensive therapy group had better control for 6.5 years, then both groups maintained the same HbA1c. Years later, the intensive group still has fewer complications and lower mortality. How do you explain this?
Metabolic memory (legacy effect). A period of improved glycemic control has durable protective effects on complications even after glycemic control equalizes. The intensive therapy group had a 57% reduction in cardiovascular events and 33% reduction in mortality in the EDIC follow-up despite equivalent subsequent HbA1c. Estimated legacy effect: 10+ years.
Trap 06Blood Pressure Control > Glycemic Control (UKPDS)
In the UKPDS, which intervention had a greater impact on DM complications: intensive glycemic control or blood pressure control?
Blood pressure control. Strict BP control (to 144/82 mmHg) reduced DM-related death, stroke, microvascular endpoints, retinopathy, and heart failure by 32–56%. These effects were greater than those of glycemic control. ADA goal: <130/80 mmHg.
Trap 07SGLT2 Inhibitors: When They Fail
You want to add an SGLT2 inhibitor for nephroprotection in a T2DM patient with diabetic kidney disease. The patient's eGFR is 18 mL/min/1.73 m². Appropriate?
No. SGLT2 inhibitors are not effective when eGFR <20 mL/min/1.73 m². They require sufficient nephron mass to work. The indication for SGLT2 inhibitor addition in CKD requires eGFR >20. At eGFR <30, nephrology consultation is also indicated.
Trap 08One UACR Reading Is Not Enough
A diabetic patient's spot urine shows UACR of 45 mg/g on a single measurement. Should you diagnose microalbuminuria and start ACE inhibitor now?
Not yet. Confirm on 2–3 occasions over 3–6 months. UACR can be falsely elevated by strenuous exercise, infection, fever, CHF, marked hyperglycemia, hypertension, or prostate disease. Only a persistently elevated UACR >30 mg/g confirms albuminuria. Then initiate ACE-i/ARB and uptitrate to maximum tolerated dose.
Trap 09Malignant Otitis Externa in Severe Hyperglycemia
A severely hyperglycemic diabetic patient presents with severe ear pain and discharge that is rapidly progressing. What rare life-threatening diagnosis must you consider?
Invasive (malignant) otitis externa. Caused by Pseudomonas aeruginosa in soft tissue surrounding the external auditory canal. Begins with pain and discharge, may rapidly progress to osteomyelitis and meningitis. Seen almost exclusively in diabetic patients, associated with severe hyperglycemia. Needs urgent imaging and systemic antibiotics.
Trap 10Aspirin for Primary Prevention — Know the Age/Risk Threshold
A 42-year-old T2DM patient with no prior cardiovascular events and no other risk factors (no hypertension, no dyslipidemia, no smoking) asks about aspirin. Recommend?
Do NOT recommend aspirin for primary prevention. The ADA recommends considering aspirin for primary prevention only in DM + age >50 AND at least one additional risk factor (hypertension, dyslipidemia, smoking, family history, or albuminuria). This 42-year-old with no additional risk factors does not meet the threshold.
Trap 11Metoclopramide — Short-Term Only
A patient with diabetic gastroparesis wants to continue metoclopramide long-term for nausea management. What do you tell them?
Metoclopramide is restricted to SHORT-TERM treatment only in both the US and Europe, due to the risk of tardive dyskinesia with long-term use. Optimize glycemic control, use small low-fat/fiber meals, avoid GLP-1 agonists and opioids. Gastric electrical stimulatory devices are available for refractory cases.
Trap 12CABG vs PCI in Multivessel DM — CABG Wins
A T2DM patient has multivessel coronary artery disease. Both PCI and CABG are technically feasible. Which should you recommend?
CABG + optimal medical management has better outcomes than PCI in DM with multivessel disease. While initial PCI success rates are similar, DM patients have higher restenosis rates and lower long-term patency and survival after PCI. For multivessel disease in DM, CABG is generally preferred.
Trap 13Erectile Dysfunction — Earliest Sign of Neuropathy
A 50-year-old T2DM patient reports new-onset erectile dysfunction but denies any other symptoms. Could this be diabetes-related?
Yes. Erectile dysfunction may be one of the earliest signs of diabetic neuropathy and can occur in the absence of other signs of diabetic autonomic neuropathy. It increases in frequency with patient age and duration of diabetes. Treatment: PDE5 inhibitors are effective but slightly less so than in the nondiabetic population.
