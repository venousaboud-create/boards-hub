BP stage scenarios

Uncomplicated HTN — Stage 1, low risk

BP target: <130/80
◎ Acceptable
Thiazide / thiazide-likeChlorthalidone ★, HCTZ, Indapamide
ACEILisinopril, Enalapril, Ramipril
ARBLosartan ★gout, Valsartan, Olmesartan
DHP CCBAmlodipine ★, Nifedipine LA, Felodipine
💡Try nonpharmacologic therapy alone for 3–6 months first (DASH diet, sodium <2g/day, weight loss, aerobic exercise, limit alcohol). Add drug only if SBP/DBP ≥130/80 after lifestyle changes. If drug needed, any of the 4 standard first-line classes is acceptable. Reassess every 3–6 months.

Uncomplicated HTN — Stage 1, high risk

High risk
BP target: <130/80
✓ Preferred

ARBLosartan ★gout, Valsartan, Olmesartan

💡High risk = prior CVD event, or ASCVD 10-year risk ≥10%, or DM, or CKD. Start drug therapy immediately alongside lifestyle changes. Any one of: Chlorthalidone, ACEI/ARB, or DHP CCB is first-line. Single agent usually sufficient initially. Reassess in 1 month.

Cardiac scenarios

HTN + IHD / Stable angina

BP target: <130/80
✓ Preferred
ACEILisinopril, Enalapril, Ramipril
DHP CCBAmlodipine ★, Nifedipine LA, Felodipine
Beta-blockerMetoprolol, Carvedilol, Nebivolol
◎ Acceptable
Thiazide / thiazide-likeChlorthalidone ★, HCTZ, Indapamide
ARBLosartan ★gout, Valsartan, Olmesartan
Non-DHP CCBDiltiazem ER, Verapamil ER
💡Beta-blocker is first-line for angina (reduces demand) and improves survival in IHD. ACEI reduces cardiac remodeling — add to all IHD patients. DHP CCB (amlodipine) is excellent for angina relief and BP. Non-DHP CCB (diltiazem/verapamil) may be used for angina if NO HFrEF — never combine with beta-blocker (severe bradycardia + AV block risk).

BP target: <130/80
✓ Preferred
Non-DHP CCBDiltiazem ER, Verapamil ER
Beta-blockerMetoprolol, Carvedilol, Nebivolol
💡Rate control goal: HR <110 at rest (lenient) or <80 at rest (strict, symptomatic patients). First-line rate control: Beta-blocker OR Non-DHP CCB. Choose based on comorbidities — see combination cards below. If NO comorbidities, either is appropriate.

Renal scenarios

HTN + CKD (eGFR ≥30, no proteinuria)

BP target: <130/80
✓ Preferred
ACEILisinopril, Enalapril, Ramipril
ARBLosartan ★gout, Valsartan, Olmesartan
◎ Acceptable
Thiazide / thiazide-likeChlorthalidone ★, HCTZ, Indapamide
DHP CCBAmlodipine ★, Nifedipine LA, Felodipine
SGLT2 inhibitorEmpagliflozin, Dapagliflozin
⚠ Caution
MRASpironolactone, Eplerenone, Finerenone
💡ACEI or ARB preferred (dilate efferent arteriole → reduce intraglomerular pressure → slow CKD progression). Do NOT combine ACEI + ARB: hyperkalemia + AKI. MRA: use with caution (hyperkalemia risk). CCBs are safe and well-tolerated in CKD. Thiazides effective when eGFR ≥30.

BP target: <130/80

SGLT2 inhibitorEmpagliflozin, Dapagliflozin

MRASpironolactone, Eplerenone, Finerenone
💡ACEI or ARB is ESSENTIAL — reduces proteinuria AND slows CKD progression (↓ intraglomerular pressure + ↓ TGF-β). Use ONE, not both. Monitor K+ and creatinine at 1–2 weeks after starting. SGLT2 inhibitors: proven additional nephroprotection (CREDENCE, DAPA-CKD, EMPA-KIDNEY trials). A small rise in creatinine ≤25–30% on starting ACEI/ARB is expected and acceptable.

Exam traps — antihypertensive drug selection

Trap 01A-fib rate control in a patient with HFrEF
Use BETA-BLOCKER, NOT non-DHP CCB. Non-DHP CCBs (diltiazem, verapamil) are absolutely contraindicated in HFrEF. The HFrEF CI overrides the A-fib rate-control indication for non-DHP CCB.
Trap 02A-fib rate control in a patient with asthma
Use NON-DHP CCB (diltiazem/verapamil), NOT beta-blocker. Beta-blockers are absolutely contraindicated in asthma (bronchospasm). Only safe if NO HFrEF is also present.
Trap 03"Give a diuretic to a patient with primary aldosteronism bilateral"
WRONG. Bilateral hyperplasia → give MRA (spironolactone) as definitive medical treatment. Surgery is NOT indicated (would only remove one gland).
Trap 04"Surgery is too risky for this adrenal adenoma patient, just give MRA"
MRA is the correct bridge and an option if surgery is declined or high-risk, but surgery (adrenalectomy) is the goal and can CURE hypertension. Always recommend surgery first for unilateral adenoma.
Trap 05"Give ACEI + ARB for maximum renal protection in CKD"
NEVER combine ACEI + ARB. This doubles the hyperkalemia and AKI risk without additional benefit. Use ONE class: ACEI or ARB (not both).
Trap 06"Stage 1 HTN — start antihypertensives immediately"
Depends on risk. Stage 1, LOW RISK (ASCVD <10%, no CVD/DM/CKD): try nonpharmacologic therapy alone for 3–6 months first. Stage 1, HIGH RISK: start drugs immediately.
Trap 07"Thiazides don't work in advanced CKD (creatinine >3)"
This was the old teaching. NEW: CLICK trial (2021) showed chlorthalidone reduced ambulatory SBP by 10.5 mmHg even in stage 4 CKD (eGFR <23). Chlorthalidone is now evidence-based in advanced CKD.
Trap 08"CPAP will control my patient's hypertension — no antihypertensives needed"
WRONG. CPAP provides only ~1–2 mmHg BP reduction per meta-analysis. Standard antihypertensive drug therapy is REQUIRED in patients with OSA and HTN.
Trap 09Pregnant patient with HTN — what drugs to avoid?
ACEI, ARB, and SGLT2 inhibitors are absolutely contraindicated (teratogenic). Safe agents: Labetalol, Nifedipine (DHP CCB), Methyldopa, Hydralazine (IV). BP target in pregnancy: <140/90 (not <130/80).
Trap 104th drug for resistant hypertension
The 4th drug is MRA (spironolactone) — this is the most effective add-on agent in resistant HTN (PATHWAY-2 trial). Also ensure the patient is on chlorthalidone (long-acting diuretic), not HCTZ.
Trap 11"Losartan and lisinopril are interchangeable in all scenarios"
Not exactly. Losartan has UNIQUE uricosuric properties (blocks URAT1 transporter) — it lowers serum uric acid. In patients with HTN + gout, Losartan is the preferred ARB.
Trap 12"DHP CCB is contraindicated in HFrEF — use non-DHP CCB instead"
WRONG. BOTH DHP CCB AND Non-DHP CCB are contraindicated in HFrEF. Non-DHP CCBs have stronger negative inotropic effects and are especially dangerous. Do not substitute one for the other in HFrEF.
Trap 13Black adult with Stage 2 HTN — start with ACEI alone
ACEI/ARB are less effective as monotherapy in Black adults (low-renin physiology). In Black adults, thiazide (chlorthalidone) + DHP CCB are especially effective. Most need combination therapy. Add ACEI/ARB if DM or CKD present.
Trap 14Post-MI patient — should you give an MRA?
Give MRA IF EF <40% develops post-MI (i.e., post-MI HFrEF). Not routinely for all post-MI patients without HFrEF. The indication is specifically for post-MI with reduced EF.
Trap 15"Beta-blockers are safe in gout"
True — beta-blockers do NOT affect uric acid. The drugs to AVOID in gout are thiazide diuretics and loop diuretics (both raise uric acid by reducing renal excretion). ACEI, ARB (especially losartan), DHP CCBs, and beta-blockers are all safe in gout.
Trap 16Which antihypertensive has bone-protective properties?
Thiazide diuretics reduce urinary calcium excretion and increase osteoblast activity — bone-protective in women with osteoporosis. In contrast, loop diuretics increase calcium excretion (though observational studies haven't shown increased fracture risk).
Trap 17"DM patient needs HTN treatment — just give a diuretic"
While thiazides are first-line for uncomplicated HTN, in DM the PREFERRED agents are ACEI or ARB (especially with microalbuminuria/proteinuria) + SGLT2 inhibitor (additional CV/renal benefit). Thiazides are acceptable as add-on but should not be the primary choice.
Trap 18"Doxazosin (alpha-1 blocker) is ideal for elderly patients with prostate issues"
Doxazosin has dual benefit for BPH + HTN but is less cardioprotective than diuretics. ALLHAT trial stopped the doxazosin arm early due to higher heart failure rates vs chlorthalidone. Always combine with standard agents for CV protection.
