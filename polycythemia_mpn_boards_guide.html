Myeloproliferative Neoplasms — Overview

WHO Classification (8 Disorders)

Primary Myeloid Phenotype
  • Chronic myeloid leukemia (CML) — BCR-ABL positive
  • Chronic neutrophilic leukemia (CNL)
  • Chronic eosinophilic leukemia (CEL), NOS
Erythroid/Megakaryocytic Predominance
  • Polycythemia vera (PV)
  • Primary myelofibrosis (PMF)
  • Essential thrombocytosis (ET)
  • Mastocytosis
  • MPN, unclassifiable
🔑 Critical Distinction
PV, PMF, and ET can transform into each other. CML, CNL, and CEL cannot. This chapter focuses on PV, PMF, and ET because their clinical features and driver mutation overlap are substantial.

Shared Features of All MPNs

  • Origin in a hematopoietic stem cell
  • Overproduction of one or more formed blood elements without significant dysplasia
  • Predilection to extramedullary hematopoiesis, myelofibrosis, and transformation to acute leukemia at varying rates

Genetic Basis of Each MPN

JAK2 — The Master Kinase

JAK2 is an evolutionarily well-conserved, nonreceptor tyrosine kinase. It serves as the cognate tyrosine kinase for the erythropoietin (EPO) and thrombopoietin (TPO) receptors, and is also utilized by the granulocyte colony-stimulating factor (G-CSF) receptor.

  • Functions as an obligate chaperone for EPO and TPO receptors in the Golgi apparatus — responsible for their cell-surface expression
  • Ligand binding → conformational change → JAK2 autophosphorylation → receptor phosphorylation → proteins involved in cell proliferation, differentiation, and resistance to apoptosis
  • Transgenic animals lacking JAK2 die as embryos from severe anemia
⚠️ JAK2 V617F Mutation
Mutation in the autoinhibitory pseudokinase domain of JAK2 — replaces valine with phenylalanine (V617F) → constitutive kinase activation. This is found in >95% of PV, ~55% PMF, ~50% ET. However, it is probably NOT the initiating lesion in any of the MPNs.

Polycythemia Vera — Etiology & Pathophysiology

Definition & Epidemiology

PV is a clonal hematopoietic stem cell disorder in which phenotypically normal red cells, granulocytes, and platelets accumulate in the absence of a recognizable physiologic stimulus.

Incidence

2.5 per 100,000 persons; spares no adult age group; increases with age to rates >10/100,000

Demographics

Familial transmission infrequent; women under age 50 predominate among sporadic cases

Rank

Most common of the myeloproliferative neoplasms

Genetics & Molecular Pathogenesis

  • Nonrandom chromosome abnormalities (del20q, del13q, trisomy 9) in up to 30% of untreated PV — but NO consistent cytogenetic abnormality (unlike CML)
  • JAK2 V617F: >95% of PV patients express this mutation
  • The JAK2 gene is located on chromosome 9p; loss of heterozygosity on 9p (via mitotic recombination → uniparental disomy) leads to homozygosity for JAK2 V617F in ~60% of PV
  • Most PV without JAK2 V617F express a JAK2 exon 12 mutation — not clinically different, except higher frequency of isolated erythrocytosis
  • Predisposition to acquire JAK2 mutations is associated with a specific JAK2 haplotype: GGCC
🔬 Why JAK2 V617F Is Not the Whole Story
  • PV patients with same phenotype and documented clonal disease can have LNK mutations (a JAK2 inhibitor) or rarely CALR mutations
  • ET and PMF patients have the same JAK2 mutations but different clinical phenotypes
  • Familial PV can occur without the mutation, even when other family members express it
  • In some JAK2 V617F–positive PV/ET patients, acute leukemia occurs in a JAK2 V617F–negative progenitor cell → evidence of an ancestral precursor cell

Consequences of Constitutive JAK2 Activation

  • EPO hypersensitivity — cells respond to much lower EPO levels than normal
  • EPO-independent erythroid colony formation — colonies form even without EPO
  • Rapid terminal differentiation
  • Increased Bcl-XL expression → apoptosis resistance even in the absence of EPO
  • Increased blood cell production and inflammatory cytokine production

Polycythemia Vera — Clinical Features

Presenting Manifestations

Although PV is a panmyelopathy (all lineages involved), isolated thrombocytosis, leukocytosis, or splenomegaly can be the presenting manifestation. Most often, the disorder is first recognized by incidental discovery of a high hemoglobin, hematocrit, or red cell count.

🔑 Pathognomonic Symptoms
With the exception of aquagenic pruritus or erythromelalgia, no symptoms distinguish PV from other causes of erythrocytosis. These two symptoms, when present, are strong clues to PV.

Symptoms from Hyperviscosity (Erythrocytosis)

  • Neurologic symptoms: vertigo, tinnitus, headache, visual disturbances, transient ischemic attacks (TIAs)
  • Systolic hypertension — from red cell mass elevation

Thrombotic Manifestations

Venous or arterial thrombosis may be the presenting manifestation of PV. Any vessel can be affected, but cerebral, cardiac, and mesenteric vessels are most commonly involved.

  • Hepatic venous thrombosis (Budd-Chiari syndrome): particularly common in young women; may be catastrophic if sudden and complete hepatic vein obstruction occurs
  • Portal vein thrombosis: more common in male PV patients
🚨 Board Pearl — Budd-Chiari
PV should be suspected in any woman who develops hepatic vein thrombosis. This is the only type of thrombosis specifically associated with JAK2 V617F expression.

Hemorrhagic Manifestations (From Extreme Thrombocytosis)

When platelet count is >900,000/mL, absorption and proteolysis of high-molecular-weight von Willebrand multimers by the large platelet mass causes acquired von Willebrand's disease.

  • Digital ischemia, easy bruising, epistaxis
  • Acid-peptic disease, gastrointestinal hemorrhage

Erythromelalgia

A syndrome of erythema, burning, and pain in the extremities — primarily lower extremities. It is a complication of thrombocytosis due to increased platelet stickiness. Some CNS symptoms in PV (e.g., ocular migraine) appear to represent a variant of erythromelalgia.

✅ Treatment
Usually responsive to salicylates (platelet cyclooxygenase-1 inhibitors). Platelet number reduction can help if salicylates are not effective.

Metabolic Manifestations

  • Hyperuricemia with secondary gout, uric acid stones — due to large turnover of hematopoietic cells
  • Constitutional symptoms — from inflammatory cytokine production
  • Peptic ulcer disease — also related to H. pylori (increased incidence in PV)
  • Pruritus — thought to be a consequence of mast cell activation by JAK2 V617F

Polycythemia Vera — Diagnosis

When Diagnosis Is Apparent vs Complex

  • Apparent: erythrocytosis + leukocytosis + thrombocytosis + splenomegaly (any combination)
  • Complex: elevated Hgb/Hct/RBC alone → requires ruling out many causes
⚠️ Critical Diagnostic Pitfall
Unless the hemoglobin level is ≥20 g/dL (hematocrit ≥60%), it is not possible to distinguish true erythrocytosis from disorders causing plasma volume contraction. This is because — uniquely in PV — there is expansion of the plasma volume, which can mask the elevated red cell mass (especially in women). Therefore, red cell mass AND plasma volume determinations are necessary to establish absolute erythrocytosis vs relative erythrocytosis (Gaisböck's syndrome).

Note: Isolated erythrocytosis is uncommon as an initial PV manifestation. Not all low JAK2 V617F allele burden (VAF ≤5%) indicate actual blood disease.

Role of Erythropoietin Level

  • A normal serum EPO level does NOT exclude PV
  • An elevated EPO level is most consistent with a secondary cause of erythrocytosis

Other Helpful Laboratory Tests

  • RBC count, MCV, RDW — particularly when Hct/Hgb <60%/20 g/dL
  • Only 3 causes of microcytic erythrocytosis: β-thalassemia trait, hypoxic erythrocytosis, and PV
    • β-thal trait: RDW usually normal
    • Hypoxic erythrocytosis or PV: RDW may be elevated (due to associated iron deficiency)
  • Today: quantitative JAK2 V617F or JAK2 exon 12 mutation assay by next-generation sequencing has superseded other surrogate tests

What Bone Marrow Does (and Doesn't) Tell You

⚠️ Boards Trap
Bone marrow aspirate and biopsy provide no specific diagnostic information — may be normal or indistinguishable from ET or PMF. Similarly, no specific cytogenetic abnormality is associated with PV, and absence of a cytogenetic marker does NOT exclude the diagnosis.

Polycythemia Vera — Complications

Mechanism of Complications

Complications relate directly to increased blood viscosity from red cell mass elevation and indirectly to increased turnover of red cells, leukocytes, and platelets → increased uric acid and inflammatory cytokine production.

Myelofibrosis as a Complication of PV

  • Myelofibrosis in PV is a reactive, reversible process that does not itself impede hematopoiesis and has no prognostic significance per se in PV
  • In ~15% of patients, myelofibrosis is associated with hematopoietic stem cell failure → substantial extramedullary hematopoiesis in liver and spleen + transfusion-dependent anemia
  • Organomegaly → mechanical discomfort, portal hypertension, progressive cachexia

Acute Leukemia Transformation

  • Incidence of AML is increased in PV — but incidence in patients NOT exposed to chemotherapy or radiation is very low
  • Interestingly, chemotherapy (including hydroxyurea) has been associated with AML in JAK2 V617F–negative HSCs in some PV patients
  • Hydroxyurea antagonizes TP53 and causes del17p → TP53 haploinsufficiency → chronic use should be constrained
  • Acquisition of TP53 mutations (detected by NGS) is usually associated with leukemic transformation

Thrombosis and Splenomegaly

  • Left uncontrolled, erythrocytosis can lead to thrombosis of vital organs: liver, heart, brain, lungs
  • Patients with massive splenomegaly: particularly prone to thrombotic events because the associated increase in plasma volume masks the true extent of the red cell mass elevation
🚨 Critical Clinical Pearl
A "normal" hematocrit or hemoglobin level in a PV patient with massive splenomegaly should be considered indicative of an elevated red cell mass until proven otherwise.

Polycythemia Vera — Treatment

🔑 Core Principle
PV is generally an indolent disorder measured in decades. Management should reflect its tempo. Thrombosis due to erythrocytosis is the most significant complication and often the presenting manifestation.

Hematocrit Targets (MANDATORY)

Men

Hgb ≤ 14 g/dL (140 g/L)
Hematocrit < 45%

Women

Hgb ≤ 12 g/dL (120 g/L)
Hematocrit < 42%

These targets are mandatory to avoid thrombotic complications.

Phlebotomy — First-Line

  • Initially: reduces hyperviscosity by reducing red cell mass to normal while further expanding the plasma volume
  • Periodic phlebotomies: maintain red cell mass within normal range and induce a state of iron deficiency that prevents accelerated reexpansion of the red cell mass
  • Once iron-deficient state achieved: phlebotomy usually only required at 3-month intervals
  • Neither phlebotomy nor iron deficiency increases the platelet count relative to the effect of the disease itself
  • Neither thrombocytosis nor leukocytosis is correlated with thrombosis in PV, in contrast to the strong correlation between erythrocytosis and thrombosis
🚨 Chemotherapy for RCM Control — NEVER
Chemotherapy is NEVER indicated to control the red cell mass in PV. If venous access is an issue, ruxolitinib or pegylated IFN is the preferred therapy.

Drug Therapies — Mechanisms, Indications, Pearls

Ruxolitinib (JAK1/2 Inhibitor) ⭐ Key Drug

  • Mechanism: Nonspecific JAK1/2 inhibitor
  • Indications in PV:
    • Symptomatic splenomegaly (reduces spleen size)
    • Alleviates constitutional symptoms and pruritus (via cytokine inhibition)
    • Reduces phlebotomy requirement
    • Effective in PV without myelofibrosis who are intolerant or refractory to hydroxyurea (Phase 3 trial)
  • Targets involved HSCs in PV and is not mutagenic

Pegylated IFN-α ⭐ Key Drug

  • Targets involved HSCs in PV and is not mutagenic
  • Allows biweekly administration
  • Produced complete hematologic and molecular remissions in ~20% of PV patients
  • Preferred with ruxolitinib when venous access is an issue

Hydroxyurea — Use with Caution

  • Does NOT target the involved HSCs in PV and is mutagenic
  • Does NOT prevent thrombosis or myelofibrosis in PV
  • Itself leukemogenic
  • Antagonizes TP53 and causes del17p → TP53 haploinsufficiency
  • Should be considered as short-duration therapy only

Anagrelide (Phosphodiesterase Inhibitor)

  • Reduces platelet count
  • Preferable to hydroxyurea: lacks marrow toxicity; also protective against venous thrombosis (hydroxyurea is not)
  • Chronic use: cardiotoxic and nephrotoxic, particularly in older PV patients

Other JAK2 Inhibitors (After Ruxolitinib Failure)

  • Three agents approved for PV with myelofibrosis after ruxolitinib failure/intolerance:
    • Fedratinib
    • Pacritinib
    • Momelotinib

Agents to AVOID

🚨 Leukemogenic — Avoid
Alkylating agents and radioactive sodium phosphate (³²P) are leukemogenic in PV. Their use should be avoided.

Primary Myelofibrosis — Etiology & Clinical Features

Definition & Epidemiology

Chronic PMF (also called idiopathic myelofibrosis, agnogenic myeloid metaplasia, myelofibrosis with myeloid metaplasia) is a clonal HSC disorder associated with mutations in JAK2, MPL, or CALR, characterized by marrow fibrosis, extramedullary hematopoiesis, variable suppression of hematopoiesis, and splenomegaly.

  • Least common MPN
  • Primarily afflicts men in their sixth decade or later (contrast: acute/malignant myelofibrosis can occur at any age)
  • Diagnosis of PMF is one of exclusion

Molecular Biology of Fibrosis

  • Fibrosis associated with overproduction of TGF-β and tissue inhibitors of metalloproteinases and thrombopoietin
  • Osteosclerosis: overproduction of osteoprotegerin (osteoclast inhibitor)
  • Marrow angiogenesis: increased production of VEGF
  • Key point: Fibroblasts in PMF are polyclonal and NOT part of the neoplastic clone — but can be induced by the clone to produce inflammatory cytokines
  • The degree of myelofibrosis and the extent of extramedullary hematopoiesis are NOT related

Mutation Landscape

JAK2 V617F

~55% of PMF; often as homozygotes; usually older patients with higher hematocrits

CALR

~36%; exon 9 somatic mutations (type 1 = deletions, type 2 = insertions); binds and activates MPL at cell surface. Type 1 has survival advantage

MPL

~4%; thrombopoietin receptor mutations; more anemic + lower leukocyte counts than JAK2 V617F patients

🚨 Triple-Negative PMF
PMF patients lacking a known MPN driver mutation (triple-negative: no JAK2, MPL, or CALR) appear to have the worst prognosis.
  • Small number with low JAK2 V617F allele burden (≤25%): faster time to anemia/leukopenia and shortened survival

Clinical Features

  • No specific signs or symptoms
  • Many asymptomatic at presentation — detected by splenic enlargement and/or abnormal blood counts on routine exam
  • Night sweats, fatigue, and weight loss: common presenting complaints (contrast to companion MPNs)

Peripheral Blood Smear — Characteristic Findings

  • Teardrop-shaped red cells (dacrocytes) — membrane damage from passage through fibrotic marrow/spleen
  • Nucleated red cells (normoblasts)
  • Myelocytes, promyelocytes
  • Myeloblasts may be present
  • This is a leukoerythroblastic smear — indicative of extramedullary hematopoiesis (not specific to PMF)

Other Findings

  • Anemia: usually mild initially; common
  • Leukocyte and platelet counts: normal or increased; either can be depressed
  • Mild hepatomegaly may accompany splenomegaly; isolated lymphadenopathy → suggest another diagnosis
  • Elevated serum LDH and alkaline phosphatase
  • Marrow inaspirable ("dry tap") due to myelofibrosis
  • Bone x-rays: osteosclerosis
  • Markedly increased circulating CD34+ cells (>15,000/μL) — due to impaired CXCL4 expression

Complications of Extramedullary Hematopoiesis

  • Ascites
  • Portal, pulmonary, or intracranial hypertension
  • Intestinal or ureteral obstruction
  • Pericardial tamponade
  • Spinal cord compression
  • Skin nodules
  • Splenic infarction with rapid spleen enlargement → fever + pleuritic chest pain
  • Hyperuricemia, secondary gout

Autoimmune Abnormalities in PMF

An intriguing feature of PMF is the occurrence of autoimmune abnormalities: immune complexes, antinuclear antibodies, rheumatoid factor, positive Coombs' test. Whether these represent a host reaction to the disorder or are involved in its pathogenesis is unknown.

Primary Myelofibrosis — Diagnosis & Prognosis

Key Diagnostic Points

  • All clinical features of PMF can be seen in PV or CML → PMF is a diagnosis of exclusion
  • Massive splenomegaly in PV commonly masks erythrocytosis → intraabdominal thrombosis in PMF most likely represents unrecognized PV
  • Bone marrow biopsy: usually reveals hypercellular marrow with trilineage hyperplasia + increased megakaryocytes in clusters with large, dysplastic nuclei
  • No specific bone marrow morphologic abnormalities distinguish PMF from other MPNs
  • Cytogenetic analysis: useful to exclude CML and for prognosis (complex karyotype → poor prognosis)

🔑 Additional Prognostic Factors
Mutations in ASXL1, EZH2, SRSF2, and IDH1/2 have been identified as risk factors for early death or leukemic transformation. These have proved more useful for PMF risk assessment than clinical scoring systems. Complex cytogenetic abnormalities also portend poor prognosis.

These prognostic scoring systems are NOT accurate for risk assessment in polycythemia vera or essential thrombocytosis patients who have developed myelofibrosis.

Complications and Natural History

  • Survival in PMF is much shorter than in PV and ET
  • Natural history: increasing marrow failure → transfusion-dependent anemia + increasing organomegaly
  • Can evolve from chronic to accelerated phase: constitutional symptoms + increasing marrow failure
  • ~10% spontaneous transformation to aggressive acute leukemia → usually refractory to treatment

Primary Myelofibrosis — Treatment

⚠️ Core Challenge
No specific therapy exists for PMF. Treatment is supportive and targeted at symptoms.

Anemia Management

Causes are multifactorial: ineffective erythropoiesis, hemodilution (splenomegaly), splenic sequestration, blood loss (thrombocytopenia/portal hypertension), folic acid deficiency, systemic inflammation, autoimmune hemolysis.

  • Recombinant EPO: not consistently effective; may worsen splenomegaly; ineffective if serum EPO >125 mU/L
  • Androgens (danazol): not consistently effective
  • Glucocorticoids: can ameliorate anemia + constitutional symptoms (fever, chills, night sweats, anorexia, weight loss)
  • Low-dose thalidomide + prednisone: effective for both anemia and thrombocytopenia

Splenomegaly — Most Distressing Problem

Splenomegaly causes abdominal pain, portal hypertension, easy satiety, and cachexia.

  • Splenectomy: associated with significant postoperative complications:
    • Mesenteric venous thrombosis
    • Hemorrhage
    • Rebound leukocytosis and thrombocytosis
    • Hepatic extramedullary hematopoiesis
    • No amelioration of anemia or thrombocytopenia when present
    • Also increases risk of blastic transformation
  • Splenic irradiation: at best temporarily palliative; significant risk of neutropenia, infection, subsequent operative hemorrhage if splenectomy attempted
  • Ruxolitinib: effective in reducing splenomegaly + alleviating constitutional symptoms in majority of advanced PMF; possibly prolongs survival; side effects: anemia + thrombocytopenia (dose-dependent)

Essential Thrombocytosis — Etiology, Diagnosis & Complications

Definition & Epidemiology

ET is a clonal hematopoietic stem cell disorder with mutations in JAK2 (V617F), MPL, or CALR, manifested clinically by overproduction of platelets without a definable cause.

Incidence

1–2 per 100,000; distinct female predominance

Mutations

JAK2 V617F ~50%, CALR ~30%, MPL ~8%; canonical mutations in 90%

Prognosis

ET lacking canonical driver mutation: usually benign prognosis. Survival = general population

Thrombopoietin Physiology (Key Concepts)

  • Thrombopoietin produced only in the liver
  • Maintains HSCs quiescent in endosteal niches; once released, promotes proliferation in sinusoidal niches
  • Plasma thrombopoietin level controlled by size of platelet and megakaryocyte progenitor pool
  • Unlike EPO: thrombopoietin is only constitutively produced
  • Paradoxically in MPNs: plasma thrombopoietin is increased despite increased megakaryocytes and platelets — because MPL expression is impaired
  • Terminal megakaryocyte maturation and platelet production do NOT require thrombopoietin

Clinical Features

  • Most often identified incidentally on routine platelet count; often present but overlooked for years
  • No specific symptoms or signs
  • Hemorrhagic tendencies: easy bruising
  • Thrombotic tendencies (microvascular occlusive events): erythromelalgia, ocular migraine, TIA
  • Physical exam: generally unremarkable
  • Splenomegaly indicates ANOTHER MPN (PV, PMF, or CML), not ET

Laboratory Findings

  • Anemia: unusual; mild neutrophilic leukocytosis: not unusual
  • Blood smear: remarkable for number of platelets (some very large)
  • Spurious hyperkalemia: large platelet mass → potassium release on clotting → test-tube artifact; NO EKG abnormalities
  • Arterial O₂ measurements: inaccurate unless collected on ice
  • PT/PTT: NORMAL
  • Platelet function: prolonged bleeding time, impaired platelet aggregation — but no test predicts bleeding/thrombosis risk
  • Marrow biopsy: megakaryocyte hypertrophy and hyperplasia + overall increase in marrow cellularity
  • If marrow reticulin increased → consider another diagnosis
  • Absent marrow iron demands explanation — iron deficiency can cause thrombocytosis; absent iron + marrow hypercellularity is a feature of PV

Diagnosis & Key Distinctions

  • Absolute platelet count level is NOT a useful diagnostic aid to distinguish benign from clonal thrombocytosis
  • If JAK2 V617F absent → cytogenetic evaluation mandatory to rule out CML or myelodysplastic disorders (5q– syndrome)
  • BCR-ABL RT-PCR associated with false-positives → FISH for BCR-ABL is preferred assay when cytogenetic Ph study is negative
🚨 ET vs PV — Critical Distinction
In ET, the quantitative JAK2 V617F neutrophil allele is NEVER greater than 50%. Importantly, 64% of JAK2 V617F–positive ET patients in one study were actually found to have PV when red cell mass and plasma volume determinations were performed. ET and PV do NOT form a biological continuum — different gene expression profiles and different natural histories.
  • ET can evolve into:
    • PV: usually in women with JAK2 V617F
    • PMF: usually in men with type 1 CALR mutations

Causes of Thrombocytosis (Table 108-6)

Clonal
  • MPNs: PV, PMF, ET, CML
  • Myelodysplastic: 5q– syndrome, idiopathic refractory sideroblastic anemia
  • Familial: thrombopoietin overproduction, JAK2/CALR/MPL mutations
Reactive
  • Tissue inflammation: collagen vascular disease, IBD
  • Malignancy, Infection, Surgery, Hemorrhage
  • Iron-deficiency anemia, Hemolysis
  • Post-splenectomy or hyposplenism
  • Rebound: correction of B₁₂/folate, post-ethanol, post-splenectomy

Complications — Rethinking Thrombocytosis

🔑 The Truth About Platelet Count and Thrombosis
No controlled clinical study has ever established that a high platelet count causes thrombosis. In patients younger than age 60 years, the incidence of thrombosis was not greater in patients with thrombocytosis than in age-matched controls. Tobacco use appears to be the most important risk factor for thrombosis in ET patients.
  • Very high platelet counts: associated primarily with HEMORRHAGE due to acquired vWD (high-MW vWF multimers removed from circulation)
  • Migraine-related neurologic problems: respond only to lowering the platelet count
  • Erythromelalgia: responds to platelet cyclooxygenase-1 inhibitors (aspirin, ibuprofen) WITHOUT reducing platelet number

Focus should be on the patient, not the platelet count.

Essential Thrombocytosis — Treatment

✅ Survival Context
Survival of ET patients is not different from the general population regardless of driver mutation. An elevated platelet count in an asymptomatic patient without cardiovascular risk factors or tobacco use requires no therapy.

When Treatment Is Needed

Before any therapy is initiated, the cause of symptoms must be clearly identified as due to the elevated platelet count.

  • Platelet count >1 × 10⁶/μL → substantial quantity of high-MW vWF multimers removed → acquired vWD
    • Identified by reduction in ristocetin cofactor activity
    • In this situation: aspirin could PROMOTE hemorrhage
    • Bleeding: rarely spontaneous; responds to tranexamic acid or ε-aminocaproic acid
    • These can be given prophylactically before/after elective minor surgery
🚨 Avoid Leukemogenic Therapy
ET patients treated with ³²P or alkylating agents are at risk of developing acute leukemia without any proof of benefit. Combining either with hydroxyurea increases this risk further.

HydroxyureaCan reduce platelet count; but leukemogenic; not uniformly effective
PlateletpheresisAt best temporary and inefficient; rarely required

None of the above agents are uniformly effective or without significant side effects.

Hydroxyurea vs Anagrelide — Key Comparison

Hydroxyurea + Aspirin
  • More effective than anagrelide + aspirin for prevention of TIA (hydroxyurea is a nitric oxide donor)
  • NOT more effective for other arterial thromboses
  • LESS effective for venous thrombosis
  • Higher risk of GI bleeding with anagrelide + aspirin
Critical Note

Normalizing the platelet count does NOT prevent either arterial or venous thrombosis in ET.

Evolution to acute leukemia is more likely to be a consequence of therapy than of the disease itself.

JAK Inhibitors in ET

  • Pegylated IFN: can produce complete molecular remission in some ET patients
  • Role of ruxolitinib in ET management has not yet been established
🔑 Guiding Principle
As more clinical experience is acquired, ET appears more benign than previously thought. In managing patients with thrombocytosis, the physician's first obligation is to do no harm.

Exam Traps — Board Coach Format

Trap 01The Bone Marrow in PV
A 58-year-old woman is found to have elevated hemoglobin and platelet count. JAK2 V617F is positive. A bone marrow biopsy is performed. The result is "normal or indistinguishable from ET/PMF." The question asks: does this result change the diagnosis of PV?
NO. Bone marrow biopsy provides NO specific diagnostic information in PV. It may be normal or indistinguishable from ET or PMF. No specific cytogenetic abnormality is associated with PV, and absence of a cytogenetic marker does NOT exclude the diagnosis. The JAK2 mutation assay is the key diagnostic test.
Trap 02Budd-Chiari in a Young Woman
A 32-year-old woman presents with acute hepatic vein thrombosis (Budd-Chiari syndrome). Her CBC shows a hematocrit of 44% and platelets of 550,000. What should be the next step?
Test for JAK2 V617F mutation. PV should be suspected in ANY woman who develops hepatic vein thrombosis. This is the ONLY type of thrombosis specifically associated with JAK2 V617F expression. The hematocrit may appear "normal" in PV due to plasma volume expansion masking the elevated red cell mass.
Trap 03Normal Hematocrit in PV with Splenomegaly
A known PV patient develops massive splenomegaly. His hematocrit is now 42%. Is he adequately treated?
NO — a "normal" hematocrit in a PV patient with massive splenomegaly should be considered indicative of an elevated red cell mass until proven otherwise. Massive splenomegaly increases plasma volume, which masks the true red cell mass elevation. Red cell mass and plasma volume determinations are needed.
Trap 04High Platelet Count ≠ Thrombosis Risk
A 45-year-old ET patient has a platelet count of 1.4 million. She is asymptomatic with no cardiovascular risk factors and does not smoke. She is worried about thrombosis. What is the best management?
Watchful waiting — NO therapy required. No controlled clinical study has ever established that a high platelet count causes thrombosis. In patients younger than 60, thrombosis incidence is not greater than age-matched controls. Tobacco use is the most important thrombosis risk factor in ET. Very high platelet counts are primarily associated with HEMORRHAGE (acquired vWD), not thrombosis. The physician's first obligation is to do no harm.
Trap 05Aspirin in Acquired vWD
An ET patient has a platelet count of 1.2 million. Ristocetin cofactor activity is reduced, consistent with acquired von Willebrand's disease. Should you give aspirin to prevent thrombosis?
NO — aspirin could PROMOTE hemorrhage in this situation. When platelet count exceeds 1 × 10⁶/μL, high-MW von Willebrand multimers are removed from circulation → acquired vWD. In this setting, aspirin inhibits platelet function and worsens the bleeding risk. Instead, tranexamic acid or ε-aminocaproic acid are appropriate for bleeding management.
Trap 06EPO Level in PV
A patient has erythrocytosis. Her serum erythropoietin level is in the normal range. Does this exclude polycythemia vera?
NO — a normal serum EPO level does NOT exclude PV. Only an ELEVATED EPO level is most consistent with a secondary cause of erythrocytosis. The JAK2 V617F mutation assay is necessary to properly evaluate suspected PV.
Trap 07Hydroxyurea — The Leukemogen Trap
A PV patient is on long-term hydroxyurea. A question asks: does this therapy protect against myelofibrosis or leukemia? What are the concerns with chronic hydroxyurea?
Hydroxyurea does NOT prevent thrombosis or myelofibrosis in PV, is itself leukemogenic, and antagonizes TP53 (causing del17p → TP53 haploinsufficiency). It does not target the involved HSCs in PV and is mutagenic. Chronic use should be constrained. Acute leukemia associated with hydroxyurea occurs in JAK2 V617F–NEGATIVE HSCs. It should be considered short-duration therapy only.
Trap 08Splenomegaly in ET
A patient with presumed essential thrombocytosis has an elevated platelet count AND significant splenomegaly. What does the splenomegaly suggest?
Splenomegaly in a patient thought to have ET indicates ANOTHER MPN — specifically PV, PMF, or CML. Splenomegaly is NOT a feature of true ET. It can mask the presence of erythrocytosis in PV. Red cell mass and plasma volume determinations should be performed to evaluate for occult PV.
Trap 09Microcytic Erythrocytosis — Three Causes Only
A patient has elevated RBC count with a low MCV. What are the only three conditions that cause microcytic erythrocytosis? How do you distinguish them?
Only three causes: β-thalassemia trait, hypoxic erythrocytosis, and polycythemia vera. Key distinction: in β-thalassemia trait, the RDW is usually NORMAL. In hypoxic erythrocytosis or PV, the RDW may be elevated due to associated iron deficiency. Today, JAK2 V617F/exon 12 NGS assay is the definitive test for PV diagnosis.
Trap 10PMF Prognosis — Scoring Nuance
A question asks about the IPSS/DIPSS/DIPSS Plus scoring in a patient with PV who has developed myelofibrosis. Are these scoring systems appropriate?
NO — these prognostic scoring systems are NOT accurate for risk assessment in PV or ET patients who have developed myelofibrosis. These systems were designed and validated for primary myelofibrosis (PMF) at diagnosis, not for post-PV or post-ET myelofibrosis. The distinction between "primary myelofibrosis" and "myelofibrosis as a complication of PV" is critical for applying the correct prognostic tool.
Trap 11ET vs PV — The 50% JAK2 Neutrophil Allele Burden Rule
A patient appears to have ET by history. The JAK2 V617F quantitative assay shows a neutrophil allele burden of 60%. What does this mean?
In ET, the quantitative JAK2 V617F neutrophil allele is NEVER greater than 50%. A JAK2 neutrophil allele burden >50% should prompt re-evaluation for PV. In one study, 64% of JAK2 V617F–positive "ET" patients actually had PV when red cell mass and plasma volume determinations were performed. ET and PV are NOT a biological continuum.
Trap 12Curative Therapy in PMF
A young patient with PMF asks about curative options. Which therapy offers a potential cure?
Allogeneic bone marrow transplantation is the ONLY curative treatment for PMF. Ruxolitinib and other agents reduce splenomegaly and constitutional symptoms and may prolong survival but are not curative. No role for BMT in PV has been defined. Nonmyeloablative conditioning regimens permit BMT to be extended to older PMF patients.
