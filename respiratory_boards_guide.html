§ 01

Lung Mechanics & Volumes

The Three-Component Model
① Lung
Natural tendency = collapse (surface tension + elastic recoil). Must be kept inflated by transpulmonary pressure.
② Chest Wall
Everything NOT lung/active muscles. Includes rib cage, abdomen (obese belly!), heart (enlarged heart). Natural tendency = OUTWARD recoil.
③ Neuromuscular System
Force generated to overcome elastic loads of both. Weakness here = Neuromuscular restriction pattern (MG, ALS, GBS).
The clinical payoff: Each component causes a DISTINCT PFT pattern. Identifying which is failing = identifying the diagnosis.

Lung Volume Definitions & Their Determinants

VolumeDefinitionKey DeterminantCritical Points
TLCTotal volume at full inflationInspiratory muscle strength vs lung stiffness (at high volumes, lung becomes very stiff — limiting factor)NOT measured by spirometry. Low in restriction; HIGH in emphysema (↓recoil allows over-inflation)
RVVolume remaining after maximal exhalationAirway closure (small airways tethered open by radial traction; as lung deflates, they close, trapping gas)NOT measured by spirometry. RISES in obstruction (airway closure earlier). Also high in muscle weakness
FRCPassive resting point after quiet exhalationBalance: outward chest wall recoil = inward lung recoilLOW in obesity/fibrosis (more inward pull). HIGH in emphysema (less inward pull from lost recoil). NORMAL in neuromuscular disease

FVCForced vital capacity (VC done forcefully)TLC and RV; also airflowCan be reduced in BOTH obstruction AND restriction. Must check FEV₁/FVC to distinguish!
⚡ Fundamental Rule: Spirometry measures FEV₁, FVC, and their ratio. It CANNOT measure TLC, FRC, or RV. You need body plethysmography or gas dilution for absolute volumes.

Measuring Absolute Volumes: Two Methods

Body Plethysmography (Gold Standard)
  • Airtight transparent box; patient pants against closed shutter at FRC
  • Uses Boyle's Law (P₁V₁ = P₂V₂): pressure fluctuations at mouth + box volume changes → thoracic gas volume
  • Measures ALL gas, including trapped gas in poorly ventilated regions
  • Accurate even in severe COPD/emphysema
  • Then: TLC = FRC + IC; RV = FRC − ERV
Inert Gas Dilution (Helium / Neon)
  • Known amount of non-absorbable inert gas inhaled; diluted by resident lung gas → concentration reveals volume
  • Critical limitation: Gas doesn't reach poorly ventilated/obstructed regions
  • Result: UNDERESTIMATES true lung volumes in obstruction
  • Gap between plethysmography and helium TLC = volume of trapped gas
If helium TLC << plethysmography TLC → significant gas trapping (obstructive physiology)

FRC — The Most Clinically Useful Volume

FRC Decreases When:

  • Obesity: Fat weight → ↓ chest wall outward recoil → lung wins → FRC falls. TLC usually NORMAL (muscle strength intact, lung recoil normal)
  • Pulmonary fibrosis: ↑ lung inward recoil → FRC + TLC + RV all fall
  • Supine position: Gravity assists inward chest wall recoil; abdominal organs push up diaphragm → FVC drops 3–8% in normal subjects, >10–25% in diaphragm weakness

FRC Increases When:

  • Emphysema: Loss of elastic recoil → less inward pull → FRC rises dramatically (220% in severe emphysema)
  • Dynamic hyperinflation (obstructive disease): Expiratory flow so slow that next breath begins before static FRC reached → operating FRC > static FRC → may approach TLC
  • Clinical result: At very high lung volumes, respiratory system is stiff → each tidal breath costs much more elastic work → patient feels unable to inhale, even though the root problem is expiratory obstruction

Dynamic Airflow Limitation — The Bernoulli Mechanism

Why Harder Effort Doesn't Increase Flow Past the Flow Limit
  • Airways are collapsible tubes (not rigid). During exhalation, gas speeds up as it travels from periphery (large summed cross-section) to central airways (small total area)
  • Faster flow → Bernoulli effect: intraluminal pressure drops → transmural pressure falls → airway narrows
  • Harder effort → faster velocity → more narrowing → no net gain in flow → flow limit reached
  • After peak expiratory flow, the FVC maneuver is effort-independent — effort doesn't change measured values, making it reproducible
  • Diseases that ↓ airway lumen (asthma, chronic bronchitis) or ↓ elastic recoil tethering airways open (emphysema) lower this flow limit → reduced maximal expiratory flows

Respiratory Muscle Strength Testing

Standard Test (MIP / MEP)
  • Maximal inspiratory/expiratory effort against closed shutter at FRC
  • Pressure monitored at mouth
  • Normal: >±60 cmH₂O at FRC → muscle weakness unlikely cause of any identified ventilatory dysfunction
Supine Spirometry (More Sensitive)
  • Supine position → diaphragm must work against abdominal contents (gravity no longer assists)
  • Normal: FVC drops only 3–8% supine vs upright
  • Diaphragm weakness / hemidiaphragm paralysis / ALS / MG: drops 10–>25%
  • More sensitive AND better tolerated than formal pressure testing

Spirometry & Flow-Volume Loops

Key Spirometric Values
ParameterFull NameWhat It MeasuresNormal
FVCForced Vital CapacityTotal air exhaled in maximal forced breath from TLC to RV≥80% predicted
FEV₁Forced Expiratory Volume in 1 secondAir exhaled in FIRST SECOND — a flow rate (volume/time)≥80% predicted
FEV₁/FVCTiffeneau ratioFraction of FVC exhaled in 1st second — the KEY ratio≥0.70 (70%)
Spirometry DIRECTLY measures FEV₁ and FVC (and their ratio). It CANNOT measure TLC, FRC, or RV.

Step 1: Obstructive vs Restrictive Pattern

Obstructive Pattern
  • FEV₁/FVC < 0.70
  • FEV₁ reduced; FVC may be normal OR reduced (if RV is very high)
  • Airway obstruction slows expiratory flow, especially at low lung volumes
  • Causes: Asthma, COPD, emphysema, bronchiectasis, CF
TRAP: In severe obstruction, RV rises so much that FVC also falls → both numerator and denominator fall → FEV₁/FVC appears "normal." ALWAYS check TLC. If TLC is HIGH (not low) → obstruction with gas trapping, NOT restriction.
Restrictive Pattern
  • FEV₁/FVC normal or ELEVATED (often ≥0.8–0.9)
  • Both FEV₁ and FVC reduced proportionally
  • Requires TLC < 80% to formally confirm
  • Lungs are small — flow per unit volume is preserved or elevated
  • Causes: ILD/IPF, obesity, neuromuscular disease, chest wall deformity, pleural disease
A "normal" FEV₁/FVC with low FEV₁ and FVC does NOT equal obstruction — could be restriction. Must measure TLC!

Bronchodilator Reversibility Testing

Criteria & Interpretation

Positive Response (Asthma favored):

  • FEV₁ improves ≥12% AND ≥200 mL after bronchodilator
  • Or FVC improves ≥12% AND ≥200 mL
  • Supports reversible airflow obstruction = asthma

No Response (COPD favored):

  • <12% or <200 mL improvement
  • Favors irreversible obstruction = COPD
  • BUT: Lack of response does NOT rule out asthma!
  • GOLD criteria: post-BD FEV₁/FVC <0.70 = COPD (by definition)
FeNO test: Elevated FeNO (≥30 ppb) → type 2 eosinophilic inflammation → supports asthma diagnosis AND predicts ICS response. Main use in practice: assessing ICS adherence.

★ Goiter Pearl — Fixed vs Variable

A large compressive goiter encircles the trachea as a rigid external ring. Unlike vocal cord disease (stented open by expiratory pressure), the goiter is rigid — it compresses equally in both phases → BOTH inspiratory AND expiratory plateaus = fixed central airway obstruction. NOT variable extrathoracic (which would be inspiratory plateau only). Pattern recognition: bilateral flat loops = goiter/tracheal stenosis.

§ 03

Diffusing Capacity (DLCO)

How DLCO Works
  • Inhale small safe amount of CO + inert tracer gas; hold breath for 10 seconds
  • CO measured in exhaled air → quantity that crossed alveolar membrane and bound hemoglobin = DLCO
  • Why CO? Binds Hb with very high affinity → no back-pressure → ideal to measure diffusion
  • Inert gas (helium/methane) distributes by ventilation only → used to calculate alveolar volume (VA)
  • Note: In obstruction, DLCO by gas dilution may underestimate (same reason as lung volumes)
DLCO ∝ (Surface Area × Hemoglobin) / Membrane Thickness
// All three factors must be considered when interpreting DLCO
Causes of DECREASED DLCO
  • Thickened/destroyed alveolar membrane:
    • IPF — scarring thickens membrane AND destroys surface area
    • Emphysema — destroys alveolar walls → ↓ surface area
  • Reduced pulmonary vasculature:
    • Pulmonary hypertension (PAH) — less capillary surface
    • Pulmonary embolism — dead space (ventilated, not perfused)
  • Reduced alveolar capillary hemoglobin:
    • Anemia — always correct DLCO for hemoglobin!
Causes of ELEVATED DLCO
  • Asthma: ↑ pulmonary blood volume during attack; mild polycythemia
  • Polycythemia: More Hb in capillaries → more CO captured
  • Pulmonary hemorrhage (DAH, Goodpasture): Free Hb in alveolar space binds CO → DLCO paradoxically elevated
  • Obesity: More blood in pulmonary circuit (unless PAH develops)
Classic Board: Hemoptysis + elevated DLCO = alveolar hemorrhage (blood in alveoli acts like extra hemoglobin binding CO)

§ 04

Gas Exchange — Alveolar Gas Equation & V/Q

Alveolar Gas Equation

The Formula & How to Use It
PAO₂ = FiO₂ × (Pbar – PH₂O) – PaCO₂/R
// FiO₂ = 0.21 on room air    Pbar = 760 mmHg at sea level    PH₂O = 47 mmHg at 37°C    R ≈ 0.8

On room air at sea level:
PAO₂ = 0.21 × (760–47) – PaCO₂/0.8 = 150 – PaCO₂/0.8
Example: PaCO₂ = 60 mmHg
PAO₂ = 150 – 60/0.8 = 150 – 75 = 75 mmHg
Example: PaCO₂ = 40 mmHg
PAO₂ = 150 – 40/0.8 = 150 – 50 = 100 mmHg
Key insight: Even severe hypoventilation (high PaCO₂) only modestly reduces PAO₂ at sea level on room air → severe hypoxemia from hypoventilation ALONE is rare. There must be V/Q mismatch or shunt to cause severe hypoxemia.

A-a Oxygen Gradient

A-a Gradient = PAO₂ – PaO₂
A-a gradient = PAO₂ (calculated from alveolar gas equation) – PaO₂ (measured from ABG)
// Normal: Age/4 + 4 mmHg   (upper limit ~15–20 mmHg in young adults; rises with age)

Normal A-a Gradient + Hypoxemia:

= Pure Hypoventilation
Lungs working fine — not enough ventilation reaching them.

Causes: ALS, MG, GBS, opioid overdose, central hypoventilation, severe obesity, chest wall restriction

Classic clue: Normal CXR + high PaCO₂ + normal A-a gradient = think neuromuscular disease

Elevated A-a Gradient + Hypoxemia:

= Lung parenchymal problem
V/Q mismatch: COPD, asthma, PE, pneumonia → O₂ responsive

Shunt (V/Q = 0): Alveolar filling (ARDS, pneumonia, pulmonary edema), AVM → O₂ NOT responsive

Diffusion limitation: ILD at exercise, extreme altitude (rare)

V/Q Mismatch vs Shunt — The Critical Distinction

How O₂ Separates the Two
FeatureV/Q Mismatch (Low V/Q)True Shunt (V/Q = 0)
DefinitionSome ventilation reaches perfused alveoli; just not enoughNo ventilation at all — alveoli filled or collapsed
Response to 100% O₂PaO₂ rises — O₂ can reach the low-V/Q alveolusPaO₂ does NOT rise — no gas can reach those alveoli
A-a GradientElevatedElevated (more so)
Clinical examplesCOPD, asthma, PE, partial pneumoniaARDS, lobar pneumonia (consolidated), pulmonary edema (severe), AVM
ManagementSupplemental O₂ usually sufficientPEEP/mechanical ventilation to recruit collapsed alveoli
Mechanism of shunt refractoriness to O₂: Raising FiO₂ has NO effect on nonventilated alveoli. In ventilated alveoli, Hb is already near 100% saturation — only a tiny increase in dissolved O₂ occurs in plasma. Not enough to offset the large volume of deoxygenated shunt blood.
§ 05

Disease PFT Profiles

🫁

Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis (IPF)

Restriction due to Increased Elastic Recoil

TLC ↓↓FRC ↓↓RV ↓↓FVC ↓↓FEV₁ ↓FEV₁/FVC ↑ (preserved/elevated)Raw NORMALDLCO ↓↓↓

Mechanism:

  • Fibrosis → ↑ elastic recoil → all volumes fall (TLC, FRC, RV)
  • FEV₁ and FVC fall proportionally → FEV₁/FVC preserved or elevated
  • Stiff parenchyma pulls airways open via radial traction → Raw NORMAL, flows elevated per unit volume
  • Flow-volume loop: miniature normal loop shifted to lower absolute volumes
  • Fibrosis destroys alveolar surface + capillaries → DLCO ↓↓↓

Clinical Keys:

  • CT: bibasilar honeycombing + traction bronchiectasis + GGO = UIP pattern
  • Hypoxemia from low V/Q: alveoli underventilated (fibrosed, stretched to max) but still perfused
  • A-a gradient elevated; worsens with exercise (diffusion limitation)
  • Clubbing common; bibasilar "Velcro" crackles
💨

Severe Emphysema

Obstruction due to Decreased Elastic Recoil

TLC ↑↑ (130%)FRC ↑↑↑ (220%)RV ↑↑↑ (310%)FVC ↓↓ (60%)FEV₁ ↓↓ (35%)FEV₁/FVC ↓Raw NORMAL (1.5)DLCO ↓↓↓ (40%)

Mechanism:

  • Alveolar destruction → ↓ elastic recoil → FRC and TLC BOTH rise
  • Small airways no longer tethered open (lost radial traction) → collapse on expiration → air trapping → RV massively elevated
  • FVC falls because RV is so high (bottom of VC displaced upward)
  • Central airways relatively preserved → Raw NORMAL in "pure" emphysema
  • Loss of alveolar surface + capillaries → DLCO ↓↓↓

Flow-Volume & Clinical:

  • Marked scooping; initial transient spike (air from collapsing central airways at start)
  • At rest: hypoxemia mild (poorly ventilated acini also poorly perfused — they match)
  • During exercise: PaO₂ may fall sharply if vasculature destroyed → can't ↑ cardiac output → mixed venous O₂ falls
  • CT: centrilobular (upper lobes, smoking) or panlobular (lower lobes, α₁-AT deficiency)
Key distinction from asthma: DLCO very low in emphysema; normal/elevated in asthma. Obstructive PFTs + very low DLCO = emphysema or PAH, NOT asthma
🫧

Acute Bronchial Asthma

Obstruction due to Decreased Airway Diameter (Reversible)

TLC normal (100%)FRC ↑ (104%) — dynamic hyperinflationRV ↑ (120%)FVC ↓ (90%)FEV₁ ↓↓ (35% pre-BD → 75% post-BD)FEV₁/FVC ↓Raw ↑ (2.5)DLCO ↑ (120%)

Mechanism:

  • Smooth muscle bronchoconstriction + mucosal inflammation + thickened walls → ↑ Raw → scooped loop
  • Airway closure at low lung volumes → RV ↑ → FRC ↑ (dynamic hyperinflation)
  • TLC usually normal (elastic recoil and muscle strength preserved)
  • DLCO normal or elevated (more pulmonary blood volume)

Diagnosis Keys:

  • Reversibility: FEV₁ improves ≥12% AND ≥200 mL after bronchodilator
  • Mild hypoxemia from low V/Q mismatch (responsive to O₂)
  • PaCO₂ initially LOW (hyperventilation) → if PaCO₂ normalizes in severe attack = DANGER SIGN
  • FeNO elevated (type 2 eosinophilic inflammation) → predicts ICS response; elevated FeNO on treatment = poor adherence
💪

Myasthenia Gravis (Neuromuscular Restriction)

Restriction due to Reduced Muscle Strength

TLC ↓ (75%)FRC NORMAL (100%)RV ↑ (120%)FVC ↓↓ (60%)FEV₁ ↓ (60%)FEV₁/FVC NORMAL (1.0)Raw NORMALDLCO NORMAL (80%)

Mechanism (the WHY):

  • FRC is NORMAL: Passive balance of lung vs chest wall recoil — no muscles needed at rest
  • TLC low: Inspiratory muscles too weak to inflate lungs fully
  • RV elevated: Expiratory muscles too weak to complete exhalation
  • FVC = TLC − RV: Both extremes moving toward each other → FVC compressed from both ends ("innocent bystander")
  • Airways and vasculature untouched → Raw and DLCO NORMAL

How to Diagnose:

  • Supine FVC drops >10–25% → diaphragm weakness
  • MIP and MEP reduced
  • Normal A-a gradient (lungs innocent — pure hypoventilation)
  • Normal CXR (or bibasilar atelectasis from poor sighs)
  • ACh receptor antibody + → diagnostic; CT chest for thymoma
Board Favorite: FVC and FEV₁ low + FEV₁/FVC normal + DLCO normal + FRC normal → NEUROMUSCULAR DISEASE. The preserved DLCO and FRC distinguish this from ALL parenchymal causes of restriction.
⚖️

Moderate Obesity

Restriction due to Chest Wall Abnormality

TLC NORMAL (95%)FRC ↓↓ (65%)RV NORMAL (100%)FEV₁/FVC NORMALRaw NORMALDLCO NORMAL (95%)
  • Fat weight → ↓ chest wall outward recoil → lung's inward recoil wins → FRC falls
  • Lung and muscle strength intact → TLC NORMAL (key distinction from ILD or neuromuscular)
  • Mild hypoxemia: low FRC → breathing near FRC → dependent airways close → low V/Q → mild O₂-responsive hypoxemia
  • DLCO decreases ONLY if OSA develops → chronic intermittent hypoxemia → pulmonary arterial hypertension
🩸

Pulmonary Embolism (PE)

Vascular Obstruction — Isolated DLCO reduction

TLC normalFVC normalFEV₁ ≈98%FEV₁/FVC normal (≈0.9)DLCO ↓↓ (50%)A-a gradient ↑
Classic Board Pattern: Isolated DLCO reduction with otherwise normal spirometry and volumes. The vasculature is obstructed (dead space) but airways and parenchyma are intact — ONLY DLCO falls. CTA shows filling defects. DLCO falls because less pulmonary capillary surface is available to take up CO.
Hypoxemia mechanism in PE: V/Q mismatch (NOT true shunt) — blood diverted to unobstructed zones → overloads normal units → low V/Q. Also ↓ cardiac output → ↑ O₂ extraction → low mixed venous O₂ → worsens hypoxemia. Responds to supplemental O₂.
🔬

Sarcoidosis

Restrictive ILD + Bilateral Hilar Adenopathy

TLC ↓FVC ↓FEV₁/FVC normal or ↑DLCO ↓
  • Granulomatous inflammation → restrictive ILD pattern (similar to IPF but less severe DLCO typically)
  • CT: bilateral hilar adenopathy + upper lobe peribronchial nodules (classic perilymphatic distribution)
  • Diagnosis: EBUS-TBNA sensitivity ≥80% for sarcoidosis; higher if combined with endobronchial + transbronchial biopsies
  • DLCO can be preserved early, falls as disease progresses
  • FEV₁/FVC may appear falsely normal even with low spirometry values
Key from questions: CT showing "Egg Shell" calcifications in paratracheal nodes (calcification at periphery) → classic sarcoidosis or silicosis → next step = EBUS-TBNA
🌀

Chronic Bronchiectasis

Obstruction with Air Trapping + Variable DLCO

TLC ↑ (130%)FRC ↑RV ↑↑ (190%)FVC normal or ↓FEV₁ ↓FEV₁/FVC ↓DLCO normal or ↓
  • Obstructive pattern (↑ TLC, FRC, RV) with DLCO relatively preserved vs emphysema (no alveolar destruction)
  • CT: dilated airways (signet ring sign), cylindrical bronchiectasis lower lobes (COPD) or cystic upper lobes (CF)
  • Chronic productive cough; clubbing common
  • DLCO falls if disease progresses or PAH develops

Diagnostic Procedures

Flexible vs Rigid Bronchoscopy
FeatureFlexibleRigid
ReachDistal airwaysCentral airways only
SedationConscious sedationGeneral anesthesia
Airway controlNo — cannot ventilate through scopeYES — ventilate through scope
Instrument channelConstrained working channelFree passage of large tools
Main useBAL, EBUS, biopsiesMassive hemoptysis, foreign body, large airway interventions, cryobiopsy backup
Bronchoalveolar Lavage (BAL)
  • Gold standard for respiratory secretion sampling
  • Scope wedged in distal airway; 1–3 aliquots ~50 mL saline instilled then suctioned back
  • Samples distal airways and alveolar space — areas NOT directly visible
  • Avoids salivary contamination (vs sputum)
  • Alveolar hemorrhage: Serial BALs from same site → progressively bloodier returns (rising RBC count) → diagnostic
  • Useful when sputum can't be obtained or when specific lobe sampling needed

Biopsy Techniques — Forceps vs Cryobiopsy

Forceps Transbronchial Biopsy
  • 2.0 or 2.8 mm flexible forceps
  • Malignant nodules: 3 samples adequate
  • Lung transplant rejection: ≥5 distinct pieces of alveolated lung required
  • Guided by fluoroscopy, radial EBUS, or navigation systems
Cryobiopsy
  • Liquid nitrogen/CO₂ probe freezes lung tissue → adheres to probe → removed with bronchoscope
  • Higher diagnostic yield for diffuse parenchymal disease (ILD/IPF)
  • Higher risk: major bleeding + pneumothorax
  • Rigid bronchoscope sometimes used as safety backup
  • Tradeoff: better histology for IPF vs higher complication rate

EBUS-TBNA — The Most Important Procedure to Know

Endobronchial Ultrasound-Guided Transbronchial Needle Aspiration
  • Specialized flexible bronchoscope with convex ultrasound probe at distal tip + video camera
  • Under real-time ultrasound, needle passes through airway wall into mediastinal target
  • Has largely REPLACED surgical mediastinoscopy as first-line for mediastinal lymph node sampling
  • Surgery reserved for EBUS-negative cases where suspicion remains high
DiseaseEBUS-TBNA Sensitivity
Epithelial malignancy (lung cancer staging)~90%
Lymphoma (de novo)~70%
Lymphoma (recurrence)Higher than de novo
Sarcoidosis≥80% (higher if + endobronchial + transbronchial biopsies)
ROSE (Rapid On-Site Cytologic Evaluation): Cytotechnologist examines aspirate immediately → tells bronchoscopist whether sample is adequate → reduces need for repeat procedures
EUS-B adjunct: Esophageal approach with EBUS bronchoscope → samples mediastinal stations better accessed via esophagus → combined sensitivity slightly higher than either alone
From questions — CT with "Egg Shell" calcifications in paratracheal nodes: Classic for silicosis or sarcoidosis → next step = EBUS-TBNA. Not CT-guided TTNA (not optimal for mediastinal nodes), not VATS (too invasive first), not BAL alone (won't sample nodes)

TTNA — Transthoracic Needle Aspiration

CT-Guided TTNA for Peripheral Lung Lesions
  • CT most common guidance modality; sensitivity + specificity >90%
  • Sensitivity increased for peripheral lesions
  • US-guided: shorter procedure time, lower cost, comparable diagnostic rate, real-time dynamic imaging
  • FNA vs core needle: core gives better material for molecular testing

Complications:

  • Pneumothorax: 17–28% (majority don't need chest tube)
  • Hemorrhage: 25–33%
  • Core needle complications: 38.8% vs FNA: 24%
  • Risk factors: smaller nodule, larger needle, more lung traversed, central location
NPV ~70% → negative TTNA does NOT rule out malignancy

Miscellaneous Tests

FeNO (Fraction of Exhaled Nitric Oxide)
  • Marker of type 2 airway eosinophilic inflammation
  • High FeNO (≥30 ppb) = elevated; suggests asthma and ICS response
  • Primary clinical use: assessing ICS adherence
  • High FeNO on "ICS therapy" → patient likely not taking medication
  • Does NOT reliably guide dose titration
  • Influenced by ethnicity — no universal standards yet
  • NOT formally approved for clinical use in all guidelines
Sweat Test — Cystic Fibrosis
  • Pilocarpine iontophoresis stimulates sweat → chloride measured
  • ≥60 mmol/L = CF confirmed
  • 30–59 mmol/L = Indeterminate (updated from old 40–59 to catch milder phenotypes)
  • <30 mmol/L = CF unlikely
  • Must be correlated with genetics + clinical manifestations
Sputum Testing — Key Points

For Infection:

  • Microscopy + culture; safe induction with nebulized saline even in airflow limitation
  • TB, Legionella, Pneumocystis: raise concern even in poor quality sample
  • Multiplex PCR: analyze ALL samples regardless of quality (40% of detections missed if only high-quality analyzed)

For Malignancy (Cytology):

  • Highly specific if malignant cells seen
  • Sensitivity <40% — very low
  • No benefit combined with CXR for cancer screening
  • Use only when more invasive techniques not possible

§ 08

Chest Imaging

CXR — Key Points
  • Standard views: PA + lateral (outpatient); AP (bedside — cardiac appears larger)
  • 2D representation of 3D structures — both views used for superimposed structures
  • CHF on CXR: Kerley B lines (lymphatics), cephalization (blood redistribution), bronchial cuffing (thickened walls)
  • Many airway/vascular diseases have NORMAL CXR (COPD, PAH early, PE)
  • CXR = initial diagnostic for nonspecific symptoms (can discriminate pulmonary vs extrapulmonary)
CT — Hounsfield Units & Secondary Pulmonary Lobule
  • Density in Hounsfield units (HU): water = 0, air = –1000
  • Lung windows = low-density parenchyma; Mediastinal windows = high-density structures
  • Secondary pulmonary lobule: Fundamental subunit — central airway + artery, parenchyma, surrounding interstitium/veins
  • CLE = centrilobular emphysema (upper lobes, smoking)
  • PLE = panlobular emphysema (basilar predominant, α₁-AT)
  • PA trunk >3 cm = elevated pulmonary vascular pressure (PAH)
  • Basal/dependent ILD distribution → IPF; apical → NSIP
  • Traction bronchiectasis → indicates reduced lung compliance → ILD severity marker
CT with Contrast (CTPA) — PE
  • Timed iodinated contrast bolus through pulmonary arteries
  • PE = filling defects (dark voids in bright contrast-filled vessels)
  • Also useful: mediastinal lymph node assessment, pleural disease
PET-CT & US vs CT
  • PET tracer [¹⁸F]-FDG: taken up proportional to metabolic activity
  • Main use: benign vs malignant nodule; lung cancer staging
  • Co-registered with CT → structural source of activity
  • US advantage: Real-time (dynamic imaging), portable, no radiation, good for biopsy guidance, soft tissue detection
  • CT advantage: Superior anatomical detail, mediastinal assessment, PE
  • Mediastinal nodes: CT cannot separate blood from lymph → EBUS-TBNA for tissue

§ 10

Exam Traps — Expert Board Coach Analysis

How to use this section: Each trap shows what examiners WANT you to think, what is actually TRUE, why it matters clinically, and then presents a BOLDED case answer.
1

"Normal FEV₁/FVC = No Obstruction"

🪤 The Trap

If FEV₁/FVC ≥0.70, there is no airflow obstruction — look for restriction instead.

✓ The Truth

In severe obstructive disease, rising RV compresses FVC from below. Both FEV₁ and FVC fall → ratio may appear "normal" — the artifactually normal FEV₁/FVC. You MUST measure TLC. If TLC is elevated (not low), you have obstruction with gas trapping, NOT restriction.

⚡ Why It Matters

Calling obstruction "restriction" leads to ILD workup and wrong diagnosis.

📋 Case
Patient: FEV₁ 35%, FVC 60%, FEV₁/FVC 0.58 → plethysmography TLC 130%, RV 310%. Question: "What pattern?" Answer: OBSTRUCTIVE (emphysema) — elevated TLC confirms obstruction with gas trapping. The low FVC is due to massively elevated RV, not reduced TLC.
2

"Low FVC + Low FEV₁ = Restriction — Order HRCT for ILD"

🪤 The Trap

Whenever FVC and FEV₁ are low with preserved ratio, it's restriction → immediately think pulmonary fibrosis.

✓ The Truth

You CANNOT diagnose restriction without TLC. A "restrictive pattern" on spirometry could be: actual restriction (ILD, chest wall) OR severe obstruction with air trapping (ratio normalizing). Only TLC <80% confirms restriction. Furthermore, restriction can come from chest wall (obesity, normal TLC), neuromuscular (normal FRC, DLCO), or parenchymal (low DLCO) — DLCO is the key discriminator.

⚡ Why It Matters

MG or ALS patient could receive ILD diagnosis and unnecessary immunosuppression.

📋 Case
68-year-old woman, FVC 55%, FEV₁ 55%, FEV₁/FVC 0.95, DLCO 80%, FRC normal, supine FVC drops 22%. Best diagnosis: Myasthenia Gravis (neuromuscular restriction) — normal DLCO, normal FRC, supine FVC drop >10% = diaphragm/neuromuscular weakness, NOT ILD. Order AChR antibody + EMG, not HRCT.
3

"DLCO Reduced = Parenchymal Disease (ILD or Emphysema)"

🪤 The Trap

Low DLCO = damaged alveolar membrane → always think fibrosis or emphysema.

✓ The Truth

DLCO = Surface Area × Hb / Membrane Thickness. It can fall from: anemia (↓Hb), pulmonary vascular disease (↓capillary surface: PAH, chronic PE), as well as parenchymal disease. An isolated DLCO reduction with normal spirometry and normal TLC is the classic presentation of PAH or chronic thromboembolic disease — NOT ILD. Always check for anemia and vascular disease first. Correct for hemoglobin before interpreting results.

⚡ Why It Matters

Missing PAH means missing a life-threatening, treatable condition.

📋 Case
35-year-old woman, exertional dyspnea, FEV₁ 98%, FVC 90%, TLC 95%, DLCO 50%, normal CT chest. Best next step: Echocardiogram to assess for pulmonary arterial hypertension — isolated DLCO reduction with normal spirometry, normal TLC, and normal CT means the parenchyma is fine. The vascular bed is the problem.
4

"Normal A-a Gradient = No Lung Disease"

🪤 The Trap

If A-a gradient is normal, the lungs are fine — look for systemic/non-pulmonary cause of hypoxia.

✓ The Truth

Normal A-a gradient + hypoxemia = pure hypoventilation. The lungs are innocent but ventilation is impaired. This is the neuromuscular disease pattern. The A-a gradient checks whether there is an intrinsic lung problem — if normal, there is not. Elevated PaCO₂ with normal A-a gradient = neuromuscular, central, or chest wall cause.

⚡ Why It Matters

Treating hypoventilation with bronchodilators or steroids misses the correct diagnosis. These patients need NIV/BiPAP, not inhalers.

📋 Case
61-year-old, normal CXR, pH 7.31, PaCO₂ 70, PaO₂ 50, HCO₃ 37. A-a gradient = PAO₂ – PaO₂ = [150–70/0.8] – 50 = [150–87.5] – 50 = 12.5 (NORMAL). Correct diagnosis: ALS (pure hypoventilation) — normal A-a gradient + high PaCO₂ = lung parenchyma fine, muscles failing. Order nerve conduction studies + EMG, initiate NIV discussion.
5

"Shunt Hypoxemia Responds to High-Flow Oxygen"

🪤 The Trap

Give all hypoxemic patients high-flow O₂ — it will always improve saturation.

✓ The Truth

True shunt (V/Q = 0) is REFRACTORY to supplemental oxygen. When alveoli are completely filled or collapsed, no inspired oxygen can reach them regardless of FiO₂. Raising FiO₂ only improves oxygenation in V/Q mismatch (some ventilation still reaches the unit). In shunt: raising FiO₂ has no effect on the nonventilated alveoli; in ventilated alveoli Hb is already ~100% saturated — only dissolved O₂ increases marginally.

⚡ Why It Matters

Understanding shunt physiology is essential for ARDS management.

📋 Case
Patient with bilateral infiltrates (ARDS), PaO₂ 55 on 100% FiO₂ non-rebreather mask — barely improves. Best next step: Intubation with PEEP — refractory hypoxemia despite maximal FiO₂ defines shunt physiology. PEEP recruits collapsed alveoli → converts shunt to low V/Q → restores O₂ responsiveness. More O₂ via same route will not help.
6

"Goiter Compresses Trachea = Variable Extrathoracic Obstruction"

🪤 The Trap

Goiter is extrathoracic → variable extrathoracic obstruction → inspiratory plateau only on flow-volume loop.

✓ The Truth

A large compressive goiter creates FIXED obstruction — it encircles the trachea as a rigid external ring. Unlike vocal cord disease (stented open by expiratory pressure), the goiter doesn't change its compression based on respiratory phase. Result: FIXED bilateral plateaus on BOTH inspiration AND expiration. Variable extrathoracic (e.g., vocal cord paralysis) causes inspiratory plateau ONLY.

⚡ Why It Matters

Pattern recognition on flow-volume loops is a high-yield exam topic. Goiter = bilateral plateau (Pattern C in textbook). Variable extrathoracic = inspiratory plateau only (Pattern D).

📋 Case
35-year-old woman with goiter, inspiratory stridor, exertional dyspnea. Flow-volume loop shows flattening of BOTH expiratory and inspiratory curves. Correct answer: Fixed central airway obstruction (bilateral plateau) — Pattern C. NOT variable extrathoracic (which only affects inspiration). The goiter is a rigid external mass compressing the trachea equally in both phases.
7

"Body Plethysmography and Helium Dilution Give the Same TLC"

🪤 The Trap

Both methods measure the same lung volumes — pick whichever is available.

✓ The Truth

In obstructive disease with significant gas trapping, helium dilution UNDERESTIMATES TLC because helium cannot enter poorly ventilated/obstructed regions. Plethysmography measures ALL gas (including trapped gas) using Boyle's Law. The gap between them = trapped gas volume. Plethysmography is the gold standard in suspected obstruction.

⚡ Why It Matters

Using helium dilution in COPD will underestimate severity. Use plethysmography for accurate obstruction assessment.

📋 Case
COPD patient: TLC by helium dilution = 110%, TLC by plethysmography = 140%. The 30% discrepancy means: Significant gas trapping — trapped gas in poorly ventilated bullae/obstructed lung units that helium cannot access. Plethysmography is the gold standard. This is consistent with severe emphysema with bullous disease.
8

"High FeNO + Poor Asthma Control = Step Up Treatment / Add Biologic"

🪤 The Trap

Elevated FeNO in asthmatic not responding to ICS → step up therapy → add LABA or anti-IL-5 biologic.

✓ The Truth

The most important interpretation of persistently elevated FeNO despite being "on" ICS therapy is POOR ADHERENCE. FeNO measures airway eosinophilic inflammation — this should be suppressed by ICS if properly taken. If FeNO remains high, the patient is likely not taking their ICS. Stepping up therapy before confirming adherence is incorrect. FeNO is primarily a tool for assessing adherence, NOT for titrating biologics.

⚡ Why It Matters

Incorrect escalation wastes resources and exposes patients to unnecessary biologics.

📋 Case
Asthma patient on ICS, still symptomatic, FeNO 45 ppb (high). Best interpretation: Poor adherence to inhaled corticosteroid therapy — high FeNO indicates ongoing type 2 inflammation that should be suppressed by ICS. Confirm adherence and proper technique before changing treatment. FeNO monitors adherence — not the indication for biologics.
9

"Mediastinal Lymphadenopathy → Proceed to Surgical Mediastinoscopy"

🪤 The Trap

For mediastinal masses, the definitive diagnostic test is surgical mediastinoscopy — it's the gold standard.

✓ The Truth

EBUS-TBNA has largely REPLACED mediastinoscopy as the FIRST-LINE approach for mediastinal lymph node sampling. Surgery is reserved for cases where needle-based sampling is negative BUT suspicion remains high. EBUS-TBNA sensitivity: ~90% for epithelial malignancies, ~70–80% for lymphoma, ≥80% for sarcoidosis. Combined EBUS + EUS-B gives even higher sensitivity without surgery.

⚡ Why It Matters

Performing surgery first is inappropriate when a less invasive, equally effective technique exists.

📋 Case
55-year-old with mediastinal lymphadenopathy with "Egg Shell" calcifications on CT. Best next step: EBUS-TBNA — minimally invasive first-line approach for mediastinal sampling. Sensitivity ~90% for malignancy, ≥80% for sarcoidosis. Mediastinoscopy reserved for EBUS-negative cases with high clinical suspicion.
10

"Obesity Pattern on PFTs = Always Restriction (Low TLC)"

🪤 The Trap

Obese patient with reduced FVC → low TLC → restriction → standard restrictive workup.

✓ The Truth

In moderate obesity, TLC is typically NORMAL. Only FRC is reduced (fat pushing up chest wall's resting position). FVC may be mildly reduced because ERV is reduced (FRC is low), but TLC is preserved. DLCO is normal. Only morbid obesity causes TLC reduction. FRC is the obesity-sensitive volume, NOT TLC.

⚡ Why It Matters

Could send an obese patient for unnecessary bronchoscopy or HRCT when the problem is purely mechanical.

📋 Case
55-year-old BMI 38, FVC 80%, FEV₁/FVC 0.88, TLC 95%, FRC 65%, DLCO 95%. Correct interpretation: Normal lung function consistent with moderate obesity — only abnormality is reduced FRC (mechanical effect of adipose tissue). TLC, DLCO, and FEV₁/FVC all normal. No further respiratory workup needed; weight management is the intervention.
11

"IPF Has a High FEV₁/FVC = No Obstruction: Simple Restriction"

🪤 The Trap

A high FEV₁/FVC in a patient with ILD on CT confirms pure restriction — airways are fine.

✓ The Truth

In IPF, the stiff fibrotic parenchyma tethers airways OPEN via radial traction → airways are actually wider than normal → Raw NORMAL and FEV₁/FVC elevated. This elevated FEV₁/FVC is a direct physiological consequence of the fibrotic process pulling airways open, not an independent finding. Additionally, in a smoker with both emphysema and IPF (CPFE), the two conditions "cancel each other out" — FEV₁/FVC and volumes may appear paradoxically near-normal while DLCO is severely reduced.

⚡ Why It Matters

Combined Pulmonary Fibrosis and Emphysema (CPFE) has poor prognosis and misleading PFT pattern.

📋 Case
75-year-old heavy smoker, CT: upper lobe emphysema + bibasilar honeycombing. FEV₁ 75%, FVC 72%, FEV₁/FVC 0.85, TLC 90%, DLCO 40%. Best diagnosis: Combined Pulmonary Fibrosis and Emphysema (CPFE) — emphysema hyperinflates while fibrosis shrinks the lung → paradoxically near-normal volumes; the severely disproportionate DLCO drop is the key indicator of severe gas exchange impairment.
12

"Cryobiopsy Has Higher Yield = Use It Always for ILD Diagnosis"

🪤 The Trap

Since cryobiopsy gives better diagnostic yield for ILD, it should replace forceps biopsy in all cases.

✓ The Truth

Cryobiopsy has HIGHER diagnostic yield than forceps for diffuse parenchymal disease like IPF, BUT it comes with significantly higher complication rates: major bleeding and pneumothorax. Because of this risk, rigid bronchoscope is sometimes used as a precautionary backup. The tradeoff must be assessed per patient — severely compromised patients (low FVC, low FEV₁) are at prohibitive risk.

⚡ Why It Matters

Selecting biopsy technique requires weighing diagnostic yield against complication risk, especially in frail patients with impaired respiratory reserve.

📋 Case
65-year-old with ILD, FVC 45%, FEV₁ 40%. Bronchoscopy planned for ILD diagnosis. Choice of biopsy: In this severely compromised patient, cryobiopsy carries prohibitively high risk of major bleeding and pneumothorax. Forceps biopsy (lower yield but safer) or multidisciplinary team review for surgical biopsy (VATS) should be considered. Cryobiopsy higher yield does NOT outweigh risk in severe restriction.
13

"Emphysema Has Elevated Airway Resistance (Raw)"

🪤 The Trap

Emphysema destroys lung tissue → airways become floppy → airway resistance (Raw) is elevated.

✓ The Truth

In PURE emphysema, Raw is NORMAL because the central airways (which dominate airway resistance) are relatively unaffected. The problem is loss of elastic recoil (which causes dynamic airflow limitation during forced exhalation) — not increased Raw per se. The scooped flow-volume loop and low FEV₁ are due to reduced driving pressure (lost recoil) and airway collapse at low lung volumes. Raw is elevated in COPD when there is concurrent chronic bronchitis (mucus + wall thickening) or in asthma (smooth muscle contraction).

⚡ Why It Matters

Directly tested in PFT interpretation tables — Raw normal = pure emphysema; Raw elevated = asthma or chronic bronchitis.

📋 Case
PFT table: TLC 130%, FRC 220%, RV 310%, FEV₁ 35%, FEV₁/FVC 0.38, Raw 1.5 cmH₂O/L/s, DLCO 40%. "What is Raw?" Correct answer: 1.5 — NORMAL. In pure emphysema, central airways are intact so resistance is normal. The dramatically reduced FEV₁ is due to loss of recoil and airway collapse during forced exhalation, not to increased resistance.
14

"Alveolar Hemorrhage Causes Low DLCO (Blood Damages the Membrane)"

🪤 The Trap

Blood in alveoli damages the membrane → diffusion is impaired → DLCO falls.

✓ The Truth

In alveolar hemorrhage, DLCO is paradoxically ELEVATED. Free hemoglobin in the alveolar space acts like an "extra" pool of hemoglobin available to bind CO during the single-breath maneuver. The CO combines with this alveolar Hb → measured uptake increases → DLCO rises. This is one of the key features of pulmonary hemorrhage alongside hemoptysis and hypoxemia.

⚡ Why It Matters

Elevated DLCO with hemoptysis should always prompt consideration of alveolar hemorrhage syndrome (Goodpasture, ANCA vasculitis, DAH).

📋 Case
Patient with hemoptysis and progressive dyspnea, DLCO 140% predicted. Correct interpretation: Elevated DLCO in the setting of hemoptysis = alveolar hemorrhage — free Hb in alveoli binds CO, artificially elevating DLCO. Confirm with serial BALs (progressively bloodier returns). Order ANCA, anti-GBM antibody. Do NOT reassure that lungs are "fine" based on elevated DLCO.
15

"FEV₁/FVC is Low in ILD — Always Need to Confirm Obstruction First"

🪤 The Trap

If spirometry shows low FEV₁, low FVC, and FEV₁/FVC around 0.65–0.70 in a patient with ILD on CT → there must be coexisting obstruction → treat COPD + ILD.

✓ The Truth

In end-stage ILD (advanced pulmonary fibrosis), the FVC can fall so low that even though FEV₁ is also very low, the FEV₁/FVC ratio approaches or falls below 0.7 as an artifact of the severely restricted lung (RV proportionally relatively elevated for the small TLC). This is NOT true obstruction — TLC will be very LOW (not elevated), and DLCO will be very low. Look at TLC and DLCO together, not just the ratio.

⚡ Why It Matters

Treating end-stage IPF as COPD with bronchodilators misses the actual management (antifibrotics, transplant evaluation).

📋 Case
IPF patient on spirometry: FEV₁ 62%, FVC 70%, FEV₁/FVC 0.65, TLC 60%, DLCO 45%. Question: "Is there coexisting obstruction?" Answer: No — this is advanced restrictive disease only. TLC is very LOW (60%), not elevated. DLCO severely reduced. Low FEV₁/FVC is an artifact of advanced fibrosis, not true obstruction. Do not add inhaled bronchodilators as primary management. Continue antifibrotic therapy and assess for transplant listing.
