01

Overview & Classification of ILD

What is ILD?

Diffuse parenchymal lung diseases (ILDs) include over 200 heterogeneous conditions affecting the lung parenchyma with varying degrees of inflammation and fibrosis. The dominant site of injury is the interstitial space — the region between the alveolar epithelium and the endothelium — though alveolar epithelium and endothelial cells are also critically involved.

Core Concept
Most patients present with progressive exertional dyspnea or persistent dry cough. Because some ILDs are multisystem, patients may also present with nonrespiratory features (e.g., skin thickening in scleroderma, ulnar deviation in RA). ILD can also be discovered incidentally on chest CT or abnormal PFTs.

Classification Framework

The classic approach divides ILDs into known and unknown causes:

Known Causes
  • Occupational exposures: Asbestosis, Silicosis
  • Medications: Nitrofurantoin, Amiodarone, Methotrexate, Bleomycin
  • Systemic disease: CTD-associated ILD (scleroderma, RA, polymyositis)
  • Granulomatous (known): Hypersensitivity pneumonitis
Unknown Causes
  • Idiopathic Interstitial Pneumonias (IIPs):
    • IPF (most common)
    • NSIP
    • COP (Cryptogenic Organizing Pneumonia)
    • AIP (Acute Interstitial Pneumonia)
    • RB-ILD, DIP (smoking-related)
  • Granulomatous (unknown): Sarcoidosis
  • Other: LAM, Pulmonary alveolar proteinosis, PLCH

Epidemiology — Most Common ILDs

Among all ILDs, IPF, sarcoidosis, and CTD-associated ILDs are the most common forms encountered in clinical practice and on boards.

Prevalence Clue
IPF prevalence is estimated at 50–200:100,000. It is the most common ILD of unknown cause. Prevalence increases with age — most common in patients over 60.
02

Diagnostic Approach to ILD

Central Tenet of ILD Diagnosis
No single piece of data makes the diagnosis. The combined weight of clinical data + laboratory + PFTs + imaging + histopathology (if obtained) are jointly required. A multidisciplinary team (pulmonologist, rheumatologist, radiologist, pathologist) is standard of care.

History — Key Clues

Age (Critical!)

  • IPF: almost always >60 years; rare under 50
  • In patients >65 with atypical CT → still most likely UIP/IPF histopathologically
  • Sarcoidosis, CTD-ILD, LAM, PLCH: typically present ages 20–40
  • NSIP: typically fifth decade, nonsmoking females

Sex

  • LAM: almost exclusively young women
  • CTD-ILD: mostly women (except RA-ILD which is more common in men)
  • IPF and occupational ILDs: more common in men

Duration of Symptoms

  • Acute (days–weeks): Eosinophilic pneumonia, AIP, HP, GPA — also consider acute exacerbation of IPF
  • Subacute (weeks–months): Sarcoidosis, COP, CTD-ILD, drug-induced ILD
  • Chronic indolent (months–years): Typified by IPF

Smoking History

  • RB-ILD and DIP: strongly smoking-related (causative)
  • IPF: ~75% of patients have history of smoking

Medications — Major Offenders (Boards Favorite!)

Drug-Induced ILD — Must Know List
  • Antibiotics: Nitrofurantoin
  • Antiarrhythmics: Amiodarone
  • Chemotherapy: Bleomycin, methotrexate (also used in RA), cyclophosphamide
  • Immunosuppressants: Azathioprine, rituximab, TNF-α blockers
Note: The same drugs used to treat ILD (e.g., methotrexate, rituximab) can ALSO cause ILD.

Exposures & Family History

  • Occupational: Asbestos → asbestosis; bird or organic dust → hypersensitivity pneumonitis
  • Family history: Up to 20% of pulmonary fibrosis may be familial (MUC5B promoter variant, TERT gene variants)
  • A close relative with IIP is one of the strongest risk factors for IPF

Physical Examination

  • Bibasal end-inspiratory fine crackles (Velcro rales): Most patients with IPF; may be the earliest sign
  • Digital clubbing: Common in IPF; less common in NSIP; rare in RB-ILD; very rare in sarcoidosis/HP
  • Wheezing: Uncommon in ILD — if present, think sarcoidosis, HP, eosinophilic GPA
  • Advanced disease: Cyanosis, digital clubbing, cor pulmonale

Chest Imaging — HRCT is Standard of Care

HRCT is the most important imaging study in ILD evaluation. It can be diagnostic in the right clinical context (e.g., UIP pattern in the right patient = IPF without biopsy).

UIP Pattern Features (= IPF signature)
  • Honeycombing — clustered cysts, basal and subpleural (the "classic" IPF finding)
  • Traction bronchiectasis — bronchi pulled open by surrounding fibrosis
  • Subpleural reticular markings — posterior, basal predominance
  • Absence of: extensive GGO, upper lobe predominance, micronodules, mosaic attenuation → if present, think of alternate diagnosis

Lung Biopsy

  • Bronchoscopy (BAL + transbronchial biopsy): Good for sarcoidosis, HP, eosinophilic pneumonia, DAH. Generally insufficient for most IIPs (including IPF).
  • Surgical lung biopsy (VATS): Gold standard when diagnosis unclear. Short-term mortality up to 5% — weigh risk vs benefit. Higher risk in IPF patients and those presenting acutely.
  • Cryobiopsy: Emerging technique; larger samples than transbronchial; role still being defined.
When Can You Skip Biopsy?
If HRCT shows a classic UIP pattern in the right clinical context (male, >60, smoker, bibasal crackles, no alternate explanation), a diagnosis of IPF can be made WITHOUT surgical biopsy. This is a major boards and clinical point.
03

Pulmonary Function Tests in ILD

The Classic Restrictive Pattern

Most forms of ILD produce a restrictive deficit in pulmonary function. This is the hallmark PFT finding across ILDs. Understanding this pattern is essential for both diagnosis and monitoring progression.

↓ TLC

Total Lung Capacity reduced. The defining feature of restriction. Fibrotic lungs are stiff — they cannot inflate fully.

↓ FVC

Forced Vital Capacity reduced. The most important parameter for monitoring IPF progression — a decline in FVC correlates with elevated mortality.

↓ FEV₁

Also reduced in proportion to FVC. The FEV₁/FVC ratio is preserved (≥70%) or even elevated — this is RESTRICTION, not obstruction.

↓ DLCO

Diffusing capacity for CO. Often the first parameter to fall, reflecting impaired gas exchange. May precede a reduction in lung volumes. Less specific than other measures.

Normal/↑ FEV₁/FVC

Preserved or elevated ratio — this distinguishes restriction from obstruction (COPD, asthma). Boards LOVE to test this distinction.

Normal/↓ RV

Residual volume is normal or slightly reduced in pure restriction. Very elevated RV = air trapping = obstruction (COPD).

PFTs — How to Read a Restrictive Pattern on Boards

The Boards PFT Question — IPF Pattern

Look for the option that shows: ↓FVC + ↓TLC + ↓DLCO + Normal or ↑ FEV₁/FVC ratio

In IPF specifically: FVC is reduced (e.g., 59–75%), FEV₁ reduced (e.g., 55–80%), FEV₁/FVC preserved or elevated (e.g., 68–91%), TLC reduced (e.g., 69–84%), DLCO significantly reduced (e.g., 42–65%).

Tricky Scenario — Emphysema + IPF
Some IPF patients are smokers and can have concurrent emphysema. In this case, the obstructive emphysema can "mask" the restrictive IPF pattern, resulting in a falsely normal or near-normal FVC — but the DLCO will still be very low. This combination is called "combined pulmonary fibrosis and emphysema" (CPFE).

Amiodarone ILD — PFT Key Point
Amiodarone causes interstitial lung disease with a restrictive pattern. The FEV₁/FVC ratio remains normal or preserved. The DLCO is reduced, and lung volumes are reduced. Do NOT expect an obstructive pattern from amiodarone lung toxicity.

PFTs as a Monitoring Tool

  • FVC decline is the most important predictor of mortality in IPF — a drop in FVC of ≥10% over 6–12 months significantly increases mortality risk
  • DLCO decline also correlates with worsening disease and may precede FVC changes
  • PFTs rarely diagnose a specific ILD but help characterize disease extent and guide decisions about antifibrotic therapy initiation
04

Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis (IPF)

High-Yield Summary
IPF = most common ILD of unknown cause. Older male, smoker, dry cough, clubbing, bibasal Velcro crackles, honeycombing + traction bronchiectasis on CT, restrictive PFTs with low DLCO. Treatment: antifibrotic therapy (Pirfenidone or Nintedanib). Steroids are NOT helpful — they are harmful.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Demographics: Most common in men >60 years; rare under 50; ~75% have history of smoking or environmental exposures
  • Prevalence: 50–200:100,000; increases with age
  • Onset: Chronic indolent course — months to years
  • Primary symptoms:
    • Progressive exertional dyspnea (hallmark complaint)
    • Persistent dry cough — often the most prominent symptom in IPF
    • No hemoptysis — this is rare in IPF. If hemoptysis present → think bronchiectasis, GPA, aspergilloma, or DAH
  • Physical exam:
    • Bibasal end-inspiratory Velcro crackles (most common finding)
    • Digital clubbing — common and characteristic
    • Advanced disease: cor pulmonale, cyanosis
Why No Hemoptysis in IPF?
IPF causes progressive fibrosis of the interstitium without vasculitis or vascular invasion. Hemoptysis in ILD should direct you toward granulomatous vasculitides (GPA, EGPA), diffuse alveolar hemorrhage, bronchiectasis (secondary to traction), or aspergilloma (in a cavity). IPF itself does NOT cause hemoptysis.

Pathology — UIP Pattern

The histopathologic pattern in IPF is called Usual Interstitial Pneumonia (UIP). Its key features are:

  • Temporal and spatial heterogeneity — areas of fibrosis alternate with areas of normal alveolar architecture (this "patchwork" pattern is pathognomonic for UIP)
  • Fibroblast foci — subepithelial collections of myofibroblasts and collagen (the active leading edge of fibrosis)
  • Honeycombing — cystic spaces lined by bronchiolar epithelium
  • Subpleural and basal predominance
UIP Is Not Unique to IPF
UIP pattern on biopsy can also occur in CTD (RA-ILD, scleroderma). UIP on CT in the right clinical context is enough to diagnose IPF without biopsy. UIP on biopsy without classic clinical context requires excluding CTD.

HRCT — Classic Findings of IPF (UIP Pattern)

  • Subpleural reticular markings — posterior, basal predominance (most important finding)
  • Honeycombing — clustered cystic airspaces (3–10 mm), basal and subpleural (confirms advanced UIP)
  • Traction bronchiectasis — airways pulled open by surrounding fibrosis
Findings That Should Make You Question IPF
If you see any of these on HRCT → think ALTERNATIVE diagnosis (not IPF):
  • Extensive ground-glass opacities (GGO) → more typical of NSIP or drug-induced
  • Upper or mid-lung predominance → think HP or sarcoidosis
  • Micronodules → think sarcoidosis, HP
  • Mosaic attenuation → small airways disease
  • Bronchovascular distribution → sarcoidosis
  • Subpleural sparing → NSIP (GGO sparing the subpleural area)

Prognosis

  • IPF carries a poor prognosis — median survival 3–5 years from diagnosis
  • 50% mortality at 3–5 years — this is the key survival number
  • IPF is near one of the most common ILDs AND has the worst prognosis among IIPs
  • Compare: NSIP has >80% 5-year survival; COP responds well to treatment

Treatment of IPF — Antifibrotic Therapy

2014 — A Landmark Year in IPF
Before 2014, IPF was considered refractory to all medical therapy. In 2014, two landmark clinical trials demonstrated that antifibrotic therapy can slow the decline in FVC and potentially improve survival. These drugs are now standard of care.
Pirfenidone

Mechanism

Pleiotropic antifibrotic agent. Inhibits TGF-β–mediated fibroblast proliferation and collagen production. Also has anti-inflammatory properties. Works on multiple pathways simultaneously.

Key Trials

  • CAPACITY trial (2011): Slowed FVC decline vs placebo
  • ASCEND trial (2014): Confirmed FVC slowing and reduced mortality trend

Key Points

  • Oral medication taken with food (reduces GI side effects)
  • Side effects: photosensitivity (counsel to use sunscreen), GI upset, liver enzyme elevation
  • Should be used alongside NAC (N-acetylcysteine) — NAC alone is not beneficial but was used as adjunct
Nintedanib

Mechanism

A tyrosine kinase inhibitor targeting VEGFR, PDGFR, and FGFR — three receptor tyrosine kinases critical for fibroblast activation and proliferation. More targeted than Pirfenidone.

Key Trials

  • INPULSIS trial (2014): Two phase III trials showing significant reduction in FVC decline
  • Meta-analyses suggest also reduces all-cause mortality

Key Points

  • Primary side effect: diarrhea (most common, dose-limiting in some patients)
  • Also approved for: systemic sclerosis-associated ILD and progressive pulmonary fibrosis
  • Does NOT reverse fibrosis — slows progression only
⚠ Immunosuppression in IPF — HARMFUL!
Historically, IPF patients were treated with steroids and immunosuppression (prednisone, azathioprine, N-acetylcysteine). A landmark trial (PANTHER-IPF, 2012) showed that the combination of prednisone + azathioprine + NAC was associated with INCREASED mortality and hospitalizations in IPF. Immunosuppression is now contraindicated in IPF.

Key concept: Steroids help COP, sarcoidosis, drug-induced ILD, and eosinophilic pneumonia — but they HARM IPF patients.

Additional Management in IPF

  • Physical therapy and supplemental oxygen: Improve exercise tolerance and reduce risk of pulmonary hypertension — helpful as supportive measures
  • Lung transplantation: Can extend survival and improve quality of life in a select subset of patients who meet transplant criteria. Last resort but a viable option. The only curative option historically before antifibrotic era.
  • Nintedanib reduces acute exacerbation rate — there is some evidence for this beneficial effect
  • GERD management: GERD may contribute to microaspiration and lung injury; proton pump inhibitors considered in IPF patients

What NOT to Give in IPF

Acute Exacerbations of IPF

  • Defined as: acute onset (<30 days) of respiratory deterioration in a patient with known IPF, not explained by infection, heart failure, or pulmonary embolism
  • Mortality is extremely high (>85%)
  • Histopathology shows diffuse alveolar damage (DAD) on top of UIP pattern
  • Treatment is supportive; steroids and cytotoxic agents are commonly used without proven benefit
  • Nintedanib may reduce the rate of acute exacerbations

Nonspecific Interstitial Pneumonia (NSIP)

Boards Summary
NSIP = younger nonsmoking female, often with CTD, GGO on CT with subpleural sparing, no honeycombing. MUCH better prognosis than IPF (>80% 5-year survival). Treatment: steroids + immunosuppression. CAN have progressive pulmonary fibrosis requiring antifibrotic.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Demographics: Typically nonsmoking females in their 5th decade (contrasts with IPF: older males, smokers)
  • Frequently associated with CTD (scleroderma, myositis, RA, Sjögren's) — look for CTD features in the history
  • Also occurs idiopathically, with familial ILD, drug toxicity, infection
  • Positive serologies for CTD are frequently found — always screen
  • Symptoms: progressive dyspnea, dry cough — subacute onset

HRCT Findings — Contrasts with IPF

  • Diffuse subpleural, symmetric GGO — ground-glass opacity dominates (not honeycombing)
  • Reticular pattern — bilateral
  • Subpleural sparing — a hallmark feature that helps distinguish from IPF (IPF has subpleural predominance; NSIP has subpleural sparing)
  • Traction bronchiectasis — present in fibrotic NSIP
  • Honeycombing — uncommon (if present, think IPF or CTD-ILD with UIP)
  • HRCT of NSIP is NOT diagnostic — it can overlap with UIP pattern
IPF vs NSIP on HRCT — The Key Distinction
  • IPF: Honeycombing + subpleural predominance + basal predominance = UIP (classic)
  • NSIP: GGO dominant + subpleural SPARING + lower lobes + NO honeycombing = NSIP
  • If you see GGO in a young woman with known CTD → NSIP until proven otherwise

Histopathology

  • Varying amounts of interstitial inflammation and fibrosis with a uniform appearance (in contrast to the temporal and spatial heterogeneity of UIP/IPF)
  • Honeycomb changes usually absent; fibroblast foci are rare
  • Cellular NSIP (predominantly inflammatory) → more responsive to therapy, better prognosis
  • Fibrotic NSIP (predominantly fibrotic) → less responsive to therapy, worse prognosis

Prognosis

Prognosis — Much Better Than IPF
  • 5-year survival: >80% (contrast with IPF: 50% at 3–5 years)
  • Cellular NSIP has a more favorable prognosis than fibrotic NSIP
  • NSIP associated with CTD has better prognosis than idiopathic NSIP
  • However: NSIP with progressive pulmonary fibrosis carries worse prognosis

Treatment

  • CTD-associated pulmonary fibrosis is commonly treated with immunosuppression (though randomized trials are limited)
  • Idiopathic NSIP treatment options:
    • Oral steroids (prednisone) — first line
    • Cytotoxic agents: Mycophenolate (MMF), azathioprine, cyclophosphamide
    • Biologics: Rituximab, tocilizumab
  • Recent trials: NSIP patients with progressive pulmonary fibrosis may benefit from antifibrotic therapy (Nintedanib)
  • Oxygen therapy, pulmonary rehabilitation, and lung transplantation may be needed in progressive disease

Cryptogenic Organizing Pneumonia (COP)

Boards Summary
COP = subacute flu-like illness (cough, fever, dyspnea), migratory patchy consolidations on CT, restrictive PFTs. RESPONDS VERY WELL to corticosteroids. Relapse rate is high → need ≥6 months of treatment. Confused with pneumonia clinically. NOT antifibrotic therapy.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Demographics: Typically 50–60s age group
  • Presentation: Subacute flulike illness — cough, dyspnea, fever, fatigue. The presence of fever is important — it's unusual in IPF or NSIP but common in COP.
  • Inspiratory rales on exam; restrictive pattern on PFTs with hypoxemia
  • Commonly mistaken for pneumonia — this is a major boards trap. Patient gets antibiotics repeatedly for "pneumonia" with no improvement → consider COP
  • Can occur: isolated (idiopathic), secondary to CTD, malignancy, medications, or following infection
COP Clinical Clue
A patient treated multiple times for "pneumonia" that keeps recurring, or consolidations that are MIGRATORY on serial imaging = strongly suggests COP. Normal antibiotics do not help. Steroids are the answer.

HRCT Findings

  • Patchy subpleural consolidative opacities — often bilateral, can be peribronchovascular
  • Ground-glass opacities (GGO) surrounding the consolidation
  • Migratory pattern — opacities that change location over time on serial imaging (very characteristic of COP)
  • Reversed halo sign (atoll sign) — a rim of consolidation surrounding a central area of GGO. Seen in COP and is helpful for diagnosis. Also called "reversed halo" or "atoll sign."
  • Peribronchiolar or perilobar opacities can also be present

Treatment — Corticosteroids

COP: The ILD That LOVES Steroids
  • Corticosteroids result in substantial clinical improvement in most patients with COP
  • Treatment must be continued for at least 6 months — relapse rate is high if stopped early
  • Relapse often occurs when steroids are tapered too quickly
  • For relapse or steroid-refractory COP: cytotoxic agents (mycophenolate, cyclophosphamide) or biologics (rituximab) can be helpful
What NOT to Do in COP
Do NOT give antifibrotic therapy (Pirfenidone or Nintedanib) for COP. Antifibrotics are specifically for IPF (and progressive pulmonary fibrosis). COP is inflammatory and responds to steroids — it does not need antifibrotic treatment.

Drug-Induced Interstitial Lung Disease

Why Drug-Induced ILD Matters on Boards
Many common medications cause ILD. The diagnosis requires a high index of suspicion + careful medication history. Treatment = stop the offending drug. May also need steroids depending on severity.

Major Drug Classes Causing ILD

Drug ClassDrug NamesNotes
AntiarrhythmicsAmiodaroneMost common antiarrhythmic to cause ILD. Restrictive pattern + reduced DLCO. Can cause pulmonary toxicity in 5–15% of patients. Accumulated drug in the lung. Phospholipidosis on pathology.
AntibioticsNitrofurantoinClassic antibiotic causing ILD (used for UTI prophylaxis). Acute or chronic forms. Ask about long-term UTI prevention!
ChemotherapyBleomycin, Cyclophosphamide, BusulfanBleomycin is the #1 boards answer for chemo-induced ILD. Dose-dependent pulmonary toxicity. Potentiated by supplemental oxygen!
DMARDs / ImmunomodulatorsMethotrexate, Azathioprine, LeflunomideMethotrexate is paradoxically used to treat RA (which causes ILD) but also causes ILD itself.
BiologicsRituximab, TNF-α blockers (infliximab, etanercept)TNF blockers and rituximab — used to treat inflammatory diseases — can also cause ILD. Rituximab more commonly associated with organizing pneumonia pattern.
AntiepilepticsPhenytoinLess common but recognized cause.
The Boards Mnemonic: "NAIB-TP" — Drug-Induced ILD
  • Nitrofurantoin
  • Amiodarone
  • Infliximab / biologics (TNF-α inhibitors)
  • Bleomycin (and other chemotherapy)
  • Tocilizumab / Rituximab (biologics)
  • Procainamide / Phenytoin (older drugs, less tested)
Remember: Nitrofurantoin, Amiodarone, Methotrexate, Bleomycin, Rituximab, TNF-α blockers

Amiodarone Pulmonary Toxicity — In Depth

Amiodarone is an antiarrhythmic drug (Class III) used for serious arrhythmias. It accumulates in the lung tissue and can cause progressive interstitial lung disease.

  • Incidence: 5–15% of patients on long-term amiodarone
  • Presentation: Progressive dyspnea, dry cough, weight loss, fever (may mimic infection or heart failure)
  • PFT Pattern: Restrictive pattern — ↓TLC, ↓FVC, normal/preserved FEV₁/FVC ratio, significantly ↓DLCO
  • HRCT: Bilateral ground-glass opacities, consolidation, and interstitial thickening. Pleural effusions possible.
  • Treatment: Discontinue amiodarone + corticosteroids if severe
Amiodarone PFT — Boards Key Point
Amiodarone-induced ILD causes a RESTRICTIVE PFT pattern. FEV₁/FVC will be NORMAL (not decreased). DLCO will be reduced. This is the same pattern as IPF — but the context (amiodarone use) tells you the cause. Do not be tricked into choosing an obstructive pattern for amiodarone lung toxicity.

Bleomycin Pulmonary Toxicity

  • Bleomycin (used in testicular cancer, lymphoma, germ cell tumors) is the most board-tested chemotherapy causing ILD
  • Mechanism: Generates reactive oxygen species → direct pulmonary endothelial injury → fibrosis
  • Risk factors: Cumulative dose, age, renal impairment, supplemental oxygen (FiO₂ >25% markedly potentiates bleomycin toxicity — critical surgical/anesthesia pearl)
  • Presentation: Progressive dyspnea and restrictive pattern on PFTs
  • Management: Stop bleomycin; steroids for severe cases

General Approach to Drug-Induced ILD

Progressive dyspnea + restrictive PFTs
→
Check COMPLETE medication history
↓
Identify offending drug (Amiodarone? Nitrofurantoin? Bleomycin? MTX?)
→
Stop the drug immediately
↓
Mild: may improve with drug discontinuation alone
→
Severe: add corticosteroids

CTD-Associated ILD & ILD Steroid Response

Which ILDs Respond to Steroids?

This is one of the highest-yield distinctions on boards. Understanding steroid response guides treatment decisions across all ILD types.

Good Steroid Response ✓
  • COP (Cryptogenic Organizing Pneumonia) — excellent response; may achieve complete remission
  • Eosinophilic pneumonia — dramatic, often rapid response
  • Sarcoidosis — steroids are primary treatment for symptomatic/progressive sarcoidosis
  • Drug-induced ILD — steroids help after stopping the offending drug
  • Hypersensitivity pneumonitis — steroids speed recovery
  • AIP (Acute Interstitial Pneumonia) — commonly given, though not clearly proven effective
  • CTD-associated NSIP — partial response
Poor/Harmful Response ✗
  • IPF (Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis) — steroids are HARMFUL. Increased morbidity and mortality (PANTHER-IPF trial). This is the most important "steroid doesn't work" in ILD.
  • RB-ILD / DIP — smoking cessation is the treatment; steroids have no strong evidence
The #1 Boards Trap: Steroids in IPF
A question describes IPF features (older male, bibasal crackles, honeycombing on CT, restrictive PFTs) and asks for treatment. Never choose steroids/prednisone for IPF. Always choose antifibrotic therapy (Pirfenidone or Nintedanib). Steroids worsen IPF.

CTD-Associated ILD — Overview

ILD is a common manifestation of many CTDs. The most common CTDs causing ILD are:

  • Systemic sclerosis (SSc/scleroderma) — most common CTD with ILD (~50% of diffuse SSc patients)
  • Rheumatoid arthritis — RA-ILD more common in men; UIP pattern most common
  • Polymyositis/Dermatomyositis — NSIP or COP patterns; anti-synthetase antibodies
  • Less common: Sjögren's syndrome, SLE

Systemic Sclerosis ILD

  • ILD occurs in ~50% of SSc patients with diffuse disease, ~30% with limited disease
  • HRCT: NSIP pattern most common; UIP pattern also seen; also dilated esophagus (characteristic SSc feature)
  • Treatment: Cyclophosphamide (modest benefit), Mycophenolate (similar efficacy, better tolerability), Tocilizumab (anti-IL-6), Nintedanib (approved for SSc-ILD)

RA-Associated ILD

  • Clinically evident in ~10% of RA patients; up to 40–50% have radiologic abnormalities on CT
  • More common in males with RA (despite RA being more common in females) — tested frequently
  • UIP pattern is most common histopathologic pattern
  • Treatment: Immunosuppression (prednisone, mycophenolate, azathioprine). Antifibrotic therapy may slow FVC decline in RA-ILD with progressive pulmonary fibrosis

Progressive Pulmonary Fibrosis (PPF)

A newer concept that encompasses various ILDs (beyond IPF) that show progressive fibrosis despite treatment:

  • Includes: NSIP, sarcoidosis, CTD-ILD, HP when they show progressive FVC decline
  • Nintedanib has been approved for progressive pulmonary fibrosis (INBUILD trial) — a major expansion of its indication beyond IPF

Hemoptysis in ILD — What Does and Doesn't Cause It

ILD WITH Hemoptysis (rare but tested)
  • Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (GPA, formerly Wegener's) — vasculitis + DAH
  • Diffuse Alveolar Hemorrhage (DAH) — Goodpasture's, Wegener's, SLE, anti-GBM disease
  • Aspergilloma — fungal ball in pre-existing cavity can bleed massively
  • Bronchiectasis (traction) — secondary to fibrosis, vessels near dilated airways
  • LAM — can have hemoptysis via cystic rupture
ILD WITHOUT Hemoptysis
  • IPF — No hemoptysis. IPF is purely fibrotic, not vasculitic. If hemoptysis occurs in IPF, look for secondary cause (superinfection, aspergilloma in cavity, lung cancer).
  • NSIP — No hemoptysis
  • COP — No hemoptysis
  • Drug-induced ILD — No hemoptysis

Antifibrotic Drug Quick Reference

Pirfenidone
  • Mechanism: Pleiotropic antifibrotic; inhibits TGF-β, anti-inflammatory
  • Indication: IPF
  • Key trial: CAPACITY + ASCEND (2014)
  • Outcome: Slows FVC decline; reduces mortality trend
  • Side effects: Photosensitivity, GI upset, LFT elevation
  • Dosing: With food (reduces GI side effects)
Nintedanib
  • Mechanism: Tyrosine kinase inhibitor (VEGFR, PDGFR, FGFR)
  • Indication: IPF; SSc-ILD; Progressive Pulmonary Fibrosis
  • Key trial: INPULSIS (2014); INBUILD (PPF); SENSCIS (SSc-ILD)
  • Outcome: Slows FVC decline; may improve survival; reduces acute exacerbation rate
  • Side effects: Diarrhea (most common), nausea, LFT elevation
  • Important: Does not REVERSE fibrosis — only slows progression

Exam Traps

Trap 01IPF + Steroids — The Classic Mistake
A 66-year-old male with progressive dry cough, digital clubbing, bibasal Velcro crackles, and CT showing honeycombing with traction bronchiectasis in the lower lobes. PFTs show restrictive pattern with low DLCO. What is the best treatment?

Options: (A) Prednisone, (B) Nintedanib, (C) Pirfenidone + Prednisone, (D) Azathioprine
Answer: Nintedanib (or Pirfenidone) — antifibrotic therapy only. This is classic IPF. Steroids, azathioprine, and combination immunosuppression are CONTRAINDICATED — they increase morbidity and mortality in IPF (proven in PANTHER-IPF trial). Antifibrotic monotherapy is the standard of care.
Trap 02FEV₁/FVC in ILD — Restriction vs Obstruction
A 75-year-old with IPF has: FVC 59%, FEV₁ 58%, FEV₁/FVC 91%, TLC 69%, DLCO 42%. Which PFT pattern does this represent?

Options: (A) Obstructive, (B) Restrictive, (C) Mixed, (D) Normal
Answer: Restrictive. The FEV₁/FVC ratio is 91% — this is ELEVATED (not decreased), which means NO obstruction. Both FVC and FEV₁ are reduced proportionally. TLC is reduced. DLCO is markedly reduced. This is the classic restrictive pattern of IPF. The key: normal or elevated FEV₁/FVC = restriction, not obstruction.
Trap 03Drug-Induced ILD — Which Drug Is the Culprit?
A question asks which medication is most likely to cause interstitial lung disease. Options include: Doxycycline, Bleomycin, Vancomycin, Lisinopril.
Answer: Bleomycin. Bleomycin is the classic chemotherapy drug that causes pulmonary fibrosis/ILD. It is dose-dependent and potentiated by supplemental oxygen. Lisinopril causes cough but not ILD. Doxycycline and vancomycin do not cause ILD. Key drug-ILD associations: Bleomycin → chemotherapy ILD; Amiodarone → antiarrhythmic ILD; Nitrofurantoin → antibiotic ILD.
Trap 04NSIP vs IPF — The Key Imaging Distinction
A 64-year-old female café owner with 10 years of progressive cough. CT chest shows bilateral ground-glass opacities in the lower lobes with subpleural sparing. No honeycombing. Serologies positive for anti-CCP. What is the most likely diagnosis?

Options: IPF, NSIP, COP, Sarcoidosis
Answer: NSIP (nonspecific interstitial pneumonia). The combination of: young-ish female + CTD serologies (anti-CCP = RA) + GGO with subpleural sparing + NO honeycombing = NSIP. IPF would show honeycombing and subpleural PREDOMINANCE (not sparing). COP would show patchy consolidation. Sarcoidosis would show hilar adenopathy + upper lobe nodules.
Trap 05COP Mistaken for Pneumonia
A 55-year-old female has been treated 3 times in the past year for "pneumonia" with antibiotics, with partial improvement each time. CT shows bilateral subpleural consolidation with ground-glass opacity. What is the next best management step?

Options: (A) IV antibiotics, (B) Antifungal, (C) Corticosteroids, (D) Antifibrotic therapy
Answer: Corticosteroids. Recurrent/non-resolving "pneumonia" + migratory consolidations = COP (Cryptogenic Organizing Pneumonia) until proven otherwise. COP responds dramatically to corticosteroids (need at least 6 months to prevent relapse). Antibiotics won't help. Antifibrotic therapy is for IPF — NOT COP.
Trap 06Nintedanib vs Riociguat — Look-Alike Trap
Which drug has been shown in clinical trials to slow the rate of FVC decline in patients with Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis?

Options: Nintedanib, Rituximab, Mycophenolate mofetil, Riociguat
Answer: Nintedanib. Rituximab has no role in IPF treatment. MMF has no proven benefit in IPF. Riociguat (a soluble guanylate cyclase stimulator) was actually studied in IPF and the trial was STOPPED EARLY due to harm signals — it is approved for PAH and CTEPH but NOT for IPF. Only Nintedanib and Pirfenidone are approved antifibrotics for IPF.
Trap 07Prognosis Comparison — NSIP vs IPF
A 54-year-old woman (no CTD history) is told she has NSIP based on lung biopsy. What is the correct prognosis to communicate?

Options: (A) >80% 5-year survival, (B) Similar prognosis to IPF (50% at 3–5 years), (C) Self-limiting illness resolving in 6 months, (D) Better prognosis if fibrotic pattern
Answer: >80% 5-year survival (Cellular pattern gives even better prognosis). NSIP has a much better prognosis than IPF. IPF has 50% 3–5 year mortality. Fibrotic NSIP is WORSE than cellular NSIP (opposite of option D). NSIP does not self-limit — it requires treatment. This is a high-yield prognosis distinction: IPF = poor; NSIP = good.
Trap 08Hemoptysis in ILD — IPF Does NOT Cause It
A 73-year-old man with longstanding IPF presents with acute hemoptysis. He coughs up blood-tinged sputum. Which of the following correctly explains the hemoptysis?

Options: (A) It is a typical symptom of IPF, (B) It is due to a complication (aspergilloma or lung cancer), (C) It confirms acute exacerbation of IPF, (D) It is from traction bronchiectasis naturally
Answer: Complication — aspergilloma or lung cancer. IPF itself does NOT cause hemoptysis. IPF causes dry cough. When hemoptysis occurs in IPF, always look for a COMPLICATION — aspergilloma in honeycombing cavities, or lung cancer (IPF patients have higher lung cancer risk). Acute exacerbation of IPF causes worsening dyspnea and respiratory failure, not hemoptysis.
Trap 09Amiodarone PFT — Normal FEV₁/FVC Is Key
A 67-year-old woman on chronic amiodarone develops progressive dyspnea. CXR shows bilateral infiltrates. PFTs are done — what pattern do you expect?

Options: (A) FEV₁/FVC decreased, (B) FEV₁/FVC normal with low DLCO, (C) Increased TLC with low DLCO, (D) Normal PFTs with elevated RV
Answer: FEV₁/FVC normal (or preserved) with low DLCO. Amiodarone causes interstitial lung disease → RESTRICTIVE pattern. Restrictive = low FVC, low FEV₁, but NORMAL or elevated FEV₁/FVC ratio. DLCO is reduced. TLC is reduced (not elevated). Elevated TLC + low DLCO suggests emphysema, not drug-induced ILD.
Trap 10Pirfenidone in UIP Pattern — Even Without Classic Presentation
A 73-year-old male with dry cough and progressive dyspnea. FVC 75%, FEV₁ 80%, DLCO 65%. CT scan shows bilateral subpleural reticular markings, traction bronchiectasis, and honeycombing consistent with UIP pattern. Spirometry available today. What is the most appropriate treatment?

Options: (A) Pirfenidone, (B) Azathioprine + Prednisone, (C) Levofloxacin, (D) Mepolizumab
Answer: Pirfenidone. Classic UIP pattern on CT in an older male + restrictive PFTs = IPF. The antifibrotic treatment of choice is Pirfenidone or Nintedanib. Azathioprine + Prednisone is CONTRAINDICATED in IPF (PANTHER trial). Levofloxacin has no role. Mepolizumab (anti-IL-5) is for eosinophilic conditions (EGPA, eosinophilic asthma), not IPF.
