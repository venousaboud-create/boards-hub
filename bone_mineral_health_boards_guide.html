Bone Structure & Metabolism

Types of Bone

Cortical (Compact) Bone
  • Forms the cylindrical shell of long bones
  • Organized in concentric sheets = lamellar bone
  • Contains Haversian systems (osteons) — lamellae around blood vessels
  • BMUs tunnel through it during remodeling
Cancellous (Trabecular) Bone
  • Plate-like meshwork that internally supports cortical shell
  • ~4% surface involved in active resorption at any time
  • 10–15% surfaces covered with osteoid (new unmineralized bone)
  • BMUs move across trabecular surfaces

Bone Composition

  • Mineral phase: Calcium + phosphate → poorly crystalline hydroxyapatite; deposited in the "holes" between collagen fibrils
  • Organic matrix: 90–95% is Type I collagen; also serum proteins (albumin), cell attachment proteins (thrombospondin, osteopontin, fibronectin), calcium-binding proteins (osteocalcin, matrix gla protein), proteoglycans (biglycan, decorin)
  • Mineralization: Requires osteoblast-derived alkaline phosphatase, which hydrolyzes inhibitors of mineralization (e.g., pyrophosphate)
  • Primary mineralization: Begins soon after matrix secretion; secondary mineralization: not complete for several weeks
⚠ Osteogenesis Imperfecta
Single amino acid substitutions in α1 (COL1A1) or α2 (COL1A2) chains of type I collagen → disrupt bone organization → severe skeletal fragility. Highlights the critical structural role of the fibrillar matrix.

Bone Cells

Osteoblasts
  • Mesenchymal origin (periosteum, bone marrow, hypertrophic chondrocytes)
  • Synthesize & secrete organic matrix
  • Regulate mineralization
  • Key markers: alkaline phosphatase, osteocalcin, procollagen I
  • Fate: become osteocyte, lining cell, or die
Osteoclasts
  • Hematopoietic lineage (common precursor with macrophages)
  • Multinucleated; form in Howship's lacunae
  • Secrete protons + cathepsin K → acidify and digest matrix
  • Ruffled border: specialized proton pump ATPase
  • Attach via αvβ3 integrin
  • Marker: collagen hydroxypyridinium cross-links (urine/blood)
Osteocytes
  • Vast majority of bone cells
  • Embedded in matrix; communicate via canalicular network
  • Mechanosensors → transmit signals to osteoblasts & osteoclasts
  • Secrete sclerostin (Wnt inhibitor → suppress bone formation)
  • Secrete FGF23 (phosphate regulator)
  • Also perform osteocytic osteolysis (stimulated by PTH/PTHrP)

  • PTH + 1,25(OH)₂D: Activate receptors on osteoblasts → regulate mineral homeostasis

Osteoclast Regulation — RANK/RANKL/OPG Axis

🔑 Key Mechanism
RANK ligand (RANKL) — expressed on surface of osteocytes, osteoblasts, stromal fibroblasts — binds RANK receptor on osteoclast progenitors → stimulates osteoclast differentiation & activation.

Osteoprotegerin (OPG) — soluble decoy receptor — competes with RANK, binds RANKL → inhibits osteoclast differentiation (acts as a brake).

M-CSF (macrophage colony-stimulating factor) — critical at multiple steps → leads to fusion of osteoclast progenitors → multinucleated active osteoclasts.
  • PTH & 1,25(OH)₂D: Increase M-CSF and RANKL on osteoblast-lineage cells → indirectly ↑ osteoclast number and activity
  • Calcitonin: Binds directly to osteoclasts (basal surface) → directly inhibits osteoclast function
  • Estradiol: Targets osteoclasts, immune cells, osteoblasts → decreases osteoclast number and bone resorption
  • Cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, IL-11, TNF, IFN-γ) also modulate osteoclast differentiation and function

  • Cortical BMU: tunnels through tissue; Trabecular BMU: moves across surface

  • Remodeling follows lines of force from mechanical stress — sensed by osteocytes
  • Mechanical forces suppress sclerostin → ↑ Wnt → ↑ bone formation

🔑 Albumin Correction Formula
Corrected Ca (mg/dL) = measured Ca + 0.8 × (4 − serum albumin g/dL)
Also: +0.5 × deficit in immunoglobulin (g/dL)
Warning: These are rough approximations — measure ionized Ca directly in acute illness. Acidosis ↑ ionized Ca (reduces protein binding).

Forms of Serum Calcium

  • ~50% ionized (free, biologically active)
  • ~40% protein-bound — predominantly to albumin and immunoglobulins
  • ~10% complexed — loosely bound to phosphate, citrate, sulfate

Serum protein changes alter total calcium WITHOUT changing ionized calcium — this is clinically important (e.g., hypoalbuminemia gives falsely low total Ca).

Hormonal Regulation — PTH & Vitamin D Loop

↓ Ionized Ca
→
CaSR on parathyroid
→
↑ PTH secretion
→
↑ Ca from bone + kidney
  • CaSR (calcium-sensing receptor): On parathyroid cells — high Ca suppresses PTH by reducing PTH mRNA, promoting PTH cleavage, suppressing granule release
  • PTH → kidney: Stimulates 1α-hydroxylase → ↑ 1,25(OH)₂D synthesis
  • 1,25(OH)₂D: Inhibits PTH production (negative feedback); ↑ intestinal Ca absorption; ↑ renal Ca reabsorption

Intestinal Calcium Absorption

  • Recommended daily intake: 1000–1200 mg/day (adults, National Academy of Medicine)
  • Active (transcellular): 20–70% of dietary calcium; via TRPV6 channels in intestine (TRPV5 in kidney distal tubule); regulated by 1,25(OH)₂D; mainly in proximal small bowel (duodenum & proximal jejunum)
  • Passive (paracellular): ~5% of daily intake; nonsaturable; not regulated
  • Requires gastric acid — especially calcium carbonate; use calcium citrate in achlorhydrics, those on PPIs, or post-bariatric surgery
  • At high calcium intake: ↓ 1,25(OH)₂D synthesis → ↓ active absorption (adaptive mechanism)
  • Obligate intestinal secretion: ~2.5–5 mmol/d (not regulated)

Very high Ca (>100 mmol/d = >4 g/d): Passive absorption continues despite maximal downregulation → severe hypercalciuria, nephrocalcinosis, renal failure, hypercalcemia = "milk-alkali syndrome"

Phosphorus Metabolism

Key Facts

Distribution
  • 85% in bone mineral
  • Major intracellular constituent
  • Component of ATP, creatine-PO₄, nucleic acids, structural proteins
  • Unlike Ca: intracellular ≈ ECF (1–2 mmol/L)
Normal Levels
  • Adults: 0.75–1.45 mmol/L (2.5–4.5 mg/dL)
  • Children/neonates: up to 2.4 mmol/L (7 mg/dL)
  • Varies by 50% throughout the day
  • Nadir: 7–10 AM
  • Best measured: fasting, basal state
Forms in ECF
  • Mostly H₂PO₄⁻ or NaHPO₄⁻
  • ~10% as HPO₄²⁻
  • ~12% protein-bound in serum
  • Referred to collectively as "phosphate"

Intestinal Phosphate Absorption

  • Phosphate is abundant in foods — 65% absorbed even without vitamin D
  • With 1,25(OH)₂D activation: up to 85–90% absorbed (activates Na⁺/PO₄²⁻ co-transporter Npt2b)
  • Daily net intestinal absorption: 500–1000 mg/d
  • Inhibited by: large calcium salt doses, sevelamer (Renagel), aluminum hydroxide antacids

Renal Phosphate Regulation — FGF23 & PTH

  • Proximal tubule is the primary site of regulated reabsorption via apical NaPi-2a and NaPi-2c co-transporters
  • Filtered load: 4–6 g/d; fractional excretion: 10–15%
  • PTH: Rapidly reduces NaPi-2a/2c levels at apical surface → ↑ phosphaturia
  • FGF23 (synthesized by osteocytes): also inhibits NaPi-2a/2c → ↑ phosphaturia; additionally reduces 1,25(OH)₂D synthesis (↓ 1α-hydroxylase) → worsens hypophosphatemia; induced by 24-hydroxylase → ↓ circulating 1,25(OH)₂D
  • High-phosphate diets → ↑ FGF23; low-phosphate → ↓ FGF23
  • Serum phosphate and 1,25(OH)₂D elevation → ↑ FGF23 (feedback loop)
  • Dietary phosphate restriction → dramatic ↓ urinary phosphate within hours (precedes serum change) — partially via FGF23 changes
FGF23 Pathophysiology
↑ FGF23 effects: ↓ proximal tubule phosphate reabsorption + ↓ 1,25(OH)₂D synthesis → hypophosphatemia + osteomalacia
↓ FGF23 (or resistance): ↑ phosphate reabsorption + ↑ 1,25(OH)₂D → hyperphosphatemia + hypercalcemia = Tumoral Calcinosis

Hypophosphatemia

Three Primary Mechanisms of Hypophosphatemia

1. Reduced Renal Reabsorption
  • PTH/PTHrP-dependent: primary/secondary hyperPTH, PTHrP-mediated hypercalcemia of malignancy, familial hypocalciuric hypercalcemia
  • PTH/PTHrP-independent: excess FGF23 (XLH, ADHR, ARHP, TIO, McCune-Albright, epidermal nevus), Fanconi syndrome, cystinosis, Wilson's disease, NaPi-2a/2c mutations
  • Drugs: ethanol, acetazolamide, cisplatin, ifosfamide, high-dose estrogens/glucocorticoids, heavy metals
2. Impaired Intestinal Absorption
  • Aluminum-containing antacids (less common now)
  • Sevelamer (Renagel)
  • Note: Phosphate is so abundant in food that dietary deficiency alone is virtually never the cause
3. ECF → Intracellular Shift
  • IV glucose / insulin therapy (DKA treatment — paradigm case)
  • Catecholamines (epinephrine, dopamine, albuterol)
  • Acute respiratory alkalosis
  • Gram-negative sepsis / toxic shock
  • Recovery from starvation or acidosis
  • Rapid cellular proliferation (leukemic blast crisis, erythropoietin therapy)
⚠ Hungry Bone Syndrome
Transient hypophosphatemia over weeks-to-months after parathyroidectomy for severe primary hyperPTH, or treatment of vitamin D deficiency / lytic Paget's disease. Most prominent in patients with very high preoperative bone turnover (↑ alkaline phosphatase). Osteoblastic metastases can also cause this.

Clinical Manifestations

⚡ Pathophysiology: ATP Depletion + Oxidative Phosphorylation Shift
All manifestations reflect generalized cellular energy deficiency: ATP depletion + shift from oxidative phosphorylation toward glycolysis.

Neuromuscular (severe, <0.25 mmol/L = <0.8 mg/dL for seizures/paralysis)

  • Muscle weakness, lethargy, confusion
  • Dysarthria, dysphagia, oculomotor palsies, nystagmus
  • Ataxia, hyporeflexia, impaired sphincter control
  • Paresthesia
  • Guillain-Barré–like ascending paralysis
  • Seizures, coma, death
  • Rhabdomyolysis: CPK peaks 1–2 days AFTER phosphate nadir; up to 30% of acute hypoPO₄ patients

Cardiopulmonary (0.5–0.8 mmol/L = 1.5–2.5 mg/dL)

  • Respiratory failure (reversible with treatment)
  • Cardiac dysfunction (reversible)

Hematologic (↓ intracellular ATP + ↓ 2,3-DPG)

  • Erythrocyte microspherocytosis + hemolysis
  • Impaired oxyhemoglobin dissociation
  • Defective leukocyte chemotaxis, phagocytosis, bacterial killing
  • Platelet dysfunction

Chronic

  • Bone pain, osteomalacia, pseudofractures
  • Proximal muscle weakness
  • Rickets + short stature (children)

Treatment of Hypophosphatemia

🚨 Indication for IV Therapy
Severe hypophosphatemia (<0.75 mmol/L = <2 mg/dL) + underlying phosphate depletion = dangerous → correct promptly. Avoid Ca × PO₄ product >50 mg²/dL² (risk of heterotopic calcification). Correct hypocalcemia FIRST before giving IV phosphate.

Hyperphosphatemia

Definition & Causes

Definition
Fasting serum PO₄ >1.8 mmol/L (5.5 mg/dL) in adults. Higher in children/neonates (up to 2.4 mmol/L / 7 mg/dL).

Impaired Renal Phosphate Excretion

  • Renal insufficiency — most common cause (↓ GFR → phosphate retention)
  • Hypoparathyroidism (↑ NaPi-2 expression in proximal tubule): developmental, autoimmune, post-surgical/radiation, activating CaSR mutations
  • Parathyroid suppression: PTH-independent hypercalcemia (vitamin D or A intoxication, sarcoidosis, immobilization, milk-alkali), severe hyper/hypomagnesemia, pseudohypoparathyroidism
  • Acromegaly, heparin therapy
  • Tumoral calcinosis: low active FGF23 (FGF23 gene mutations, GALNT3 inactivation, or Klotho mutations → FGF23 resistance)

Massive ECF Phosphate Loads

  • Overzealous IV phosphate therapy
  • Oral/rectal phosphate-containing laxatives or enemas (especially children)
  • Extensive soft tissue injury/necrosis: crush injuries, rhabdomyolysis, hyperthermia, fulminant hepatitis, cytotoxic chemotherapy
  • Severe hemolytic anemia
  • Transcellular shifts: metabolic acidosis, respiratory acidosis
Hyperphosphatemia in CKD — Vicious Cycle
↓ GFR → phosphate retention → ↑ FGF23 + ↓ 1,25(OH)₂D synthesis + ↓ ionized Ca → ↑ PTH secretion → parathyroid hypertrophy → secondary hyperparathyroidism. Hyperphosphatemia is thus a major driver of renal osteodystrophy.

Clinical Findings

Due mainly to calcium-phosphate precipitation → widespread soft tissue calcification → hypocalcemia:

  • Tetany, seizures
  • Accelerated nephrocalcinosis → renal failure, hyperkalemia, hyperuricemia, metabolic acidosis
  • Pulmonary or cardiac calcifications → acute heart block
  • PO₄ can reach 7 mmol/L (20 mg/dL) in massive soft tissue injury / tumor lysis syndrome
Tumoral Calcinosis
Elevated serum 1,25(OH)₂D, parathyroid suppression, increased intestinal Ca absorption, focal hyperostosis with large lobulated periarticular heterotopic ossifications (especially shoulders/hips). Caused by loss of active FGF23 (GALNT3 mutations, FGF23 gene mutations, or Klotho resistance mutations).

Treatment of Hyperphosphatemia

  • Volume expansion: Enhances renal phosphate clearance
  • Aluminum hydroxide antacids or sevelamer: Chelate and limit intestinal phosphate absorption
  • Hemodialysis: Most effective — consider early in severe hyperphosphatemia, especially with renal failure + symptomatic hypocalcemia

Magnesium Metabolism

Key Facts

Normal Serum Mg

0.7–1 mmol/L
(1.5–2 meq/L; 1.7–2.4 mg/dL)
30% protein-bound; 15% complexed

Total Body Mg

25 g (1000 mmol)
50% in bone (half insoluble in mineral)
Almost all extraskeletal Mg is intracellular

Function

Major intracellular divalent cation. Key ATP complex. Cofactor for enzymes, transporters, nucleic acids. Critical for neuromuscular activity.

Warning: Only 1% of body Mg is in ECF → serum Mg levels may NOT accurately reflect total body stores.

Hypomagnesemia — Causes

Impaired Absorption / Increased Losses

  • Genetic: TRPM6 mutations (primary infantile hypomagnesemia type 1); with secondary hypocalcemia
  • Malabsorption syndromes + vitamin D deficiency
  • Protracted vomiting/diarrhea; intestinal drainage, fistulas
  • Proton pump inhibitors (omeprazole etc.) — mechanism unknown, no renal wasting

Drugs & Toxins (Renal Wasting)

  • Ethanol (directly impairs tubular Mg reabsorption)
  • Loop diuretics, thiazides, osmotic diuretics
  • Cisplatin, cyclosporine, aminoglycosides, amphotericin B
  • Pentamidine, foscarnet
  • Cetuximab (anti-EGFR antibody → ↓ TRPM6 expression in DCT)
  • Persistent glycosuria (osmotic diuresis in DM)

Genetic Renal Wasting Syndromes

  • Gitelman's syndrome: DCT NaCl co-transporter (SLC12A3) inactivation
  • Bartter's syndrome: cTAL Na-K-2Cl transport proteins
  • Claudin 16 or 19 mutations: Autosomal recessive; hypomagnesemia + hypercalciuria
  • DCT K⁺ channels (Kv1.1, Kir4.1) mutations
  • Na⁺,K⁺-ATPase γ-subunit (FXYD2) mutations
  • Activating CaSR mutations → hypoMg + hypocalcemia

Rapid Shifts from ECF

  • Recovery from DKA, refeeding syndrome, correction of respiratory acidosis, catecholamines
  • Rapid bone formation: post-parathyroidectomy, treatment of vitamin D deficiency, osteoblastic metastases
  • Pancreatitis, burns, excessive sweating
  • Pregnancy (third trimester) and lactation

Hypomagnesemia — Clinical Findings

  • Usually asymptomatic when Mg >0.5 mmol/L (1 meq/L; 1.2 mg/dL)
  • Neuromuscular: tetany, seizures, muscle weakness, ataxia, nystagmus, vertigo, depression, irritability, psychosis
  • Cardiac arrhythmias: sinus tachycardia, supraventricular tachycardias, ventricular arrhythmias
  • ECG: prolonged PR/QT, T-wave flattening/inversion, ST straightening; ↑ sensitivity to digitalis toxicity
  • Associated electrolyte abnormalities (resistant to correction without Mg): hypocalcemia (with hypocalciuria) + hypokalemia
Why HypoCa occurs in HypoMg
Very low serum Mg (<0.4 mmol/L = <0.8 meq/L = <1 mg/dL) → defects in PTH secretion + impaired 1,25(OH)₂D synthesis + cellular resistance to PTH → all reversible with Mg treatment.

Treatment of Hypomagnesemia

  • Mild/asymptomatic: Oral Mg salts (MgCl₂, MgO, Mg[OH]₂) in divided doses totaling 20–30 mmol/d (40–60 meq/d). Diarrhea may occur with larger doses.
  • Severe: IV MgCl₂ — continuous infusion of up to 50 mmol/d (100 meq Mg²⁺/d) if GFR normal. Reduce 50–75% if GFR reduced
  • Avoid IM MgSO₄ — painful, provides little Mg (2 mL of 50% MgSO₄ = only 4 mmol)
  • MgSO₄ IV acceptable but sulfate anions may bind Ca in serum/urine → worsen hypocalcemia
  • Monitor serum Mg every 12–24 hours during therapy (therapy may continue days — impaired renal conservation retains only 50–70% of daily IV dose; intracellular deficits may be 1–1.5 mmol/kg)
  • Supplement Ca, K, PO₄ as needed. Treat coexistent vitamin D deficiency (use vitamin D or 25(OH)D, NOT 1,25(OH)₂D — may impair tubular Mg reabsorption via PTH suppression)
⚠ Pitfall: Severe HypoMg + HypoCa + HypoPO₄
IV Mg alone → may worsen hypophosphatemia → provoke neuromuscular symptoms or rhabdomyolysis (rapid stimulation of previously suppressed PTH secretion). Administer BOTH calcium and magnesium together.

Hypermagnesemia

Causes
Rare without renal insufficiency (normal kidneys excrete 250 mmol/d). Causes: excessive Mg intake (cathartics/enemas/laxatives with GI ileus, parenteral Mg), rapid soft tissue mobilization (trauma, shock, sepsis, cardiac arrest, burns), renal failure, FHH (CaSR mutations → excessive cTAL reabsorption), adrenal insufficiency, hypothyroidism, hypothermia. Also: Mg infusion in eclampsia → hypocalcemia.
Clinical Findings by Level
>2 mmol/L: Vasodilation, neuromuscular blockade, refractory hypotension
Progressive: Nausea, lethargy, weakness → respiratory failure, paralysis, coma
>4 mmol/L: Hypoactive tendon reflexes, GI hypomotility/ileus, facial flushing, pupillary dilation, paradoxical bradycardia, prolonged PR/QRS/QT, heart block
~10 mmol/L: Asystole
Also: Hypocalcemia + hypercalciuria (CaSR activation → parathyroid suppression + ↓ cTAL Ca reabsorption)

Treatment

  • Identify and interrupt source of Mg
  • Magnesium-free cathartics/enemas to clear intestinal Mg
  • Vigorous IV hydration
  • Hemodialysis — effective; required in renal insufficiency
  • IV calcium 100–200 mg over 1–2 h → temporary improvement (antagonizes neuromuscular effects)

Synthesis & Activation Pathway

Step 1 — Skin (UV-dependent)

  • UV radiation → photochemical cleavage of 7-dehydrocholesterol → Vitamin D
  • Cutaneous production ↓ by: melanin, high-SPF sunblocks
  • D₂ (plant sources) = D₃ (animal sources) — equivalent biologic activity
  • Enters circulation bound to vitamin D–binding protein (α-globulin, synthesized in liver)

Step 2 — Liver: 25-Hydroxylation

  • Cytochrome P450 oxidase (mitochondria + microsomes)
  • Not tightly regulated → activity reflects vitamin D intake/production
  • 25(OH)D = major circulating & storage form
  • 88% bound to binding protein; 0.03% free; rest bound to albumin
  • Half-life: 2–3 weeks (25(OH)D₂ shorter than D₃)
  • Half-life ↓ with ↓ binding protein (e.g., nephrotic syndrome → ↑ urinary losses)

Step 3 — Kidney: 1α-Hydroxylation (the critical regulated step)

  • 25-hydroxyvitamin D-1α-hydroxylase (CYP27B1) — in proximal convoluted tubule cells
  • Inducers: PTH, hypophosphatemia
  • Repressors: calcium, FGF23, the product itself (1,25(OH)₂D)
  • Also present in: macrophages of granulomas/lymphomas (induced by IFN-γ and TNF-α, NOT regulated by Ca or 1,25(OH)₂D) → hypercalcemia in sarcoidosis

Inactivation — 24-Hydroxylation

  • Vitamin D 24-hydroxylase — expressed in most tissues
  • Induced by: 1,25(OH)₂D (promotes its own inactivation) and FGF23
  • CYP24A1 mutations → infantile hypercalcemia; in less severe: hypercalciuria, nephrocalcinosis, nephrolithiasis

Actions of 1,25(OH)₂D (via VDR)

Mechanism
1,25(OH)₂D binds VDR (vitamin D receptor) — nuclear receptor superfamily. VDR binds DNA as heterodimer with retinoid X receptor → recruits coactivators → alters chromatin → induces/represses target genes. VDR affinity for 1,25(OH)₂D is ~1000× greater than for other metabolites.

Intestine (main site)

  • ↑ Calbindin 9K (Ca-binding protein → active Ca transport in enterocytes)
  • ↑ TRPV5 and TRPV6 (Ca transporters)
  • ↑ Efficiency of intestinal calcium absorption
  • ↑ Npt2b → ↑ phosphate absorption

Bone

  • ↑ Osteocalcin and osteopontin (bone matrix proteins)
  • ↓ Type I collagen (transcriptionally repressed)
  • ↑ RANKL on osteoblasts → ↑ osteoclast differentiation → bone resorption
  • ↑ FGF23 expression in osteocytes

Parathyroid Gland

  • Antiproliferative on parathyroid cells
  • Suppresses PTH gene transcription (negative feedback)
  • Rationale for using vitamin D analogues to treat secondary hyperPTH in CKD

Extra-mineral Tissues (VDR widespread)

  • Antiproliferative: keratinocytes, breast cancer cells, prostate cancer cells
  • Hair follicle: VDR is primarily a transcriptional repressor in keratinocytes → alopecia seen with mutant VDR (NOT vitamin D deficiency) — ligand-independent action
  • Bone-tendon attachment (enthesis) maturation

Vitamin D Deficiency

Causes (Table 421-6)

  • ↓ Cutaneous production (↑ sunblock use, reduced solar exposure, elderly, dark skin)
  • Dietary absence / malabsorption (short gut, gastric bypass)
  • Accelerated metabolism: barbiturates, phenytoin, rifampin (↑ hepatic CYP P450) or gain-of-function CYP3A4 mutations
  • Impaired enterohepatic circulation: terminal ileal disease
  • Nephrotic syndrome (↑ urinary losses of binding protein)
  • Impaired 25-hydroxylation: severe liver disease, isoniazid, 25-hydroxylase mutation
  • Impaired 1α-hydroxylation: hypoparathyroidism, ketoconazole, CYP27B1 mutation (→ VDDR Type I), FGF23 excess, CKD (creatinine clearance <30 mL/min)
  • Target organ resistance: VDR mutations (→ VDDR Type II, +alopecia)
  • Obesity (poorly understood mechanism)
VDDR Type I vs Type II
VDDR Type I (Pseudovitamin D-Deficiency Rickets): CYP27B1 (1α-hydroxylase) mutation — AR. Presents in year 1 of life: growth retardation, rickets, hypocalcemic seizures. Serum 1,25(OH)₂D low; 25(OH)D normal; PTH ↑. Treatment: calcitriol or 1α-hydroxyvitamin D analogues (lifelong).

VDDR Type II (Hereditary Vitamin D-Resistant Rickets): VDR mutation — AR. Same presentation + alopecia. Serum 1,25(OH)₂D dramatically elevated (↑ production due to secondary hyperPTH + ↓ 24-hydroxylase induction). Treat: daily IV calcium + phosphate to bypass intestinal absorption defect.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Mild-moderate: asymptomatic
  • Long-standing: hypocalcemia + secondary hyperparathyroidism + ↓ bone mineral density (osteopenia on XR) + proximal myopathy (rapid resolution with treatment)
  • Associated with ↑ overall mortality including cardiovascular
  • Rickets (children, pre-epiphyseal fusion): Growth retardation + expanded growth plate. Expansion due to impaired apoptosis of late hypertrophic chondrocytes. Hypophosphatemia (from secondary hyperPTH) is key etiologic factor. Rachitic rosary (costochondral expansion).
  • Osteomalacia: Impaired bone matrix mineralization → biomechanically inferior bone → bowing of weight-bearing extremities + fractures. Pseudofractures (Looser's zones) = radiolucent lines at sites of large artery pulsation (scapula, pelvis, femoral neck)

Diagnosis of Vitamin D Deficiency

🔑 Best Screening Test: Serum 25(OH)D
Deficiency: <37 nmol/L (<15 ng/mL) — associated with ↑ PTH + ↓ bone density
Sufficiency (National Academy of Medicine): >50 nmol/L (>20 ng/mL)
Do NOT use 1,25(OH)₂D to diagnose — often normal or even elevated in severe deficiency (because ↑ PTH stimulates 1α-hydroxylase) — misleading in normal renal function

Treatment of Vitamin D Deficiency

Recommended Daily Intake (National Academy of Medicine 2010)
600 IU: Ages 1–70 years
800 IU: Age >70 years (also shown to ↓ hip fracture risk in elderly with calcium supplementation)
Toxicity: Usually only at doses ≥40,000 IU/day (large safety margin)
  • Severe deficiency with intact activation pathway: 50,000 IU weekly × 3–12 weeks, then maintenance 800 IU/day
  • If 1α-hydroxylation is impaired: Use metabolites that skip this step — calcitriol [1,25(OH)₂D₃, Rocaltrol] 0.25–0.5 μg/d; or doxercalciferol [1α-OH-D₂] 2.5–5 μg/d; or alfacalcidol [1α-OH-D₃] 0.25–1.0 μg/d
  • Always replete with calcium supplementation (1.5–2 g/d elemental Ca)
  • Drug interactions: Barbiturates, phenytoin, rifampin → ↑ hepatic CYP P450 → accelerate vitamin D catabolism → need higher doses
  • Normocalcemia: usually within 1 week; PTH + alkaline phosphatase may persist ↑ for 3–6 months
  • Monitoring: Serum and urinary calcium. 24-h urinary Ca: target 100–250 mg/24h (low = poor adherence/absorption; >250 = predisposes to nephrolithiasis → reduce dose)
  • VITAL Trial finding: Supraphysiologic vitamin D in adults with normal levels does NOT further improve BMD, skeletal microarchitecture, or prevent falls. Small daily doses (e.g., 400 IU) prevent fractures; large bolus intermittent doses can increase fractures and falls.
Sarcoidosis-Associated Hypercalcemia
Macrophage 1α-hydroxylase induced by IFN-γ + TNF-α (not regulated by Ca or 1,25(OH)₂D) → ↑ 1,25(OH)₂D → hypercalcemia. Treat with glucocorticoids, ketoconazole, or chloroquine (reduce 1,25(OH)₂D production). Note: chloroquine does NOT work in lymphoma.

Exam Traps

Trap 01The "Normal" 1,25(OH)₂D in Vitamin D Deficiency
A patient with severe vitamin D deficiency has a normal serum 1,25(OH)₂D level. You conclude she does NOT have vitamin D deficiency. Is this correct?
WRONG. Do not use 1,25(OH)₂D to diagnose vitamin D deficiency in patients with normal renal function. In severe deficiency, secondary hyperparathyroidism stimulates the renal 1α-hydroxylase → 1,25(OH)₂D can be paradoxically normal or even elevated. Use serum 25(OH)D for screening. Deficiency = <37 nmol/L (<15 ng/mL); sufficiency = >50 nmol/L (>20 ng/mL).
Trap 02Rhabdomyolysis Peak AFTER Phosphate Nadir
A patient with acute hypophosphatemia is treated and their serum phosphate normalizes. One to two days later, CPK is found to be markedly elevated. The lab is called a mistake. Is this correct?
NOT a mistake — this is expected. Up to 30% of patients with acute hypophosphatemia (<0.7 mM) develop rhabdomyolysis. CPK peaks 1–2 days AFTER the phosphate nadir — by then, phosphate released from injured myocytes may normalize serum phosphate levels, masking the diagnosis. The timing of the CPK elevation is the clue.
Trap 03Alopecia — VDR Mutation, NOT Vitamin D Deficiency
A child with rickets also presents with alopecia. You diagnose severe vitamin D deficiency and start supplementation. Is this the right path?
Alopecia is NOT a feature of vitamin D deficiency. It is specifically associated with vitamin D receptor (VDR) mutations (VDDR Type II / Hereditary Vitamin D-Resistant Rickets). The VDR effect on hair follicle regeneration is ligand-independent. The serum 1,25(OH)₂D will be dramatically elevated (not low). These patients require IV calcium + phosphate infusions to bypass the intestinal defect, not more vitamin D.
Trap 04Correcting Hypocalcemia Before IV Phosphate
A patient with severe hypophosphatemia also has mild hypocalcemia. You go ahead and infuse IV phosphate immediately to correct the phosphate urgently. Is this correct?
WRONG — and potentially dangerous. Hypocalcemia must be corrected BEFORE administering IV phosphate. Giving phosphate in the setting of hypocalcemia can worsen hypocalcemia by forming Ca-PO₄ precipitates and increase risk of heterotopic calcification. Also avoid Ca × PO₄ product >50 mg²/dL² throughout treatment.
Trap 05Magnesium and Hypocalcemia Resistant to Ca Replacement
A patient has persistent hypocalcemia that fails to respond to calcium supplementation. You keep increasing the calcium dose. What are you missing?
Check magnesium. Hypomagnesemia causes impaired PTH secretion (at very low Mg <0.4 mmol/L) + cellular resistance to PTH + impaired 1,25(OH)₂D synthesis. Hypocalcemia CANNOT be corrected without replacing magnesium first. Similarly, hypokalemia coexisting with hypomagnesemia is refractory to K replacement alone until Mg is repleted.
Trap 06Calcitonin vs PTH — Mechanism of Osteoclast Inhibition
Which hormone directly inhibits osteoclasts, and which works indirectly through osteoblast-lineage cells?
Calcitonin acts DIRECTLY on osteoclasts (binds receptor on the basal surface of osteoclasts → directly inhibits function).
PTH and 1,25(OH)₂D act INDIRECTLY — they target osteoblast-lineage cells (osteoblasts, osteocytes, stromal cells) → ↑ M-CSF + RANKL production → osteoclast differentiation and activation. PTH does NOT have direct osteoclast receptors in this pathway.
Trap 07DKA + Insulin: When to Worry About Phosphate
A patient with DKA starts insulin therapy. Initial serum phosphate at presentation is only slightly low. When is hypophosphatemia worst?
Hypophosphatemia is greatest HOURS after initiation of insulin therapy — NOT at baseline. Initial serum phosphate does not predict the nadir. Insulin drives PO₄ into cells. The severity of subsequent hypophosphatemia relates to the extent of antecedent phosphate depletion. This is the paradigm for redistributive hypophosphatemia. Monitor phosphate serially during DKA treatment.
Trap 08Calcium Carbonate vs Citrate in Achlorhydria
A patient post-bariatric surgery needs calcium supplementation. Which preparation should you recommend, and how should it be taken?
Calcium citrate is preferred — it does not require gastric acid for absorption. Calcium carbonate requires acid for dissociation; large boluses also neutralize gastric acid → poorly absorbed. For patients on PPIs, with achlorhydria, or post-bariatric surgery: use calcium citrate and take supplements WITH meals to optimize absorption through whatever acid is available.
Trap 09Thiazides vs Loop Diuretics — Calcium Effects
A patient with recurrent calcium nephrolithiasis and hypercalciuria is started on a diuretic to reduce urinary calcium. Which diuretic should be used?
Thiazide diuretics — they act at the DCT to induce sodium depletion → augment proximal calcium reabsorption → decrease urinary calcium excretion (calciuric effect reversed). Also used to prevent nephrocalcinosis in patients on phosphate therapy for hypophosphatemic disorders.

Loop diuretics do the OPPOSITE — they increase distal sodium delivery → induce calciuresis → increase urinary calcium loss. Never use loop diuretics to treat hypercalciuria.
Trap 10Sarcoidosis Hypercalcemia — Why Chloroquine Fails in Lymphoma
Chloroquine successfully reduced 1,25(OH)₂D and serum calcium in a patient with sarcoidosis. Should it be used similarly for lymphoma-associated hypercalcemia?
No — chloroquine has NOT been shown to lower elevated serum 1,25(OH)₂D in patients with lymphoma. Both states involve macrophage/lymphoma cell 1α-hydroxylase activity induced by IFN-γ/TNF-α (not regulated by Ca or 1,25(OH)₂D). However, chloroquine works in sarcoidosis but NOT lymphoma. For sarcoidosis: glucocorticoids, ketoconazole, or chloroquine. For lymphoma: treat the underlying disease; glucocorticoids are first-line.
Trap 11Intermittent Large-Bolus Vitamin D — A Trap for Elderly
An elderly patient is given large intermittent bolus doses of vitamin D₃ as a simpler regimen. Is this safer or equally effective as small daily doses?
WRONG — large intermittent bolus doses can INCREASE incidence of fractures and falls in older adults. The VITAL trial showed that supraphysiologic doses in adults with normal vitamin D levels do not improve BMD or prevent falls. Small daily doses (e.g., 400 IU D₃) prevent fractures and falls. Always use daily dosing, not large intermittent boluses, for elderly patients.
