Overview & SpA Spectrum

Definition
Spondyloarthritis (SpA) is a family of immune-mediated inflammatory arthritis disorders that share clinical, genetic, and pathologic features. First recognized by Moll and Wright in 1974 as distinct from rheumatoid arthritis (RA). Divided by ASAS in 2009 into axial (axSpA) and peripheral (pSpA).

Shared Features of All SpA

Musculoskeletal

  • Sacroiliitis — cornerstone of axial involvement
  • Inflammatory spinal lesions
  • Peripheral inflammatory arthritis — asymmetric, large joints, lower limbs
  • Enthesitis — inflammation at tendon/ligament attachment to bone
  • Tendonitis & tenosynovitis
  • Dactylitis — "sausage digits" (whole finger/toe swelling)

Extra-Articular

  • Uveitis — acute anterior, unilateral, episodic
  • Skin psoriasis
  • IBD (Crohn's or ulcerative colitis)
  • RF negative and no nodules (differentiates from RA)
  • Familial clustering
  • HLA-B27 association

Pattern Recognition — Key Diagnostic Clue
Typical SpA pattern: asymmetric, mono- or oligoarticular (≤4 joints) inflammatory arthritis of large joints of the lower limb (usually knee) + enthesitis + tendonitis + dactylitis. This contrasts with RA: polyarticular, symmetric MCP/PIP involvement. Cumulative SpA prevalence = 2–3% of population — much more common than RA.

Pathology & Pathogenesis

Core Pathology Concept

Unique Feature of SpA
SpA couples inflammation to structural damage. Uniquely, inflammation-induced bone loss coexists with pathologic new bone formation at specific sites: sacroiliac joints, anterior spinal ligaments, and peripheral entheses. Uncontrolled axSpA → complete ankylosis of SI joints + spine = "bamboo spine."

Pathogenesis — Three Triggers

Trigger 1

Genetic Predisposition
HLA-B27 is the dominant risk gene. Additional non-MHC genes in GWAS link to innate immunity, IL-23/IL-17 axis, bone remodeling, epithelial function.

Trigger 2

Barrier Integrity Loss
Loss of intestinal/skin/urogenital barrier → microbial translocation → immune activation. ReA follows enteric infections (Salmonella, Shigella, Yersinia, Campylobacter, Chlamydia).

Trigger 3

Mechanical Stress
Entheseal microdamage → immune activation. Enthesitis more common in lower limbs (higher mechanical strain). Physical labor = more structural damage in AS.

Gut–Joint Axis

  • Germ-free HLA-B27 transgenic rats fail to develop SpA → intestinal microbiota is crucial
  • HLA-B27 shapes gut microbiome even before disease onset — intestinal dysbiosis is preclinical
  • Gut inflammation is seen histologically in up to 50% of new-onset treatment-naïve SpA patients (2 types: acute resembling infectious enterocolitis; chronic resembling Crohn's)
  • Chronic gut lesions → risk factor for evolution to full-blown Crohn's disease and progression to AS
  • Skin microbiome alterations also described in psoriasis and PsA

Cytokine Hubs — The Therapeutic Battleground

Core Cytokines
TNF-α and IL-17 are the signature cytokines of axSpA. Different disease domains share overlapping (TNF-α) but also distinct (IL-17, IL-23) cytokines — this explains why some biologics work across all domains and others are domain-specific.

Critical Board Point
Even though axSpA and IBD both have IL-23R polymorphisms, they differ in cytokine dependency: IL-17A blockade works in axSpA but is contraindicated in active IBD (IL-17 has barrier-protective roles in the gut). Conversely, IL-23 blockers work in IBD but do not benefit axSpA.

New Bone Formation Pathway

  • Chronic entheseal inflammation → osteoblast differentiation via prostaglandin E₂ + IL-17A/F + IL-22
  • → Activation of bone morphogenic proteins (BMPs) and Wnt proteins
  • → Generation of bony spurs at vertebral bodies and SI joints → ankylosis
03

HLA-B27 & Genetics

Core Fact
HLA-B27 is an MHC class I allele — the strongest genetic risk factor for SpA and specifically axSpA. MHC class I molecules are expressed on all nucleate cells and present peptides to CD8+ T cells.

HLA-B27 Prevalence Numbers

r-axSpA (AS)

85–90% of patients are HLA-B27 positive

nr-axSpA

50–90% of patients are HLA-B27 positive

General Population

~5% of healthy Caucasians are HLA-B27 positive

First-Degree Relatives

Risk of AS in HLA-B27+ first-degree relatives of AS patients = 20%. Risk in all HLA-B27+ individuals = 5%

Geography Pearl
In Europe, HLA-B27 frequency increases from south to north, and so does the prevalence of axSpA. Spain: HLA-B27 7%, AS 0.1%. Norway: HLA-B27 9%, axSpA 0.56%.

Three Theories: How HLA-B27 Causes SpA

Theory 1 — Arthritogenic Peptide

  • HLA-B27 presents pathological microbial peptides to CD8+ cytotoxic T cells
  • These T cells target cross-reactive human peptides → inflammation (molecular mimicry)
  • First exposure likely in gut or urogenital tract
  • Not all HLA-B27 subtypes are pathogenic — only those that can present specific peptides:
    • Pathogenic subtypes: HLA-B*2704 and HLA-B*2705 → confer risk
    • Non-pathogenic (despite differing by only 1 amino acid): HLA-B*2706 and HLA-B*2709 → NOT associated with axSpA
  • HLA-B27-restricted CD8+ T cells found in synovial fluid of AS patients; specific CDR3 motif (CAS**STDTQYF) clonotypes expanded in AS blood and synovium

Theory 2 — HLA-B27 Homodimerization

  • HLA-B27 can homodimerize and be expressed on the cell surface
  • Interacts with innate immune receptors (e.g., KIR3DL2) on T cells and NK cells
  • → IL-17 production (key pathogenic cytokine in axSpA)
  • Controversy: not all HLA-B27 allotypes form homodimers equally

Theory 3 — Unfolded Protein Response (UPR) & Autophagy

  • HLA-B27 linked to UPR and autophagy → may promote type 17 immune responses via IL-23 release
  • Support from HLA-B27 transgenic rat model
  • BUT no evidence of increased UPR in synovial tissue or blood of HLA-B27+ AS patients in steady state → role in humans questioned

Beyond HLA-B27

  • Other MHC class I: HLA-Cw6 in PsA
  • IL-23R gene polymorphisms shared across most SpA forms
  • Non-MHC genes linked to: innate immunity, IL-23/IL-17 axis, bone remodeling, epithelial function, antigen presentation
  • ERAP1 (peptide-trimming enzyme): SNPs associated with AS and interact epistatically with HLA-B27
  • HLA-B27 is NOT a risk factor for IBD

Axial SpA — Epidemiology & Clinical Features

Definitions

r-axSpA = Ankylosing Spondylitis (AS)

Sacroiliitis meeting modified New York criteria: Grade 2 bilateral OR grade 3–4 unilateral or bilateral on plain x-ray. Interreader reliability (kappa): 0.35–0.45 — significant variability even among trained radiologists.

nr-axSpA

Sacroiliitis grade 1 (unilateral/bilateral) or grade 2 (unilateral only) — below the modified NY threshold. Same clinical features and disease burden as r-axSpA. In late-stage r-axSpA, spine/hip fusion adds functional loss.

Sacroiliitis GradeDescription
Grade 0Normal
Grade 1Suspicious change only
Grade 2Minimum abnormality — small localized erosions or sclerosis, without joint width change
Grade 3Unequivocal abnormality — moderate/advanced sacroiliitis: erosions, sclerosis, widening/narrowing, partial ankylosis
Grade 4Severe abnormality — total ankylosis

Epidemiology

  • Sex ratio: r-axSpA = 2:1 (M:F); nr-axSpA = 1:1
  • US (NHANES 2009–2010): HLA-B27 prevalence 6%; axSpA prevalence 0.9–1.4%; AS 0.5%
  • Diagnostic prevalence = 1/10 of population prevalence → large undiagnosed population
  • Diagnostic delay: 5–14 years from symptom onset (due to commonality of mechanical back pain; women mistaken for fibromyalgia; lower HLA-B27 prevalence in nonwhite populations)
  • Incidence of AS: 0.005–0.01 per 100 patient-years globally
  • Close to 50% of patients progress from nr to radiographic stage over 20 years; <10% progress to bamboo spine

Clinical Manifestations

Back Pain (Most Common Presenting Feature)

Onset Pattern
Starts before age 40–45, usually in the 20s–30s. Insidious onset, chronic (>3 months), improves with exercise/activity, worsens with rest, worse in second half of the night, prolonged morning stiffness (>30 min).
Misnomer Alert
"Inflammatory back pain" (IBP) is a misnomer: only 15% of patients with IBP have demonstrable inflammation in their axial skeleton on imaging. CRP/ESR elevated in only 30–40% of active axSpA — neither sensitive nor specific.

Other Musculoskeletal Manifestations

  • Peripheral arthritis: 30–40% of axSpA; asymmetric, oligoarticular, large lower-extremity joints; rarely small joints of hands and feet
  • Hip involvement: Most common nonspinal joint — "root joint." Pain in groin radiating to medial thigh or knee. Significant functional impairment.
  • Enthesitis: Common — heel pain (plantar fasciitis, Achilles tendon), chest wall pain, epicondylitis, patellar/quadriceps tendon, pelvic rim
  • Dactylitis: Uncommon in axSpA (more typical of PsA)
  • Spinal fusion: Bony fusion of SI joints + apophyseal joints + syndesmophytes from osteoproliferation in annulus fibrosus → limits spinal and neck mobility

Extra-Musculoskeletal Manifestations

Uveitis (40%)

Most common extra-MSK manifestation. Acute, anterior, unilateral, episodic. Eye discomfort → redness, pain, photophobia, miosis.

IBD (10% frank; 50% subclinical)

Frank IBD in 10% of axSpA. Histologic subclinical gut inflammation in up to 50% of patients.

Psoriasis (10%)

Seen in 10% of axSpA patients.

Osteoporosis (40%)

Common morbidity despite osteoproliferative changes. Prevalence ~25% after 10 years.

Renal

IgA nephropathy (microscopic hematuria, any stage) and renal AA amyloidosis (late complication from prolonged inflammation).

Cardiac

Conduction abnormalities (any stage); aortic valve insufficiency (late); cauda equina syndrome (late — urinary hesitancy, saddle anesthesia).

Risk Factors for Osteoproliferation/Progression

  • Male sex
  • Persistent inflammation (MRI of SI joints/spine, high CRP)
  • Syndesmophytes at baseline
  • HLA-B27 positive
  • Smoking

Imaging in axSpA

Plain X-Ray (First Line)

  • Single anteroposterior (AP) view or Ferguson view of pelvis — sufficient to image SI joints
  • Multiple/oblique views add little and increase gonadal radiation — avoid
  • Features: marginal sclerosis, erosions, narrowing/widening, fusion
  • Avoid routine spine x-rays in chronic back pain patients
  • Spine findings: Romanus lesions (shiny corner sign), squaring of vertebral bodies, Andersson lesion (erosion at disk level), syndesmophytes (vertical — differentiates from horizontal osteophytes in OA), bamboo spine (late, <10%)

MRI (Key for Early Disease)

  • Most sensitive — detects early inflammation before x-ray changes
  • Early: osteitis (bone marrow edema on STIR) = active inflammation; may be only finding
  • Progression: osteitis → fatty metaplasia → new bone formation (sclerosis → backfill → ankylosis)
  • Multiple types of lesions increase confidence of axSpA diagnosis
  • Pitfall: MRI very sensitive; mechanical stress in athletes, postpartum women, and normal individuals >40 can mimic osteitis → inappropriate MRI can lead to overdiagnosis
  • Fat metaplasia also seen in degenerative arthritis and normal individuals

Low-Dose CT

  • Emerging as alternative to plain radiography for diagnosis and progression assessment

axSpA — Treatment

Treatment Target
Low disease activity or remission. Principles identical for nr-axSpA and r-axSpA (treated as one disease). Some agents approved for AS only. Shared decision-making is key.

Non-Pharmacologic

  • Physical exercise (on land or aquatic) and self-directed exercise — strongly recommended by all international guidelines
  • Physical therapy improves fatigue, mobility, and posture
  • Smoking cessation encouraged in all patients

Pharmacologic Stepwise Approach

Step 1: NSAIDs (highest tolerable dose, continuously if active)
↓ If active disease after 4-week trial of full-dose NSAIDs
Step 2: Biologic — TNFi OR IL-17i (either class, provider/patient choice)
↓ Primary failure of TNFi → switch to IL-17i or JAKi; Secondary failure → alternative TNFi
Step 3: JAKi (after failing TNFi — per FDA black box)

Drug Details — axSpA

NSAIDs

  • Used at highest tolerable doses continuously in active disease; as needed if stable
  • No particular NSAID preferred over another
  • Monitor: gastric ulcer disease, hypertension, renal insufficiency, cardiovascular disease

csDMARDs (Peripheral Only — NOT for Axial Disease)

  • Sulfasalazine up to 3 g/day — peripheral joints and entheses
  • Methotrexate up to 25 mg/week — peripheral joints and entheses
  • Systemic glucocorticoids have NO efficacy in axSpA and should be avoided
  • Local glucocorticoid injections: indicated for upper extremity enthesitis or intraarticular for peripheral arthritis. Injections around lower limb weight-bearing entheses NOT recommended (risk of rupture)

Biologic Screening Before Initiating (All Biologics + JAKi)

  • Screen for latent tuberculosis
  • Screen for hepatitis B and hepatitis C

Surgical Interventions

  • Total hip arthroplasty: For refractory hip pain or functional decline with radiographic structural damage
  • Spinal osteotomies: Reserved for severe fixed kyphotic deformities in advanced AS affecting horizontal vision — perform only in centers of expertise

Complications of axSpA

  • Osteoporosis: ~25% prevalence after 10 years
  • Vertebral fractures: ~10% of AS patients; lower cervical spine most common; often neurologic compromise
  • Cauda equina syndrome: From long-standing AS → arachnoiditis of lumbosacral nerve roots
  • Cardiac: Aortic valve insufficiency, conduction abnormalities (heart blocks)
  • Pulmonary: Chest expansion restriction (costovertebral ossification), upper lobe ILD (rare), sleep apnea (late — cervical/rib changes)
  • Renal: IgA nephropathy (any stage), renal AA amyloidosis (late, untreated)
  • Cardiovascular disease: Common comorbidity linked to chronic inflammation — counsel all AS patients about traditional CV risk factors

Psoriatic Arthritis (PsA)

Definition (Moll & Wright, 1973)
Inflammatory arthritis (peripheral arthritis ±sacroiliitis/spondylitis) + cutaneous psoriasis + absence of RF. Associated with HLA-B*27. Involves peripheral joints, axial skeleton, entheses, tendon sheaths ± uveitis ± IBD.

Epidemiology

  • Skin psoriasis prevalence: 2–3% of population
  • PsA in patients with psoriasis: 30% (range 6–42%)
  • Incidence of PsA in psoriasis patients: 3 per 100 patient-years (range 2–10)
  • Likelihood of developing PsA does not change over time in psoriasis patients
  • Psoriasis usually comes first in ~80% of PsA; arthritis and psoriasis simultaneously in ~10%; arthritis first in ~10%

Five Clinical Phenotypes (Moll & Wright)

1

Asymmetric oligoarticular inflammatory arthritis (most common early)

2

Symmetric polyarticular arthritis (resembles RA but RF negative)

3

Distal interphalangeal (DIP) joint arthritis — closely linked to nail changes

4

Spondylitis — axial involvement ± sacroiliitis

5

Arthritis mutilans — severe end manifestation; joint destruction/lysis

Added

Enthesitis and dactylitis — added over the years to the five phenotypes. These phenotypes frequently coexist.

Key Clinical Features

Dactylitis

  • Inflammatory swelling of an entire finger or toe — due to inflammation of joints, tendons, bones, and soft tissues within the digit
  • Marker of PsA severity — persistent dactylitis → structural damage
  • Examine feet carefully — a single dactylitic toe or heel enthesitis may be the only MSK manifestation

Enthesitis

  • Most common sites: plantar fascia and Achilles tendon insertion (plantar fasciitis and Achilles enthesitis)
  • Other: quadriceps on patella, patellar tendon on tibia, medial/lateral epicondyle, pelvic rim, spinous processes, intercostal insertions

Nail Involvement

  • 40% of psoriasis patients without PsA have nail lesions; but ~90% of PsA patients have nail involvement
  • Nail matrix disease: pitting, leukonychia, crumbling, red spots in lunula
  • Nail bed disease: onycholysis, subungual hyperkeratosis, oil drop change, splinter hemorrhages

Axial Involvement

  • 5% of PsA patients in early stage; up to 50% after 20 years
  • May be asymptomatic or overshadowed by peripheral symptoms
  • Radiographic differences from AS: asymmetric sacroiliitis, nonmarginal and asymmetric bulky ("chunky") syndesmophytes from sites away from vertebral corners, frequent cervical involvement

Comorbidities

  • Cardiovascular disease risk as high as patients with diabetes mellitus; correlates with severity of skin disease, duration, inflammatory markers
  • Hypertension, type 2 diabetes, obesity, metabolic syndrome, fatty liver disease, angina, MI — all more prevalent in PsA vs general population
  • Obesity has adverse impact on PsA disease activity and treatment response
  • Increased rates of depression and suicidality

PsA Imaging Features

  • Early: soft tissue swelling around involved joints; periarticular osteopenia generally absent
  • Marginal erosions with "fluffy" new bone formation — characteristic of PsA
  • "Pencil-in-cup" deformity, bony ankylosis in ray distribution — pathognomonic
  • Acro-osteolysis — resorption of terminal phalanx in late-stage arthritis mutilans
  • Sacroiliitis in PsA: more likely unilateral (vs bilateral in axSpA)
  • Chunky "nonmarginal" syndesmophytes in PsA — grow from sites AWAY from vertebral corners (different from AS)
Ultrasound Role
Ultrasound + Doppler: differentiates enthesitis from fibromyalgia tender points; detects erosions before x-ray; shows tendon thickening, hypoechoic lesion, neovascularization at entheses. Highly operator-dependent — limit use to expert hands.

PsA Disease Activity

  • DAPSA — Disease Activity in Psoriatic Arthritis (most common in clinical practice)
  • MDA — Minimal Disease Activity
  • Skin assessment: simple palm-count method (1 palm = ~1% BSA)

PsA Differential Diagnosis

  • OA of DIP joints: Heberden's nodes + psoriasis; psoriatic nail involvement and enthesitis on ultrasound distinguishes PsA
  • Seronegative RA: Examine for hidden psoriasis (scalp, umbilicus, gluteal cleft, nails); family history of psoriasis; many "seronegative RA" patients actually have pSpA
  • Gout: High skin turnover in psoriasis → hyperuricemia; gout can coexist — always analyze synovial fluid in monoarticular flares
  • CPPD: In elderly with psoriasis; chondrocalcinosis of triangular fibrocartilage, "hook-like" MCP osteophytes, absence of erosions or fluffy new bone formation

Reactive Arthritis & IBD-Associated Arthritis

Reactive Arthritis (ReA)

Definition
Arthritis following urogenital or gastrointestinal infection with well-defined triggering microorganisms not cultivable from the affected joints. Restricted to HLA-B27- and SpA-related infections. Other post-infectious arthritides (e.g., after rheumatic fever, meningococcus, COVID) should NOT be called ReA.

Triggering Organisms

  • Gastrointestinal: Yersinia, Campylobacter, Salmonella, Shigella
  • Urogenital: Chlamydia trachomatis

Clinical Features

  • Hallmark: Time delay from infection to arthritis — 1 day to 4 weeks
  • Typical: asymmetric oligoarticular arthritis, preferentially lower limbs
  • Additional: enthesitis, tendonitis, bursitis, dactylitis, IBP, sacroiliitis
  • Mucocutaneous: uveitis/conjunctivitis, erythema nodosum, oral ulcers, circinate balanitis, keratoderma blenorrhagica
  • HLA-B27 positive in 50–80% of ReA patients
  • Incidence: 3.4 cases per 100,000 person-years
  • Estimated 2.6% of enteric infections → ReA

Diagnosis

  • Clinical features + identification of antecedent infection from history and labs
  • Lab: stool cultures (Salmonella, Shigella, Campylobacter, Yersinia); serology; All patients offered screening for Chlamydia
  • Diagnostic focus: HLA-B27 + molecular testing for Chlamydia
  • Note: GI symptoms often resolved when arthritis appears → pathogens may not be retrievable

Treatment — Reactive Arthritis

  • No formal treatment guidelines exist
  • Acute ReA: NSAIDs + intraarticular glucocorticoids (mono/oligoarthritis) + short-term low-dose systemic GC (polyarticular)
  • Antibiotics: NOT routinely used; one study showed 6-month course of combination antibiotics (doxycycline or azithromycin + rifampicin) better than placebo for chronic Chlamydia-induced ReA — but NOT replicated → long-term antibiotics NOT recommended
  • HLA-B27 → promotes evolution to chronicity (HLA-B27 promotes intracellular survival of causative organisms)
  • Chronic ReA (>6 months, inadequate response): csDMARDs (sulfasalazine, methotrexate), even TNFi — follows peripheral SpA principles

IBD-Associated Arthritis

Key Relationship
Both UC and Crohn's disease are associated with SpA. The gut–joint link is reciprocal: SpA patients (even without GI symptoms) show histologic gut inflammation on ileocolonoscopy in up to 50% (acute or chronic Crohn's-like lesions, primarily in terminal ileum). Chronic gut lesions = risk factor for evolution to AS and full-blown Crohn's.

Prevalence

  • r-axSpA develops in up to 10% of IBD patients
  • pSpA prevalence in IBD varies widely: 10–30%
  • Conversely, axSpA patients have 5–10% prevalence of IBD (increases with disease duration)
  • Microscopic IBD-like gut lesions + elevated fecal calprotectin/CRP in ~50% of new-onset treatment-naïve SpA

Clinical Features

  • Axial IBD-arthritis clinically similar to idiopathic axSpA; may precede or follow IBD diagnosis
  • Peripheral IBD-arthritis: acute oligoarthritis co-occurring with IBD relapses; or chronic forms causing structural damage

Undifferentiated pSpA, SAPHO & Hidradenitis Suppurativa

Undifferentiated Peripheral SpA

Patients with the typical SpA phenotype (oligoarticular inflammatory arthritis, usually lower limbs, asymmetric, or peripheral enthesitis/dactylitis) who do not fit into any defined category (not axSpA, PsA, ReA, or IBD-arthritis).

ASAS Criteria for Peripheral SpA (2011)
Must have: Arthritis OR enthesitis OR dactylitis

Plus ≥1 of: psoriasis, IBD, preceding infection, HLA-B27, uveitis, sacroiliitis on imaging

OR Plus ≥2 of: arthritis, enthesitis, dactylitis, IBP in the past, positive family history for SpA

Some of these patients likely have ReA (silent inciting infection); others may develop psoriasis/IBD features later. Treatment is individualized.

SAPHO Syndrome

  • Synovitis, Acne, Pustulosis, Hyperostosis, Osteitis
  • Sometimes associated with Propionibacterium acnes infection on bone biopsy
  • Included under SpA because some patients have asymmetric inflammatory arthritis (typically sternoclavicular joints and chest wall costochondral junctions), sacroiliitis, spinal hyperostosis
  • Skin: acne conglobata and palmoplantar pustular lesions pathologically identical to psoriasis
  • Considered adult phenotype of juvenile chronic recurrent multifocal osteomyelitis (CRMO) — osteomyelitis is sterile
  • Treatment: TNFi, IL-17i, JAKi; case reports of pamidronate (bisphosphonate) being effective

Hidradenitis Suppurativa-Associated Arthritis

  • Hidradenitis suppurativa patients may develop SpA phenotype
  • MSK: peripheral arthritis; rarely asymmetric sacroiliitis

Exam Traps

High-yield board traps — each one tested in the format you'll see on Stage A.

Trap 01Inflammatory vs Mechanical Back Pain
A 28-year-old man presents with back pain for 6 months that is worse after waking up in the morning, lasts over 45 minutes, and improves significantly after a morning jog. He takes ibuprofen and gets >50% relief within 48 hours. X-ray shows no sacroiliitis. What is the most likely diagnosis and next step?
Answer: This is inflammatory back pain (IBP) — insidious onset <45 years, prolonged morning stiffness >30 min, improves with activity, responds well to NSAIDs. Axial SpA (nr-axSpA) is most likely. Next step: assess SpA features (HLA-B27, MRI SI joints). Do NOT dismiss because x-ray is normal — nr-axSpA by definition has no definitive sacroiliitis on x-ray.
Trap 0202HLA-B27 Subtypes — NOT All Are Pathogenic
A question lists HLA-B*2706 and asks if this subtype confers risk for ankylosing spondylitis. The patient is of Southeast Asian ancestry.
Answer: NO — HLA-B*2706 and HLA-B*2709 are NOT associated with axSpA, even though they differ from the pathogenic subtypes (B*2704, B*2705) by only ONE amino acid/peptide residue. This supports the arthritogenic peptide theory — only specific peptides presented by pathogenic subtypes trigger disease.
Trap 03IL-17 Inhibitor CONTRAINDICATED in Active IBD
A patient with axial SpA is started on secukinumab. Two months later she develops a Crohn's disease flare. Should secukinumab be continued?
Answer: NO — IL-17 inhibitors are CONTRAINDICATED in active IBD. IL-17 has barrier-protective roles in the gut. Paradoxically, IL-23 blockers are approved in IBD but do NOT benefit axSpA. Switch to a TNF monoclonal antibody or JAKi for a patient who has both axSpA and IBD.
Trap 04Etanercept NOT Preferred in Uveitis or IBD
A patient with AS also has recurrent acute anterior uveitis. Which TNF inhibitor should be used?
Answer: TNF monoclonal antibodies (adalimumab, certolizumab, golimumab, infliximab) are preferred — NOT etanercept. Etanercept is a soluble TNF receptor, NOT a monoclonal antibody, and is not preferred in patients with concomitant uveitis or IBD. Monoclonal antibodies are effective against both joint and eye/gut manifestations.
Trap 05csDMARDs Do NOT Work for Axial Disease
A patient with AS fails NSAIDs. Her physician starts methotrexate to treat the spinal inflammation. Is this appropriate?
Answer: NO — csDMARDs (methotrexate, sulfasalazine) are effective for peripheral joints and entheses ONLY, NOT for axial disease in axSpA. For active axial SpA not responding to full-dose NSAIDs after 4 weeks, escalate to a biologic (TNFi or IL-17i), NOT a csDMARD. Systemic glucocorticoids also have no efficacy in axSpA.
Trap 06JAKi After TNFi — FDA Restriction
A 68-year-old male smoker with AS and a prior history of lung cancer asks to start tofacitinib as his first advanced therapy. Is this appropriate?
Answer: NO — JAK inhibitors should be reserved for patients who have failed at least one TNF inhibitor per FDA black box warning. JAKis carry higher risk of major cardiovascular events and cancers (lymphoma, lung cancers) — especially in patients ≥65 years, current/past smokers, history of CVD or malignancy. This patient should first try a TNFi.
Trap 07DISH vs Bamboo Spine
A 58-year-old obese diabetic man has a spine x-ray showing "flowing" ossification of the right anterior longitudinal ligament across ≥4 vertebral bodies with bulky osteophytes. Sacroiliac joints are normal. Is this AS?
Answer: NO — this is DISH (Diffuse Idiopathic Skeletal Hyperostosis), NOT AS. Key distinction: DISH = NORMAL sacroiliac joints. DISH is non-inflammatory, seen in obese diabetic males >50, produces "flowing" anterior/posterior ligamentous ossification typically on the RIGHT side of the thoracic spine. Bamboo spine (AS) always has sacroiliitis.
Trap 08OCI vs Sacroiliitis — The Erosion Clue
A 35-year-old woman, G3P3, has x-ray showing triangular dense sclerosis at the lower iliac portion of both SI joints bilaterally. She is asymptomatic. MRI looks similar to early axSpA. What distinguishes this from sacroiliitis?
Answer: This is Osteitis Condensans Ilii (OCI) — distinguished from axSpA by the ABSENCE of erosions. OCI: multiparous women, asymptomatic, triangular dense sclerosis on the ILIAC side. No erosions = no axSpA. Early MRI can be indistinguishable. Also note: OCI can occur in nulliparous women and even men.
Trap 09Infection Side Effects by Biologic Class
A patient on a biologic for SpA develops recurrent mucosal Candida infections of the mouth and genitals. Which drug class is responsible?
Answer: IL-17 inhibitors (IL-17i) — mucosal and skin Candida infections are characteristic of this class. Memory: TB reactivation → TNFi (screen before starting) | Herpes zoster → JAKi | Candida (mucosal/skin) → IL-17i. Bacterial infections occur with both biologics and JAKis.
Trap 10CASPAR Criteria — Points Required
A patient has: inflammatory arthritis of the knee (confirmed), psoriasis present now (2 pts), negative RF (1 pt), and no dactylitis or nail changes. Does she meet CASPAR for PsA?
Answer: YES — she has 3 points (current psoriasis = 2, negative RF = 1) plus inflammatory articular disease, meeting the CASPAR threshold of ≥3 points. The CASPAR criteria require inflammatory articular disease (joint, spine, or entheseal) PLUS ≥3 points. Specificity 98.7%, sensitivity 91.4%. Note: current psoriasis alone gives 2 points — the highest single-item score.
Trap 11Vedolizumab Can Cause SpA Flare
A patient with Crohn's disease who had no prior joint symptoms starts vedolizumab. 3 months later she develops inflammatory back pain and sacroiliitis on MRI. What happened?
Answer: Vedolizumab (gut-selective integrin inhibitor) can paradoxically cause new-onset or flare of SpA/pSpA in up to 15% of IBD patients treated with it. Vedolizumab works selectively in the gut — it may redirect systemic immune activity to joints. This is an important adverse event to know.
Trap 12Diagnostic Delay & The IBP Misnomer
Boards often try to imply that "inflammatory back pain" means confirmed inflammation on imaging. Is this accurate?
Answer: NO — "inflammatory back pain" (IBP) is a clinical term and a misnomer. Only 15% of patients with IBP have demonstrable inflammation in the axial skeleton on imaging. Also: the diagnostic delay for axSpA is 5–14 years from symptom onset. ESR/CRP are elevated in only 30–40% of active axSpA — not sensitive enough to rule in or out. Women are commonly misdiagnosed with fibromyalgia, adding further delay.
Trap 1313PsA + IBD — Which Biologic Is Safe?
A patient with PsA and active Crohn's disease needs a biologic. What should you choose?
Answer: TNF monoclonal antibodies (adalimumab, infliximab, certolizumab, golimumab), anti-IL-12/23 (ustekinumab), anti-IL-23 (guselkumab, risankizumab), or JAKi. IL-17 inhibitors are contraindicated in active IBD (can use if only history of IBD, not currently active). For patients with both active IBD and uveitis — prioritize TNF monoclonal Ab.
Trap 14Syndesmophytes in PsA vs AS
Spine x-ray shows large, bulky, asymmetric syndesmophytes that seem to arise from the middle of the vertebral body, NOT from the corners. The patient has psoriasis. What disease does this pattern suggest?
Answer: Psoriatic Arthritis (PsA). PsA syndesmophytes are nonmarginal, chunky, and asymmetric — they arise from sites AWAY from vertebral corners. In contrast, AS syndesmophytes are vertical, marginal, symmetric. PsA also more commonly shows unilateral sacroiliitis, whereas AS is typically bilateral.
