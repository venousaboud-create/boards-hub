Definition & Overview
What IBD is · What it is NOT · The three-word conceptual anchor
What is IBD?
  • IBD = Chronic, idiopathic inflammatory disease of the gastrointestinal tract
  • Two major subtypes: Ulcerative Colitis (UC) → mucosal, continuous, rectum-based; and Crohn's Disease (CD) → transmural, segmental, any GI segment
  • "Idiopathic" is critical — no single identified cause
  • Results from interaction of genetics + environment + microbiota + immune dysregulation
Is IBD an Autoimmune Disease?
  • Search for a single infectious agent has been unrewarding
  • Current consensus: IBD = inappropriate immune response to endogenous commensal microbiota
  • May or may not include a component of true autoimmunity
  • The "Supraorganism" model: microbiota + intestinal epithelial cells + mucosal immune system normally exist in homeostasis
  • In IBD: genetic + environmental factors disrupt this homeostasis → chronic dysregulated inflammation
UC vs. CD — Quick Conceptual Anchor
  • UC: Mucosal · Continuous · Rectal (always involves rectum; extends proximally)
  • CD: Transmural · Segmental · Any segment (mouth to anus; rectum often spared)
  • ~15% cannot be classified initially → indeterminate colitis
  • ~5% of surgical specimens remain unclassifiable due to overlapping histology

Etiology & Pathogenesis
Supraorganism Model · Inflammatory Cascade · Drug Target Cytokines · T-Cell Biology

🧫 The "Supraorganism" Model

Under physiologic (normal) conditions, homeostasis exists between three compartments operating as an integrated supraorganism: (1) Commensal microbiota, (2) Intestinal epithelial cells (IECs), (3) Mucosal immune cells. In IBD: genetic + environmental factors disrupt this homeostasis → chronic state of dysregulated inflammation.

🔄 Normal Intestinal Immune Regulation

  • Normal intestine contains many immune cells in a unique state of controlled activation — restrained by:
  • T regulatory cells (Tregs) expressing FoxP3 transcription factor → secrete anti-inflammatory cytokines: IL-10, IL-35, TGF-β
  • Oral (mucosal) tolerance mechanisms (deletion + anergy of antigen-reactive T cells)
  • Local parenchymal cells: nerve, endothelial, stromal cells; commensal microbiota protective factors
  • During infections: full activation occurs but is rapidly superseded by dampening and tissue repair
  • In IBD: these dampening processes are NOT regulated normally

Genetic Considerations
GWAS · Monogenic IBD · Key Loci · Shared Immune Disease Genetics

🧬 Genetic Architecture

Monogenic IBD (VEOIBD <10%)
  • >60 different gene defects identified by whole-exome sequencing (WES)
  • Key mutations: IL-10 + IL-10 receptor (IL-10R) → severe, refractory early IBD
  • CTLA4 (cytotoxic T-lymphocyte-associated protein 4)
  • NCF2 (neutrophil cytosolic factor 2)
  • XIAP (X-linked inhibitor of apoptosis)
  • LRBA (lipopolysaccharide responsive beige-like anchor protein)
  • TTC7 (tetratricopeptide repeat domain 7A)
  • May also occur in small subset of adult IBD patients
Polygenic IBD (Majority)
  • Studied via candidate gene studies, linkage analysis, GWAS, WES, whole-genome sequencing
  • ~240 genetic loci identified by GWAS
  • Two-thirds of loci associated with BOTH UC and CD
  • One-third specific for either UC or CD
  • Only ~20% of disease risk explained by current genetic information
  • Most risk loci in regulatory (noncoding) regions
  • Creates a syndromic nature → multiple clinical subgroups

Commensal Microbiota & Immune Dysregulation
Dysbiosis · Oral Tolerance · Animal Models · Protective Paradox
Normal Microbiota Development
  • Humans born with sterile guts
  • Colonized initially from mother during birth (vaginal canal), then environmental sources
  • By age 3 years: stable configuration of up to 1,000 species of bacteria
  • Biomass: ~10¹² CFU/gram of feces
  • Each individual has a unique combination of species — likely persists into adult life
  • Intestines also contain: fungi, archaea, viruses, protists
Microbiota Functions & Control
  • Microbiota is a critical and sustaining component of the human organism
  • Control of composition: host (immune + epithelial responses), environmental (diet, antibiotics), genetic (e.g., NOD2)
  • Microbiota influences: epithelial function, immune function, via epigenetic effects → may have durable consequences
  • Effects during early life (when microbiota is being established) particularly important for later IBD risk
Dysbiosis in IBD
  • IBD patients have demonstrably different microbiome → called dysbiosis
  • ↑ Disease-driving: Proteobacteria (enteroinvasive and adherent E. coli)
  • ↓ Inflammation-preventing: Firmicutes (especially Faecalibacterium prausnitzii)
  • Many microbiota changes occur AS A CONSEQUENCE of inflammation → secondary drivers
  • Agents that alter microbiota improve CD: metronidazole, ciprofloxacin, elemental diets
  • CD responds to fecal diversion → demonstrating luminal contents exacerbate disease

🧘 Oral (Mucosal) Tolerance — The Normal State

Mucosal immune system does NOT normally respond to luminal contents. Mechanisms: (1) Deletion of antigen-reactive T cells, (2) Anergy (nonresponsiveness), (3) Induction of FoxP3+ Tregs → secrete IL-10, IL-35, TGF-β. This explains why we do not react to dietary antigens or commensal bacteria. In IBD: this suppression is altered → uncontrolled inflammation.

🐭 Animal Models Confirm the Cascade

  • Deleting cytokines (IL-2, IL-10, TGF-β) or their receptors → spontaneous colitis/enteritis
  • Deleting T-cell antigen recognition molecules → colitis
  • Interfering with IEC barrier function or regulation of responses to commensal bacteria → colitis
  • In majority: intestinal inflammation requires the presence of commensal microbiota
  • In some cases: activation of intestinal immune elements may be exacerbated by the absence of bacteria → emphasizes protective properties of microbiota (paradox)

Pathology: Macroscopic & Microscopic
UC vs. CD Distribution · Endoscopic Appearance · Histology · Montreal Classification

🔴 ULCERATIVE COLITIS — Macroscopic Features

Disease Distribution
  • UC = mucosal disease starting in rectum; extends proximally in a continuous fashion
  • 40–50%: Limited to rectum + rectosigmoid (ulcerative proctitis)
  • 30–40%: Extends beyond sigmoid but not whole colon (left-sided)
  • 20%: Pancolitis (entire colon involved)
  • Skip areas are unusual for UC.
    • BUT: effective medical therapy can change appearance
    • Variations in macroscopic activity may suggest skip areas
    • Biopsies from normal-appearing mucosa are usually still abnormal
    • → Must always obtain multiple biopsies from apparently uninvolved mucosa
Backwash Ileitis
  • Occurs in 10–20% of pancolitis — inflammation extends 2–3 cm into terminal ileum
  • Mechanism:
    • Severe pancolitis → ↑ intraluminal pressure
    • → Ileocecal valve becomes incompetent
    • → Inflammatory mediators + colonic contents wash backward into terminal ileum
  • Superficial, mild, little clinical significance
  • Distinctions from Crohn's ileitis:
    • Short extension (only 2–3 cm)
    • Mucosal changes are superficial
    • NO deep transmural destructive inflammation
    • NO strictures or fissures as seen in Crohn's
Endoscopic Appearance Progression
  • Mild: Mucosa erythematous with fine granular surface — resembles sandpaper
  • Severe: Hemorrhagic, edematous, ulcerated; superficial ulcers
  • Long-standing: Inflammatory polyps (pseudopolyps) from epithelial regeneration — remnant of tissue trying to heal itself. Colon becomes atrophic.
  • "Lead pipe" colon (barium study): colon loses its normal haustra folds; bowel shortens + narrows → becomes stiff, smooth tube
  • Remission: Mucosa may appear normal OR atrophic and featureless
  • Fulminant: Toxic colitis or megacolon; bowel wall thins; may perforate
  • Important caveat: Effective medical therapy can change appearance → must biopsy apparently uninvolved mucosa

🔬 UC — Microscopic Features

Process is limited to mucosa and superficial submucosa. Deeper layers unaffected (except in fulminant disease).
Chronicity Markers (distinguish from acute/infectious colitis)
  • Distorted crypt architecture: Normally crypts are straight and parallel.
    • In UC: crypts become bifid (branched)
    • Crypts reduced in number
    • Gap between crypt bases and muscularis mucosae
  • Basal plasma cells + multiple basal lymphoid aggregates
Active Disease (Flare Signs)
  • Mucosal vascular congestion:
    • Edema and focal hemorrhage
  • Inflammatory cell infiltrate:
    • Neutrophils
    • Lymphocytes
    • Plasma cells
    • Macrophages
  • Neutrophils invade epithelium → cryptitis → ultimately crypt abscesses
    • Note: Crypt abscesses can also be seen in Crohn's — in UC they are a classic sign of activity
Backwash Ileitis Histology
  • Villous atrophy
  • Crypt regeneration with increased inflammation
  • Increased neutrophil and mononuclear inflammation in lamina propria
  • Patchy cryptitis and crypt abscesses

🔵 CROHN'S DISEASE — Macroscopic Features

Disease Distribution
  • CD can affect any part of GI tract from mouth to anus
  • Distribution:
    • 30–40%: Small-bowel disease alone
    • 40–55%: Both small + large intestine (ileocolonic)
    • 15–25%: Colitis alone
  • In 75% with small-intestinal disease: terminal ileum involved in 90%
  • Unlike UC: rectum is often SPARED
  • Segmental disease with skip areas throughout diseased intestine
  • Perianal disease: present in one-third of patients, especially with colonic involvement
  • Pseudopolyps can form in CD, as in UC (epithelial regeneration after ulceration — "As in UC, pseudopolyps can form in CD" — Harrison's 337)
  • Rarely: CD may involve liver and pancreas
Endoscopic Appearance (Transmural)
  • Mild:
    • Aphthous or small superficial ulcerations
  • Active:
    • Stellate ulcerations fuse longitudinally + transversely
    • Demarcate islands of mucosa
  • Classic "cobblestone" appearance:
    • Deep linear ulcers run longitudinally AND transversely → crisscross like a grid
    • Between ulcers: islands of relatively normal mucosa
    • Submucosa beneath is inflamed → islands "rise up" = cobblestoning
Active CD — 4 Key Macroscopic Changes
  • 1. Focal inflammation + fistula tract formation
    • Resolves by fibrosis + stricturing of the bowel
  • 2. Bowel wall thickens
    • Becomes narrowed + fibrotic
    • → Chronic, recurrent bowel obstructions
  • 3. "Creeping fat"
    • Mesenteric fat gets inflamed
    • Creates projections of thickened mesentery that wrap the bowel
    • Visible at surgery
  • 4. Serosal + mesenteric inflammation
    • Promotes adhesions + fistula formation

🔬 CD — Microscopic Features

Granulomas (Pathognomonic)
  • Earliest lesions:
    • Aphthoid ulcerations
    • Focal crypt abscesses with loose macrophage aggregations
  • Form noncaseating granulomas in ALL layers of bowel wall
    • Granuloma = collection of histiocytes/macrophages that cluster together
    • NON-caseating = no necrosis in the center
    • Less commonly found on mucosal biopsies than on surgical resection specimens
    • Found in approximately 60% of biopsies
    • Absence of granuloma in biopsy does NOT rule out CD
Other Histologic Features
  • Submucosal or subserosal lymphoid aggregates (particularly away from ulceration areas)
  • Gross and microscopic skip areas
  • Transmural inflammation accompanied by fissures that penetrate deeply into bowel wall
  • Fissures sometimes form fistulous tracts or local abscesses

Clinical Presentation
UC Symptoms · CD Patterns · Labs · Endoscopy · Radiology · Complications

🔴 ULCERATIVE COLITIS — Signs & Symptoms

Core Symptoms (In Order of Importance)
  • Diarrhea (nocturnal and/or postprandial)
  • Rectal bleeding
  • Tenesmus (urgency with feeling of incomplete evacuation)
  • Passage of mucus
  • Crampy abdominal pain
  • Severity correlates with extent of disease
  • Usually present for weeks to months before diagnosis
Proctitis Symptoms (Distal Disease)
  • Fresh blood or blood-stained mucus (mixed with stool or streaked on surface of normal stool)
  • Tenesmus + urgency with feeling of incomplete evacuation
  • Rarely have abdominal pain
  • Constipation (counter-intuitive!):
    • Inflammation in rectum causes spasm
    • → Acts like functional obstruction → slows proximal transit
    • Patient has terrible urgency + passing only blood/mucus from rectum
    • But actual fecal matter is hard and backed up behind the spasm
More Extensive Disease (Beyond Rectum)
  • Blood usually mixed with stool OR grossly bloody diarrhea
    • Due to ↓ water absorption capacity of inflamed colon
  • Bloody diarrhea is often nocturnal
    • Red flag for inflammatory disease (not IBS)
  • Colonic motility altered → rapid transit through inflamed intestine
  • Severe disease: pass liquid stool containing blood, pus, fecal matter
  • Other symptoms in moderate-severe:
    • Anorexia, nausea, vomiting, fever, weight loss
    • Lower abdominal discomfort or mild central abdominal cramping

Physical Signs & Labs (UC)

Physical Exam
  • Proctitis: tender anal canal, blood on rectal exam
  • More extensive disease: tenderness over the colon
  • Toxic colitis: severe pain and bleeding
  • Megacolon: hepatic tympany
  • Both may have signs of peritonitis if perforation has occurred
Laboratory Markers (Active Disease)
  • ↑ CRP, ↑ Platelet count, ↑ ESR, ↓ Hemoglobin, ↓ Albumin (severely ill), Leukocytosis
  • Fecal lactoferrin: glycoprotein in activated neutrophils — highly sensitive + specific for detecting intestinal inflammation
  • Fecal calprotectin: protein in cytoplasm of neutrophils + monocytes — when inflamed, neutrophils migrate into lumen + shed this protein. Correlates with histologic inflammation; predicts relapses; detects pouchitis
  • Both markers used to rule out active inflammation vs. IBS symptoms or bacterial overgrowth
Endoscopic Features
  • Mild: Erythema, decreased vascular pattern, mild friability
  • Moderate: Marked erythema, absent vascular pattern, friability, erosions
  • Severe: Spontaneous bleeding + ulcerations
  • Histologic features change more slowly than clinical features
  • Intestinal ultrasound: newer tool to assess UC activity + mucosal healing
  • Sigmoidoscopy: used in acute settings (flare) to avoid perforation from full colonoscopy
  • Colonoscopy: not in acute flare → assesses extent + activity

🔵 CROHN'S DISEASE — Signs & Symptoms by Site

CD presents as acute or chronic bowel inflammation evolving toward: (1) Fibrostenotic obstructing pattern, or (2) Penetrating fistulous pattern — each has different treatment and prognosis. Site of disease determines clinical manifestations.

1. Ileocolitis (Most Common Pattern)

Core Symptoms
  • Chronic history of recurrent right lower quadrant pain + diarrhea
  • Pain characteristics:
    • Colicky (in waves)
    • Precedes and is relieved by defecation
  • Fever:
    • Low-grade fever: usually present with active disease
    • High-spiking fever: suggests intraabdominal abscess formation
  • Weight loss: typically 10–20% of body weight
    • Due to: diarrhea, anorexia, and fear of eating
Physical Signs
  • May initially mimic acute appendicitis:
    • Pronounced RLQ pain
    • Palpable mass
    • Fever
    • Leukocytosis
  • Palpable inflammatory mass in RLQ:
    • Composed of: inflamed bowel + mesenteric induration + enlarged abdominal lymph nodes
  • "String sign" on barium/radiographic studies:
    • Severely narrowed loop of bowel → lumen resembles a frayed cotton string
    • Caused by incomplete filling due to edema, irritability, and spasms
    • Seen in BOTH nonstenotic AND stenotic phases — does NOT distinguish inflammatory from fibrotic
Bowel Obstruction (Fibrostenotic Pattern)
  • Early stages:
    • Bowel wall edema + spasm
    • → Intermittent obstructive symptoms + postprandial pain
  • Over several years:
    • Persistent inflammation → fibrostenotic narrowing
    • Body heals with collagen → scar tissue contracts + stiffens → stricture
    • Diarrhea decreases, replaced by chronic bowel obstruction
    • May cause: bilious vomiting, severe distention, obstipation
  • Acute obstruction episodes precipitated by:
    • Bowel inflammation and spasm
    • Impaction of undigested food or medication
    • Usually resolve with IV fluids + gastric decompression

2. Fistula Formation (Penetrating Pattern)

Mechanism
  • Severe inflammation of ileocecal region → localized wall thinning
  • → Microperforation → fistula formation to adjacent structures or abscess cavity in mesentery
A. Enterovesical Fistula
  • Dysuria
  • Recurrent bladder infections
  • Pneumaturia (air in urine) — less common
  • Fecaluria (fecal matter in urine) — less common
B. Enterocutaneous Fistula
  • Follow tissue planes of least resistance
  • Usually drain through abdominal surgical scars
C. Enterovaginal Fistula (Rare)
  • Dyspareunia
  • Feculent or foul-smelling, often painful vaginal discharge
  • Unlikely to develop without a prior hysterectomy
D. Enteroenteric Fistula
  • Malabsorption by "short-circuiting" absorptive surface
  • Bacterial overgrowth

3. Jejunoileitis

Malabsorption Syndrome
  • Extensive inflammatory disease → loss of digestive + absorptive surface
  • → Malabsorption + steatorrhea (fatty, floating, foul-smelling stool)
  • Nutritional deficiencies from:
    • Poor intake
    • Enteric losses of protein and other nutrients
  • Intestinal malabsorption can cause:
    • Anemia (iron, B12, folate)
    • Hypoalbuminemia
    • Hypocalcemia + hypomagnesemia
    • Coagulopathy
    • Hyperoxaluria with nephrolithiasis (requires intact colon)
  • Additional complications:
    • Vertebral fractures: vitamin D deficiency + hypocalcemia + prolonged glucocorticoid use
    • Pellagra (niacin deficiency) in extensive small-bowel disease
    • Megaloblastic anemia + neurologic symptoms: vitamin B12 malabsorption from terminal ileum
    • Many patients need IV iron (oral poorly tolerated + often ineffective)
  • Nutrients to check and replete:
    • Folate, vitamins A, E, K
    • Zinc, selenium, copper, magnesium
    • Most patients: daily multivitamin + calcium + vitamin D
4 Causes of Diarrhea in Jejunoileitis
  • 1. Bacterial overgrowth
    • In obstructive stasis or fistulization
  • 2. Bile acid malabsorption
    • Due to diseased or resected terminal ileum
  • 3. Intestinal inflammation
    • ↓ Water absorption
    • ↑ Secretion of electrolytes
  • 4. Enteroenteric fistula(e)
    • Short-circuits absorptive surface

4. Colitis & Perianal Disease

Core Symptoms
  • Low-grade fevers, malaise, diarrhea, crampy abdominal pain
  • Sometimes hematochezia
  • Gross bleeding less common than in UC:
    • Appears in about one-half of patients with exclusively colonic disease
    • Only 1–2% exhibit massive bleeding
  • Pain: caused by passage of fecal material through narrowed + inflamed segments
  • Decreased rectal compliance: another cause of diarrhea in Crohn's colitis patients
Stricturing + Fistulization
  • Stricturing in colon: 4–16%
    • → Bowel obstruction symptoms
    • If colonoscopist cannot traverse stricture + patient has chronic obstruction symptoms → surgical resection
  • Colonic fistulization:
    • Into stomach or duodenum → feculent vomiting
    • Into proximal or mid-small bowel → malabsorption ("short-circuiting") + bacterial overgrowth
    • 10% of women with Crohn's colitis → rectovaginal fistula
Perianal Disease (1/3 of Crohn's colitis)
  • Manifestations:
    • Incontinence
    • Large hemorrhoidal tags
    • Anal strictures
    • Anorectal fistulas
    • Perirectal abscesses
  • Important: not all patients with perianal fistula have endoscopic evidence of colonic inflammation

5. Gastroduodenal Disease (Upper GI CD)

Symptoms
  • Nausea, vomiting, epigastric pain
  • Patients usually have H. pylori–negative gastritis
  • Second portion of duodenum more commonly involved than the bulb
  • Advanced disease: chronic gastric outlet obstruction
  • ~30% of children diagnosed with CD have esophagogastroduodenal involvement
Fistula Note
  • Fistulas involving stomach or duodenum arise from the small or large bowel
  • They do NOT necessarily signify the presence of upper GI tract involvement

🔬 CD — Laboratory, Endoscopic & Radiographic Features

Laboratory

Blood Tests
  • Elevated ESR and CRP
  • More severe disease:
    • Hypoalbuminemia
    • Anemia
    • Leukocytosis
Fecal Biomarkers
  • Fecal calprotectin:
    • Distinguishes IBD from IBS
    • Assesses whether CD is active
    • Detects postoperative recurrence
    • More sensitive for ileocolonic or colonic inflammation than isolated ileal inflammation
  • Fecal lactoferrin:
    • Glycoprotein from activated neutrophils
    • Highly sensitive + specific for intestinal inflammation
Serologic Markers
  • ASCA (anti-Saccharomyces cerevisiae antibody) → CD
  • pANCA (perinuclear antineutrophil cytoplasmic antibody) → UC
  • Clinical utility:
    • PPV and NPV vary depending on IBD prevalence in the tested population
    • Clinical factors at diagnosis are more helpful than serologics for predicting natural history
    • Clinical usefulness remains unclear

Endoscopic Features

Colonoscopy Findings
  • Features:
    • Rectal sparing
    • Aphthous ulcerations
    • Fistulas
    • Skip lesions
  • Colonoscopy allows:
    • Examination + biopsy of mass lesions or strictures
    • Biopsy of the terminal ileum
  • Upper endoscopy: useful for gastroduodenal involvement in patients with upper tract symptoms
Stricture Dilation
  • Ileal or colonic stricture may be dilated with a balloon via colonoscope
  • Best response with strictures:
    • ≤4 cm in length
    • At anastomotic sites
  • Perforation rate: as high as 10%
  • Most endoscopists dilate only fibrotic strictures — NOT those associated with active inflammation
Wireless Capsule Endoscopy (WCE)
  • Allows direct visualization of entire small-bowel mucosa
  • Diagnostic yield for active CD: higher than CT or MR enterography
  • CONTRAINDICATED with small-bowel stricture
  • Capsule retention:
    • <1% in suspected CD
    • 4–6% in established CD
  • Patency capsule protocol:
    • Give barium patency capsule → starts dissolving at 30h
    • Abdominal X-ray at ~30h after ingestion
    • If capsule still present in small bowel → stricture confirmed → WCE contraindicated
    • → Use MR enterography instead

Radiographic Features

Small Bowel Findings (Sequential)
  • Early findings:
    • Thickened folds
    • Aphthous ulcerations
  • Cobblestoning:
    • From longitudinal + transverse ulcerations
    • Most frequently involves the small bowel
  • Advanced disease:
    • Strictures
    • Fistulas
    • Inflammatory masses
    • Abscesses
  • Transmural inflammation effects:
    • Decreased luminal diameter + limited distensibility
    • As ulcers progress deeper → fistula formation
    • Segmental nature → wide gaps of normal or dilated bowel between involved segments
Colonic Findings (Sequential)
  • Earliest macroscopic findings:
    • Aphthous ulcers: small, multiple, separated by normal intervening mucosa
  • Progressive disease:
    • Aphthous ulcers enlarge + deepen
    • Occasionally connect → form longitudinal stellate, serpiginous, and linear ulcers
Imaging Modalities Compared
  • CT enterography vs. MR enterography:
    • Equally accurate for identifying active small-bowel inflammation
  • MRI advantages:
    • Superior soft tissue contrast
    • No ionizing radiation
    • Preferred for: younger patients; serial monitoring of response to therapy
  • Pelvic MRI:
    • Superior to pelvic CT for ischiorectal abscesses + perianal fistulas
  • Small-bowel ultrasound (SBUS):
    • Sensitivity: 94%; Specificity: 97%; PPV: 97%; NPV: 94%
    • With oral contrast: sensitivity + specificity → 100%
    • Best suited for distal small bowel
    • Limitations: availability; operator dependence

⚠️ CD — Complications

Serosal Adhesions (Common)
  • CD is a transmural process → serosal adhesions develop
  • Provide direct pathways for fistula formation
  • Reduce the incidence of free perforation (adhesions contain the leak)
Perforation (1–2%)
  • Usually in the ileum
  • Occasionally in the jejunum
  • Or as a complication of toxic megacolon
  • Peritonitis of free colonic perforation may be fatal
Intraabdominal & Pelvic Abscesses (10–30%)
  • Occur at some time in the course of illness
  • Standard therapy: CT-guided percutaneous drainage
  • Despite adequate drainage: most patients still need resection of offending bowel segment
  • Abdominal wall abscesses: especially high failure rate for percutaneous drainage
  • Systemic glucocorticoids: ↑ risk of abscesses in CD patients who have never had an operation
Intestinal Obstruction (40% — Most Common Complication)
  • Acute: bowel inflammation and spasm (reversible)
  • Chronic: fibrotic stricture (requires surgery)
Massive Hemorrhage (Rare)
  • Rarely massive compared to UC
  • Management: transfusion; rarely surgery
Other Complications
  • Malabsorption (extensive jejunoileitis)
  • Severe perianal disease

🫀 Hepatobiliary Disorders (10–35%)

1. Fatty Liver (Hepatic Steatosis)
  • Detectable in about one-half of abnormal liver biopsies from CD and UC patients
  • Presentation: hepatomegaly
  • Causes:
    • Chronic debilitating illness
    • Malnutrition
    • Glucocorticoid therapy
  • Treatment: improve nutrition; reduce steroids
2. Cholelithiasis (Gallstones)
  • Occurs in 10–35% of CD patients with ileitis or ileal resection
  • Mechanism:
    • Bile acid malabsorption (diseased/resected terminal ileum)
    • → Depletion of bile salt pool
    • → Secretion of lithogenic bile
    • → Gallstone formation
  • Treatment: reduce bowel inflammation; cholecystectomy in symptomatic patients
3. Primary Sclerosing Cholangitis (PSC)
  • Epidemiology:
    • ~5% of UC patients have PSC
    • 50–75% of PSC patients have IBD
    • PSC occurs less often in patients with CD
  • Nature: INDEPENDENT of bowel activity
    • Can be recognized after, concurrent with, or even years AFTER proctocolectomy
    • Both IBD and PSC are commonly pANCA positive
  • Pathology:
    • Intrahepatic + extrahepatic bile duct inflammation + fibrosis
    • → Biliary cirrhosis + hepatic failure over 5–10 years
    • → Liver transplantation required
  • Symptoms (at diagnosis: usually none):
    • When present: fatigue, jaundice, abdominal pain, fever, anorexia, malaise
  • Diagnosis:
    • Gold standard: ERCP (endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography)
    • But MRCP is sensitive, specific, and safer → reasonable initial diagnostic test
    • Both ERCP + MRCP demonstrate: multiple bile duct strictures alternating with relatively normal segments
  • Malignancy risk:
    • Cholangiocarcinoma: 10–15% lifetime risk (narrative text; Table 337-7 states 7–10%)
    • If cholangiocarcinoma develops → cannot receive liver transplant
  • Gallbladder management:
    • Gallbladder polyps → cholecystectomy regardless of size (even <1 cm) due to high malignancy risk
    • Annual gallbladder ultrasound surveillance in all PSC patients
  • Other PSC management:
    • Endoscopic stenting: may be palliative for cholestasis secondary to bile duct obstruction
    • IBD + PSC → YEARLY colonoscopy (even in remission) from time of PSC diagnosis
  • Small-duct PSC variant ("pericholangitis"):
    • Involves small-caliber bile ducts only
    • Normal cholangiography (ERCP/MRCP)
    • Similar biochemical + histologic features to classic PSC
    • Significantly better prognosis than classic PSC
    • May evolve into classic PSC
4. Granulomatous Hepatitis & Hepatic Amyloidosis (Rare)
  • Described by Harrison's as "much rarer EIMs of IBD"
  • Both can occur in the setting of IBD, especially in long-standing disease
  • Granulomatous hepatitis: hepatic granuloma formation; may mimic other hepatic diseases
  • Hepatic amyloidosis: amyloid deposition in liver (also part of systemic amyloidosis)

🫘 Urologic

Genitourinary Complications
  • The most frequent complications are:
    • 1. Calculi (nephrolithiasis) — most frequent
    • 2. Ureteral obstruction
    • 3. Ileal bladder fistulas
Nephrolithiasis (10–20%)
  • Highest frequency: CD patients following small-bowel resection
  • Type: calcium oxalate stones most common
  • Mechanism:
    • Normally: dietary Ca²⁺ + luminal oxalate → insoluble CaOx → eliminated in stool

    • REQUIRES AN INTACT COLON for this mechanism to operate
  • Treatment: low-oxalate diet; control of bowel inflammation; surgical intervention if needed

מהם ה-ICIs?
  • ICIs = Immune Checkpoint Inhibitors — תרופות המעכבות נקודות ביקורת של מערכת החיסון כדי לאפשר תקיפת גידולים

  • שימוש ב: מלנומה, סרטן ריאות, כליות, ועוד
ICI Colitis — עובדות מפתח
  • ICIs מופעלים מאוד אפקטיביים מאוד בהפעלת מערכת החיסון כנגד הגידול, אך הם גם כרוכים בתופעות לוואי אוטואימוניות שונות

  • יש לשלול אבחנות חלופיות (כגון אישכמיה ומזהמים דוגמת CDT ו-CMV), הטיפול תלוי בחומרת הקלינקה

5-ASA Agents

Mechanism
5-ASA binds PPAR-γ in colonic epithelial cells → triggers cascade that blocks NF-κB nuclear localization → ↓ transcription of inflammatory mediators.

CLASS 2 — Glucocorticoids

Systemic Glucocorticoids — INDUCTION ONLY
Prednisone (PO) · Hydrocortisone (IV) · Methylprednisolone (IV)
UC ✅ CD ✅ ⚠️ INDUCTION ONLY — NEVER Maintenance
✅ Indications & Doses
  • Prednisone oral: 40–60 mg/d — active UC unresponsive to 5-ASA; moderate-severe CD flares. Taper ≤5–10 mg/week once remission achieved.
  • Hydrocortisone IV: 300 mg/d — hospitalized patients with moderate-severe UC or CD
  • Methylprednisolone IV: 40–60 mg/d — alternative IV agent for hospitalized patients
  • When: patient has active moderate-severe IBD requiring rapid response; failed 5-ASA; OR hospitalized for severe disease
❌ Contraindications & Cautions
  • Maintenance therapy — absolutely contraindicated in UC and CD
  • Active untreated infection:
    • C. difficile — must rule out before starting/escalating steroids
    • CMV — check in steroid-refractory UC
    • Entamoeba histolytica — steroids → fulminant colitis + >50% mortality
    • TB — screen before starting
  • Uncontrolled diabetes (relative CI)
  • Uncontrolled hypertension (relative CI)
  • Avoid debridement of PG while on steroids (pathergy)
⚠️ Side Effects
Most are dose + duration dependent:
  • Fluid retention, hypertension
  • Striae, fat redistribution (moon face, buffalo hump)
  • Hyperglycemia (steroid-induced diabetes)
  • Subcapsular cataracts
  • Myopathy, emotional disturbances, psychosis
  • Osteoporosis: up to 20% bone mass loss/year — check DEXA; supplement calcium + vitamin D
  • Osteonecrosis: 4.3% of patients within 6 months of starting; hips > knees > shoulders; diagnose with MRI or bone scan
  • Adrenal suppression + withdrawal symptoms if stopped abruptly
💡 Steroid-dependent = undertreated. Rule out C. diff and CMV before escalating steroids in any IBD flare. Osteonecrosis can occur within just 6 months — do NOT ignore hip/knee pain in patients on steroids.
Budesonide — INDUCTION ONLY

UC ✅ (MMX) CD ✅ (ileal-release) ⚠️ INDUCTION ONLY — NEVER Maintenance
✅ Indications & Doses
  • Uceris MMX (UC): 9 mg/d × 8 weeks for mild-moderate UC; released entirely in colon; no taper needed
  • Controlled-ileal-release (CD): 9 mg/d × 2–3 months then taper; for mild-moderate ileocolonic CD; nearly equal efficacy to prednisone with fewer systemic side effects

❌ Contraindications
  • Maintenance therapy — contraindicated
  • Active systemic infection
⚠️ Notes
  • Extensive first-pass hepatic metabolism → fewer systemic side effects than prednisone
  • BUT: can still suppress pituitary-adrenal axis → carries osteoporosis risk; supplement calcium + vitamin D
💡 Budesonide = steroid with a "liver escape hatch" — deactivated by first-pass metabolism. Fewer side effects but NOT maintenance. Still causes adrenal suppression and osteoporosis risk with prolonged use.

CLASS 4 — Immunomodulators

Azathioprine (AZA) / 6-Mercaptopurine (MP)
AZA 2–3 mg/kg/d PO · MP 1–1.5 mg/kg/d PO · Onset 3–6 months
UC ✅ CD ✅ 2nd Line Maintenance Only — NOT Induction
Mechanism
Purine analogues → AZA rapidly converted to MP → MP metabolized to 6-thioguanine (6-TG) → inhibits purine ribonucleotide synthesis → ↓ lymphocyte proliferation. Onset: 3–4 weeks minimum; full effect 4–6 months → cannot use for induction alone.
✅ Indications
  • Steroid-sparing maintenance therapy in UC and CD
  • Combined with anti-TNF biologics:
    • ↓ Anti-drug antibody formation → ↑ drug trough levels
    • AZA + infliximab: more effective than either alone for both moderate-severe UC and CD
  • Patients unable to maintain remission on 5-ASA alone
Pre-treatment (Required)
  • Check TPMT enzyme activity before starting:
    • 1 in 300: complete TPMT deficiency (homozygous) → life-threatening bone marrow suppression → avoid AZA/MP
    • 11%: heterozygotes (intermediate activity) → ↑ toxicity risk → use lower dose + close monitoring
  • Baseline CBC + liver function tests (LFTs)
❌ Contraindications
  • TPMT complete deficiency (homozygous) — severe bone marrow toxicity
  • Induction therapy (too slow; onset 4–6 months)
  • Concomitant allopurinol: allopurinol blocks xanthine oxidase → ↑↑ 6-TG accumulation → severe toxicity. If must use together: reduce AZA/MP dose by 75% + close monitoring.
  • Pregnancy: category D — data suggest minimal risk; use with caution if benefits outweigh risks
⚠️ Side Effects & Monitoring
  • Pancreatitis: 3–4%; occurs in first few weeks; completely reversible on stopping; do NOT rechallenge
  • Leukopenia: dose-related, often delayed → monitor CBC q1–3 months
  • Lymphoma risk (4× baseline):
    • Age <50: 0.37/1000 pt-years
    • Age 50–65: 2.58/1000 pt-years
    • Age >65: 5.41/1000 pt-years — highest risk; discuss carefully
  • HSTCL (Hepatosplenic T-Cell Lymphoma) — nearly universally fatal:
    • 37 cases total in IBD; 86% male; median age 26 years; mean 10 years of IBD
    • 36/37 used MP/AZA; 28/37 also on infliximab
    • Highest risk: young males on prolonged AZA/MP + anti-TNF combination
  • Nonmelanoma skin cancer: 2–3× ↑ → yearly skin check
  • Hepatitis, rash, fever, nausea
💡 HSTCL: "Young Male + AZA + Infliximab = HSTCL." 86% male, age ~26, nearly universally fatal. Age >65 on AZA = highest lymphoma risk of all age groups.
Methotrexate (MTX)
15–25 mg/week SC or IM preferred over PO
CD ✅ UC — Limited evidence 2nd Line Induction + Maintenance
Mechanism
  • Inhibits dihydrofolate reductase → impaired DNA synthesis → ↓ rapidly proliferating cells (lymphocytes)
  • Additional anti-inflammatory effect: ↓ IL-1 production
  • Route preference: SC or IM > oral (oral absorption is unreliable and variable)
✅ Indications
  • Steroid-sparing maintenance in CD (limited evidence for UC)
  • Concomitant with anti-TNF to ↓ antibody formation + ↑ trough levels
  • Patients who cannot tolerate AZA/MP (e.g., pancreatitis)
❌ Contraindications
  • PREGNANCY — TERATOGENIC: stop ≥3 months before conception in BOTH partners (male and female)
  • Significant hepatic disease (liver fibrosis risk)
  • Significant renal impairment
  • Active infection
⚠️ Side Effects & Monitoring
  • Leukopenia → monitor CBC regularly
  • Hepatic fibrosis → monitor LFTs periodically
  • Hypersensitivity pneumonitis (rare but serious — new dyspnea/cough on MTX → stop immediately + investigate)
💡 MTX = teratogenic for BOTH partners — 3 months washout. SC/IM > oral. Consider in AZA-intolerant patients or when anti-TNF antibody prevention is needed.
Cyclosporine (CSA)
2–4 mg/kg/d IV · Target trough 150–350 ng/mL · RESCUE ONLY
UC ✅ RESCUE CD ❌ Not used Rescue Only Induction ONLY — Bridge therapy
Mechanism
Lipophilic peptide → binds cyclophilin → complex inhibits calcineurin (cytoplasmic phosphatase involved in T-cell activation) → blocks IL-2 production by T helper cells → also indirectly inhibits B-cell function by blocking helper T cells.
✅ Indications
  • Severe UC refractory to IV glucocorticoids → 82% response rate (more rapid onset than AZA/MP)
  • Alternative to infliximab before colectomy in steroid-refractory UC
  • Bridge therapy: must transition patient to maintenance therapy (AZA/MP or biologic) — CSA cannot be used long-term
  • NOT used in CD
  • Monitoring: target serum levels 150–350 ng/mL (monoclonal RIA or HPLC)
❌ Contraindications
  • Serum cholesterol <3.1 mmol/L (<120 mg/dL) → risk of seizures
  • Hypomagnesemia → risk of seizures
  • Uncontrolled hypertension
  • Significant renal impairment
  • Active uncontrolled infection
  • CD (not indicated)
⚠️ Side Effects & Monitoring
  • Nephrotoxicity → monitor creatinine; reduce dose or stop if elevated
  • Hypertension, gingival hyperplasia, hypertrichosis
  • Paresthesias, tremors, headaches, electrolyte abnormalities
  • Seizures: especially if hypomagnesemic or serum cholesterol <120 mg/dL → check both before starting
  • Opportunistic infections: PCP → give TMP-SMX prophylaxis
💡 Before CSA: check cholesterol AND magnesium — low = seizure risk. Target 150–350 ng/mL. Give TMP-SMX prophylaxis. Always plan bridge to AZA/MP or biologic.
Tacrolimus
Macrolide immunomodulator · 100× more potent than CSA · Oral or IV
UC ✅ (refractory) CD ✅ (children/extensive SB) Rescue / Refractory
Mechanism & Key Advantage
Binds FKBP-12 → complex inhibits calcineurin → ↓ IL-2 production. Similar to CSA but 100× more potent. Critical advantage: NOT dependent on bile or mucosal integrity for oral absorption → effective even with proximal small-bowel CD involvement or poor gut function (unlike CSA which requires intact mucosa for oral absorption).
✅ Indications
  • Children with refractory IBD (strong evidence)
  • Adults with extensive proximal small-bowel CD (where oral absorption is unreliable for CSA)
  • Steroid-refractory UC as alternative to CSA
  • Monitor trough levels: target 5–20 ng/mL depending on indication
⚠️ Notes
  • Side effects similar to CSA: nephrotoxicity, neurotoxicity, hypertension, infection risk, new-onset diabetes
  • Use is decreasing due to side effects + expanding biologic and small-molecule choices
💡 Tacrolimus = CSA's more potent sibling with better oral absorption. Key board point: works even without intact gut → preferred over oral CSA in proximal small-bowel disease.

CLASS 5 — Biologic Therapy: When & Who

Indications for Initiating Biologic Therapy
Which patients need biologics? — The clinical decision
General Indications (UC + CD)
  • Moderate to severe active disease not responding to conventional therapy
  • Steroid-dependent disease: patient requires steroids to stay in remission (= undertreated)
  • Steroid-refractory disease: active disease despite adequate steroid doses
  • After failure of or intolerance to immunomodulators (AZA/MP, MTX)
  • Disease requiring frequent hospitalizations
High-Risk UC — Biologic Early
  • Moderate to severe disease at presentation
  • Steroid-dependent or steroid-refractory UC
  • Refractory pouchitis after IPAA
  • Hospitalized with severe acute UC (ASUC): infliximab OR cyclosporine IV if steroid-refractory
High-Risk CD — Biologic Early
  • Age <30 at diagnosis
  • Extensive disease
  • Perianal or severe rectal disease
  • Deep ulcerations in the colon
  • Stricturing (B2) or penetrating (B3) disease behavior at diagnosis
  • Prior surgical resection
  • Fistulizing disease (enterovesical, enterocutaneous, rectovaginal fistulas)
Goals of Biologic Therapy
  • Treat early in disease course → prevent progression to complications
  • Aim for deep remission: clinical + endoscopic + histologic remission
  • ↓ Hospitalizations, surgeries, disability, fatigue, depression
  • Check drug levels + antibodies to optimize therapy

Anti-TNF Agents — Individual Drug Profiles

TNF-α is a key pro-inflammatory cytokine that regulates immune cells and coordinates the systemic inflammatory response. In IBD: dysregulation of TNF production → promotes TH1/TH17 activity, macrophage activation, and sustained intestinal inflammation. Blocking TNF → rapid reduction in inflammation + mucosal healing.
Infliximab (Remicade)
Chimeric IgG1 anti-TNF-α mAb · IV · First biologic approved for IBD · 25% mouse protein
UC ✅ CD ✅ + Fistulizing CD 1st–2nd Line
✅ Indications & Doses
  • Moderate-severe UC + CD (induction + maintenance)
  • Fistulizing CD — infliximab is the most studied and preferred anti-TNF for fistula closure
  • Standard dose: 5 mg/kg at weeks 0, 2, 6 → then q8wk
  • Dose escalation: may ↑ to 10 mg/kg or shorten to q4wk based on trough levels and clinical response
  • Hospitalized steroid-refractory UC: may need intensive dosing (high inflammatory burden → protein-losing enteropathy → ↓ serum levels)
  • AZA + infliximab combination: more effective than either alone for both moderate-severe UC and CD
❌ Contraindications
  • NYHA class III/IV heart failure
  • Active TB or untreated latent TB (screen with PPD/QuantiFERON-TB Gold before starting)
  • Active HBV (reactivation risk → screen HBsAg, HBsAb, HBcAb; treat HBV first)
  • Active serious infection
  • MS or history of demyelinating disorder
  • Malignancy (relative — within 5 years)
⭐ Drug-Specific Notes
  • Chimeric (25% mouse, 75% human) → most immunogenic anti-TNF agent → highest risk of antibody formation of the 4 agents
  • IV administration → requires clinic visits but allows dose flexibility
  • Infusion reactions (acute or delayed serum-sickness-like) → premedicate with glucocorticoids + antihistamines; scheduled infusions reduce risk vs episodic use
  • Pregnancy: IgG1 → actively transported across placenta (FcRn) in late 2nd + 3rd trimester → infant has detectable levels → no live vaccines until 12 months of age
💡 Infliximab = first biologic in IBD. Most immunogenic → must combine with AZA/MP + schedule infusions. Best studied for fistulizing CD. Intensive dosing needed in hospitalized severe UC.
Adalimumab (Humira)
Fully human IgG1 anti-TNF-α mAb · SC · Self-administered
UC ✅ CD ✅ 1st–2nd Line
✅ Indications & Doses
  • Moderate-severe UC + CD (induction + maintenance)
  • Standard dose: 160 mg day 0 → 80 mg day 14 → 40 mg q2wk
  • Dose escalation: may ↑ to 40 mg weekly when response decreases
  • VARSITY trial: vedolizumab MORE effective than adalimumab as first-line biologic in moderate-severe UC

  • Equally effective to ustekinumab as first-line biologic in CD (head-to-head trial)
❌ Contraindications
  • Same as infliximab: CHF III/IV, active TB, active HBV, active serious infection, MS/demyelination, malignancy
⭐ Drug-Specific Notes
  • Fully human → less immunogenic than infliximab
  • SC self-injection → patient convenience and independence from infusion center
  • Pregnancy: IgG1 → FcRn-mediated active placental transport → infant levels → no live vaccines until 12 months
💡 "Human" = less immunogenic than infliximab. SC = convenient. VARSITY: vedolizumab beats ADA as first-line UC biologic. Dose can escalate to weekly 40 mg before switching class.

  • Anti-drug antibodies (ADA) form against biologic agents → neutralize drug → lower trough levels → loss of response

    • Combine with immunomodulator (AZA/MP/MTX) → ↓ antibody formation + ↑ drug trough levels

    • Detect + quantify anti-drug antibodies
  • Results guide dose optimization, combination therapy decisions, and switching

    • Melanoma: IBD patients have slightly elevated intrinsic risk; anti-TNF increases risk almost 2× (not thiopurines)
    • Nonmelanoma skin cancer: ↑ with both thiopurines AND anti-TNF
    • → Yearly skin check required
  • Neurologic (rare):
    • Optic neuritis
    • Seizures
    • New onset or exacerbation of demyelinating disorders including MS
  • Autoimmune: drug-induced lupus; autoimmune hepatitis; cholestasis
  • Cardiac: may exacerbate symptoms in NYHA class III/IV CHF → contraindicated
  • HSTCL: 37 cases; 86% male; median age 26; nearly universally fatal; linked to AZA/MP + infliximab combination
NHL Risk Stratification
  • Baseline NHL in IBD: ~2/10,000 (slightly > general population)
  • AZA/MP alone: ↑ to ~4/10,000
  • Anti-TNF alone: no excess lymphoma risk after adjusting for thiopurine co-treatment (Danish cohort)
  • Combination AZA/MP + anti-TNF: synergistic risk (HSTCL)
Pre-Treatment Requirements (ALL Anti-TNF)
  • Before starting: TB screen (PPD or QuantiFERON-TB Gold); HBV screen (HBsAg, HBsAb, HBcAb); CBC + chemistry
  • Ongoing maintenance: Influenza vaccine (annual); pneumococcal vaccine; Hep B vaccine (if not immune); yearly skin check
IgG1 Pregnancy Implications (Infliximab, ADA, Golimumab)
  • All IgG1 antibodies → actively transported across placenta via FcRn receptor in late 2nd + 3rd trimester
  • Infant serum levels detectable well into first year of life
  • → AVOID live vaccines in infants until 12 months of age
  • Exception: Certolizumab (Fab fragment, passive diffusion only → minimal infant levels → no vaccine restriction)

CLASS 6 — Anti-Integrin (Gut-Selective)

Natalizumab (Tysabri) ⚠️ No Longer Used for IBD
Anti-α4 integrin · IgG4 mAb · Historical/comparison context
DISCONTINUED — Fatal PML Risk
Mechanism & Why It Caused PML
  • IgG4 mAb against α4-integrin (both α4β1 AND α4β7) → blocks leukocyte adhesion to VCAM-1 (systemic vessels) AND MAdCAM-1 (gut vessels)
  • By blocking both gut AND CNS T-lymphocyte trafficking → eliminates immune surveillance in the brain
  • → JC virus (John Cunningham virus) reactivates in brain → Progressive Multifocal Leukoencephalopathy (PML) — frequently fatal
Why Vedolizumab Is Safe (No PML)
  • Vedolizumab blocks α4β7 specifically (gut-homing integrin only)
  • Does NOT block α4β1 → does NOT affect CNS T-cell trafficking
  • Does NOT interact with VCAM-1 (systemic endothelium)
  • → No PML risk with vedolizumab
  • → No systemic immunosuppression
💡 Board distinction: Natalizumab = α4 (blocks gut + CNS) → PML. Vedolizumab = α4β7 (gut only) → NO PML. The β7 subunit selectivity is everything.
Vedolizumab (Entyvio)
Anti-α4β7 integrin mAb · IV · Gut-selective · Safest infection profile
UC ✅ CD ✅ 1st–2nd Line
Mechanism
Monoclonal Ab against α4β7 integrin specifically → inhibits adhesion of gut-homing T lymphocytes to MAdCAM-1 on gut endothelium. Does NOT interact with VCAM-1 → gut-selective immunosuppression → no systemic immunosuppression → no CNS trafficking effect → no PML risk.
✅ Indications & Dose
  • Moderate-severe UC + CD (induction + maintenance)
  • Dose: 300 mg IV at weeks 0, 2, 6 → then q8wk; may shorten to q4wk for non-responders
  • VARSITY trial: vedolizumab > adalimumab as first-line biologic in moderate-severe UC
  • Preferred when infection risk is high (immunocompromised patients, prior serious infections)
  • After anti-TNF failure (PD failure) → excellent option for both UC and CD
❌ Contraindications
  • Active serious infection
  • Active TB (screen before starting)
  • Active HBV (screen before starting)
⚠️ Side Effects & Safety
  • NO increased risk of serious systemic or opportunistic infections
  • NO increased malignancy risk
  • Common (mild): nasopharyngitis, headache, arthralgias, nausea
  • Pregnancy: IgG1 → FcRn-mediated active placental transport → no live vaccines in infant until 12 months
Pre-Treatment
  • TB screen; HBV screen; influenza + pneumococcal vaccines; Hep B vaccine if not immune; yearly skin check
💡 Vedolizumab = safest immunosuppression profile. Gut-selective = no brain risk, no systemic infection risk. First-choice biologic when infection risk is a concern or in older/immunocompromised patients.

CLASS 8 — Small Molecules (Oral)

Small Molecules — Class Overview
JAK inhibitors · S1P modulators · How they differ from biologics
Why Small Molecules Differ from Biologics
  • Molecular weight <1 kDa (vs ~150 kDa for mAb biologics)
  • Orally administered (vs. IV/SC biologics)
  • No immunogenicity — no antibody formation → no drug level monitoring needed → no PK failure
  • Diffuse through cell membranes → act intracellularly (vs. biologics acting extracellularly)
  • Inhibit multiple cytokine pathways simultaneously (synergistic) → potentially more efficacious than single-target biologics
JAK/STAT Pathway (Tofacitinib + Upadacitinib)
  • JAK family: JAK1, JAK2, JAK3, TYK2 — intracellular tyrosine kinases
  • Normal signaling: cytokine → receptor → JAK activation → JAK phosphorylates STAT proteins → STAT enters nucleus → transcription of immune response genes
  • JAK inhibitor mechanism: competes with ATP at JAK kinase domain → blocks JAK phosphorylation → ↓ downstream cytokine production + immune activation
    • Tofacitinib: pan-JAK inhibitor; higher specificity for JAK1 + JAK3
    • Upadacitinib: selective JAK1 inhibitor; less potent on JAK2, JAK3, TYK2
S1P Pathway (Ozanimod)
  • S1P (Sphingosine-1-Phosphate): bioactive lipid mediator
  • High S1P in blood vs. low S1P in lymph nodes → lymphocytes follow gradient and exit lymph nodes via S1PR1
  • Ozanimod: binds S1P1 + S1P5 → functional antagonism → lymphocytes cannot respond to S1P gradient → trapped in lymph nodes → ↓ circulating lymphocytes → ↓ gut trafficking → immunomodulation
Universal Pre-Treatment (ALL Small Molecules)
  • First dose of Shingrix (recombinant, non-live zoster vaccine) — REQUIRED before starting any JAK inhibitor or ozanimod. Safe in immunosuppressed patients. Reason: significantly ↑ herpes zoster risk.
  • TB screen; HBV screen
  • Influenza + pneumococcal vaccines
  • Lipid panel (JAK inhibitors → ↑ cholesterol)
  • CBC (neutropenia + anemia risk)
Tofacitinib (Xeljanz)
Pan-JAK inhibitor (JAK1 + JAK3 preferentially) · Oral · 10→5 mg BID
UC ✅ ONLY CD ❌ Not approved 2nd Line — Post Anti-TNF Failure
✅ Indications & Dose
  • Moderate-severe UC only
  • FDA-approved as second-line after anti-TNF failure
  • Induction: 10 mg BID × 8 weeks
  • Maintenance: 5 mg BID (10 mg BID if needed for sustained remission)
❌ Contraindications
  • CD (not approved)
  • History of blood clots (VTE)
  • NYHA class III/IV heart failure
  • Active serious infection
  • Caution in: age >50 + cardiovascular risk factors; smokers; history of MI or stroke → consider alternatives
  • Pregnancy — avoid; teratogenic in animal studies
⚠️ FDA Black Box + Side Effects
  • FDA Black Box: ↑ risk of MI, stroke, cancer, blood clots (VTE + PE), death in patients with cardiovascular risk factors (ORAL Surveillance trial data)
  • Herpes zoster risk significantly ↑ → Shingrix BEFORE starting
  • ↑ risk of viral infections including herpes zoster; bacterial + invasive fungal infections
  • Elevated lipids → monitor lipid panel
  • Neutropenia, anemia, elevated liver enzymes → monitor CBC + LFTs
💡 Tofacitinib = UC ONLY (not CD). Pan-JAK = broader = higher cardiovascular risk than JAK1-selective upadacitinib. Dose: 10 mg BID induction → 5 mg BID maintenance.
Upadacitinib (Rinvoq)
Selective JAK1 inhibitor · Oral · 45→15 or 30 mg daily · First JAK approved for both UC and CD
UC ✅ CD ✅ 2nd Line — Post Anti-TNF Failure
Mechanism vs. Tofacitinib
Selective, reversible JAK1 inhibitor (potently inhibits JAK1; less potent against JAK2, JAK3, TYK2). More selective than tofacitinib → potentially fewer off-target effects + cardiovascular events. Demonstrates rapid clinical + endoscopic improvement at end of induction — one of the fastest-acting IBD agents.
✅ Indications & Dose
  • Moderate-severe UC AND CD — first JAK inhibitor approved for BOTH diseases
  • FDA-approved as second-line after anti-TNF failure
  • UC induction: 45 mg PO daily × 8 weeks
  • CD induction: 45 mg PO daily × 12 weeks
  • Maintenance (both): 15 or 30 mg PO daily
❌ Contraindications
  • Same as tofacitinib: history of VTE, CHF III/IV, active infection
  • Caution: age >50 + cardiovascular risk factors
  • Pregnancy — avoid
⚠️ FDA Black Box + Side Effects
  • Same FDA Black Box as tofacitinib: ↑ MI, stroke, cancer, clots, death
  • Herpes zoster risk ↑ → Shingrix BEFORE starting
  • Elevated lipids, neutropenia, anemia, elevated liver enzymes
💡 Upadacitinib = UC AND CD (unlike tofacitinib which is UC only). JAK1-selective = potentially safer CV profile. High 45 mg induction → step down to 15 or 30 mg. Rapid endoscopic improvement.
Ozanimod (Zeposia)
S1P receptor modulator (S1P1 + S1P5) · Oral · Mandatory dose titration
UC ✅ ONLY CD ❌ Not approved 2nd Line
Mechanism
Potent S1P receptor modulator → binds S1P1 + S1P5 selectively → lymphocytes cannot respond to S1P gradient → trapped in lymph nodes → ↓ disease-exacerbating lymphocytes trafficking to gut → immunomodulation. Not a JAK inhibitor.
✅ Indications & Dose (Titration MANDATORY)
  • Moderate-severe UC only
  • Dose titration schedule (CANNOT skip):
    • Days 1–4: 0.23 mg PO daily
    • Days 5–7: 0.46 mg PO daily
    • Day 8 onwards: 0.92 mg PO daily
  • Reason for titration: gradual escalation prevents first-dose bradycardia + AV block
❌ Contraindications
  • CD (not approved)
  • Recent cardiac events (within 6 months): MI, unstable angina, stroke, TIA, decompensated cardiac failure
  • Mobitz type II 2nd-degree or 3rd-degree AV block (without pacemaker)
  • Sick sinus syndrome (without pacemaker)
  • Severe untreated sleep apnea
  • Concurrent Class Ia or III antiarrhythmics (↑ arrhythmia risk)
  • Active serious infection; pregnancy
⚠️ Side Effects
  • Bradycardia + AV conduction delay (reason for mandatory titration)
  • Hypertension
  • Macular edema → baseline ophthalmology evaluation recommended
  • Posterior reversible encephalopathy syndrome (PRES)
  • Infections, elevated liver enzymes
💡 Ozanimod = UC ONLY. Mandatory titration: 0.23 → 0.46 → 0.92 mg (4 days + 3 days + forever). Always cardiac screen before starting. Shingrix required. Not a JAK inhibitor — different mechanism.

Surgical Therapy
Indications · IPAA · Pouchitis · CD Surgery · Post-op Recurrence

UC Surgery

Frequency & Indications
  • Nearly one-half of patients with extensive chronic UC undergo surgery within first 10 years
  • Morbidity:
    • ~20% for elective proctocolectomy
    • ~30% for urgent proctocolectomy
    • ~40% for emergency proctocolectomy
  • Indications:
    • Intractable disease
    • Fulminant disease
    • Toxic megacolon unresponsive to medical therapy
    • Colonic perforation
    • Massive colonic hemorrhage
    • Extracolonic disease
    • Colonic obstruction
    • Colon cancer prophylaxis
    • Colon dysplasia or cancer
Operation of Choice: IPAA
  • UC is a mucosal disease → rectal mucosa dissected and removed down to dentate line (or ~2 cm proximal)
  • Ileum fashioned into J-pouch → sutured circumferentially to anus (end-to-end)
  • Preserves anal sphincter; maintains continence
  • Operative outcomes:
    • Overall operative morbidity: 10%
    • Major complication: bowel obstruction
    • Pouch failure → permanent ileostomy in 5–10% of patients
  • Surveillance requirements:
    • Some inflamed rectal mucosa remains → endoscopic surveillance necessary
    • Primary dysplasia of the ileal mucosa of the pouch has occurred rarely
  • Functional outcome: ~6–10 bowel movements/day
  • Quality of life: better performance in sports + sexual activities than ileostomy patients
Pouchitis
  • Most frequent complication of IPAA: ~30–50% of UC patients
  • Syndrome: ↑ stool frequency, watery stools, cramping, urgency, nocturnal leakage, arthralgias, malaise, fever
  • Pouch biopsies distinguish true pouchitis from underlying CD
  • Usually responds to metronidazole and/or ciprofloxacin
  • 3–5% remain refractory → chronic antibiotics, biologics, or pouch removal

CD Surgery

Frequency
  • Majority of CD patients require ≥1 operation in lifetime
  • Chance of requiring surgery:
    • Small-bowel disease: 80% chance
    • Colitis alone: 50% chance
  • Key principle: as little intestine as possible resected — no surgical cure for CD
  • IPAA is CONTRAINDICATED in CD — high incidence of pouch failure
Surgical Options
  • Resection: Most frequently performed; primary anastomosis. End-to-end anastomosis provides best functional outcome (antiperistaltic side-to-side can cause distention + pain)
  • Strictureplasty:
    • Used when much small bowel already resected + strictures are short with normal intervening mucosa
    • Stricture incised longitudinally → sutured transversely → widens narrowed area
    • Complications:
      • Prolonged ileus
      • Hemorrhage
      • Fistula
      • Abscess
      • Leak
      • Restricture
  • Segmental colonic resection with primary anastomosis where possible; 20–25% have rectal sparing → rectal preservation
  • Diverting colostomy: may help severe perianal disease; disease almost always recurs with reanastomosis
Risk Factors for Early Recurrence After CD Surgery
  • Risk factors:
    • Cigarette smoking
    • Penetrating disease (internal fistulas, abscesses, wall penetration)
    • Early recurrence since previous surgery
    • Multiple surgeries
    • Young age at time of first surgery
  • Management of high-risk patients:
    • → Aggressive postoperative biologic therapy
    • → Colonoscopy 3–6 months post-surgery to assess endoscopic recurrence

IBD & Pregnancy
Fertility · Disease Course · Drug Safety · Delivery · Surgery in Pregnancy
Fertility
  • Quiescent UC + CD: normal fertility rates
  • Factors that can reduce fertility:
    • In CD: fallopian tubes may be scarred (especially right side, due to proximity of terminal ileum)
    • Perirectal/perineal/rectovaginal abscesses + pelvic surgery → dyspareunia
    • Male infertility: caused by sulfasalazine → reverses when treatment stopped
    • Women with IPAA: ↓ fertility due to tubal scarring/occlusion
  • IPAA fertility improvement: laparoscopic IPAA → better fertility than open IPAA
  • Patients should be in remission for 3–6 months before conceiving
Pregnancy Outcomes & Delivery
  • Mild or quiescent UC/CD: no effect on birth outcomes
  • Disease course during pregnancy correlates with disease activity at time of conception
  • Delivery route:
    • Most CD patients: can deliver vaginally
    • Cesarean preferred for: patients with anorectal/perirectal abscesses + fistulas (↓ risk of fistula extending into episiotomy scar)
    • UC patients with IPAA: may consider cesarean due to ↑ risk of future fecal incontinence
  • Surgery in pregnancy: Emergency indications only. Total colectomy + ileostomy → 50% risk of postoperative spontaneous abortion. Best time if necessary: second trimester.
  • IPAA in pregnancy: Patients with IPAA have increased nighttime stool frequency during pregnancy — this resolves postpartum.
  • Ileostomy in pregnancy: Transient small-bowel obstruction or ileus noted in up to 8% of patients with ileostomies during pregnancy.

Cancer in IBD & Surveillance

CRC Risk in UC
  • Overall: 1.5–2× higher than general population
  • Historical risk (one large meta-analysis):
    • 2% after 10 years
    • 8% after 20 years
    • 18% after 30 years of disease

  • Risk factors:
    • Long-duration disease
    • Extensive disease
    • Family history of colon cancer

    • Colon stricture

20 Exam Traps — Harrison's Verified Edition
Cross-referenced against Harrison's Ch. 337 PDF · All traps confirmed against primary source
TRAP 01 — Smoking is Bad for All IBD
🎯 THE TRAP: "Smoking is bad for the gut — it must worsen all IBD."
📚 THE TRUTH: Smoking has OPPOSITE effects. UC: PROTECTIVE (OR 0.58). CD: RISK FACTOR (OR 1.76). Appendectomy is also PROTECTIVE in UC but NOT in CD.
Board Case: A patient with long-standing UC quits smoking. Two months later she has her first major flare. Most likely contributing factor? → SMOKING CESSATION — quitting removes the anti-inflammatory protective effect of nicotine in UC.
TRAP 02 — Colectomy Cures Pyoderma Gangrenosum
🎯 THE TRAP: "A UC patient with PG should get a colectomy to cure the skin disease."
📚 THE TRUTH: PG is INDEPENDENT of bowel activity. It DOES NOT respond to colectomy. Can occur BEFORE IBD diagnosis, run independently, and develop YEARS after proctocolectomy. NEVER debride PG (pathergy worsens it).
Board Case: A UC patient in remission post-colectomy develops an expanding ulcerated lesion with violaceous borders on the shin. Next step? → IV steroids, CSA, or infliximab. NOT debridement. NOT repeat surgery.
TRAP 03 — PSC in Remission Doesn't Need Colonoscopy
🎯 THE TRAP: "A UC patient with PSC in clinical remission doesn't need colonoscopy."
📚 THE TRUTH: IBD + PSC → YEARLY colonoscopy regardless of disease activity. Starts from the TIME PSC IS DIAGNOSED, not from 8–10 years of IBD.
Board Case: A 34-year-old with UC diagnosed 3 years ago is found to have PSC. When should surveillance colonoscopy begin? → NOW — yearly from diagnosis of PSC, not after 8–10 years of IBD.
TRAP 04 — After Ileal Resection the Stones Are Uric Acid
🎯 THE TRAP: "A CD patient had ileal resection and now has kidney stones — probably uric acid stones."
📚 THE TRUTH: After ileal resection, the predominant stones are CALCIUM OXALATE. Mechanism: fatty acid malabsorption → fatty acids bind Ca²⁺ in lumen → oxalate unbound → colon absorbs free oxalate → hyperoxaluria → CaOx stones. REQUIRES AN INTACT COLON.
Board Case: A CD patient with ileal resection + intact colon develops nephrolithiasis. Stone analysis: calcium oxalate. Best dietary intervention? → LOW-OXALATE DIET (not low-purine).
TRAP 05 — Maintenance Steroids Are Acceptable in IBD
🎯 THE TRAP: "This IBD patient is doing well on long-term prednisone — let's continue maintenance steroids."
📚 THE TRUTH: Glucocorticoids have NO ROLE in maintenance therapy in either UC or CD. INDUCTION ONLY. Steroid dependence = inadequate treatment → needs escalation to biologic/immunomodulator.
Board Case: A CD patient requires prednisone every time you try to taper it. Most appropriate next step? → Initiate biologic therapy ± immunomodulator — this is steroid-dependent CD, not an indication for continued steroids.
TRAP 06 — All Anti-TNF Biologics Are Equally Safe in Pregnancy
🎯 THE TRAP: "All anti-TNF biologics are equally safe in pregnancy."
📚 THE TRUTH: Certolizumab pegol is the SAFEST anti-TNF in pregnancy. It is a Fab fragment (NOT intact IgG1) → crosses placenta only by PASSIVE DIFFUSION (not FcRn-mediated active transport) → minimal infant drug levels → live vaccines are NOT withheld. All other anti-TNF agents are intact IgG1 → ACTIVELY transported across placenta → detectable infant serum levels throughout first year → live vaccines must be withheld until 12 months.
Board Case: A pregnant IBD patient at 28 weeks requires biologic therapy. Which anti-TNF results in least fetal drug exposure? → Certolizumab pegol — passive diffusion, minimal cord blood levels.
TRAP 07 — Ankylosing Spondylitis Resolves After Colectomy
🎯 THE TRAP: "Ankylosing spondylitis in IBD is cured by controlling bowel inflammation or by colectomy."
📚 THE TRUTH: AS is INDEPENDENT of bowel activity. Does NOT remit with glucocorticoids or colectomy. 2/3 of IBD-AS patients express HLA-B27. Progressive + permanent skeletal damage. Treatment: anti-TNF (reduces spinal inflammation), IL-17 inhibitors, JAK inhibitors, physical therapy.
Board Case: A UC patient undergoes colectomy. Six months later she has progressive low back pain + morning stiffness with sacroiliac joint fusion on imaging. Colectomy's effect on back disease? → NO effect — AS is independent of bowel disease.
TRAP 08 — IPAA Is Appropriate for Crohn's Colitis
🎯 THE TRAP: "A CD patient needs colectomy — offer IPAA to restore GI continuity."
📚 THE TRUTH: IPAA is CONTRAINDICATED in CD due to high incidence of pouch failure. CD recurs in the pouch. Most CD patients requiring proctocolectomy receive a permanent ileostomy.
Board Case: A 28-year-old with extensive Crohn's colitis requires proctocolectomy and asks about J-pouch surgery. Best response? → IPAA is contraindicated in CD. Recommend permanent ileostomy.
TRAP 09 — Eye Symptoms in IBD Remission Must Be Unrelated
🎯 THE TRAP: "A UC patient in clinical remission complains of eye pain — must be stress or dry eyes since IBD is quiet."
📚 THE TRUTH: UVEITIS is INDEPENDENT of bowel activity and can occur during IBD REMISSION and even after bowel resection. Presents with ocular pain, photophobia, blurred vision, headache. URGENT — treat to prevent scarring and permanent visual impairment.
Board Case: A UC patient in remission presents with 2 days of right eye pain + photophobia + blurred vision. Normal ESR/CRP. Next step? → Urgent ophthalmology referral — suspect uveitis; treat with systemic glucocorticoids.
TRAP 10 — JAK Inhibitors Can Be Started Without Zoster Vaccine
🎯 THE TRAP: "Start tofacitinib — the shingles vaccine can wait until after they stabilize."
📚 THE TRUTH: Shingrix (recombinant zoster vaccine) should be given BEFORE starting JAK inhibitors (tofacitinib, upadacitinib, or ozanimod). Herpes zoster risk is significantly increased. Shingrix is a recombinant subunit vaccine (NOT live) → safe in immunosuppressed patients — but give before therapy for maximal efficacy.
Board Case: Before starting upadacitinib for moderate CD, which vaccine is specifically recommended? → First dose of Shingrix — required before initiating JAK inhibitor therapy.
TRAP 11 — Give WCE Without Checking for Strictures in CD
🎯 THE TRAP: "Order wireless capsule endoscopy (WCE) — it's the most sensitive test for small bowel CD."
📚 THE TRUTH: WCE IS the most sensitive tool — BUT CONTRAINDICATED when a small-bowel stricture is known or suspected. Retention: <1% in suspected CD; 4–6% in established CD. Must give a patency capsule FIRST (barium capsule that dissolves at 30h → abdominal X-ray at 30h; if retained = stricture → WCE contraindicated → use MR enterography instead).
Board Case: A CD patient with suspected small bowel inflammation is being evaluated. Before ordering WCE, appropriate next step? → Administer patency capsule first — check X-ray at 30h; if retained → WCE contraindicated.
TRAP 12 — All Anti-TNF Failure Should Switch Class
🎯 THE TRAP: "The patient failed infliximab — switch to a different biologic class."
📚 THE TRUTH: You must distinguish PHARMACOKINETIC vs. PHARMACODYNAMIC failure:
• PK failure: HIGH anti-drug antibodies + LOW trough level → switch WITHIN class (try adalimumab)
• PD failure: THERAPEUTIC drug level + ACTIVE disease → drug present but not working → switch to DIFFERENT CLASS (vedolizumab, ustekinumab, JAK inhibitor)
Board Case: A CD patient on infliximab has worsening symptoms. Anti-infliximab antibodies HIGH, trough infliximab level LOW. Best next step? → Switch to adalimumab (within class — pharmacokinetic failure). If trough level were therapeutic with active disease → switch to vedolizumab/ustekinumab (pharmacodynamic failure).
TRAP 13 — C. diff Flare Can Be Treated With Steroids Alone
🎯 THE TRAP: "An IBD patient is flaring — start high-dose steroids to calm it down."
📚 THE TRUTH: Every IBD flare must rule out C. difficile first. In IBD patients, C. diff can look like a worsening of the underlying IBD. Starting high-dose steroids without treating C. diff can precipitate TOXIC MEGACOLON with ~15% mortality from perforation. Always check C. diff toxin PCR before escalating immunosuppression during a flare.
Board Case: A UC patient in apparent flare has 8 bloody stools/day + fever. Before initiating IV methylprednisolone, what must be done? → Send stool for C. difficile toxin PCR — do NOT give steroids until C. diff is ruled out.
TRAP 14 — PSC Cholangiocarcinoma Risk: 7–10% vs 10–15%?
🎯 THE TRAP: Boards may ask "what is the lifetime risk of cholangiocarcinoma in PSC?" — Harrison's Table 337-7 gives 7–10%, but the narrative text gives 10–15%. Which is correct?
📚 THE TRUTH: The narrative text (10–15%) is the clinically correct figure used on boards. Harrison's Table 337-7 caption shows "7–10%" but the chapter narrative explicitly states "PSC patients have a 10–15% lifetime risk of developing cholangiocarcinoma and then cannot be transplanted." Use 10–15% on boards. More importantly: cholangiocarcinoma DISQUALIFIES the patient from liver transplant.
Board Case: A UC patient with PSC is told she has a 7–8 cm dominant biliary stricture. ERCP shows features suspicious for cholangiocarcinoma. What does this mean for transplant candidacy? → If cholangiocarcinoma is confirmed, she cannot receive a liver transplant. This is the most devastating complication of PSC.
TRAP 15 — Pseudopolyps Are a UC-Only Finding
🎯 THE TRAP: "This patient has pseudopolyps on colonoscopy — it must be UC."
📚 THE TRUTH: Pseudopolyps can form in both UC AND CD. Harrison's explicitly states: "As in UC, pseudopolyps can form in CD." They represent epithelial regeneration after ulceration — the same mechanism in both diseases. Pseudopolyps are also a risk factor for CRC in UC. Their presence in UC patients increases cancer surveillance priority.
Board Case: A patient with skip lesions, granulomas on biopsy, and perianal fistulas has pseudopolyps on colonoscopy. Does this change the diagnosis to UC? → NO — pseudopolyps occur in CD as well. The other features (skip lesions, granulomas, perianal disease) confirm CD.
TRAP 16 — All Biologic Patients Need Only TB + Hepatitis B Testing
🎯 THE TRAP: "Before starting infliximab, check TB and Hep B — that's all the pre-treatment testing needed."
📚 THE TRUTH: All biologic patients (anti-TNF, vedolizumab, ustekinumab, risankizumab) need: (1) TB testing, (2) Hepatitis B testing (HBsAb, HBsAg, HBcAb), AND for ongoing maintenance: (3) Influenza vaccine, (4) Pneumococcal vaccine, (5) Hepatitis B vaccine if not immune, (6) Yearly skin check (Harrison's Table 337-9). For JAK inhibitors and ozanimod: additionally Shingrix BEFORE starting.
Board Case: An IBD patient starting vedolizumab is asked which vaccines are recommended. The correct answer includes: Influenza, pneumococcal, Hep B (if not immune) as maintenance vaccines — not just pre-treatment TB/Hep B screening.
TRAP 17 — 5-ASA Is Always Safe to Continue in IBD
🎯 THE TRAP: "My IBD patient on mesalamine has new-onset shortness of breath and dry cough. It must be the IBD."
📚 THE TRUTH: 5-ASA agents can rarely cause interstitial lung disease. Harrison's explicitly states: "Stop 5-ASA agents as they can rarely cause interstitial lung disease." This is listed under Cardiopulmonary EIMs of IBD. 5-ASA agents rarely can also cause: renal impairment, hematuria, pancreatitis, and paradoxical worsening of colitis.
Board Case: A patient on long-term sulfasalazine for UC develops progressive dyspnea and bilateral infiltrates on chest X-ray. Most appropriate next step? → STOP sulfasalazine and evaluate for 5-ASA-induced interstitial lung disease. Renal function + urinalysis should be checked yearly on any 5-ASA agent.
TRAP 18 — IPAA Is Safe in Pregnancy Without Any Concerns
🎯 THE TRAP: "The patient had IPAA surgery and is now pregnant — no special concerns."
📚 THE TRUTH: IPAA patients have specific pregnancy complications: (1) Increased nighttime stool frequency during pregnancy — this is common and resolves postpartum; (2) Women with IPAA may consider cesarean delivery due to ↑ risk of future fecal incontinence; (3) Ileostomy patients: up to 8% develop transient small-bowel obstruction or ileus during pregnancy.
Board Case: A pregnant UC patient who previously underwent IPAA reports 8–10 loose stools per night. The most likely explanation? → Increased nighttime stool frequency is expected and normal in IPAA patients during pregnancy — it resolves postpartum without intervention.
TRAP 19 — The String Sign Means Permanent Stricture
🎯 THE TRAP: "A CD patient has a string sign on barium study — they need immediate surgery for fixed stricture."
📚 THE TRUTH: The string sign results from a severely narrowed loop of bowel where contrast resembles a frayed cotton string. It is caused by incomplete filling due to edema, irritability, and spasms. Harrison's states it "may be seen in both nonstenotic and stenotic phases of the disease." A string sign does NOT automatically mean fibrotic stricture — it can represent reversible inflammatory edema. Fibrotic strictures are treated surgically; inflammatory narrowing may respond to medical therapy.
Board Case: A CD patient with RLQ pain and string sign on barium study. CRP is markedly elevated. Best next step? → Medical therapy first (steroids/biologics) for potentially reversible inflammatory narrowing. String sign + high CRP = active inflammation, not necessarily fixed stricture.
TRAP 20 — Collagenous and Lymphocytic Colitis Can Be Distinguished Endoscopically
🎯 THE TRAP: "Colonoscopy shows grossly inflamed mucosa — this rules out microscopic colitis."
📚 THE TRUTH: Both collagenous colitis and lymphocytic colitis have NORMAL endoscopic appearance. Diagnosis requires biopsy. Key differences: Collagenous = F:M 9:1; subepithelial collagen deposition; risk: NSAIDs, PPIs, beta blockers. Lymphocytic = equal M:F; NO subepithelial collagen; additional risk: sertraline; associated with celiac disease in 9–27% → celiac should be excluded in all patients. Treatment: budesonide is effective for both.
Board Case: A 65-year-old woman on sertraline has chronic watery diarrhea. Colonoscopy: completely normal. Next step? → Biopsies — she may have lymphocytic colitis. Also rule out celiac disease. Consider stopping sertraline.
