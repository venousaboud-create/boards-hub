Disease Nomenclature — Know the New Names

🔄 The Rename — Why It Matters for Boards
In 2023, the field adopted new nomenclature under the umbrella term Steatotic Liver Disease (SLD). Old terms NAFLD and NASH are replaced. Almost complete overlap (~98%) exists between NAFLD/NASH and the new MASLD/MASH definitions.
Old TermNew TermDefinition

NASHMASH — Metabolic Dysfunction–Associated SteatohepatitisMASLD + inflammatory injury (steatohepatitis on biopsy)
Simple steatosis onlyMASL — Metabolic dysfunction–Associated Steatosis (without hepatitis)Fat accumulation without overt injury or inflammation

ALDALD (unchanged)Hazardous alcohol: ≥50 g/day (women); ≥60 g/day (men) — regardless of metabolic factors

Cardiometabolic Risk Factors (for MASLD Diagnosis)

At least one of the following must be present: overweight/obesity, hypertension, dyslipidemia, insulin resistance, or type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM).

Why the Rename?

  • Emphasizes that SLD in non-excessive drinkers is strongly associated with metabolic dysfunction — it is fundamentally a metabolic disease
  • Removes stigma from terms nonalcoholic (implies drinking is the only liver disease) and fatty (stigmatizing for patients)
  • Creates the new MetALD category for the grey zone of metabolic + moderate alcohol consumption
02

Epidemiology & Natural History

Prevalence

  • SLD is the most common cause of chronic liver disease in the United States and worldwide; main causes are ALD and MASLD
  • 25–30% of the general U.S. adult population has MASLD
  • At least 3–6% of those with MASLD have MASH
  • MASH found in 14% of asymptomatic patients ≥50 years undergoing colon cancer screening
  • Clinically significant hepatic fibrosis (≥F2) has increased more than twofold in the past two decades alone
  • MASLD is currently a leading indication for liver transplantation in the United States
  • Cirrhosis from MASLD: risk of primary liver cancer (HCC) = 1–2% per year
  • Decompensation rate in cirrhosis: 3–20% per year
  • MASLD is rising in parallel with the global obesity and metabolic syndrome epidemics
  • Pediatric MASLD is increasingly documented; childhood obesity is the major driver

Ethnic and racial factors influence liver fat accumulation. MASLD can occur in lean individuals — particularly common with truncal obesity and lipodystrophy (paucity of peripheral fat depots). Lean MASLD prevalence: ~4% in USA up to ~19% in Asia.

⭐ "At-Risk MASH" — Critical Concept
Once fibrosis reaches ≥F2 in a MASH patient, they are designated "at-risk MASH". This triggers:
  • More intensive monitoring
  • Consideration of pharmacotherapy (e.g., resmetirom)
  • Primary treatment goal: prevent fibrosis progression → decrease liver-related and all-cause mortality

03

Molecular & Cellular Pathogenesis

Steatosis — How Fat Accumulates in Hepatocytes

Steatotic hepatocytes accumulate triglyceride. Sources of excess hepatocyte fat:

  • (1) Dietary fatty acids — increased uptake
  • (2) Lipolysis from adipose depots — insulin resistance in peripheral adipose → increased free fatty acid release
  • (3) De novo lipogenesis (DNL) — within hepatocytes themselves, driven by fructose, saturated fat, and hyperinsulinemia
  • Impaired fatty acid oxidation → fatty acids not consumed → pool available for triglyceride synthesis increases
  • Impaired triglyceride export → triglyceride accumulates in hepatocytes
💡 Key Concept
Triglyceride accumulation per se (steatosis) does NOT damage the liver. Steatosis is a biomarker of metabolic dysfunction but is not directly toxic. It is the dysregulated processing of fatty acids into toxic intermediates (lipotoxicity) that causes MASH. The transition from steatosis to steatohepatitis requires lipotoxic stress.

Steatohepatitis — The Lipotoxicity Step

  • Lipotoxicity = lipid-associated injury from dysregulated processing of fatty acids → accumulation of toxic intermediates/by-products from lipid droplets
  • Damaged hepatocytes must be replaced → triggers regenerative responses → when these become maladaptive, functional parenchyma replaced by fibrous scar → cirrhosis
  • Malignant transformation of liver cells → HCC
  • The severity and duration of MASH dictate fibrosis and cancer risk

Key Drivers of Lipotoxicity and Steatosis

  • Fructose — drives DNL → ↑ risk for steatosis, MASH, fibrosis independent of calorie intake
  • Saturated fat (vs unsaturated fat) — more lipotoxic
  • Insulin resistance → ↑ lipolysis from adipocytes → ↑ free fatty acid substrate for hepatic triglyceride production → also → hyperinsulinemia → promotes lipid uptake, fat synthesis, fat storage (vicious cycle)
  • Gut microbiota alterations (obesity-associated): ↑ energy harvest from diet + ↑ intestinal permeability → hepatic exposure to gut-derived products → inflammatory mediators → worsen insulin resistance
  • Adipokines from obese adipose tissue → inhibit tissue insulin sensitivity
  • Uric acid production, oxidative stress, cytokine-mediated inflammation — all contribute to MASH
04

Disease Modifiers & Risk Factors

T2DM — Strongest Single Risk Factor
  • Prevalence of MASLD in T2DM: 30–75%
  • T2DM = nearly 2× increased risk for MASH with fibrosis vs normal glycemic control
  • Risk of advanced fibrosis increases with duration and severity of T2DM
  • Relationship is bidirectional: MASLD ↑ risk of T2DM by 2–5×; T2DM worsens MASLD
  • As liver disease progresses, insulin resistance worsens → T2DM control becomes harder
  • Screen all MASLD patients for T2DM
Obesity & Body Fat Distribution
  • Obesity present in 50–90% of MASLD patients
  • Can occur in lean individuals (BMI <25 kg/m² or <23 in Asian) — especially with truncal obesity or lipodystrophy
  • Android body fat (truncal/visceral fat) > gynoid fat distribution (hips/buttocks) for MASLD/MASH risk
  • Visceral adiposity → insulin resistance → ↑ free fatty acids → hepatic steatosis
Metabolic Syndrome Components
  • MASLD may precede development of metabolic syndrome — it can be the first manifestation
  • Hypertension — adds to MASH and fibrosis risk
  • Dyslipidemia — plasma lipid abnormalities are 2× more prevalent in MASLD; more atherogenic subfractions → ↑ cardiovascular risk
  • MASLD patients with MASH: 2–3× increased risk for developing metabolic syndrome
CVD & Obstructive Sleep Apnea
  • Both CVD and OSA are associated with MASLD and independently associated with MASH and fibrosis
  • MASLD is independently associated with endothelial dysfunction, ↑ carotid intimal thickness, coronary plaques
  • Screen MASLD patients for OSA and CVD (and vice versa)
Endocrine Disorders
  • Hypothyroidism — associated with MASLD (promotes obesity/metabolic syndrome)
  • Hypogonadism and GH deficiency — associated with MASLD
  • PCOS — hyperinsulinemia + excess androgens → 2–4× increased prevalence of MASLD, MASH with fibrosis, T2DM
  • Postmenopausal women have higher MASLD than premenopausal (role of estrogen/free testosterone)
Genetic & Epigenetic Factors
  • PNPLA3 I148M — impairs lipolysis of triglyceride lipid droplets; strong influence on hepatic fat, MASH severity, fibrosis
  • TM6SF2 — influences cholesterol metabolism
  • MBOAT7 — influences phospholipid metabolism
  • HSD17B13 loss-of-function — protective against MASH, fibrosis, liver cancer (localizes to lipid droplets)
  • Twin studies: genetics accounts for ~50% of cirrhosis risk
  • First-degree relatives of MASH cirrhosis patients: 12× higher risk of advanced fibrosis

Clinical Features & Diagnosis

⚠ Asymptomatic Disease
Most MASLD patients are asymptomatic. The diagnosis is often made incidentally: abnormal liver enzymes or fatty liver on imaging done for other reasons. MASLD may present with vague right upper quadrant pain, hepatomegaly, or an abnormal-appearing liver found at abdominal surgery.

Clinical Associations of MASLD

  • Obesity, T2DM, hypertriglyceridemia, hypertension, CVD
  • Chronic fatigue, mood alterations
  • Obstructive sleep apnea
  • Thyroid dysfunction, PCOS
  • Chronic pain syndrome
  • MASLD is an independent risk factor for metabolic syndrome AND T2DM

Diagnosis — Key Rules

  • MASLD suspected when steatosis is present in context of metabolic dysfunction (≥1 of 5 metabolic syndrome criteria)
  • Must exclude other causes of chronic hepatitis: viral hepatitis, autoimmune liver disease, iron/copper overload, α1-antitrypsin deficiency
  • Normal liver enzymes do NOT exclude MASLD — liver may be normal even with significant disease
  • True normal ALT: 29–33 U/L in men; 19–25 U/L in women (standard "normal ranges" are inflated by undiagnosed disease in reference populations)
  • Liver may not be enlarged; signs of liver disease may be absent; all other liver tests (ALP, bilirubin, albumin, PT) may be completely normal
  • No single blood test is specific for MASLD/MASH — diagnosis requires clinical context

Alternative Causes of Hepatic Steatosis (Differential Diagnosis)

Drug-Induced

Amiodarone, glucocorticoids, tamoxifen, methotrexate, valproate, NRTIs, tetracyclines, cocaine — all cause steatosis via distinct mechanisms

Other Liver Diseases

ALD, HCV genotype 3, Wilson's disease

Metabolic / Systemic

Lipodystrophy, bacterial overgrowth, starvation, parenteral nutrition, IBD, renal disease

Surgical Causes

Jejunoileal bypass, gastric bypass, extensive small-bowel resection, biliopancreatic diversion

Inborn Errors

Abetalipoproteinemia, glycogen storage disease, galactosemia, Wilson's disease, Wolman's disease

Pregnancy-Related

Acute fatty liver of pregnancy, HELLP syndrome, Reye's syndrome

Staging & Noninvasive Tests (NITs)

The goal of staging is to (1) distinguish MASH from steatosis and (2) identify which MASH patients have advanced fibrosis. Staging determines prognosis and drives treatment decisions.

FIB-4 Score — The First-Line Tool

FIB-4 = (Age × AST) ÷ (Platelet count × √ALT)
Based on: age, AST, ALT, and platelet count

⚠ FIB-4 has low accuracy in patients <35 or >65 years — use a secondary test in these age groups.

Secondary NITs — After FIB-4 ≥ 2.67

Vibration-Controlled Transient Elastography (VCTE / FibroScan)

  • LSM <8 kPa → rules out advanced fibrosis
  • LSM 8–12 kPa → may be associated with fibrotic MASH
  • LSM >12 kPa → high likelihood of advanced fibrosis (though positive predictive value is low: 0.34–0.71)
  • ≥20% increase in LSM may indicate disease progression
  • False elevation factors: obesity, nonfasting state, severe hepatic inflammation, hepatic congestion, iron overload — reduces specificity; interpret with caution

ELF Panel (Enhanced Liver Fibrosis)

  • Proprietary blood test: hyaluronic acid + tissue inhibitor of metalloproteinase-1 + N-terminal procollagen III peptide
  • FDA-approved for prognostication in MASLD
  • ELF ≥9.8 → increased risk of progression to cirrhosis and liver-related events
  • ELF >11.8 → suspect cirrhosis
  • Use when access to VCTE is limited

MRI-Based Tools (Specialist Setting)

  • MRE (magnetic resonance elastography) — most accurate elastography for fibrosis
  • MRI-cT1 (corrected T1) — additional MRI fibrosis marker
  • MRI-PDFF — most accurate noninvasive fat quantification; superior to CAP; not point-of-care
  • ≥30% reduction in MRI-PDFF liver fat → 5× improved likelihood of MASH resolution

Liver Biopsy — When is it Indicated?

  • Indeterminate or discordant NITs
  • Clinical suspicion of advanced disease not confirmed by NITs
  • Competing or concomitant diagnoses (iron overload, autoimmune hepatitis, DILI)
  • Persistent elevated liver enzymes (>6 months) without steatosis or metabolic risk factors
  • Limitations: sampling variability, inter/intraobserver variability, single time point (static), impractical at population level

Treatment

Lifestyle Modification — The Foundation

✓ Cornerstone of All MASLD Management
Healthy diet and routine physical activity are foundational for ALL MASLD patients regardless of weight loss goals or disease stage. No pharmacotherapy replaces lifestyle modification.
  • 3–5% weight loss → improves hepatic steatosis
  • >10% weight loss → improves MASH and fibrosis
  • <10% of patients achieve effective weight loss despite structured interventions at 1 year
  • In advanced fibrosis/cirrhosis: moderate-to-high intensity exercise may decrease portal hypertension, sarcopenia, and HCC risk

Dietary Recommendations

  • Avoid excess saturated fats, refined carbohydrates, and sugar-sweetened beverages ("Western obesogenic diet")
  • Fructose — independently increases MASLD/MASH/fibrosis risk regardless of total calorie intake; limit fructose consumption
  • Mediterranean diet — often recommended: long-term adherence, culturally adaptable, cardiovascular benefits
  • Coffee ≥3 cups/day — epidemiologic evidence supports reduced risk of hepatic fibrosis and HCC
  • Mediterranean green diet, low-fat/low-carbohydrate, and intermittent fasting are comparably effective

Exercise

  • Moderate exercise: ≥5 times/week for total ≥150 min/week
  • More intensive exercise: ≥60 min/week
  • Dose-dependent benefit; aerobic vs resistance can be individualized
  • Improves insulin sensitivity and cardiometabolic risk independent of weight loss

Pharmacologic Therapies

Resmetirom — The First FDA-Approved Liver-Directed Therapy

⭐ Resmetirom (Rezdiffra) — New for Boards
  • Mechanism: Selective thyroid hormone receptor beta (THR-β) agonist — mimics thyroid hormone in the liver → promotes lipid metabolism, reduces liver fat, improves liver function
  • Indication (FDA-approved): "At-risk MASH" — MASH with F2–F3 fibrosis (not yet cirrhosis)
  • Approval status: Accelerated approval
  • MAESTRO-NASH trial (52 weeks, n=966, biopsy-proven NASH F1–F3 without cirrhosis):
    • NASH resolution: resmetirom 100 mg = 30%; 80 mg = 26%; placebo = 10% (p<0.0001)
    • Fibrosis improvement: resmetirom 100 mg = 26%; 80 mg = 24%; placebo = 14% (p<0.0001)
  • Side effects: Early transient GI symptoms — nausea, loose stools (predominant treatment-emergent effects; generally well tolerated)
  • Ongoing MAESTRO-NASH study for long-term safety and liver-related outcomes (full FDA approval pending)

GLP-1 Receptor Agonists (Incretin Mimetics)

  • Mechanism: Act on pancreas to optimize insulin/glucagon release; improve lipids, glucose, and weight; decrease cardiovascular risk
  • Approved for obesity and T2DM — benefit MASH via weight loss and insulin sensitization
  • Liraglutide (small study): improved steatosis, resolved NASH, reduced fibrosis progression vs placebo
  • Semaglutide (n=320, F1–F3, 72 weeks): NASH resolution without worsening of fibrosis — 59% (0.4 mg) vs 17% placebo (p<0.001); no statistically significant fibrosis improvement, but protective against progression. Phase 3 trial ongoing.
  • Tirzepatide (GLP-1/GIP dual agonist): weight loss up to 20.9% at 72 weeks vs 3.1% placebo; significant reductions in liver fat content
  • GLP-1RAs largely have superseded pioglitazone in clinical practice

Pioglitazone (Thiazolidinedione, PPAR-γ agonist)

  • Improves insulin resistance and serum lipids; approved for T2DM
  • PIVENS trial: pioglitazone 30 mg/day × 18 months in NASH without prediabetes/T2DM → resolved NASH vs placebo (47% vs 21%, p<0.001)
  • Also showed benefit in NASH with prediabetes/T2DM (NAS score improvement)
  • Side effects: weight gain, bone loss in postmenopausal women, potential worsening of heart failure, debated bladder cancer risk

Vitamin E

  • Antioxidant (addresses oxidative stress pathway)
  • PIVENS trial: vitamin E 800 IU/day × 96 weeks → improved histologic steatohepatitis vs placebo (43% vs 19%, p<0.04) in adults without prediabetes/T2DM
  • No established antifibrotic effect
  • Retrospective study: in NASH with advanced fibrosis, vitamin E → lower hepatic decompensation and higher transplant-free survival
  • Safety concerns dampen enthusiasm: perioperative bleeding, hemorrhagic stroke, increased cardiac mortality, prostate cancer risk with long-term use

SGLT-2 Inhibitors

  • ↑ urinary glucose excretion; mild weight loss (2–3%); cardioprotective benefits; approved for T2DM
  • Limited histologic endpoint data in MASH; available data suggest improvement in aminotransferases and quantitative steatosis
  • Whether this translates to MASH resolution or fibrosis improvement remains to be defined

Metformin

  • Improves hepatic insulin sensitivity; used in T2DM
  • Not recommended as treatment for NASH/MASH — did NOT improve liver histology in TONIC study (children) or most adult trials
  • May decrease HCC risk in T2DM (meta-analyses)

Statins

  • Treat dyslipidemia and ↓ cardiovascular risk — safe across the entire spectrum of MASLD, including compensated cirrhosis
  • ↓ cardiovascular morbidity/mortality; underutilized in MASLD despite strong evidence of safety
  • Beneficial effects: decrease portal pressure → ↓ risk of hepatic decompensation; may decrease risk of cirrhosis and HCC
  • Liver enzyme elevations in MASLD patients on statins = same rate as healthy controls
  • Justified even without proven histologic MASH benefit given cardiovascular benefit and safety
  • Use caution in decompensated cirrhosis (limited data)

Drugs That Do NOT Benefit MASH (Should Not Be Used)

🚫 These Are Well-Studied But Ineffective
  • Ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA) — does not offer meaningful histologic benefit in NASH/MASH
  • Betaine — no meaningful histologic benefit
  • Dipeptidylpeptidase-4 (DPP-4) inhibitors — insulin-sensitizing, but no histologic MASH benefit
  • Silymarin (milk thistle) — no meaningful histologic benefit

Bariatric Surgery

  • Indications: BMI ≥40 (any), or BMI ≥35 with comorbidities (T2DM, hypertension, OSA)
  • Cures T2DM in majority; improves MASH and hepatic fibrosis; ↓ liver-related and all-cause mortality
  • Prospective study: bariatric surgery resolved MASH histology and improved fibrosis in 80% at 1 year; maintained in those sustaining weight loss at 5 years
  • Malabsorptive procedures (e.g., Roux-en-Y gastric bypass) > restrictive procedures (less weight loss)
  • Cannot be considered primary salvage therapy for compensated MASH cirrhosis
  • Decompensated cirrhosis + bariatric surgery = ↑ mortality; only at high-volume centers with combined liver transplant expertise
  • Weight regain after surgery → persistent MASH

Liver Transplantation

  • Indicated for end-stage MASLD
  • Outcomes generally good, but comorbidities (obesity, T2DM, CVD) often limit transplant candidacy
  • MASLD may recur after transplantation
  • Risk factors for recurrent or de novo MASLD post-transplant: hypertriglyceridemia, obesity, diabetes mellitus, and immunosuppressive therapies (especially glucocorticoids)

Exam Traps

Trap 01Normal liver enzymes do NOT exclude MASLD
A 52-year-old with T2DM and BMI 32 is found to have hepatic steatosis on ultrasound during an abdominal workup. Her ALT is 28 U/L (lab reference range: "normal ≤40"). Is MASLD excluded?
No — normal liver enzymes do NOT exclude MASLD. The standard lab "normal" ranges for aminotransferases are inflated because they include values from people with undiagnosed liver disease. The true normal ALT is 29–33 U/L in men and 19–25 U/L in women. Even with normal ALT, MASLD can be present and can be at advanced stages. The diagnosis is rendered by the combination of steatosis + metabolic risk factor(s) + exclusion of other causes.
Trap 02Lean MASLD — overweight is NOT required
A 38-year-old Asian man with BMI 22 is found to have elevated ALT and fatty liver on imaging. He has no known metabolic syndrome. Can he have MASLD?
Yes — lean MASLD is well-established. Although MASLD typically occurs with overweight/obese status, it can occur in lean individuals (BMI <25 kg/m²; <23 in Asians). It is particularly common with truncal obesity and lipodystrophy (paucity of peripheral fat depots). In Asia, lean MASLD prevalence can be as high as 19%. Lean MASLD patients still have visceral adiposity, insulin resistance, and metabolic comorbidities despite normal BMI.
Trap 03Steatosis alone does NOT damage the liver — it is lipotoxicity that does
A board question states that "triglyceride accumulation in hepatocytes is directly hepatotoxic." Is this correct?
No — triglyceride accumulation per se does NOT damage the liver. Steatosis (triglyceride accumulation) is a relatively safe storage form of lipids and is not directly toxic. It is a biomarker of metabolic dysfunction. It is the dysregulated processing of fatty acids into toxic intermediates — lipotoxicity — that causes MASH (steatohepatitis). This distinction is fundamental: MASL (simple steatosis) has negligible 10-year liver-related morbidity; MASH (with lipotoxicity) drives fibrosis progression.
Trap 04T2DM is the STRONGEST risk factor — not obesity alone
Which is the strongest single risk factor for MASLD acquisition, fibrosis progression, and HCC in MASLD?
Type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM). Not obesity. T2DM confers a nearly twofold increased risk for MASH with fibrosis, and the probability of advanced fibrosis increases with the duration and severity of T2DM. The relationship is bidirectional: MASLD increases T2DM risk by 2–5×, and T2DM worsens MASLD. All MASLD patients should be screened for T2DM. As liver disease progresses, insulin resistance worsens further, creating a vicious cycle.
Trap 05Metformin — do NOT use for MASH treatment
A patient with T2DM and biopsy-proven MASH (F2) is initiated on metformin for glycemic control. Should this count as MASH treatment?
No — metformin is not recommended as treatment for MASH. Despite improving hepatic insulin sensitivity, metformin did NOT improve liver histology in the TONIC study (children) or most adult NASH trials. It is appropriate to use for glycemic control in T2DM with MASLD, but it is not a liver-directed MASH therapy. For at-risk MASH (F2–F3), the appropriate liver-directed pharmacotherapy is resmetirom (FDA-approved). GLP-1RAs and pioglitazone have histologic evidence of benefit.
Trap 06FIB-4 is unreliable at extremes of age
A 68-year-old patient with MASLD has a FIB-4 of 1.6 (intermediate risk). Should they be referred to a specialist?
A secondary NIT is needed before deciding, because FIB-4 has low accuracy in patients >65 years AND <35 years. In older patients, FIB-4 frequently overestimates fibrosis risk because age is directly in the numerator of the formula. The algorithm specifically recommends performing a secondary NIT (VCTE or ELF) in these age groups before proceeding with specialist referral or biopsy decisions. Do not make management decisions based on FIB-4 alone in patients outside the 35–65 age range.
Trap 07Resmetirom — F2–F3 only, not cirrhosis
A patient with MASH and F4 cirrhosis asks about the new FDA-approved MASH drug. Can resmetirom be used?
No — resmetirom is indicated only for at-risk MASH with F2–F3 fibrosis, NOT in patients with cirrhosis (F4). The MAESTRO-NASH trial explicitly enrolled patients with fibrosis that had NOT yet advanced to cirrhosis. The accelerated FDA approval is for F2–F3 disease. Patients with F4 (cirrhosis) require cirrhosis-based management — varices screening per Baveno criteria, biannual HCC surveillance, and evaluation for liver transplantation.
Trap 08UDCA — commonly used but should NOT be used for MASH
A hepatologist prescribes UDCA to a patient with biopsy-proven MASH because "it helps cholestatic liver disease." Is this appropriate?
No — UDCA does not offer meaningful histologic benefit in NASH/MASH. Despite being well-studied and effective in primary biliary cholangitis (a cholestatic liver disease), UDCA is specifically listed as a drug that should NOT be used for MASH. The same applies to betaine, DPP-4 inhibitors, and silymarin. The confusion arises because UDCA is effective in other liver conditions, but the evidence is clear that it provides no meaningful histologic improvement in MASH.
Trap 09MASLD can cause HCC even WITHOUT cirrhosis
A 58-year-old with biopsy-proven MASH (F2 fibrosis, no cirrhosis) asks whether HCC surveillance is needed. What is the correct answer?
MASLD may be a premalignant condition, increasing risk for primary liver cancers even in noncirrhotic livers. Standard HCC surveillance (ultrasound + AFP every 6 months) is triggered by the diagnosis of cirrhosis. However, for noncirrhotic MASH, the question is evolving. The key board fact is: HCC can occur in MASLD WITHOUT cirrhosis. In practice, biannual HCC surveillance is formally recommended once cirrhosis is confirmed — but the risk exists before cirrhosis and is higher than in the general population.
Trap 10MASLD recurs after liver transplantation
A patient with end-stage MASH undergoes successful liver transplantation. Five years later, liver enzymes are elevated and biopsy shows steatohepatitis. What is happening?
Recurrent MASLD after liver transplantation. MASLD is a systemic metabolic disease — transplanting a healthy liver does not cure the underlying metabolic dysfunction. MASLD recurs (or can develop de novo) post-transplant. Risk factors for recurrent/de novo MASLD: hypertriglyceridemia, obesity, T2DM, and immunosuppressive therapies (especially glucocorticoids). Post-transplant metabolic management is critical to prevent disease recurrence and protect graft function.
