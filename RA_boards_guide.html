Overview & Introduction

Definition
Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) is a chronic inflammatory disease characterized by a symmetric, erosive polyarthritis. It is the most common form of chronic inflammatory arthritis. Persistently active RA → articular cartilage and bone destruction and functional disability. RA is a systemic disease with multiple extra-articular manifestations.

Why Early Aggressive Treatment Is Essential

  • Delays in starting effective treatment are associated with worse outcomes
  • Reaching low disease activity and remission is now an achievable goal for most patients
  • Primary care practitioners must refer early to rheumatologist — early referral is key
  • The past 20 years have seen remarkable improvement in RA outcomes — the crippling arthritis of years past is encountered much less frequently

Key Biomarkers

Rheumatoid Factor (RF)

IgM isotype most commonly measured. Found in 75% of RA patients. Lacks diagnostic specificity — also found in Sjögren's, SLE, cryoglobulinemia, SBE, hepatitis B/C. Present in 1–5% of healthy population. Has prognostic significance.

Anti-CCP / ACPA

Anti-citrullinated protein antibodies. Same sensitivity as RF (~75%) but specificity ~95% — far superior. Positive anti-CCP in early inflammatory arthritis is highly useful for distinguishing RA. Shows the most value for predicting worse outcomes.

Seropositivity
~75% of RA patients are seropositive (positive RF and/or ACPA). The remaining ~25% are seronegative. There is incremental value in testing BOTH RF and anti-CCP — some patients are positive for RF but negative for anti-CCP and vice versa.

Extra-articular Manifestations — Who Gets Them?

Develop in up to 40% of patients, even prior to onset of arthritis. Risk factors for extra-articular disease:

  • History of cigarette smoking
  • Early onset of significant physical disability
  • Positive test for serum RF or ACPA

Most frequent extra-articular manifestations: subcutaneous nodules, Sjögren's disease, ILD, pulmonary nodules, anemia. Recent studies show a decrease in incidence/severity of many extra-articular manifestations (especially Felty's syndrome and vasculitis) — attributed to early aggressive therapy.

Clinical Features — Joints & Deformities

Onset Pattern

  • Incidence increases between ages 25–55, then plateaus until 75, then decreases
  • Presenting symptoms: inflammation of joints, tendons, and bursae
  • Morning stiffness lasting >1 hour, eases with physical activity
  • Initial pattern: monoarticular, oligoarticular (≤4 joints), or polyarticular (>5 joints), usually symmetric
  • Some present with undifferentiated inflammatory arthritis — those most likely to later be diagnosed with RA have higher tender/swollen joint counts, positive RF or ACPA, and higher disability scores

Joint Distribution

Most Frequently Involved in Established RA
Wrists + MCP + PIP joints — symmetric involvement. DIP joint involvement in RA patients is usually due to coexistent osteoarthritis, not RA itself. This is a board-testable distinction.
  • Flexor tendon tenosynovitis: hallmark of RA → decreased ROM, reduced grip strength, "trigger" fingers
  • Most common tendon to rupture: flexor pollicis longus
  • MTP joint involvement: early feature; lateral 5th MTP often targeted first on x-ray
  • Large joints (knees, shoulders): often affected in established disease; may be asymptomatic for many years
  • Ankle/midtarsal regions → pes planovalgus ("flat feet") — usually later

Cervical Spine

Atlantoaxial Involvement
RA can involve C1 on C2 (atlantoaxial subluxation) → compressive myelopathy. Neurologic manifestations rarely the presenting sign but may slowly evolve. Prevalence is now <10% of patients (declining due to better therapy). Unlike spondyloarthritis, RA rarely affects the thoracic and lumbar spine.

Clinical Course

  • 10% undergo spontaneous remission within 6 months (especially seronegative patients)
  • The vast majority: persistent, progressive disease that waxes and wanes
  • A minority: intermittent explosive attacks with periods of quiescence
  • Mortality: 2x the general population; life expectancy shortened by 7 years for men, 3 years for women
  • High-risk for shortened survival: systemic extraarticular involvement, low functional capacity, low SES, low education, chronic prednisone use
  • HAQ disability worsens over time with poorly controlled disease
  • Historically >50% unable to work 10 years after onset; improved with newer therapies

Extra-articular Manifestations

Constitutional Symptoms

  • Weight loss, fever, fatigue, malaise, depression, cachexia (most severe cases)
  • Reflect high degree of inflammation; may precede onset of joint symptoms
  • Fever >38.3°C at any time → raise suspicion for systemic vasculitis or infection

Subcutaneous Nodules

  • Reported in 30–40% of patients (declining prevalence with modern therapy)
  • More common with: highest disease activity, positive RF, shared epitope (SE), radiographic erosions
  • Character: firm, nontender, adherent to periosteum/tendons/bursae
  • Sites: forearm, sacral prominences, Achilles tendon (areas of repeated trauma)
  • Also occur in: lungs, pleura, pericardium, peritoneum
  • Typically benign; can be associated with infection, ulceration, gangrene
  • Accelerated growth of smaller nodules in up to 10% of patients on long-term methotrexate

Pulmonary Manifestations

Pleuritis — MOST COMMON Pulmonary Manifestation

Pleuritic chest pain and dyspnea; pleural friction rub; pleural effusion on x-ray. Effusions are exudative with increased monocytes and neutrophils.

ILD — Interstitial Lung Disease

Dry cough + progressive SOB. Associated with smoking. Prevalence up to 12%; diagnosed in 3.5% before joint symptoms. Diagnosis: high-resolution CT showing infiltrative opacification or ground-glass opacities in periphery of both lungs. Confers poor prognosis.

  • Pulmonary nodules: common; solitary or multiple
  • Caplan's syndrome: rare subset — pulmonary nodules + pneumoconiosis after silica exposure
  • Less commonly: respiratory bronchiolitis and bronchiectasis
RA-ILD Treatment Challenge
RA-ILD is treated with high-dose corticosteroids + adjunctive immunosuppression (azathioprine, mycophenolate mofetil, or rituximab). Challenging because methotrexate and leflunomide themselves can cause pulmonary toxicity.

Cardiac Manifestations

Pericarditis — MOST COMMON Cardiac Manifestation

Clinical pericarditis in <10%. BUT pericardial involvement detectable by echo/autopsy in nearly half of cases. Asymptomatic pericardial effusions on echo: up to 20%.

Cardiomyopathy

From necrotizing or granulomatous myocarditis, CAD, or diastolic dysfunction. May be subclinical — identified only by echo or cardiac MRI.

CVD — Cardiovascular Disease

Most common cause of DEATH in RA. Coronary artery disease + carotid atherosclerosis higher than general population even after controlling for traditional risk factors. CHF: ~2x higher rate in RA vs general population.

  • Heart muscle may contain rheumatoid nodules or be infiltrated with amyloid (rare)
  • Elevated serum inflammatory markers → increased cardiovascular risk

Vasculitis

  • Incidence now <1% of patients (significantly decreased)
  • Occurs in: long-standing disease + positive RF/ACPA + hypocomplementemia
  • Cutaneous signs: petechiae, purpura, digital infarcts, gangrene, livedo reticularis, large painful lower-extremity ulcerations
  • Vasculitic ulcers can mimic venous insufficiency ulcers
  • Mononeuritis multiplex: sensorimotor polyneuropathy associated with systemic rheumatoid vasculitis; presents with numbness, tingling, or focal muscle weakness

Hematologic Manifestations

Anemia

  • Most common hematologic abnormality in RA
  • Normochromic, normocytic anemia (anemia of chronic disease)
  • Degree of anemia parallels degree of inflammation (correlates with CRP and ESR)
  • Thrombocytosis: platelets elevated as acute-phase reactant; immune-mediated thrombocytopenia is rare

Felty's Syndrome

  • Clinical triad: neutropenia + splenomegaly + nodular RA
  • Seen in <1% of patients (declining incidence)
  • Typically occurs in late stages of severe RA; more common in whites
  • T-LGL leukemia: similar presentation (neutropenia + splenomegaly); chronic indolent clonal LGL growth; can develop EARLY in RA course (unlike Felty's = late)
  • Leukopenia outside of these disorders = usually drug side effect

Lymphoma

  • Large cohort studies: 2–4× increased risk of lymphoma compared to general population
  • Most common type: Diffuse large B-cell lymphoma
  • Risk increases with: high disease activity or Felty's syndrome

Osteoporosis

  • Incidence nearly double age- and sex-matched healthy population
  • Prevalence approximately one-third in postmenopausal women with RA
  • Increased risk of fragility fracture, especially in women
  • Causes: inflammatory milieu activates osteoclasts; both trabecular and cortical bone affected (cortical more susceptible); chronic glucocorticoids; disability-related immobility
  • Hip fractures in RA: significant predictor of increased disability and mortality

Epidemiology & Genetics

Epidemiology

  • RA affects ~0.5–1% of adult population worldwide
  • Overall incidence decreasing; prevalence stable (patients living longer)
  • Female:male = 2–3:1 (some Latin American/African countries: 6–8:1)
  • Highest prevalence: Yakima, Pima, Chippewa Native American tribes (~7%)
  • Lower prevalence: Africa and Asia (0.2–0.4%)

Genetics — The Shared Epitope

Core Genetic Concept
The alleles conferring greatest RA risk are in the MHC class II region, specifically allelic variation in HLA-DRB1 (encodes MHC class II β-chain). Disease-associated HLA-DRB1 alleles share an amino acid sequence at positions 70–74 in the third hypervariable region of the HLA-DRβ chain — termed the shared epitope (SE). This site sits in the antigen-binding groove. Heritability estimates: 40–60% (higher in ACPA-positive patients).
SE Alleles — Risk Levels

High risk: HLA-DRB1*0401
Moderate risk: *0101, *0404, *1001, *0901
Protective: HLA-DRB1*1301 and *1302 — confer protection from ACPA-positive RA
>90% of RA patients express ≥1 risk variant

SE Risk Quantification

Carrying 1 SE allele → 4× higher risk of RA vs SE-negative
Carrying 2 SE alleles → 8× higher risk
SE carriership also associated with ACPA production and worse disease outcomes

Post-translationally modified proteins (via citrullination, acetylation, carbamylation) bind with greater avidity to the SE — providing a molecular explanation for increased disease risk.

Epigenetics
Heritable traits affecting gene expression without modifying DNA sequence. Involved in: disease etiology, perpetuation of chronic inflammation, disease severity. Mechanisms: DNA methylation, posttranslational histone modifications, microRNAs. Smoking-related ACPA-positive RA patients with the SE have higher DNA methylation levels than nonsmokers.

Environmental Factors

Smoking — Most Reproducible Environmental Link

Critical Board Numbers
SE alleles alone: 4–6× increased RA risk
Smoking alone: increases risk
SE alleles + smoking combined: 20–40× increased risk
Women who smoke: 2.5× greater risk; persists 15 years after smoking cessation. Smoking risk almost exclusively related to RF and ACPA-positive disease.
Smoking cessation does NOT reduce the severity or extent of joint inflammation.
  • Inhalant-related occupations and silica inhalants also increase RA risk
  • Theory: subclinical lung disease may be a critical early step — autoantibodies appear more than a decade before clinical joint disease
  • Smoking → increased citrullination of proteins in bronchoalveolar fluid → PADI expression in lung → neoepitopes → self-reactivity → immune complexes → joint inflammation

Microbiome

  • Porphyromonas gingivalis: the only oral bacterial species with peptidyl arginine deiminase (PAD) enzyme → catalyzes citrullination → induces ACPA. Link between periodontitis, ACPA-positive RA, and smoking.
  • Gut microbiome: Prevotella copri enriched in early untreated RA and "at-risk" population

Infectious Agents

  • EBV (Epstein-Barr virus): most interest among viruses; IgG antibodies against EBV significantly higher in RA; EBV DNA found in synovial fluid and synovial cells of RA patients. Evidence is largely circumstantial.

Pathology & Pathogenesis

Pathologic Hallmarks

Three Core Pathologic Findings
(1) Synovial inflammation and proliferation  |  (2) Focal bone erosions  |  (3) Thinning of articular cartilage
  • Synovial membrane normally thin — consists of type A synoviocytes (macrophage-derived) + type B synoviocytes (fibroblast-derived). Fibroblasts are most abundant — produce collagen, fibronectin, laminin.
  • Chronic inflammation → synovial lining hyperplasia + formation of pannus: thickened cellular membrane of fibroblast-like synoviocytes + granulation-reactive fibrovascular tissue → invades underlying cartilage and bone
  • Inflammatory infiltrate: T cells (30–50%), B cells, plasma cells, dendritic cells, mast cells, granulocytes. May form lymphoid follicles and germinal center-like structures.
  • Bone erosion mediated by osteoclasts at the pannus-bone interface (resorption lacunae)
  • Bone erosions at radial sites of MCP joints — where synovial membrane inserts into periosteal surface near articular cartilage; at attachment sites of tendons, collateral ligaments
  • Periarticular osteopenia: thinning of bony trabeculae from bone marrow inflammation; seen on MRI as signal alterations
  • Bone marrow lesions on MRI = forerunner of bone erosions

Key Cellular Players

CD4+ T Cells — Central Orchestrators

  • Signal 1: TCR binds peptide-MHC class II on APCs
  • Signal 2: CD28 binds CD80/86 on APCs (co-stimulatory signal)
  • TH1 cells: IFN-γ, lymphotoxin β, TNF-α
  • TH17 cells: IL-17, IL-21, IL-22, TNF-α, IL-26, IL-6, GM-CSF — induced by TGF-β + IL-1 + IL-6 + IL-23
  • IL-17 important for joint inflammation AND cartilage/bone destruction. BUT anti-IL-17 antibodies were NOT particularly effective in RA clinical trials
  • Treg cells: CD25+, FOXP3+; suppress inflammation via TGF-β, IL-10, IL-35. Data for suppression of RA inflammation are inconclusive.

Macrophages — The Main Cytokine Factory

  • RA is often considered a macrophage-driven disease — predominant source of proinflammatory cytokines
  • Macrophage-like synoviocytes: TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6, IL-12, IL-15, IL-18, IL-23

Synovial Fibroblasts

  • Produce: IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α; MMPs (matrix metalloproteinases — cartilage breakdown); VEGF (angiogenesis)

B Cells and Autoantibodies

  • B cells → plasma cells → RF and ACPA
  • RF forms large immune complexes inside joint → fixes complement → proinflammatory cytokines and chemokines
  • In mouse models: RF-containing + ACPA-containing immune complexes synergize to exacerbate synovial inflammatory response
  • ACPA recognized citrulline-containing regions of filaggrin, keratin, fibrinogen, vimentin; present at higher levels in joint fluid than serum

TNF-α — The Pivotal Cytokine

  • Upregulates adhesion molecules on endothelial cells → leukocyte influx
  • Stimulates production of IL-1, IL-6, GM-CSF
  • Drives osteoclastogenesis
  • Upregulates DKK-1 → inhibits Wnt pathway → suppresses bone formation

Bone Destruction vs Formation — The RANKL System

RANKL / OPG / DKK-1 Axis — High Yield
RANKL (expressed by stromal cells, synovial fibroblasts, T cells) binds RANK on osteoclast progenitors → stimulates osteoclast differentiation and bone resorption.

OPG (osteoprotegerin): decoy receptor for RANKL → blocks osteoclast formation. Wnt proteins also induce OPG formation → shut down bone resorption.

M-CSF + RANKL → monocytic cells fuse to form preosteoclasts → mature osteoclasts with ruffled membrane → secrete cathepsin K (cleaves collagen → bone matrix degradation).

DKK-1: upregulated by TNF-α → inhibits Wnt pathway → suppresses osteoblast differentiation and bone formation. In RA, both increased bone resorption AND decreased bone formation occur.

Intracellular Signaling Pathways

  • JAK/STAT — key target for JAK inhibitors
  • Syk (spleen tyrosine kinase)
  • MAPKs (mitogen-activated protein kinases)
  • NF-κB
  • JAKs are expressed in hematopoietic cells and play an important role in RA inflammatory response
  • JAK family: JAK1, JAK2, JAK3, Tyk2 — mediate signaling of IL-2, -4, -7, -9, -15, -21, IFN-γ, IL-6, IL-12, IL-23, IL-10, type I IFNs, hemopoietic cytokines

Synovial Fluid Analysis
WBC in RA synovial fluid: 5,000–50,000 WBC/μL (vs <2,000 for noninflammatory OA). Predominant cell = neutrophil (contrast to synovial tissue where T cells predominate). Most useful for: confirming inflammatory arthritis AND excluding infection or crystal-induced arthritis (gout/pseudogout).

Imaging

Plain X-Ray

Most common in practice. Initial finding: periarticular osteopenia (difficult to appreciate on digitalized films). Other findings: soft tissue swelling, symmetric joint space loss, juxtaarticular and subchondral erosions (wrists, MCPs, PIPs, MTPs). Advanced: subluxation and collapse.

MRI

Greatest sensitivity. Detects synovitis, effusions, early bone/bone marrow changes before x-ray. Bone marrow edema = early sign and predicts subsequent erosions. Limited by cost and availability.

Ultrasound + Doppler

Detects more erosions than plain x-ray (especially accessible joints). Detects synovitis (increased vascularity = inflammation). Advantages: portable, no radiation, low cost. Usefulness depends on sonographer experience.

Disease Activity Measures

Clinical Trials (Dichotomous)

ACR 20, 50, 70 improvement criteria — 20%, 50%, 70% improvement in joint counts, physician/patient global, pain scale, acute-phase reactants, HAQ disability

Clinical Practice (Continuous)

DAS (Disease Activity Score), SDAI, CDAI, RAPID3 — used for tracking disease status and documenting treatment response

ACR/EULAR Remission Definition (Table 370-3)
Option A: At any time point, ALL of: tender joint count ≤1, swollen joint count ≤1, CRP ≤1 mg/dL, patient global assessment ≤1 (on 0–10 scale)

Option B: SDAI score ≤3.3 (SDAI = tender joint count + swollen joint count + patient global + physician global + CRP in mg/dL using 28 joints)

Treatment — DMARDs & Approach

5 Principles of RA Treatment (Treat to Target)
(1) Early, aggressive therapy to prevent joint damage and disability
(2) Frequent modification of DMARD therapy to achieve treatment goals with combination therapy where appropriate
(3) Individualization of DMARD therapy — maximize response, minimize side effects
(4) Minimal use of long-term glucocorticoid therapy
(5) Achieve low disease activity or clinical remission

NSAIDs

  • Formerly core RA therapy — now adjunctive agents for symptoms uncontrolled by other measures
  • Mechanism: nonselective inhibition of COX-1 and COX-2
  • All roughly equivalent in efficacy; some individuals may prefer a particular NSAID
  • Avoid chronic use: gastritis, peptic ulcer disease, kidney injury

Glucocorticoids

  • Bridge therapy: low-to-moderate dose before DMARD fully effective (6–12 weeks)
  • Acute flares: 1–2 week burst guided by severity
  • Chronic low-dose: 5–10 mg/d prednisone for inadequate DMARD response (minimize as much as possible)
  • High dose: severe extraarticular manifestations (e.g., ILD)
  • Intraarticular: triamcinolone acetonide — limited number of inflamed joints; always exclude infection first
  • Osteoporosis prevention: oral bisphosphonates (alendronate, risedronate) OR parenteral (zoledronic acid); also denosumab, teriparatide; based on risk factors
  • No evidence-based guidelines recommend routine PPIs for GI ulcer prophylaxis with prednisone (though risk of peptic ulcer increases with concomitant NSAIDs)

Anti-TNF Contraindications
Anti-TNF agents are contraindicated in: active infection, hypersensitivity, chronic hepatitis B infection, Class III/IV congestive heart failure. All patients: screen for LTBI with PPD or IFN-γ release assay before starting. If positive for LTBI: treat for at least 1 month before starting anti-TNF therapy.

JAK Inhibitors (Targeted Synthetic DMARDs)

Why JAKi Are Important
Designed to target intracellular signaling pathways. Advantage over biologics: oral formulation. Can be used as monotherapy or with methotrexate.

JAKi Side Effects — Critical for Boards
Bacterial infections + herpes zoster. Elevated LFTs, dyslipidemia, neutropenia, hypertension, elevated creatinine.

Recent studies on tofacitinib vs TNFi: increased risk of thrombosis, major adverse cardiovascular events, and malignancies — especially in the elderly. Higher risks in elderly patients than other agents.

Pregnancy — Drug Safety Summary

Pregnancy Improvement
Up to 75% of female RA patients improve during pregnancy, but often flare shortly after delivery. Controlling disease activity during pregnancy remains a priority (active inflammation → worse pregnancy outcomes).

Elderly Patients

  • RA presents in up to one-third of patients after age 60
  • Conventional DMARDs and biologics equally effective in older vs younger patients
  • Possible exception: JAK inhibitors — higher complication risk in elderly
  • Comorbidities → increased infection risk
  • Aging → declining renal function → increased NSAIDs and methotrexate side effects
  • Methotrexate: adjust dose downward for declining renal function (mostly cleared by kidneys)

Physical Activity & Surgery

Physical Activity (ACR Guidelines)

  • All patients: prescription for exercise (150 min/week moderate-to-vigorous activity)
  • Dynamic strength training, community-based PT, physical-activity coaching → improve muscle strength, perceived health, DAS-28, inflammatory markers
  • Foot orthotics for valgus deformity; wrist splints (offset by decreased dexterity)

Surgery

  • Large joints (knee, hip, shoulder, elbow): total joint arthroplasty for advanced disease
  • Silicone MCP arthroplasty: severe decreased ROM, flexion contractures, MCP pain + radiographic abnormalities, severe ulnar drift
  • Arthrodesis and total wrist arthroplasty: severe disease with substantial pain and functional impairment
  • Hallux valgus forefoot: arthrodesis or arthroplasty; refractory hindfoot pain: primarily arthrodesis

Exam Traps

Trap 01DIP Joint Involvement = OA, Not RA
A patient with established RA presents with bilateral DIP joint swelling and tenderness. Which disease process is responsible for the DIP involvement?
Answer: Coexistent osteoarthritis — NOT RA. In RA, the most involved joints are wrists, MCPs, and PIPs. DIP joint involvement may occur in RA patients, but it is usually due to coexistent OA, not RA itself. Boards will test this. RA = spare DIP; OA/PsA = DIP involved.
Trap 02Anti-CCP vs RF — Specificity Difference is the Key
A patient has inflammatory polyarthritis. RF is positive. Anti-CCP is also positive. Which test has greater diagnostic specificity for RA? Which is better for predicting prognosis?
Answer: Anti-CCP (ACPA) — specificity ~95% vs RF which has low specificity. RF is found in many conditions (Sjögren's, SLE, cryoglobulinemia, SBE, hepatitis B/C; even 1–5% healthy population). Sensitivity of RF and anti-CCP are similar (~75%). Anti-CCP shows the most value for predicting worse outcomes. There is incremental value in testing BOTH — some patients are positive for one but not the other.
Trap 03Hydroxychloroquine is NOT a True DMARD
A patient is started on hydroxychloroquine for mild RA. Will this slow or prevent radiographic progression of joint damage?
Answer: NO — hydroxychloroquine has NOT been shown to delay radiographic progression of disease and is therefore not considered a "true" DMARD by strict definition. It is used for early, mild RA or as adjunctive therapy in combination with other DMARDs. Maximum dose: ≤5 mg/kg body weight to reduce the risk of irreversible retinal toxicity. Monitor with optical coherence tomography + visual fields every 12 months.
Trap 04Most Common Pulmonary Manifestation = Pleuritis (NOT ILD)
Which is the most common pulmonary manifestation of RA — pleuritis or ILD?
Answer: PLEURITIS is the most common pulmonary manifestation of RA. ILD is also common (prevalence up to 12%) but pleuritis comes first. Pleural effusions in RA are exudative with increased monocytes and neutrophils. ILD can appear before joint symptoms in 3.5% of patients and confers a poor prognosis. UIP pattern: honeycombing in lower lungs; NSIP: bilateral GGOs with reticulations.
Trap 05Most Common Cardiac = Pericarditis, but CLINICAL Pericarditis is Rare
What percentage of RA patients have pericardial involvement? What percentage have clinical pericarditis?
Answer: Pericardial involvement detectable by echo/autopsy in nearly HALF of cases. But clinical pericarditis occurs in <10%. Asymptomatic pericardial effusions on echocardiography: up to 20%. Most common cause of DEATH in RA is cardiovascular disease (not pericarditis), and this risk persists even after controlling for traditional cardiac risk factors.
Trap 06Smoking Risk Persists 15 Years After Cessation
A woman stopped smoking 12 years ago. Does smoking still increase her risk of RA?
Answer: YES — the increased risk from smoking persists for up to 15 years after cessation. Women who smoke have ~2.5× greater risk of RA. Smoking risk is almost exclusively related to RF and ACPA-positive disease. Critically: smoking cessation, while having many health benefits, does NOT reduce the severity or extent of joint inflammation once RA is established. SE alleles alone: 4–6× risk; combined with smoking: 20–40× risk.
Trap 07Anti-IL-17 NOT Effective in RA Despite Important Pathogenic Role
IL-17 plays an important role in RA pathogenesis. Should anti-IL-17 antibodies therefore be a good treatment for RA?
Answer: NO — therapeutic antibodies interfering with the IL-17/IL-17 receptor pathway were NOT particularly effective in clinical trials for reducing joint inflammation in RA. This is a classic boards disconnect: IL-17 is pathogenically important in RA (promotes joint inflammation + cartilage/bone destruction), but clinical trials did not show meaningful efficacy. Contrast with spondyloarthritis, where IL-17 inhibitors (secukinumab, ixekizumab) ARE approved and effective.
Trap 08Felty's = Late Severe RA; T-LGL = Can Be Early
A patient with newly diagnosed RA presents with neutropenia and splenomegaly. Is this Felty's syndrome?
Answer: More likely T-LGL leukemia — NOT Felty's syndrome. Felty's syndrome (triad: neutropenia + splenomegaly + nodular RA) typically occurs in late stages of severe, long-standing RA. T-LGL (T-cell large granular lymphocyte leukemia) has a similar presentation but can develop early in the course of RA. T-LGL = chronic indolent clonal growth of LGL cells → neutropenia + splenomegaly. If RA is new-onset, think T-LGL first.
Trap 09Rituximab — More Effective in Seropositive; Blocks Vaccine Response
A seronegative RA patient has failed two TNFi. Her physician wants to try rituximab. Is this a good choice? What vaccination issue should be considered?
Answer: Rituximab is more effective in SEROPOSITIVE than seronegative RA — consider alternative biologics (abatacept, JAKi, another mechanism) for seronegative patients who failed TNFi. Rituximab significantly inhibits vaccine response (e.g., SARS-CoV-2 vaccination) — vaccinations should ideally be updated BEFORE rituximab initiation. Also: rare PML risk (~1:25,000). Premedicate with methylprednisolone 100 mg to reduce infusion reactions.
Trap 10Anti-IL-6 → GI Perforation Risk; Avoid in Diverticulitis
A 65-year-old patient with RA and a history of diverticulitis is started on tocilizumab. What complication is this patient specifically at risk for?
Answer: Gastrointestinal perforation. Anti-IL-6 receptor agents (tocilizumab, sarilumab) have been associated with an increased rate of GI perforation and should be avoided if possible in patients with a history of diverticulitis or a recent gastric/duodenal ulcer. Also: anti-IL-6 agents increase LDL cholesterol (unknown if this increases atherosclerotic risk). Hematologic side effects (neutropenia, thrombocytopenia) are reversible upon stopping the drug.
Trap 11Pregnancy — MTX and Leflunomide Are Absolutely Contraindicated
A woman with RA on methotrexate announces she wants to become pregnant. What is the most important immediate step?
Answer: STOP methotrexate immediately — it is absolutely contraindicated in pregnancy (Pregnancy Category X). Same for leflunomide (also teratogenic). Anti-TNF agents are generally accepted as safe but should be discontinued during the third trimester per current guidelines. Hydroxychloroquine and sulfasalazine are the safest DMARDs during pregnancy. Abatacept, anti-IL-6 receptor antibodies, rituximab, and JAK inhibitors are discontinued at conception due to insufficient safety data. Active inflammatory disease = worse pregnancy outcomes — always control disease activity.
Trap 12HLA-DRB1*1301 and *1302 Are PROTECTIVE (Not Risk)
A question asks which HLA-DRB1 allele confers protection from ACPA-positive RA. What is the answer?
Answer: HLA-DRB1*1301 (and to a lesser extent *1302) confer PROTECTION from ACPA-positive RA. Memory aid: The shared epitope (SE) risk alleles are at positions *0401 (highest risk), *0101, *0404, *1001, *0901. In contrast, *1301/*1302 are protective. SE is defined by the amino acid sequence at positions 70–74 in the third hypervariable region of the HLA-DRβ1 chain. 1 SE allele = 4× risk; 2 SE alleles = 8× risk vs SE-negative individuals.
Trap 13Methotrexate Nodule Acceleration
A patient on long-term methotrexate for RA develops multiple new small subcutaneous nodules despite improved joint disease control. What is happening?
Answer: Accelerated rheumatoid nodulosis — a paradoxical side effect of methotrexate. Accelerated growth of smaller nodules occurs in up to 10% of patients taking long-term methotrexate, even when joint disease is otherwise well controlled. The mechanisms are unclear. This does NOT mean methotrexate is failing — joint disease may be well controlled. Boards may present this as a tricky scenario where the drug is working but nodules appear.
Trap 14RS3PE — Pitting Edema + Responds to Low-Dose Prednisone
An elderly patient presents with symmetric synovitis and prominent bilateral distal limb pitting edema. What condition should you consider, and how does it differ from RA?
Answer: RS3PE (Remitting Seronegative Symmetrical Synovitis with Pitting Edema). Key distinguishing features: prominent distal limb pitting edema (which is unusual in RA), typically seronegative, and responds promptly to low doses of prednisone. Paraneoplastic syndromes may also present this way — consider malignancy workup. Do NOT confuse with RA (which typically lacks pitting edema of the distal limbs).
