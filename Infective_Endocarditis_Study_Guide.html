1. Prophylaxis for Infective Endocarditis

Q1, Q2, Q6, Q8, Q21, Q29, Q30, Q33, Q39, Q42

Who Gets Prophylaxis? — High-Risk Cardiac Lesions Only (Table 133-9)

Prophylaxis is recommended ONLY for patients at highest risk for severe morbidity or death from IE. NOT recommended as widespread standard of care.

✅ High-Risk — GIVE Prophylaxis
  • Prosthetic heart valves or material (mechanical, biological, TAVI)
  • Prior endocarditis
  • Unrepaired CYANOTIC congenital heart disease, including palliative shunts/conduits
  • Completely repaired CHD within the first 6 months after repair (prosthetic material)
  • Repaired CHD with residual defects adjacent to prosthetic material
  • Surgical or transcatheter pulmonary artery valve or conduit placement
  • Valvulopathy after cardiac transplantation
  • Left ventricular assist devices (LVADs)
❌ NOT High-Risk — No Prophylaxis Needed
  • Pacemaker / CIED alone (no valve disease) Exam Trap!
  • Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM) alone — minor criterion for Duke, but NOT prophylaxis indication Exam Trap!
  • Mitral stenosis — not in high-risk list
  • MVP without significant regurgitation
  • ASD (acyanotic, unrepaired) — only CYANOTIC CHD qualifies
  • Repaired CHD after 6 months (without residual defect)
  • Hemodialysis patients (without valve disease)
Exam Pearl — Q1, Q29, Q33, Q39
  • A patient with a pacemaker alone does NOT receive prophylaxis before dental procedures
  • HCM alone does NOT receive prophylaxis (it's a predisposition for IE, but not a high-risk lesion)
  • MVP with only mild or no regurgitation does NOT require prophylaxis
  • Mitral stenosis does NOT require prophylaxis — even for invasive dental procedures
  • Post-TAVI = prosthetic valve = YES, requires prophylaxis
  • Tetralogy of Fallot repair within the last 6 months = YES; but after 6 months without residual defect = NO

When to Give Prophylaxis — Procedures

✅ Indicated Procedures
  • Manipulation of gingival tissue
  • Manipulation of periapical region of teeth
  • Perforation of oral mucosa
  • Respiratory tract surgery with incision/biopsy
❌ NOT Indicated For
  • GI tract procedures (endoscopy, sigmoidoscopy, colonoscopy)
  • GU tract procedures (cystoscopy)
  • Eye surgery (cataract, laser)
  • Skin procedures
  • Routine dental cleaning without gingival manipulation
Key Point — Q6, Q18, Q39
GU and GI procedures do NOT require prophylaxis. However, if the patient has a GU infection or skin infection, treat the infection BEFORE or AT the time of the procedure.

Drug Regimens for Prophylaxis (Table 133-8)

A. Standard Oral Regimen
  • Amoxicillin 2g PO — 1 hour before procedure
B. Cannot Take Oral Medication
  • Ampicillin 2g IV or IM — within 1 hour before procedure
C. Penicillin Allergy (can take oral)
  • Cephalexin 2g PO 1h before
  • Clarithromycin or Azithromycin 500mg PO 1h before
  • Doxycycline 100mg PO 1h before

⚠ Do NOT use cephalosporins if immediate hypersensitivity (urticaria, angioedema, anaphylaxis) to penicillin

D. PCN Allergy + Cannot Take Oral
  • Cefazolin OR Ceftriaxone 1g IV or IM — 30 min before procedure
⚠️ CLINDAMYCIN IS NO LONGER RECOMMENDED
Clindamycin has been associated with fatal and nonfatal Clostridioides difficile infections. The American Heart Association now recommends against clindamycin for IE prophylaxis. Use doxycycline or azithromycin instead for PCN-allergic patients.
Q8 — Prophylaxis for Aortic Aneurysm Surgery (Bacterial Endocarditis History, PCN Allergy)
Patient has IE history (prosthetic valve), now needs aortic aneurysm surgery, allergic to penicillin (compound allergy). For surgical prophylaxis covering oral streptococci/gut organisms → Vancomycin (IV, covers gram-positives including streptococci, in penicillin-allergic patients). Azithromycin or ciprofloxacin are NOT appropriate surgical prophylaxis for endocarditis-risk patients.

Special Case: Q Fever Prevention (C. burnetii)

In patients with aortic or mitral valve regurgitation or a prosthetic valve, treating acute Q fever for 12 months with Doxycycline + Hydroxychloroquine is highly effective in preventing C. burnetii IE.

2. Modified Duke Criteria (2023 Duke-ISCVID) — Table 133-3

Q19, Q25, Q28, Q35

The diagnosis of definite IE is established with certainty only by histology. The Duke criteria provide a validated clinical framework (sensitivity 80–93% in validated cohorts).

🔵 MAJOR CRITERIA
A. Microbiologic
  • 1. Positive blood culture with a typical IE organism in ≥2 separate sets:
    • S. aureus, S. lugdunensis, E. faecalis
    • All streptococci except S. pneumoniae and S. pyogenes
    • Granulicatella, Abiotrophia, Gemella spp.
    • HACEK group organisms
    • With intracardiac prosthetic material, also: CoNS, C. striatum, C. jeikeium, S. marcescens, P. aeruginosa, C. acnes, NTM, Candida
  • Nontypical organisms: must grow in ≥3 separate sets
  • 2. Positive lab tests:
    • PCR for C. burnetii, Bartonella spp., or T. whipplei from blood
    • Single blood culture C. burnetii OR Phase I IgG Ab titer ≥ 1:800 KEY!
    • IFA for IgM + IgG to B. henselae or B. quintana with IgG ≥ 1:800
B. Imaging
  • Echo/cardiac CT: vegetation, valvular perforation/aneurysm, abscess, pseudoaneurysm, or intracardiac fistula
  • New valvular regurgitation — requires echocardiographic evidence; new/worsening murmur alone is NOT sufficient
  • New prosthetic valve dehiscence/insufficiency
  • FDG-PET/CT: abnormal metabolic activity involving native/prosthetic valve, aortic graft, CIED leads, or prosthetic material
C. Surgical
  • Evidence of IE at direct inspection during heart surgery
🟠 MINOR CRITERIA
A. Predisposition: Prior IE, IVDU, prosthetic valve, prior valve repair, congenital heart disease (bicuspid AV), CIED, >mild regurgitation or stenosis, HCM
B. Fever: Temperature >38.0°C (100.4°F)
C. Vascular phenomenon: Arterial emboli, septic pulmonary infarcts, mycotic aneurysm, intracranial hemorrhage, conjunctival hemorrhage, Janeway lesions, cerebral/splenic abscesses, purulent purpura
D. Immunologic phenomena: Glomerulonephritis, Osler nodes, Roth spots, positive rheumatoid factor
E. Microbiologic evidence: Positive blood cultures not meeting major criteria; or positive PCR/culture from non-endovascular site
F. Imaging: Abnormal FDG-PET/CT within 3 months of prosthetic valve implantation
G. Physical exam: New valvular regurgitation on auscultation (without echo confirmation)

Q35 — Duke Criteria Calculation Example
50yo, post-biological mitral replacement, fever 38.1°C, 3/6 LSB murmur, WBC 12000, blood cultures sent (no result yet). Count what you have:
  • Predisposing condition (prosthetic valve) = 1 minor (A)
  • Fever >38°C = 1 minor (B)
  • New murmur on auscultation = 1 minor (G) — not echo-confirmed, so minor only
  • No blood culture result yet, no echo finding confirmed
With 3 minor criteria = Possible IE. If blood cultures come back positive with a typical organism = 1 Major + 3 minor = Definite IE.
Q25 — What Upgrades to Definite IE?
54yo female, fever 38.4, mitral vegetation on echo (= 1 Major imaging) + fever (1 minor). Currently "Possible." What single additional finding makes it "Definite"? Need to reach 2 major OR 1 major + 3 minor. Options:
  • Nephritic urine sediment = glomerulonephritis = 1 minor (D) — brings to 1M + 2m = still Possible
  • Conjunctival hemorrhage = vascular phenomenon = 1 minor (C) — same, still Possible
  • Coxiella Burnetti Phase II IgG ≥ 1:800 → this is a MAJOR microbiologic criterion! + existing imaging Major → 2 Major = Definite IE ✅
  • High RF = 1 minor (D) — still Possible
Answer: C. burnetii Phase II IgG ≥ 1:800 = MAJOR criterion → total 2 Major = Definite IE
Q28 — C. burnetii and Duke Criteria
50yo farmer, fever, weight loss, cardiac MRI + 2/6 murmur, no vegetation on 2/6 cardiac evaluation. C. burnetii Phase I IgG >1:800 = a MAJOR microbiologic criterion. This single finding (Phase I IgG ≥1:800) satisfies a major criterion even without a positive blood culture. Combined with MRI abnormal metabolic activity → can reach Definite IE.

3. Echocardiography — Figure 133-3 Algorithm

Q3, Q9, Q11, Q12, Q16, Q24

TTE vs. TEE — Know the Difference

Feature TTE (Transthoracic) TEE (Transesophageal)
Sensitivity for vegetations Misses 20–35% of definite IE >90% sensitivity
Vegetation size limit Cannot detect <2 mm vegetations Detects smaller vegetations
Inadequate images 20% of patients (obese, COPD, etc.) Rarely suboptimal
Prosthetic valves Not optimal (especially TAVR) Better, though TAVR-PVE still 6–18% FN
Intracardiac complications Not optimal for abscesses/fistulae Test of choice; sensitivity ≥85% for abscesses
False negative rate 20–35% 6–18% (especially TAVR-PVE)
Use in Low-risk patients, initial screening High-risk patients, suspected complications, prosthetic valves
Critical Point — Q9, Q24
  • A negative TEE does NOT exclude IE — if clinical suspicion remains high, repeat TEE in 7–10 days
  • TTE detects vegetations >2mm, not >1mm as sometimes incorrectly stated
  • PET-CT is NOT the first-line for native valve endocarditis — it is used for PVE, TAVR-PVE, CIED infection, extracardiac complications
  • New/worsening murmur on auscultation = MINOR Duke criterion (G); new regurgitation on echo = MAJOR Duke criterion (B)

When to Use PET-CT / Cardiac CTA

FDG-PET/CT — Best For:
  • Suspected PVE (including TAVR-PVE) when TEE inconclusive
  • Ascending aortic graft infection
  • CIED pocket and lead infection
  • Extracardiac complications (emboli, satellite abscesses)
  • Modifies therapy in 25% of NVE and PVE patients

⚠ False positive within 3 months of valve surgery. Requires patient preparation.

Cardiac CTA — Best For:
  • When TEE is contraindicated or not confirmatory
  • Superior to TEE for detecting pseudoaneurysm or abscess
  • Less sensitive for vegetations, valvular perforation, and paravalvular leakage than TEE
  • Can replace preoperative cardiac catheterization (coronary patency) in low-intermediate coronary risk
Q11 — MVP Patient with Enterococcus faecalis, TEE Negative
55yo MVP, Enterococcus faecalis in 2 blood cultures, TEE negative. What to recommend?
  • TEE negative ≠ IE excluded (6–18% false negative)
  • E. faecalis bacteremia with predisposing condition (MVP) and positive blood cultures = strong IE risk
  • Best answer: Complete urine and general cultures + urologic evaluation — because E. faecalis commonly enters from the GU tract and the source must be identified. Also, given TEE negative, consider repeating TEE in 7–10 days if suspicion persists.
Q12 — Pacemaker Patient, History of IE, Murmur, Fever
78yo with history of IE, now with pacemaker, fever, murmur. In an outpatient setting with no diagnosis yet: Blood cultures + TEE — Because this patient has CIED (pacemaker) + prior IE + fever + murmur = HIGH risk. TEE is the preferred initial test (not TTE) given the intracardiac device and high suspicion. Treat and assess together.

Q40 — Most Common IE 1 Month After Mitral Valve Replacement
One month post-surgery = early PVE (<2 months). The most common organism is CoNS (coagulase-negative staphylococci, e.g., S. epidermidis) — reflecting intraoperative contamination. Not viridans streptococci (which dominate community NVE).

Special Populations

PWID (IV Drug Users)
  • S. aureus — most common, often MRSA
  • Tricuspid valve in 35–60%
  • Also: Enterobacterales, Pseudomonas, Candida
  • Fever ± faint murmur; no peripheral signs in tricuspid IE
  • Septic pulmonary emboli (cough, pleuritic pain, nodular infiltrates)
CIED-IE (Pacemaker/Defibrillator)
  • S. aureus — 36%; CoNS — 41%
  • 1/3 present within 3 months of implantation
  • Fever, sepsis, minimal murmur, pulmonary emboli
  • Treatment: complete device removal + antibiotics

Portal of Entry by Organism

  • Viridans streptococci → oral cavity
  • Staphylococci → skin
  • HACEK organisms → oral cavity / upper respiratory tract Exam Pearl!
  • Enterococci → genitourinary tract (primarily E. faecalis)
  • S. gallolyticus → GI tract; associated with colonic polyps/tumors → colonoscopy required!
  • C. burnetii → animal contact (cattle, sheep); predilection for prosthetic valves
  • Bartonella → cat exposure (B. henselae) or body lice (B. quintana)

Culture-Negative IE (5–15%)

  • 1/3 to 1/2 due to prior antibiotic exposure
  • Fastidious organisms: C. burnetii, Bartonella, Granulicatella, Abiotrophia, Gemella, HACEK
  • Mimics: marantic endocarditis, antiphospholipid antibody syndrome, atrial myxoma
Q7 — Which Streptococcus Causes IE Most at High Frequency?
In high-level bacteremia, S. mutans (viridans group, from oral cavity) is the most common streptococcal cause of IE. S. pyogenes and S. pneumoniae are NOT typical IE organisms (they are specifically excluded from the "typical organisms" in Duke-ISCVID criteria).
Q9, Q31 — Know Your True/False Facts
  • True: Eikenella corrodens IS an oral pathogen (part of the HACEK group)
  • False: Blood cultures are positive in ~85–95% of IE (NOT only 20%)
  • False: TEE negative does NOT rule out IE
  • True: Clubbing occurs in 10–20% (present but not common)
  • True: AV node conduction disturbances arise from AORTIC valve paravalvular abscess (not mitral — mitral abscess is distant from conduction system)
  • True: Pericardial TEE is better for detecting MITRAL abscess than standard TTE
  • True: Coronary emboli occur in 2% → can cause myocardial infarction

5. Organism-Specific Treatment — Table 133-5

Q5, Q10, Q13, Q15, Q18, Q20, Q22, Q23, Q26, Q27, Q34, Q36, Q37, Q41, Q42

A. Streptococci (including S. gallolyticus)

Penicillin-Susceptible (MIC ≤ 0.12 μg/mL)
  • Penicillin G 2–3 mU IV q4h × 4 weeks
  • OR Ceftriaxone 2g IV daily × 4 weeks
  • OR Vancomycin 15 mg/kg q12h × 4 wks (PCN allergy)
  • Short course (2-week): Penicillin/Ceftriaxone × 2 wks + Gentamicin 3 mg/kg daily × 2 wks — NOT for PVE or complicated IE
PVE: prefer 6-week regimens
Relatively PCN-Resistant (MIC 0.12–0.5 μg/mL)
  • Penicillin G 4 mU q4h × 4 wks + Gentamicin × 2 wks
  • OR Ceftriaxone 2g daily × 4 wks + Gentamicin × 2 wks
  • OR Vancomycin × 4 wks
Moderately PCN-Resistant (MIC ≥ 0.5 μg/mL)
+ Granulicatella, Abiotrophia, Gemella
  • Penicillin G 4–5 mU q4h OR Ceftriaxone 2g daily × 6 weeks + Gentamicin 3 mg/kg daily × 6 wks
  • OR Vancomycin × 6 wks (preferred by some)
Q13 — S. gallolyticus, Resistant to All Antibiotics by Sensitivity
"Resistant to all antibiotics" in the question context means the organism was tested and found to have elevated MIC. S. gallolyticus is treated like a penicillin-susceptible streptococcus (MIC ≤0.12). The standard treatment is Penicillin G 4 weeks. Also: S. gallolyticus originates from GI tract → association with colonic polyps/tumors → always perform colonoscopy!
Q36 — S. anginosus, Post-Mitral Replacement, Still Febrile After Several Days
S. anginosus (part of anginosus group) = streptococcal species. Patient is treated with Penicillin, MIC appropriate, but still febrile. What to do? Assess the patient thoroughly and continue treatment — fever can persist for several days (up to 7 days) even with appropriate therapy. Before considering treatment failure, evaluate for paravalvular abscess, extracardiac abscess, or embolic events. Do NOT switch to Ceftriaxone or add Rifampin unless failure is confirmed.
Q42 — IE with Mechanical Aortic Valve, Dental Procedure, Best Empirical Antibiotic to Stop Bacteremia
The question asks about stopping bacteremia from oral streptococci in the context of dental work. Oral organisms = streptococci. Best synergistic bactericidal regimen for streptococcal endocarditis in prosthetic valve → Ceftriaxone + Gentamicin (the 2-week synergistic combination per Table 133-5). Ampicillin + Gentamicin and Penicillin G + Gentamicin are also valid. Ceftriaxone + Vancomycin is NOT bactericidal for streptococci (ceftriaxone alone or with gentamicin is the regimen, not with vancomycin).

B. Enterococci — The Unique Challenge

Key Concept: Why Enterococci Are Difficult
  • Resistant to oxacillin, nafcillin, and all cephalosporins
  • Only INHIBITED — not killed by cell wall–active agents (penicillin, ampicillin, vancomycin) alone
  • Require synergistic bactericidal combination to kill them
First-Line: Ampicillin + Ceftriaxone (Preferred)
  • Ampicillin 2g IV q4h + Ceftriaxone 2g IV q12h — both for 6 weeks
  • Works by expanded binding of penicillin-binding proteins → kills E. faecalis
  • Use for E. faecalis with OR without high-level gentamicin resistance
  • Preferred in patients at risk for aminoglycoside nephrotoxicity
  • NOT effective for E. faecium
Duration: NVE 4 wks if symptoms <3 months; 6 wks if >3 months. PVE always 6 wks.
Alternative: Ampicillin/Penicillin + Gentamicin
  • Penicillin G 4–5 mU q4h × 4–6 wks + Gentamicin 1 mg/kg q8h × 2–6 wks
  • OR Ampicillin 2g q4h × 4–6 wks + Gentamicin × 2–6 wks
  • Avoid if high-level gentamicin resistance (MIC ≥500 μg/mL)
PCN-Allergic Only: Vancomycin + Gentamicin
  • Vancomycin 15 mg/kg q12h × 6 wks + Gentamicin × 2–6 wks
  • Use ONLY for penicillin-allergic patients
  • Vancomycin + Ceftriaxone is NOT bactericidal for E. faecalis and is NOT recommended
Q5 — What is Synergistic with Ampicillin for Enterococci?
The synergistic combination partners are Gentamicin (aminoglycoside) OR Ceftriaxone. Oxacillin, TMP-SMX, and Ceftazidime are NOT synergistic with ampicillin against enterococci. Answer: Ceftriaxone
Q22 — Enterococcus IE, Creatinine 3 mg/dL (Renal Failure)
80yo IE with Enterococcus, creatinine = 3. Aminoglycosides (gentamicin) are nephrotoxic — avoid in renal failure. Use Ampicillin + Ceftriaxone (the gentamicin-sparing regimen) for 6 weeks. This is specifically designed for patients at risk for aminoglycoside nephrotoxicity.
Q27 — What is True About Enterococcal IE Treatment?
  • Oxacillin is NOT a good alternative to ampicillin (enterococci are resistant to oxacillin)
  • Amikacin is NOT the optimal aminoglycoside — gentamicin is; synergy with tobramycin, netilmicin, kanamycin, amikacin is unpredictable
  • Ampicillin + Ceftriaxone or Gentamicin is beneficial — TRUE
  • Linezolid is NOT first-line (static, not bactericidal)

C. Staphylococci (S. aureus and CoNS)

MSSA — Native Valve (No Foreign Device)
  • Nafcillin/Oxacillin/Flucloxacillin 2g q4h × 4–6 weeks
  • OR Cefazolin 2g q8h × 4–6 wks (for non-immediate PCN allergy)
  • Vancomycin × 4–6 wks (only for immediate/severe PCN allergy)
  • No role for routine gentamicin, rifampin, fusidic acid, or daptomycin
  • Uncomplicated right-sided MSSA IE: 2-week oxacillin/nafcillin course may suffice
MRSA — Native Valve
  • Vancomycin (15 mg/kg q8–12h) OR Daptomycin 8–10 mg/kg/day × 4–6 wks
  • Left-sided IE: minimum 6 weeks
  • Right-sided: at least 4 weeks (2-week course suboptimal for MRSA)
  • No routine rifampin for MRSA NVE
  • If vancomycin resistance (VISA/hVISA): daptomycin + nafcillin or ceftaroline
MSSA — Prosthetic Valve (PVE)
  • Nafcillin/Oxacillin/Flucloxacillin 2g q4h × 6–8 weeks
  • PLUS Gentamicin 1 mg/kg q8h × initial 2 weeks only
  • PLUS Rifampin 300 mg PO q8h × 6–8 weeks
  • ⚠ Delay rifampin until bacteremia clears (prevent resistance emergence)
MRSA — Prosthetic Valve (PVE)
  • Vancomycin × 6–8 wks + Gentamicin × 2 wks + Rifampin × 6–8 wks
  • OR Daptomycin (8–10 mg/kg) as vancomycin alternative
  • Rifampin is ESSENTIAL for PVE — kills staphylococci in biofilm on prosthetic material
Rifampin Rule — Q10, Q20, Q26, Q41
  • Rifampin for PVE = YES, ESSENTIAL — kills staphylococci in biofilm adherent to prosthetic material
  • Rifampin for NVE = NO — guidelines do NOT recommend routine rifampin for NVE (native valve)
  • Always delay rifampin until bacteremia clears + establish susceptibility to prevent emergence of resistance
Q20 — What is True About MRSA IE Treatment?
  • NOT always add rifampin — only for PVE, not NVE
  • Treatment is NOT based on oxacillin (MRSA is resistant to oxacillin by definition)
  • TRUE: When vancomycin-resistant (VISA), switch to daptomycin
  • Linezolid has limited efficacy for IE (bacteriostatic, not bactericidal)
  • Gentamicin is NOT routinely added for MRSA NVE
Q26 — MSSA PVE, Mild PCN Allergy (Rash = Non-Immediate)
70yo, MSSA prosthetic aortic valve IE, mild penicillin allergy (rash = non-immediate, non-anaphylactic). Best treatment: Cefazolin + Gentamicin + Rifampin — because: (1) non-immediate PCN allergy → cephalosporins generally safe (2) PVE requires the triple regimen. Vancomycin is reserved for immediate (urticarial/anaphylactic) penicillin allergy.
Q32 — CIED (Pacemaker), MRSA Bacteremia, No Source
70yo with pacemaker, fever, MRSA bacteremia, no external source. What to do? Remove the pacemaker + TEE + treat as IE with full antibiotic course (4–6 weeks). S. aureus bacteremia with CIED = likely CIED-IE until proven otherwise. Antibiotics alone without device removal is usually unsuccessful. After 10–14 days of effective therapy, a new device can be reimplanted at a new site.
Q41 — PVE with CoNS (S. epidermidis), Prior Aminoglycoside Course for UTI
50yo, 1 month after valve surgery, S. epidermidis (CoNS) bacteremia, received aminoglycosides for UTI. This is early PVE (CoNS = most common early PVE organism). The fact that aminoglycosides were used for UTI means the patient IS susceptible to early surgery — but also: the CoNS finding in the context of a new prosthetic valve should not be dismissed as a contaminant. The correct approach is to proceed to surgery evaluation (EARLY PVE indication for elective surgery) and continue targeted antibiotic therapy including rifampin for biofilm coverage.

D. HACEK Organisms

Haemophilus, Aggregatibacter, Cardiobacterium, Eikenella, Kingella — all from the oral cavity and upper respiratory tract.

Treatment
  • Ceftriaxone 2g IV daily × 4 weeks (preferred)
  • OR Ampicillin-sulbactam 3g q6h × 4 wks (use ampicillin alone ONLY if β-lactamase production excluded)
  • Ciprofloxacin 500mg PO q12h (if susceptible, and β-lactam not tolerated)
  • For PVE: 6-week regimens
Q19 — HCM Patient, Aggregatibacter in 2 Blood Cultures, Janeway Lesions
HACEK organisms in ≥2 blood culture sets = Major microbiologic criterion. Plus: Janeway lesions (vascular phenomenon) = minor criterion (C). Plus HCM = predisposition = minor criterion (A). Plus fever (shown in image) = minor (B). Count: 1 Major (Aggregatibacter in 2 cultures) + 3 Minor = Definite IE. The growth of Aggregatibacter in a 3rd culture is NOT needed to confirm — already at Definite IE with 1 Major + 3 Minor. Answer: Growth in 3rd culture is the WRONG answer — already definite. The actual confirming finding is the 2 positive blood cultures combined with the other criteria.

E. Coxiella burnetii (Q Fever IE)

Clinical Context — Q15, Q18, Q28, Q34, Q37
  • Fastidious intracellular organism — will NOT grow in standard blood cultures
  • Exposure: farm animals, cattle, sheep, dairy farmers, veterinarians
  • Phase I IgG Ab titer ≥ 1:800 = Duke MAJOR microbiologic criterion
  • Predilection for prosthetic valves
  • Indolent course (subacute)
Treatment — Q Fever IE
  • Doxycycline 100mg PO q12h
  • + Hydroxychloroquine 200mg PO q8h
  • Duration: ≥18 months for native valve
  • Duration: ≥24 months for prosthetic valve
  • Monitor serology: antiphase I IgG and IgA decreased 4-fold AND IgM antiphase II negative
Contraindications to Hydroxychloroquine (HCQ)
  • QTc >490 ms — HCQ prolongs the QT interval; absolute contraindication Q18 Answer!
  • Retinal disease (long-term use)
  • G6PD deficiency

If HCQ is contraindicated or not tolerated → Doxycycline alone or doxycycline + quinolone for extended duration

Q15 — 80yo Fever, Aortic Vegetation, Phase I IgG for C. burnetii >1:1000
Phase I IgG >1:800 = MAJOR criterion = Q fever IE. Next step: Doxycycline + Hydroxychloroquine for 18 months (native valve). Option 2 (Doxycycline + Gentamicin 6 wks) = treatment for Bartonella, NOT Q fever. Option 3 (Ciprofloxacin + Gentamicin + Rifampin 3 months) = treatment for Brucella. Option 1 (treat only if Phase II high) = WRONG — Phase I IgG ≥1:800 is already the criterion.
Q34 — 50yo Fever 5 Weeks, Aortic Murmur, Q Fever Phase I IgG 1:3200
Phase I IgG 1:3200 = well above 1:800 threshold = Q fever IE confirmed. Treatment: Doxycycline + Hydroxychloroquine. Do NOT use doxycycline alone (option 3), levofloxacin, meropenem, or rifampin combinations as primary Q fever IE treatment.

F. Candida and Fungal IE

Treatment
  • Lipid formulation Amphotericin B 5mg/kg IV daily ± Flucytosine 25mg/kg PO q6h
  • OR high-dose echinocandin regimen
  • Early surgery strongly advised, especially with valve dysfunction or PVE
  • Long-term (if not indefinite) oral azole suppression after definitive treatment
  • Without valve dysfunction: medical treatment + azole suppression may be comparable to surgery
Q23 — 60yo, C. albicans IE, Aortic Vegetation 7mm, Still Febrile Despite Antifungals
Candida IE is itself a surgical indication (fungal IE = surgery required). Even if responding to antifungals, long-term outcome without surgery is poor. The key point: Vegetation size alone does not mandate surgery (7mm is not >10mm threshold), but Candida IE + continued fever = the combination mandates surgical consideration. Surgery should be strongly considered even if the patient is responding if there is valve dysfunction or PVE.

G. Bartonella spp.

  • Exposure: cats (B. henselae), body lice (B. quintana)
  • Common in homeless populations
  • Treatment: Doxycycline 100mg q12h × 6 weeks + Gentamicin 1mg/kg q8h × 2 weeks

  • Some experts continue doxycycline 3–6 months unless all infection resected

6. Surgical Indications & Timing — Tables 133-6 & 133-7

Indications for Surgery (Table 133-6)

Surgery REQUIRED for Optimal Outcome
  • Moderate or severe CHF or shock due to valve dysfunction
  • Paravalvular extension: abscess, fistula, or heart block
  • Persistent bacteremia without extracardiac cause despite 7–10 days of optimal antibiotics
  • No effective antimicrobial therapy: fungal (yeasts/molds), Brucella, MDR gram-negative
  • Prosthetic valve: partially dehisced unstable prosthetic valve
Surgery STRONGLY CONSIDERED for Improved Outcome
  • PVE: S. aureus with intracardiac complications; relapse after optimal therapy
  • NVE: large (>10mm) hypermobile vegetation + prior systemic embolus + significant valve dysfunction
  • NVE: very large (>30mm) vegetation
  • Persistent unexplained fever ≥10 days in culture-negative IE
  • Poorly responsive or relapsed IE from resistant enterococci or gram-negative bacilli

Q4 — Which Finding Requires Immediate Surgery?
Options include: vegetation >40mm with regurgitation, rupture of septum, fungal infection, inadequate echo view. Rupture of the septum (septal perforation) = urgent surgical indication (within 1–2 days per Table 133-7). Fungal infection = elective/strongly considered. Vegetation >40mm alone without urgent hemodynamic compromise = urgent/elective consideration. Inadequate echo view alone is not a surgical indication.
Q17 — What Warrants Surgical Treatment in IE?
Options: bacteremia persisting >5 days after antibiotics / cardiac signs developing on day 3 / glomerulonephritis on day 5 / Roth's spots on day 5.
  • Persistent bacteremia despite antibiotics: threshold is 7–10 days, not 5 days — but it IS a surgical indication once that threshold is met
  • Development of cardiac complications (heart failure, abscess, conduction changes) = surgical indication
  • Glomerulonephritis on day 5 = immunologic complication, NOT a surgical indication
  • Roth's spots = immunologic finding, NOT surgical
Answer: Persistent unexplained bacteremia despite optimal antibiotics (after 7–10 days) OR development of intracardiac complications (abscess, valve dysfunction)
Q38 — What is an Indication for URGENT Surgery?
Options include: new diastolic murmur + desaturation + respiratory distress 3 days after antibiotics / S. aureus IE / 5-year-old prosthetic valve with fever 5 days / IE due to unique pathogen / IE from Brazil. New diastolic murmur + respiratory distress (likely acute aortic regurgitation → CHF) = URGENT surgical indication (acute aortic or mitral regurgitation with heart failure = urgent). This is exactly the Table 133-7 urgent category.

Cardiac Complications

Heart Failure (Most Common Surgical Indication)
  • CHF in 30–40% from valve dysfunction or intracardiac fistulae
  • Major indication for cardiac surgery
  • Surgery should not be delayed when hemodynamics deteriorate
Paravalvular Abscess
  • 10–15% of NVE; 45–60% of PVE
  • Most common with aortic valve infection
  • Suspect when: persistent fever + unexplained, new ECG conduction disturbances, pericarditis
  • TEE: test of choice (sensitivity ≥85%)
  • Aortic abscess → extends to upper ventricular septum → AV node → heart block
  • Mitral abscess → rarely causes conduction abnormalities (distant from conduction system)
Embolic Complications
  • Arterial emboli in up to 50% (half precede diagnosis)
  • Cerebrovascular emboli (stroke) in 15–35%
  • Only 3% of strokes occur after 1 week of effective therapy
  • Risk factors for embolization: S. aureus IE, mobile vegetations >10mm, mitral anterior leaflet involvement
Neurologic Complications
  • Stroke, encephalopathy, meningitis
  • Mycotic aneurysms: focal arterial dilations from infected vasa vasorum; common in cerebral arteries
  • Intracerebral microabscesses (especially S. aureus)
  • MRI: asymptomatic emboli found in 30–65% of left-sided IE

Renal Complications

  • Diffuse hypocomplementemic glomerulonephritis — immune complex deposition on glomerular basement membrane → renal dysfunction (improves with treatment)
  • Embolic renal infarcts → flank pain and hematuria (rarely renal dysfunction)
  • Microscopic hematuria in 30–50% of patients
Q16 — 68yo Prolonged Fever, Aortic Vegetation, To Confirm IE Diagnosis
Options: Septic pulmonary infarct, mycotic aneurysm, dysmorphic RBCs, autoimmune, nothing needed. The patient already has an aortic vegetation on echo (= Major imaging criterion). Combined with fever (minor B) and prolonged history, they may already meet criteria. But to confirm as a distinct additional finding: Dysmorphic RBCs in urine = nephritic pattern = glomerulonephritis = immunologic minor criterion (D). This is a classic finding of immune complex deposition. Septic pulmonary infarct would be a vascular phenomenon (minor C) — possible but less classic for left-sided aortic IE.

Drug Contraindications — Quick Reference

Drug Contraindication / Warning
Hydroxychloroquine (Q fever) QTc >490 ms, retinal disease, G6PD deficiency
Clindamycin (prophylaxis) No longer recommended — C. difficile risk. Use doxycycline or azithromycin instead
Aminoglycosides (gentamicin) Renal failure (nephrotoxicity), 8th cranial nerve disease (ototoxicity). Use ampicillin + ceftriaxone for enterococci in renal failure
