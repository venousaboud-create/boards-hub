🎯 Listeria — The Exam Favourite

Why it dominates: it targets vulnerable populations AND cephalosporins miss it entirely — making the antibiotic choice a life-or-death decision.

Risk Factors (all tested across Q1/Q3/Q5/Q7):

  1. Alcoholism — impaired cell-mediated immunity
  2. Age >55 years — immunosenescence
  3. Organ transplant — iatrogenic immunosuppression
  4. Malignancy + chemotherapy
  5. Pregnancy — altered immune state
  6. Chronic high-dose steroids
  7. HIV / AIDS
  8. Neonates (<1 month old)

Gram Stain Morphology — Critical Distinction:

  1. Listeria: gram-positive rods (bacilli)
  2. Pneumococcus: gram-positive diplococci (paired cocci)
  3. Meningococcus: gram-negative diplococci
💎Gram-positive rods in CSF from alcoholic / transplant / elderly = Listeria until proven otherwise. Add ampicillin immediately. Ceftriaxone alone is insufficient and dangerous.
🔴 Pneumococcal Risk Factors Q10

Q10: which is a PROVEN risk factor for pneumococcal meningitis specifically?

  1. Basilar skull fracture with CSF rhinorrhea or otorrhea — creates direct anatomical access from nasopharynx → subarachnoid space
  2. Active pneumococcal pneumonia, sinusitis, or otitis media
  3. Alcoholism — multiple immune defects
  4. Diabetes mellitus
  5. Splenectomy / functional asplenia — loss of opsonization
  6. Hypogammaglobulinemia
  7. Complement deficiency
  8. Head trauma with CSF leak (rhinorrhea or otorrhea)
⚠️
"Living in crowded places" = meningococcal risk (NOT pneumococcal). Skull fracture is the classic pneumococcal-specific risk. Chronic steroids = Listeria/fungal risk primarily.
🔵 N. meningitidis — Key Mechanisms & Vaccination Nuances

Pathogenesis:

  1. Colonizes nasopharynx → asymptomatic carrier state OR invasive disease
  2. Risk depends on bacterial virulence + host immune defense
  3. Complement deficiency (including properdin) = highest susceptibility
  4. Eculizumab (complement inhibitor) → must vaccinate BEFORE starting therapy
  5. Fulminant form: can progress to death within hours of symptom onset

Vaccination nuances (tested):

  1. Serogroup B vaccine does NOT reduce nasopharyngeal carriage → person-to-person spread continues
  2. Conjugate vaccines (A/C/W/Y) DO reduce carriage in vaccinated individuals
  3. Serogroup B polysaccharide is poorly immunogenic → vaccine uses outer membrane proteins

Rash progression:

  1. Maculopapular (mimics viral exanthem) → petechial → purpuric → skin necrosis
  2. Location: trunk, lower extremities, mucous membranes, conjunctiva, palms/soles
  3. Biopsy of petechiae may show organism on Gram stain

⭐ Core Concept: Why Neurologic Injury Outlasts the Bacteria — & Why Dexamethasone Timing Is Critical
  1. Neurologic injury results primarily from the host immune response, not direct bacterial killing
  2. Once TNF-α and IL-1β are released, the inflammatory cascade continues even after CSF is fully sterilized by antibiotics
  3. Dexamethasone inhibits TNF-α/IL-1β synthesis at mRNA level — but only BEFORE macrophages are activated by endotoxin
  4. Antibiotic lysis releases endotoxin → activates macrophages → this is the point of no return
  5. Therefore: dexa must be given 20 minutes BEFORE antibiotics
  6. Three types of edema coexist: vasogenic (BBB breakdown) + cytotoxic (cell death) + interstitial (hydrocephalus)
Three Types of Edema
  1. Vasogenic: TNF-α/IL-1β ↑ BBB permeability → serum proteins leak into brain tissue
  2. Cytotoxic: Neutrophil degranulation → reactive oxygen species → cell membrane injury → cell death
  3. Interstitial: Proteinaceous exudate obstructs CSF outflow + impairs resorption at arachnoid granulations → obstructive + communicating hydrocephalus
Vascular Complications
  1. Narrowing of basal arteries — purulent exudate compresses vessel walls
  2. Vasculitis — inflammatory infiltration → intimal thickening
  3. Middle cerebral artery branch thrombosis → stroke
  4. Major cerebral venous sinus thrombosis
  5. Cortical vein thrombophlebitis → hemorrhage → focal seizures

Meningeal Signs — Kernig's & Brudzinski's

Kernig's sign:

  1. Patient supine, thigh flexed on abdomen, knee flexed at 90°
  2. Attempt to passively extend the knee
  3. Pain or resistance = positive

Brudzinski's sign:

  1. Patient supine
  2. Passive flexion of the neck
  3. Spontaneous flexion of hips and knees = positive
⚠️ Sensitivity and specificity of both signs are uncertain. May be absent in elderly, immunocompromised, or obtunded patients. Cervical spine disease = false positives for nuchal rigidity.

🔑 Q4: “Most Pathognomonic CSF Finding in Meningitis?”

The four options given were:

  1. Protein 40 mg/dL — NORMAL (normal <45 mg/dL) → not diagnostic
  2. Glucose 80 mg/dL — NORMAL → not diagnostic at all
  3. RBC 250/µL — traumatic tap or SAH → nonspecific
  4. Opening pressure 200 mmH&sub2;O — ABNORMAL (threshold is 180; seen in >90% of bacterial meningitis) → most pathognomonic from given list
💎Among those exact four options, opening pressure 200 is the only truly abnormal value. In general, CSF glucose <40 mg/dL is the most diagnostically specific for bacterial meningitis, but it was not listed as a clearly abnormal option in Q4.
📐 CSF Glucose Ratio — Calculation (Q2)

From Q2: Blood glucose 150 mg/dL, CSF glucose 50 mg/dL

Ratio = 50 ÷ 150 = 0.33
0.33 < 0.4 → Highly suggestive of bacterial / TB / fungal
  1. Normal ratio: ≥0.6
  2. Abnormal: <0.6
  3. Highly suggestive of bacterial: <0.4
  4. Also low in: TB, fungal, carcinomatous meningitis
  5. D50 given in ER does NOT significantly alter CSF glucose unless hours have elapsed
📋 Q2 Fully Parsed — Subacute Meningitis + Low Glucose
75yo man, fever for several weeks, neck stiffness, headache. CSF: 30 lymphocytes/µL, glucose 50 mg/dL vs blood glucose 150 mg/dL → ratio = 0.33. Most likely pathogen?
Options: West Nile virus / M. tuberculosis / HSV-2 / HHV-6
✓ Answer: M. tuberculosis
  1. Subacute course (weeks): TB builds over days-to-weeks. WNV and HSV-2 are acute.
  2. Lymphocytic pleocytosis + LOW glucose (ratio 0.33 <0.4): Hallmark of TB meningitis. Viral causes have NORMAL glucose.
  3. West Nile virus: glucose NORMAL → ruled out by low glucose
  4. HSV-2: acute onset, glucose usually normal → ruled out by subacute course + low glucose
  5. HHV-6: mainly immunocompromised, glucose normal → ruled out

TB classic CSF: lymphocytes 10–500/µL, protein 1–5 g/L, glucose 20–40 mg/dL, subacute course, night sweats, fever.

  1. WBC count: NOT significantly altered for hours after antibiotics start

❌ Q8: Focal Signs Present — What Comes Next?

Scenario: fever + headache + neck stiffness + focal neurological signs

  1. CT head first — focal signs = risk of herniation with immediate LP
  2. Simultaneously: draw blood cultures
  3. Simultaneously: start antibiotics + dexamethasone (do NOT wait for CT results)
  4. After CT clears herniation: perform LP
🚨Q8 answer = CT head. Do NOT LP first when focal signs present. Start antibiotics + dexa while CT is being done.

Empirical Antibiotic Therapy

🚨
Medical Emergency: Goal is antibiotic therapy within 60 minutes of ER arrival. Empirical therapy begins BEFORE culture results, CSF PCR, or Gram stain are available.
Clinical SituationEmpirical RegimenRationale
Immunocompetent adult <55 yrs
  1. Ceftriaxone/Cefotaxime
  2. + Vancomycin
  3. + Acyclovir
  • Cover S. pneumoniae (incl. PCN-resistant), N. meningitidis, H. influenzae
  • Acyclovir: HSV encephalitis = #1 differential
Adult >55 OR alcoholism OR immunocompromised OR transplant Q1Q3Q5Q7
  1. Ampicillin
  2. + Ceftriaxone
  3. + Vancomycin
  • Ampicillin essential — covers Listeria
  • Cephalosporins have zero Listeria activity
PCN allergy + Listeria risk Q5Q7
  1. TMP-SMX
  2. + Ceftriaxone
  3. + Vancomycin
  • TMP-SMX replaces ampicillin for Listeria coverage
  • Dose: TMP 10–20 mg/kg/day + SMX 50–100 mg/kg/day q6h
Hospital-acquired / post-neurosurgery / neutropenic
  1. Vancomycin
  2. + Ceftazidime or Meropenem
  • Cover Staph (MRSA) + Pseudomonas aeruginosa
  • Ceftriaxone does NOT cover Pseudomonas
All adults (universal add-on) Acyclovir
  • HSV encephalitis cannot be clinically distinguished from bacterial meningitis
  • Add empirically in all adult patients
🧩 Why Each Antibiotic — The Clinical Logic
  1. Ceftriaxone/Cefotaxime: covers susceptible S. pneumoniae, N. meningitidis, H. influenzae, Group B Strep — but NOT Listeria, NOT Pseudomonas
  2. Vancomycin: for PCN/cephalosporin-resistant S. pneumoniae and MRSA — but dexamethasone DECREASES its CSF penetration
  3. Ampicillin: the only standard first-line agent for Listeria in community-acquired meningitis
  4. TMP-SMX: replaces ampicillin for Listeria in PCN-allergic patients
  1. Ceftazidime: Pseudomonas activity — used when nosocomial/post-neurosurgery meningitis
  2. Meropenem: carbapenem with Listeria + Pseudomonas + resistant pneumococci activity — not as monotherapy
  3. Acyclovir: HSV-1/HSV-2 encephalitis — NOT ganciclovir (ganciclovir = CMV only) Q15
  4. Cefepime: 4th-gen; Pseudomonas activity; associated with seizures/encephalopathy in critically ill — limits use
💊 Q15: 40yo, Fever + Maculopapular Rash + Focal Neuro Signs — Empirical Treatment?
40yo man, fever, maculopapular rash, headache, focal neurological signs. What empirical treatment?
Options: Ertapenem / Vancomycin / Acyclovir / Ganciclovir
✓ Answer: Acyclovir

The focal neurological signs are the key: They point toward HSV encephalitis. HSV-1 encephalitis is the #1 condition mimicking bacterial meningitis with focal features.

  1. Acyclovir → correct: empirical for HSV encephalitis; standard add-on in all adult regimens. Focal signs clinch the indication.
  2. Ertapenem → wrong: limited CNS penetration; lacks MRSA coverage; not standard first-line here
  3. Vancomycin alone → wrong: insufficient spectrum; does not cover HSV; given only as part of combination
  4. Ganciclovir → wrong: covers CMV only, not HSV. The rule: Acyclovir = HSV/VZV; Ganciclovir = CMV. Never interchange.

Dexamethasone — Complete Protocol

🚨
Most-tested topic in these 18 questions — appears in Q11, Q12, Q13, Q14, Q16, Q18. Every dose, timing, mechanism, interaction, and limitation must be mastered.
⭐ The Exact Protocol — Memorize Every Number
10 mg
Per dose (IV only)
q6h × 4d
4 times/day × 4 days = 16 total doses
−20 min
Before first antibiotic dose
  1. Drug: Dexamethasone IV — NOT hydrocortisone, NOT prednisone Q18
  2. Route: Intravenous only — NOT intrathecal, NOT oral Q13
  3. Timing: 15–20 min BEFORE or concurrent with first antibiotic dose Q11Q13Q14Q18
  1. Cutoff: If >6h after antibiotics → unlikely to provide significant benefit Q12Q16
  2. Do NOT double the dose if patient is already on steroids Q14
  3. No bloodwork required before giving — do not delay for labs Q18
🧪 Mechanism — Why 20 Min BEFORE Is Non-Negotiable
Antibiotics → bacterial lysis → endotoxin / LPS / teichoic acid released into SAS
↓
Macrophages + microglia activated by endotoxin → produce TNF-α + IL-1β
↓
Dexamethasone must block here — but ONLY before this activation step
Dexa given BEFORE → blocks mRNA synthesis → TNF-α/IL-1β never produced
↓
Dexa given AFTER activation → TNF-α already released → cascade cannot be stopped
📊 Mechanisms of Benefit & Trial Results

Mechanisms (all from Harrison's):

  1. Inhibits TNF-α + IL-1β synthesis at mRNA level
  2. Decreases CSF outflow resistance → reduces hydrocephalus
  3. Stabilizes blood-brain barrier (reduces permeability)
  4. Reduces meningeal inflammation and purulent exudate
  5. Reduces sensorineural hearing loss (H. influenzae, S. pneumoniae)

European RCT key results:

  • Unfavorable outcomes: 15% vs 25% (p=0.03)
  • Death: 7% vs 15% (p=0.04)
  • Benefit most striking in S. pneumoniae meningitis
  • Also reduces hearing loss in H. influenzae and N. meningitidis
⚠️ Critical Interaction: Dexamethasone + Vancomycin Q14Q16
❌THE TRAP: “Dexamethasone improves BBB penetration of vancomycin” — sounds logical but is WRONG
✓THE TRUTH: Dexamethasone reduces BBB permeability → DECREASES vancomycin penetration into CSF

Clinical consequences:

  1. Increase vancomycin dose to 45–60 mg/kg/day when co-administered with dexamethasone
  2. Monitor serum vancomycin levels closely
  3. Dexamethasone also delays CSF sterilization in experimental pneumococcal meningitis
  4. If IV vancomycin still fails → intraventricular route needed
✓ Where Dexamethasone Works
  1. S. pneumoniae meningitis — greatest benefit
  2. H. influenzae meningitis — reduces hearing loss
  3. N. meningitidis — reduces death and hearing loss
  4. High-income countries
  5. Early hospital presentation
  6. Microbiologically confirmed bacterial meningitis
  7. HIV-negative patients with TB meningitis (dose 12–16 mg/d × 3 weeks, then taper)
❌ Where Dexamethasone Does NOT Work
  1. Given >6 hours after antibiotics started
  2. Sub-Saharan Africa + Southeast Asia — 3 large RCTs showed NO benefit
    Reasons: late presentation, pre-antibiotic treatment, malnutrition, HIV, unproven bacterial diagnosis
  3. Patients with negative Gram stain + culture in low-income countries → do NOT give
  4. “Greatest benefit in meningococcal” → FALSE; greatest benefit = pneumococcal Q18

Q17: “Least common cause of viral meningitis from options?”
Options: HSV-2 / CMV / Enterovirus / Coxsackie / West Nile
→ Answer: CMV — Less Common category; mainly immunocompromised. Enteroviruses (including coxsackievirus) = most common by far. HSV-2 and WNV are both more common than CMV.
✓ TRUE Statements About Viral Meningitis Q9
  1. Lymphocyte count CAN reach thousands/µL — especially LCMV (often >1000) and mumps ✓
  2. After full workup (PCR + culture + serology): no etiology found in ~40% — completely normal and expected ✓
  3. Treatment is primarily supportive — analgesics, antipyretics, antiemetics, hydration ✓
  4. Adults: excellent prognosis, full recovery expected ✓
  5. Add IV Acyclovir for HSV-1/2, severe EBV/VZV meningitis ✓
❌ FALSE Statements (Exam Traps) Q9
  1. “Low CSF glucose is typical of viral meningitis” ❌ — glucose is NORMAL; low glucose = bacterial/TB/fungal
  2. “PMN predominance is typical of viral meningitis” ❌ — lymphocytes predominate; PMNs unusual (exceptions: first 48h, WNV)
  3. “CSF immunoglobulins higher than serum = diagnostic of viral” ❌ — not a standard criterion
  4. “Stupor, coma, or marked confusion occur in viral meningitis” ❌ — these indicate encephalitis, not simple viral meningitis
  5. “Seizures and focal neurological signs are typical” ❌ — suggest encephalitis, not simple meningitis
⚠️ The WNV PMN Exception — Commonly Tested
  1. WNV meningitis shows PMN pleocytosis in ~45% of cases — far more than any other viral cause
  2. This PMN predominance can persist for >1 week — much longer than the transient 48h seen with other viruses
  3. Despite PMNs: glucose is NORMAL, Gram stain negative, cultures negative — this combination points away from bacterial
  4. Diagnosis: CSF IgM — sensitivity >80% by end of week 1; prevalence increases progressively during first week
  5. CSF RT-PCR for WNV: only ~70% sensitive — serology is more reliable
  6. No hypoglycorrhachia + negative Gram stain + negative cultures = distinguishes from bacterial

Meningococcal Prophylaxis

👥 Who Qualifies as a “Close Contact”? Q6

Definition = anyone with contact with oropharyngeal secretions of the index case:

  1. Kissing
  2. Sharing toys, beverages, or cigarettes
  3. Household members living with the index case
  4. Dormitory roommates
  1. Healthcare workers who performed intubation, suctioning, or mouth-to-mouth resuscitation without appropriate PPE — this includes the doctor in Q6
  2. The index case themselves — receives prophylaxis after completing treatment to eradicate nasopharyngeal carriage

Question-by-Question Scenario Breakdown

Every clinical scenario from the 18 questions — with the answer, full reasoning, and why each distractor fails.

Q1 Alcoholic + Confusion + Fever + Neck Stiffness + Gram-positive organisms in CSF
Alcoholic patient, confusion, stiff neck, fever. Gram stain shows gram-positive organisms. Most likely organism?
Options: Listeria monocytogenes / Nocardia / Corynebacterium striatum / Bacillus anthracis / Pneumococcus
✓ Answer: Listeria monocytogenes
  1. Alcoholism = Listeria risk factor (impaired cell-mediated immunity)
  2. Gram-positive rods = Listeria morphology (Pneumococcus = gram-positive cocci in pairs, not rods)
  3. Nocardia: branching gram-positive rods but primarily pulmonary/disseminated, rarely pure meningitis
  4. Corynebacterium striatum: gram-positive rods but usually contaminant, not a typical meningitis pathogen
  5. Bacillus anthracis: anthrax — not typical community-acquired meningitis
  6. Pneumococcus: most common overall, but morphology is cocci not rods
  7. Treatment needed: Ampicillin ± Gentamicin (NOT ceftriaxone)
Q3 Kidney Transplant + 750 WBCs + Gram-positive organisms in CSF — Best Treatment?
50yo woman, kidney transplant (immunocompromised), fever, headache. CSF: 750 leukocytes, high protein, low glucose, gram-positive organisms. Best treatment?
Options: Vancomycin / Ceftriaxone / Ganciclovir / Ampicillin / Gentamicin
✓ Answer: Ceftriaxone (as backbone); complete regimen = Ampicillin + Ceftriaxone + Vancomycin
  1. Transplant = immunocompromised → Listeria must be covered → needs Ampicillin
  2. Gram-positive organisms in immunocompromised host = Listeria OR pneumococcus
  3. Full regimen: Ampicillin (Listeria) + Ceftriaxone (pneumococcus/gram-neg) + Vancomycin (resistant pneumococcus)
  4. Ganciclovir = CMV treatment only → completely wrong for bacterial meningitis
  5. Vancomycin alone: misses Listeria and gram-negatives → insufficient
  6. Gentamicin alone: adjunct only, not primary monotherapy
  7. Ceftriaxone alone: misses Listeria entirely → dangerous in a transplant patient
Q5 Lymphoma Chemotherapy + PCN Allergy + Gram-positive organisms
50yo woman on chemotherapy for lymphoma, known penicillin allergy, fever. CSF: low glucose, gram-positive organisms, mononuclear cells. Best treatment?
Options: Meropenem / TMP-SMX / Ceftriaxone / Ceftazidime
✓ Answer: TMP-SMX (Trimethoprim-Sulfamethoxazole)
  1. Immunocompromised (chemo/lymphoma) + gram-positive mononuclear picture = Listeria meningitis
  2. Penicillin allergy → cannot use ampicillin → TMP-SMX is the established alternative
  3. Meropenem: active against Listeria in vitro but not standard-of-care alternative for PCN allergy
  4. Ceftriaxone: ZERO Listeria coverage → dangerous wrong answer
  5. Ceftazidime: for Pseudomonas aeruginosa → wrong organism entirely
Q6 Healthcare Worker Prophylaxis After Intubating Meningococcal Patient
Doctor intubated meningococcal patient without protection. What preventive treatment is recommended?
Options: Can give penicillin to contacts / Rifampin effective ~100% / Prophylaxis only in first 4–8 hours / Single IM injection very effective / Doctor doesn't need treatment
✓ Answer: Rifampin ~100% effective (TRUE) AND Single IM ceftriaxone injection is very effective (TRUE)
  1. Doctor had oropharyngeal secretion contact during intubation → qualifies as close contact → needs prophylaxis
  2. Rifampin ~100% effective: TRUE statement from Harrison's (given as 600mg q12h × 2 days)
  3. Single IM ceftriaxone (250mg): very effective, preferred in pregnancy
  4. “Only effective in first 4–8 hours”: FALSE — prophylaxis is effective beyond this window
  5. Penicillin: NOT used (does not eradicate nasopharyngeal carriage)
  6. “Doctor doesn't need treatment”: FALSE — oropharyngeal contact = close contact = must receive prophylaxis
Q7 35yo + Kidney Disease + PCN Allergy + Gram-positive rods + Mononuclear CSF
35yo woman, chronic kidney disease, known PCN allergy. CSF: 700 mononuclear cells, glucose 30 mg/dL (blood 100 mg/dL = ratio 0.30), high protein, gram-positive organisms on direct smear. What is correct regarding treatment?
Options: Add IV Doxycycline / Empirical ceftriaxone sufficient / Dexamethasone improves prognosis / IV TMP-SMX is treatment of choice
✓ Answer: IV TMP-SMX is the treatment of choice
  1. Chronic kidney disease (immunosuppression) + gram-positive rods + mononuclear cells + low glucose = Listeria meningitis
  2. Penicillin allergy → cannot use ampicillin → TMP-SMX is the established alternative for Listeria
  3. Add IV Doxycycline: no role in Listeria treatment → wrong
  4. Empirical ceftriaxone alone: misses Listeria entirely → dangerous
  5. Dexamethasone improves prognosis: while true in general, this doesn't address which antibiotic to use — TMP-SMX is the direct answer to the antibiotic question

40yo healthy man, fever, headache, neck stiffness on exam. What is correct?

Exam Traps — Board Coach Section

🎯
Each trap: what the exam wants you to think → the truth → why it matters → how it appears in a case (correct answer in green).
1
Ceftriaxone Covers All Community-Acquired Bacterial Meningitis
⚡ The Trap

“Ceftriaxone is the empirical drug of choice and covers all important organisms in community-acquired bacterial meningitis — no need to add anything else.”

✓ The Truth

Cephalosporins of ALL generations have zero activity against Listeria monocytogenes. In patients over 55, alcoholics, immunocompromised individuals, or transplant recipients, ampicillin MUST be added. In PCN-allergic patients with Listeria risk, TMP-SMX is the alternative. Ceftriaxone alone in these patients leaves Listeria completely untreated and carries 15% mortality risk.

🔥 Why It Matters

Four questions (Q1, Q3, Q5, Q7) test this exact point from different angles — alcoholic, transplant patient, chemo patient, kidney disease. The exam always provides a vulnerable host and tempts you to select ceftriaxone alone.

📋 In a Case — Q1, Q3, Q5, Q7
Alcoholic, 60yo man, fever, neck stiffness, confusion. CSF: gram-positive rods.
Options: Ceftriaxone / Ganciclovir / Vancomycin / Ampicillin / Gentamicin

Correct: Ampicillin (± Gentamicin in critically ill) — gram-positive rods + alcoholic = Listeria. Ceftriaxone alone misses it entirely.
2
Dexamethasone Can Be Given Anytime and Still Be Beneficial
⚡ The Trap

“As long as you give dexamethasone during hospitalization, it will help reduce inflammation and prevent neurological sequelae.”

✓ The Truth

Dexamethasone is ONLY effective if given 20 minutes before or concurrent with the first antibiotic dose. If given more than 6 hours after antibiotics, it provides no significant benefit. The mechanism is time-locked: macrophages must be blocked BEFORE they are activated by endotoxin from bacterial lysis. Once activated, the cascade cannot be stopped by dexa.

🔥 Why It Matters

Six questions (Q11–Q18) test this from different angles. The exam offers “4 hours after” and “6 hours after” as distractors. Only “20 minutes before” or “concurrent” is correct.

📋 In a Case — Q13
60yo man, fever, CSF: 90 neutrophils, glucose 30, protein 60. Antibiotics being prepared. When should dexamethasone be given?
Options: 6h after antibiotics / 4h after antibiotics / Intrathecal route / IV 20 min before antibiotics

Correct: Give IV Dexamethasone 20 minutes BEFORE antibiotics. Intrathecal is NEVER the correct route for dexamethasone.
3
Dexamethasone Improves Vancomycin's CSF Penetration
⚡ The Trap

“Dexamethasone stabilizes the blood-brain barrier, allowing vancomycin to penetrate the CSF more effectively.”

✓ The Truth

Exact opposite. Dexamethasone reduces BBB permeability (the anti-edema mechanism) — this same effect decreases vancomycin penetration into CSF. The vancomycin dose must therefore be increased to 45–60 mg/kg/day when dexamethasone is co-administered.

🔥 Why It Matters

Under-dosing vancomycin while on dexamethasone = inadequate CNS coverage for resistant pneumococcus = treatment failure. Sounds counter-intuitive, which is exactly why it is a favourite board topic.

📋 In a Case — Q14, Q16
What is correct about dexamethasone in bacterial meningitis?
Options: Given 20 min before antibiotics / Given as single dose / Improves BBB penetration of Vancomycin / Double dose if on steroids

Correct: Given 20 minutes before antibiotics. “Improves vancomycin penetration” is the trap — dexamethasone REDUCES it.
4
Low CSF Glucose Is a Feature of Viral Meningitis
⚡ The Trap

“Low CSF glucose is a feature of meningitis in general, including viral — both bacterial and viral can cause hypoglycorrhachia.”

✓ The Truth

Normal glucose is the hallmark of viral meningitis. Low glucose redirects you to bacterial, TB, fungal, or carcinomatous meningitis. Only viral exceptions: mumps and LCMV (hypoglycorrhachia in 10–30%). In any classic viral scenario, glucose is normal.

🔥 Why It Matters

This confusion leads to: (1) withholding antibiotics from a bacterial meningitis patient; (2) misinterpreting subacute TB meningitis as viral because “it is lymphocytic.” Glucose is the most powerful discriminating feature on the CSF profile.

📋 In a Case — Q2, Q9
75yo man, weeks of headache, CSF: 30 lymphocytes, glucose 50 mg/dL, blood glucose 150 mg/dL (ratio = 0.33). Most likely pathogen?
Options: West Nile virus / M. tuberculosis / HSV-2 / HHV-6

Correct: M. tuberculosis — subacute course + lymphocytic + LOW glucose ratio (0.33 < 0.4) = TB meningitis. WNV and HSV-2 have normal glucose.
5
PMN Pleocytosis Always Means Bacterial Meningitis
⚡ The Trap

“Neutrophil (PMN) predominance in the CSF is pathognomonic of bacterial meningitis. No viral infection causes neutrophilia in the CSF.”

✓ The Truth

PMNs can predominate in viral meningitis in specific situations:

  1. In the first 48 hours of any viral meningitis
  2. West Nile Virus — PMN pleocytosis in 45% of cases, persisting >1 week
  3. Eastern equine encephalitis, Echovirus 9, Mumps (25% of cases)

Key distinguishing feature: glucose is NORMAL despite PMN pleocytosis in viral — unlike bacterial where PMN + low glucose go together.

🔥 Why It Matters

A WNV patient in summer with PMN-predominant CSF could be wrongly treated as bacterial. Normal glucose + negative Gram stain + negative cultures + CSF IgM positivity confirm WNV.

📋 In a Case — Q9
What is correct about viral meningitis?
Options: Low glucose is typical / IgG higher than serum / PMN predominance is typical / Lymphocytes can reach thousands/µL / Cause found in most after workup

Correct: Lymphocytes CAN reach thousands/µL (LCMV, mumps). PMN predominance is NOT typical. Low glucose is NOT typical of viral meningitis.
6
Skull Fracture Is a Trauma Problem, Not an Infection Risk Factor
⚡ The Trap

“Infection risk factors for meningitis are immunological — crowded living, steroid use. Skull fracture is about trauma, not infection.”

✓ The Truth

Basilar skull fracture with CSF rhinorrhea or otorrhea is one of the most classically tested pneumococcal meningitis risk factors. The fracture creates direct anatomical continuity between the nasopharynx (colonized by S. pneumoniae) and the subarachnoid space — allowing direct bacterial invasion bypassing normal mucosal defenses.

🔥 Why It Matters

The exam pairs skull fracture with “crowded living” (meningococcal risk) and “chronic steroids” (Listeria/fungal risk) as distractors. Skull fracture is specifically and classically linked to pneumococcal meningitis.

📋 In a Case — Q10
Which is a proven risk factor for pneumococcal meningitis?
Options: Hyperactivity of immune system / Living in crowded places / Skull fracture / Chronic steroid treatment

Correct: Skull fracture (basilar, with CSF rhinorrhea/otorrhea) — creates direct anatomical access from nasopharynx to subarachnoid space.
7
Acyclovir and Ganciclovir Are Interchangeable Antivirals for Herpes CNS Infections
⚡ The Trap

“Both acyclovir and ganciclovir are antiherpes drugs — either one is appropriate for viral CNS infections including encephalitis.”

✓ The Truth

They have completely different spectra and are NOT interchangeable:

  1. Acyclovir: covers HSV-1, HSV-2, VZV — first-line for HSV encephalitis and VZV meningitis
  2. Ganciclovir: covers CMV (cytomegalovirus) — not clinically effective against HSV
  3. HSV encephalitis mortality without treatment: up to 70% — selecting ganciclovir instead of acyclovir = essentially no treatment
🔥 Why It Matters

The exam will present focal neurological signs + fever + headache and ask what to add empirically. Focal signs → HSV encephalitis → acyclovir. Ganciclovir is always the wrong answer in this scenario.

📋 In a Case — Q15
40yo man, fever, maculopapular rash, headache, focal neurological signs. Empirical treatment?
Options: Ertapenem / Vancomycin alone / Acyclovir / Ganciclovir

Correct: Acyclovir — focal neuro signs + fever = HSV encephalitis in differential → IV Acyclovir empirically. Ganciclovir = CMV only, not HSV.
8
Dexamethasone Is Universally Effective — Including in Africa
⚡ The Trap

“Large clinical trials proved dexamethasone reduces mortality and complications in all patients with bacterial meningitis worldwide.”

✓ The Truth

The landmark European RCT showed clear benefit. However, three large RCTs in sub-Saharan Africa and Southeast Asia showed NO benefit from dexamethasone. Attributed reasons:

  1. Late presentation to hospital with advanced disease
  2. Antibiotic pretreatment before hospital arrival
  3. Malnutrition
  4. HIV coinfection
  5. Inclusion of patients without microbiologically proven bacterial meningitis

Guideline: Patients in sub-Saharan Africa with negative CSF Gram stain and culture should NOT receive dexamethasone.

🔥 Why It Matters

“Proven in Africa” is a specifically FALSE statement. This tests whether you know the geographic limitations of the European data.

📋 In a Case — Q16, Q18
What is correct about steroids in meningitis?
Options: Effective up to 12h after antibiotics / Can lower vancomycin CSF penetration / Effective in ALL meningitis / Proven effective in meningococcal meningitis in Africa

Correct: Can lower the penetration of vancomycin into CSF (TRUE). “Proven in Africa” = FALSE. “Effective in all” = FALSE. “Effective at 12h” = FALSE.
9
Always Perform LP Before Starting Antibiotics
⚡ The Trap

“You must confirm bacterial meningitis by LP before starting antibiotics — otherwise cultures will be negative and you lose the diagnostic opportunity.”

✓ The Truth

Bacterial meningitis is a medical emergency. Never delay antibiotics for LP. Correct sequence:

  1. Draw blood cultures immediately
  2. Start antibiotics + dexamethasone immediately
  3. If no focal signs: perform LP directly
  4. If focal signs: CT head first, then LP after CT clears
  5. Never delay antibiotics waiting for LP or CT results

Antibiotics given hours before LP will NOT significantly alter CSF WBC count, glucose, Gram stain, or PCR detection of bacterial DNA.

🔥 Why It Matters

Each hour of antibiotic delay increases mortality. The goal is antibiotics within 60 minutes of ER arrival. Culture may be slightly reduced by pre-LP antibiotics — hence blood cultures are drawn first.

📋 In a Case — Q8
Patient with fever, headache, neck stiffness, and focal neurological signs. Next step?
Options: CT head / MRI head / Lumbar puncture / Start antibiotics

Correct: CT head FIRST (focal signs = herniation risk). Simultaneously draw blood cultures and start antibiotics + dexamethasone. Do NOT wait for CT results before giving antibiotics.
