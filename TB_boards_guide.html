Epidemiology & Risk Factors

Global Burden (2023)
  • 10.8 million new cases estimated worldwide
  • 8.2 million cases reported to WHO (under-detection is common)
  • Top 8 countries = 2/3 of all cases: India (26%), Indonesia (10%), China (6.8%), Philippines (6.8%), Pakistan (6.3%), Nigeria (4.6%), Bangladesh (3.5%), DRC (3.1%)
  • 55% male, 33% female, 12% children
  • 1.25 million deaths including 160,000 among PLWH
  • TB returned as #1 infectious killer in 2023 (dethroned COVID)
U.S. Epidemiology
  • 2022: 8,300 cases (2.5 per 100,000)
  • 71% among non-U.S.-born persons
  • Highest rates: elderly ≥65 years old
  • Lowest rates: children <14 years old
  • Highest burden by race: Non-Hispanic Black (34%), White (29%), Hispanic/Latino (24%)
  • ~13 million persons are latently infected
  • Primary MDR-TB rate: <1% since 2000

🔴

Age and Timing of Disease
  • Overall: ~10% of infected persons will develop active TB in their lifetime
  • Half of those develop it within the first 18 months after infection
  • Incidence of TB is highest during late adolescence and early adulthood (post-primary reactivation window)
  • Among women, peak incidence at 25–34 years of age
  • Risk increases again in the elderly (waning immunity + comorbidity)
  • Primary TB = common in young children and immunocompromised
  • Post-primary (reactivation) TB = more common in adults; more infectious due to cavitation

Pathogenesis & Immunity

Transmission → Infection → Disease
1
Transmission: Droplet nuclei (<5–10 μm) from patient with infectious pulmonary TB; reach terminal airways. Sputum smear-positive cavitary TB = most infectious (10⁵–10⁷ AFB/mL)
2
Alveolar invasion: Bacilli phagocytosed by alveolar macrophages → M. tuberculosis blocks phagosome-lysosome fusion → survives intracellularly → macrophage rupture → cycle repeats
3
Granuloma formation (2–4 weeks): CD4+ T cells activated → macrophages aggregate → granuloma (tubercle) with caseous necrosis center. This limits spread but doesn't always eradicate
4
Latency (LTBI): Bacilli persist within granulomas (in necrotic areas). TST/IGRA become positive. No symptoms, not infectious. ~10% will reactivate over a lifetime
5
Reactivation (Post-primary TB): Upper lobe predilection (high O₂ tension). Cavitation → infectious, high bacterial load. Triggers: immunosuppression, aging, HIV, TNF inhibitors
Key Immune Players
  • CD4+ T cells (TH1): produce IFN-γ → activate macrophages; central to protective immunity
  • CD8+ T cells: cytotoxic, also produce IFN-γ and TNF-α
  • TNF-α: critical for granuloma formation and maintenance → anti-TNF therapy = major TB risk
  • Macrophages: primary effectors; can kill if activated but also harbor bacilli
  • IFN-γ: the key cytokine measured in IGRA tests
Why HIV = Highest Risk
  • CD4+ T cell depletion → impaired macrophage activation
  • Granulomas are less well-organized → higher bacterial burden
  • TB can appear at ANY stage of HIV infection
  • With CD4 <200/μL: "primary TB-like" pattern (lower lobe, no cavitation, lymphadenopathy)
  • With CD4 >200/μL: more "typical" upper lobe + cavitation pattern
  • Person with positive TST + HIV: 3–13% annual risk of active TB
⚠️
Anti-TNF = Major Risk Factor: TNF-α is essential for granuloma integrity. Blocking TNF (infliximab, adalimumab, etanercept, etc.) can cause latent TB to reactivate. Always screen for TB (PPD/IGRA) before starting any anti-TNF treatment. PPD cutoff: ≥5 mm = positive in this group.
🫁

Clinical Presentation: Pulmonary TB

Primary TB
  • Occurs soon after initial infection
  • Common in children and immunocompromised
  • Middle/lower lung zones (where air goes)
  • Ghon focus (peripheral) + hilar lymphadenopathy = Ghon complex
  • Usually asymptomatic or mild fever
  • Pleural effusion in up to 2/3 of cases
  • Generally NOT highly infectious (less cavitation)
  • Miliary TB / meningitis possible if no immune response
VS
Post-Primary (Reactivation) TB
  • Endogenous reactivation (or reinfection)
  • Common in adults
  • Upper lobes (apical/posterior segments) — high O₂
  • Cavitation → very infectious (10⁵–10⁷ AFB/mL)
  • Symptoms: cough, hemoptysis, night sweats, weight loss, fever
  • CXR: upper-lobe infiltrates with cavities
  • Sputum smear often positive
  • Hemoptysis in 20–30% of cases
Classic Symptoms of Active TB
Constitutional
Fever (low-grade, intermittent), night sweats, weight loss, anorexia, malaise, weakness
Respiratory
Cough (up to 90% eventually), initially dry → productive; purulent sputum; hemoptysis (20–30%); dyspnea in severe disease
CXR Findings
Upper-lobe infiltrates; cavitation; hilar adenopathy; miliary pattern; pleural effusion; air-fluid level in cavity
Labs
Mild anemia, leukocytosis, thrombocytosis, elevated ESR/CRP; hyponatremia (SIADH); none pathognomonic
💡
CXR in HIV: In advanced HIV (CD4 <200), CXR may show lower-zone infiltrates WITHOUT cavitation, diffuse interstitial infiltrates, or even a NORMAL CXR. Do not rely on "classic" upper-lobe cavitation in PLWH.

Extrapulmonary TB

📌
Frequency order (descending): Lymph nodes → Pleura → Genitourinary → Bones/Joints → Meninges → Peritoneum → Pericardium. Extrapulmonary TB occurs in 10–40% of all TB patients; up to 60% of PLWH with TB have extrapulmonary disease.

🔴
TB Meningitis + HIV: DO NOT start ART in the first 8 weeks of TB treatment in TB meningitis patients. ART + IRIS in CNS TB can be fatal. Start ART only after 8 weeks, regardless of CD4 count.
  • Cranial nerve palsies (especially ocular) are common due to basilar involvement
  • Neurological sequelae in 25% of treated cases (often due to delayed diagnosis)
  • Treatment extension: ATS/CDC/IDSA recommend continuation phase of 7–10 months for TB meningitis
  • Adjunctive glucocorticoids (dexamethasone): WHO recommends; reduces mortality in HIV-negative patients; no benefit shown for PLWH in recent trial
Pleural TB — HIGH YIELD ★
  • Accounts for ~20% of extrapulmonary cases
  • Isolated pleural effusion = usually primary infection (hypersensitivity response to mycobacterial antigens)
  • Fluid characteristics: straw-colored, exudate, protein >50% serum, lymphocyte predominance (neutrophils early), low glucose, pH ~7.3
  • AFB smear of pleural fluid: rarely positive
  • Culture of pleural fluid: often falsely negative
Best Diagnostic Tests for Pleural TB
  • Needle biopsy of the pleura: granulomas + positive culture in up to 80% — this is the recommended first choice over fluid analysis
  • ADA (Adenosine Deaminase): elevated = consistent with TB; very low = can exclude TB. Useful but not definitive
  • Xpert MTB/RIF on pleural biopsy: yields positive result in ~75% of cases — preferred; NOT recommended on pleural fluid (low sensitivity, ~50%)
  • IFN-γ measurement: diagnostically helpful
  • rtPCR (real-time PCR) of pleural biopsy: adds sensitivity ~75%+
What Does NOT Work Well
  • AFB smear of pleural fluid — very low yield
  • Xpert on fluid (not biopsy) — sensitivity only ~50%
  • ADA alone — not definitive
  • Pleural fluid culture — often falsely negative
  • PCR on fluid alone — not routinely recommended
⚠️
TB Empyema (different entity): Rupture of cavity → large number of organisms in pleural space → bronchopleural fistula possible. Fluid is purulent/thick, AFB smear often positive. Requires surgical drainage + chemotherapy.
Pott's Disease (Spinal TB) — HIGH YIELD ★
  • TB spondylitis = most common form of skeletal TB (~40% of all skeletal cases)
  • Usually involves 2+ adjacent vertebral bodies
  • Children: upper thoracic; Adults: lower thoracic and upper lumbar
  • Classic presentation: back pain, weight loss, kyphosis (gibbus deformity)
  • Paravertebral "cold" abscess → can track to chest wall (upper) or inguinal/psoas (lower)
  • Paraplegia is a catastrophic complication → medical emergency, requires rapid drainage
  • CT/MRI: characteristic lesion with vertebral destruction
  • Confirms by aspiration or bone biopsy
🔴
BOARDS TRAP: Pott's disease is treated with chemotherapy (isoniazid + rifampin-based). Steroids are NOT indicated in Pott's disease (unlike TB meningitis or pericarditis). Do not confuse with the paraparesis complication which requires surgical drainage — not steroids.
Lymph Node TB
  • Most common form of extrapulmonary TB in PLWH and children
  • Painless swelling of posterior cervical and supraclavicular nodes (scrofula)
  • Matted, nontender mass; fistulous tracts draining caseous material
  • Diagnosis: fine-needle aspiration (FNA) — yield up to 80%; surgical excision biopsy
  • Cultures positive in 70–80% of cases
  • In PLWH: granulomas absent/poorly formed; higher bacterial loads
🔴

TB + HIV Co-infection

Key Facts
  • HIV = strongest individual risk factor for TB (RR 21–>30)
  • TB responsible for up to 25% of all HIV-related mortality
  • Positive TST + HIV: 3–13% annual risk of active TB
  • New TB infection in PLWH → active disease in weeks (not months/years)
  • With CD4 <200: lower-zone infiltrates, no cavitation, lymphadenopathy; CXR can be NORMAL
  • 40–60% of PLWH with TB have extrapulmonary disease
TB Screening in HIV
  • TST threshold in HIV: ≥5 mm = positive (not 10 or 15)
  • IGRA is preferred in HIV (not affected by BCG, higher specificity)
  • TST can be falsely negative due to anergy → treat empirically if high-risk exposure
  • HIV patients should receive TPT regardless of TST result if exposed to TB
  • Screen ALL HIV patients at diagnosis and annually if in endemic area
  • Clinical algorithm for PLWH: absence of all 4 symptoms (cough, fever, weight loss, night sweats) → can start TPT
IRIS — Immune Reconstitution Inflammatory Syndrome
  • Occurs in ~10% of HIV-infected TB patients who start ART
  • Usually develops 1–3 months after ART initiation
  • More common: advanced immunosuppression, extrapulmonary TB, earlier ART start, lower baseline CD4
  • Presentation: worsening lymphadenopathy, fever, pulmonary infiltrates, pleural effusion — exacerbation of TB signs
  • Diagnosis of exclusion: rule out TB treatment failure or new opportunistic infection first
  • Management: mild IRIS → symptom-based; severe IRIS → prednisolone (1.5 mg/kg/day × 2 weeks → 0.75 mg/kg/day × 2 weeks)
🔴
Critical Rule: In TB MENINGITIS, do NOT start ART during the first 8 weeks of TB treatment (irrespective of CD4 count). IRIS in CNS TB can cause death.

Preferred ART with rifampin: dolutegravir (double dose = 50 mg BID) + 2 NRTIs or efavirenz. Alternatively, substitute rifabutin (less CYP induction) for rifampin.

Diagnosis of Active TB

💡
Diagnostic Hierarchy: The gold standard for definitive diagnosis is culture of M. tuberculosis. However, Xpert MTB/RIF is now the recommended first-line test (rapid, detects rifampin resistance simultaneously in <2 hours).
Nucleic Acid Amplification — Xpert MTB/RIF ★
  • Fully automated, real-time PCR system
  • Results in <2 hours
  • Simultaneously detects TB AND rifampin resistance
  • WHO recommendation: First-line diagnostic test for ALL adults and children with symptoms of TB
  • Also recommended as first-line in PLWH
PopulationSensitivitySpecificity
AFB smear-positive98%98%

🔴
A NEGATIVE Xpert MTB/RIF does NOT exclude TB. Culture is still needed to confirm. Also: Xpert is NOT appropriate for monitoring treatment response (detects dead bacilli DNA).
AFB Smear Microscopy
  • Still widely used in resource-limited settings; inexpensive
  • Sensitivity: only 40–60% in culture-confirmed pulmonary TB
  • Cannot distinguish TB from nontuberculous mycobacteria (NTM)
  • Collect 1–2 morning sputum specimens
  • LED fluorescence microscopy is now WHO recommended over traditional Ziehl-Neelsen
  • NOT useful for monitoring treatment response (dead bacilli still stain positive)
Culture (Gold Standard)
  • Liquid culture (MGIT system): results in 10 days to 2–3 weeks
  • Solid media (Löwenstein-Jensen): 4–8 weeks
  • Allows drug susceptibility testing (DST)
  • Universal DST recommended by WHO for all TB patients (at minimum, test rifampin resistance)
  • Sputum cultures monitored monthly until negative; by end of month 3, virtually all drug-susceptible TB patients should be culture-negative

TST vs IGRA — Diagnosing TB Infection (LTBI)

📌
Both TST and IGRA detect TB infection (LTBI), NOT active TB. They measure the host immune response to M. tuberculosis antigens. Neither can distinguish active disease from latent infection, nor can they predict who will progress to disease.
TST (Tuberculin Skin Test / PPD)
  • 5 TU PPD injected intradermally (Mantoux)
  • Read at 48–72 hours (induration, not erythema)
  • Measures CD4+ T cells residing in skin
  • Affected by BCG vaccination → false positives
  • Can be boosted by serial testing (boosting effect)
  • Two-step testing: first test followed by repeat in 1–3 weeks (for healthcare workers, long-term care admissions)
  • Preferred in children <5 years old
  • Cheap, widely available
VS
IGRA (Interferon-gamma Release Assay)
  • Blood test measuring IFN-γ release by T cells in response to ESAT-6 and CFP-10 (RD1 antigens)
  • Results in 24–48 hours
  • Measures recirculating memory CD4+ T cells
  • NOT affected by BCG vaccination → more specific
  • No boosting effect
  • Preferred in BCG-vaccinated individuals
  • Preferred in adults and children ≥5 years in the U.S.
  • More expensive; requires lab processing
PPD Cutoff Thresholds — MUST MEMORIZE ★
CutoffRisk Group
≥5 mmHIV-infected persons; Recent contacts of TB patient; Organ transplant recipients; Fibrotic lesions on CXR consistent with old TB; Immunosuppressed (glucocorticoids, TNF-α inhibitors); High-risk medical conditions (silicosis, end-stage renal disease/HD)
≥10 mmRecent immigrants (≤5 years) from high-prevalence countries; IV drug users; Mycobacteriology lab personnel; Healthcare workers in high-risk settings; Children <5 years; Children and adolescents exposed to high-risk adults
≥15 mmLow-risk persons with no known risk factors (rarely tested)
When to Use IGRA vs TST
Prefer IGRA
  • BCG-vaccinated individuals (IGRA is NOT affected by BCG)
  • Adults in low-incidence, high-income settings
  • IVDU patients (higher specificity than PPD)
  • When one-visit testing is preferred (no need for return visit)
  • Healthcare workers in the U.S. (preferred)
  • HIV-infected patients: IGRA provides more reliable results than PPD due to immune dysfunction affecting skin response
Prefer TST/PPD
  • Children <5 years old (limited IGRA data)
  • Resource-limited settings (cheaper)
  • High-incidence TB settings (both tests have limitations)
  • Two-step testing programs for baseline screening
  • When IGRA is not available
⚠️
IGRA in BCG-vaccinated patients: BCG causes false-positive PPD reactions (since PPD antigens are shared with BCG). IGRA uses ESAT-6 and CFP-10 antigens that are NOT present in BCG → IGRA is more specific in BCG-vaccinated persons. This is the single most important reason to prefer IGRA in BCG-vaccinated populations.
🔴
HIV patients screening for TB: A negative TST in HIV does NOT exclude TB infection (anergy). HIV patients should receive TPT regardless of TST/IGRA result if they have been exposed to an infectious TB patient. In active HIV disease (any age ≥1 year), TPT is recommended regardless of test result.
Healthcare Worker / Pre-op TB Screening
  • Hospital exposure (e.g., post-neurosurgery, ICU) → screen ALL exposed staff with PPD or IGRA
  • PPD for all staff (healthcare workers fall in the ≥10 mm category)
  • Two-step testing for new employees (baseline) to avoid misclassifying "boosted" reactions as conversions
  • Pre-transplant: screen with TST or IGRA; if BCG vaccinated → prefer IGRA
  • Pre-anti-TNF: MUST screen; threshold ≥5 mm = positive; treat LTBI before starting

Treatment of Drug-Susceptible TB

Standard 6-Month Regimen (Drug-Susceptible TB) ★
1
Intensive Phase (2 months): Isoniazid (H) + Rifampin (R) + Pyrazinamide (Z) + Ethambutol (E) — 2HRZE. Kills majority of bacilli; symptoms resolve; patient becomes non-infectious
2
Continuation Phase (4 months): Isoniazid (H) + Rifampin (R) — 4HR. Eliminates persisting mycobacteria; prevents relapse. Total = 6 months (2HRZE/4HR)
✅
Cure rate: >90% with this regimen. By end of month 2: >80% have negative sputum cultures. By end of month 3: virtually all should be culture-negative.

New 4-Month Regimen (2HPMZ/2HPM): Isoniazid + Rifapentine (1200mg/day) + Moxifloxacin + Pyrazinamide for 2 months, then Rifapentine + Moxifloxacin for 2 months. Non-inferior to standard 6-month regimen. Recommended by WHO/CDC for patients ≥12 years. NOT for: body weight <40kg, severe extrapulmonary TB, CD4 <100, pregnancy, prolonged QT history.
Monitoring Treatment Response
  • Sputum cultures monthly until negative
  • If culture-positive at 2 months → retest immediately for drug resistance, consider extending treatment
  • If cultures/smears positive at ≥3 months despite adherence → suspect drug resistance
  • Do NOT monitor with Xpert MTB/RIF (detects dead bacilli DNA → misleading positive)
  • Serial CXRs NOT routinely recommended for monitoring (lags behind bacteriologic response)
  • Monitor weight; adjust drug doses if significant weight change

Anti-TB Drug Toxicity — HIGH YIELD ★

🔴
Hepatotoxicity — most common significant adverse event overall. Drug-susceptible TB hepatotoxicity (drug-susceptible TB hepatitis) occurs in up to 20% of patients (usually mild/asymptomatic rises). The most common cause: isoniazid, but also rifampin and pyrazinamide are hepatotoxic.

When to Stop Which Drug — EXAM FAVORITE ★
STOP PERMANENTLY (Absolute Indications)
  • Rifampin: Autoimmune thrombocytopenia
  • Ethambutol: Optic neuritis (visual changes)
  • Pyrazinamide: Gouty ARTHRITIS (not just hyperuricemia)
  • Isoniazid: Active liver disease, symptomatic hepatitis (AST/ALT >3–5x ULN)
CAN CONTINUE (Manageable)
  • Pyrazinamide: hyperuricemia + arthralgia (not arthritis) → aspirin
  • Isoniazid: asymptomatic AST/ALT <3x ULN → monitor closely
  • QT prolongation: bedaquiline, levofloxacin, delamanid → monitor EKG, adjust only if QTc >500ms
  • Pruritus, GI upset → manage symptomatically without stopping
Hepatotoxicity — Step-by-Step Protocol
1
Baseline LFTs for ALL adult patients before starting treatment
2
Monitor monthly if: elderly, alcohol use, liver disease history (especially hep C), daily alcohol use
3
Asymptomatic AST/ALT ≤3x ULN: common (up to 20%), no consequence → continue, monitor
4
STOP: Symptomatic hepatitis + AST/ALT ≥3x ULN OR asymptomatic AST/ALT ≥5x ULN
5
Rechallenge: reintroduce drugs ONE AT A TIME after LFTs normalize; rifampin first (least hepatotoxic among the 3)

Drug-Resistant TB (MDR-TB)

Definitions
MDR-TB
Resistant to at minimum isoniazid AND rifampin. Most important definition. ~400,000 new cases in 2023.
RR-TB (Rifampin-Resistant)
Resistant to rifampin (with or without isoniazid resistance). Treated with same approach as MDR-TB.
XDR-TB (Extensively Drug-Resistant)
MDR-TB + resistance to any fluoroquinolone + at least one Group A drug (bedaquiline or linezolid).
🔴
When to suspect MDR-TB: Culture-positive at ≥3 months despite good adherence; exposure to known MDR case; failure of standard regimen; history of prior TB treatment (especially incomplete); origin from high-MDR-burden country (former Soviet Union, SE Asia).

WHO Drug Groups for Longer MDR-TB Regimens
Group A — All 3 should be included
  • Levofloxacin or moxifloxacin (fluoroquinolone)
  • Bedaquiline
  • Linezolid
Group B — At least 1
  • Clofazimine
  • Cycloserine or terizidone
Group C — To complete regimen
  • Ethambutol, Delamanid, Pyrazinamide
  • Imipenem-cilastatin/meropenem
  • Amikacin (or streptomycin)
  • Ethionamide, PAS
  • ❌ Kanamycin/capreomycin: NOT to be included
⚠️
When to suspect MDR-TB on boards: A patient with active TB on standard therapy who remains sputum culture-positive after 3 months despite good adherence (proven by DOT). Immediate approach: send cultures + drug susceptibility testing, and start empiric MDR regimen — do NOT wait for culture results if patient is deteriorating rapidly. Always add ≥2 new drugs at once to a failing regimen, never just one.
QT Interval Monitoring in MDR-TB
  • QT-prolonging drugs in MDR regimens: bedaquiline, delamanid, clofazimine, fluoroquinolones
  • EKG required at baseline and repeated during treatment
  • QTc >500 ms or history of ventricular arrhythmia → contraindicated
  • This is why levofloxacin QT monitoring is important — levofloxacin alone does NOT require stopping if QTc is <500ms

TB Preventive Treatment (TPT) — LTBI Treatment

Who Should Receive TPT ★
Definite Indications (WHO)
  • HIV patients: ALL PLWH (regardless of TST/IGRA result if exposed); children >12 months with HIV
  • All household contacts of infectious pulmonary TB (including children <5 years — treat even without positive test)
  • Patients starting anti-TNF therapy
  • Patients on dialysis / end-stage renal disease
  • Patients with silicosis
  • Pre-transplant (organ or hematologic)
  • Positive TST or IGRA in any appropriate risk group
Before Starting TPT — Always Do This
  • Exclude active TB first — TPT would be monotherapy for active disease → creates resistance
  • Clinical algorithm for PLWH: no cough, no fever, no weight loss, no night sweats → can start TPT
  • Any one of the 4 symptoms → investigate for active TB first
  • PPD/IGRA NOT mandatory if clinical situation clearly warrants treatment (e.g., child contact aged <5 years, PLWH in high-burden setting)

✅
Key Message for PLWH: HIV patients must receive TPT regardless of TST result if they have been exposed to TB. A negative PPD in HIV does NOT rule out LTBI (anergy). Use clinical screening algorithm (4 symptoms). ART + TPT can be given concurrently — ensure drug interactions considered (rifampin/rifapentine + protease inhibitors/NNRTIs).
Pyridoxine (Vitamin B6) Supplementation
⚠️
Add pyridoxine (10–25 mg/day) with isoniazid in patients at high risk of peripheral neuropathy:
• Alcohol use disorder
• Malnutrition
• Pregnant and lactating women
• Chronic renal failure
• Diabetes mellitus
• HIV infection
This prevents isoniazid-induced peripheral neuropathy (B6 depletion).
BCG Vaccination
  • Derived from attenuated M. bovis; first given in 1921
  • Efficacy varies widely: 0–80% in different studies
  • Most effective: prevents severe disseminated childhood TB (meningitis, miliary) in young children
  • WHO recommends for routine use at birth in high-prevalence countries
  • NOT recommended for routine use in U.S. (low prevalence; variability in protection; confounds TST)
  • Contraindicated in HIV-infected adults and children
  • BCG induces TST reactivity → reason to prefer IGRA in BCG-vaccinated persons

Exam Traps — Board Exam Coach

🎯
These traps are derived directly from the 23 exam questions. Each one shows how the question is presented, why the wrong answer is tempting, and what the correct reasoning is.
★ Trap 01 — PPD Cutoff in Hemodialysis Patient
Diabetic on Hemodialysis, Family Member with TB, PPD Induration 8mm
65-year-old man with diabetes mellitus on hemodialysis. A family member was recently diagnosed with active TB. PPD shows 8mm induration. What is the best treatment?
⚠ Distractor: "8mm is below the standard 10mm cutoff, so no treatment needed." Many students use the 10mm threshold by default.
✅ Isoniazid for 6 months (TPT). This patient's PPD cutoff is ≥5mm, not 10mm. Hemodialysis (end-stage renal disease) = HIGH RISK group → threshold is ≥5mm. 8mm > 5mm → treat.
Chronic renal failure/hemodialysis carries a relative risk of 10–25 for active TB. Combined with recent close contact exposure (family member), this patient meets criteria for preventive treatment. Also: diabetes alone → more stringent cutoff. Active TB must be excluded before starting TPT.
🔑 Keyword: HEMODIALYSIS → ≥5mm cutoff
★ Trap 02 — Culture-Positive at 3 Months: MDR-TB
Standard 4-Drug TB Therapy, Culture Still Positive at 3 Months
32-year-old with active TB on standard 4-drug therapy for 3 months. Good adherence (DOT). Monthly sputum cultures still positive. What is the most appropriate next step?
⚠ Distractor: "Add streptomycin to the regimen" or "Continue the same regimen for the remaining time." Students may not connect persistent culture positivity to MDR-TB.
✅ Send cultures for drug susceptibility testing (DST) immediately and initiate MDR-TB workup. Reclassify as MDR-TB and treat accordingly (refer for MDR regimen including Bedaquiline/Fluoroquinolone).
Culture-positive at ≥3 months despite good adherence = treatment failure → drug resistance is likely. Never add just one drug to a failing regimen (creates resistance). Always add ≥2 effective new drugs simultaneously. Start empiric MDR regimen while awaiting DST. MDR-TB requires bedaquiline-containing all-oral regimens (BPaLM preferred).
🔑 Keyword: CULTURE POSITIVE AT 3 MONTHS → suspect MDR-TB
★ Trap 03 — BCG-Vaccinated Transplant Candidate: Use IGRA
Diabetic Preparing for Transplant, BCG-Vaccinated, PPD 11mm
A diabetic patient is preparing for transplantation. She received BCG vaccination as a child. PPD shows 11mm induration. What is the next best step?
⚠ Distractor: "PPD 11mm > 10mm → treat with INH." However, BCG vaccination causes false-positive PPD reactions. The 11mm may not represent true M. tuberculosis infection.
✅ Perform IGRA (Interferon-Gamma Release Assay) — it is more specific in BCG-vaccinated patients and not affected by BCG. If IGRA is positive → treat. If IGRA negative → no treatment needed.
BCG antigens cross-react with PPD antigens → false-positive TST. IGRA uses RD1-encoded antigens (ESAT-6 and CFP-10) that are NOT present in BCG → highly specific for M. tuberculosis. In BCG-vaccinated individuals, IGRA is the preferred test. Organ transplant recipients: threshold for positive TST is ≥5mm, but specificity concerns in BCG-vaccinated patients make IGRA the better choice.
🔑 Keyword: BCG vaccinated → IGRA more specific (not PPD)
★ Trap 04 — Rifampin Toxicity: Autoimmune Thrombocytopenia
Which Condition Requires Stopping Anti-TB Therapy?
A patient on anti-TB therapy develops purpura and thrombocytopenia. Lab confirms autoimmune thrombocytopenia. Which drug is responsible and what should be done?
⚠ Distractor: "Isoniazid causes thrombocytopenia" or "Pyrazinamide causes platelet drop." Isoniazid causes hepatitis and peripheral neuropathy; pyrazinamide causes hyperuricemia/gout.
✅ Rifampin causes autoimmune thrombocytopenia → STOP rifampin PERMANENTLY. It should never be re-administered.
Rifampin is a known cause of immune-mediated thrombocytopenia. This is an absolute contraindication to re-exposure. Rechallenging with rifampin risks severe, potentially fatal thrombocytopenic episodes. The patient requires a rifampin-free regimen for the remainder of TB treatment.
🔑 Keyword: THROMBOCYTOPENIA → rifampin → stop permanently
★ Trap 05 — TB Screening in Hospital (Post-Surgical Exposure)
60-Year-Old Post-Neurosurgery, Healthcare Workers Exposed
A 60-year-old patient undergoes neurosurgery. Postoperatively, it is discovered he has active pulmonary TB. Healthcare workers in the operating and recovery rooms were exposed. What is the screening recommendation?
⚠ Distractor: "Only screen those who directly coughed on" or "Only screen those with symptoms." Some may think selective screening is sufficient.
✅ Screen ALL exposed healthcare workers with PPD (or IGRA). Surgery does not generate aerosols (unlike active coughing), but exposure in an unventilated shared space still carries risk. All staff in the OR and recovery room should be screened.
TB transmission occurs via droplet nuclei. Healthcare workers in enclosed spaces with an unrecognized infectious TB patient require screening even without direct coughing exposure. PPD cutoff for healthcare workers: ≥10mm. Two-step baseline testing for new HCW to prevent misclassification of boosted reactions as conversions.
🔑 Keyword: EXPOSED HEALTHCARE WORKERS → PPD for all
★ Trap 06 — TB Meningitis: CSF Pattern
Subacute Meningitis with High Protein, Low Glucose, Lymphocytes → Diagnosis
75-year-old man with 38 weeks of fever, headache, CSF shows: lymphocytes 30, protein elevated, glucose low. Most likely diagnosis?
⚠ Distractor: "West Nile virus" — WNV meningitis can have lymphocytic CSF but glucose is typically NORMAL; it would not present with 38 weeks of symptoms (too long a course). Herpes viral meningitis: normal glucose, shorter course.
✅ M. tuberculosis (TB meningitis). The combination of: lymphocytic pleocytosis + HIGH protein (100–800 mg/dL) + LOW glucose + SUBACUTE course (weeks) is classic for TB meningitis.
TB meningitis evolves over 1–2 weeks (longer than bacterial meningitis), is pronounced at the base of the brain, and causes cranial nerve palsies. Protein is markedly elevated (1–8 g/L). AFBs are rarely seen on direct CSF smear. Treatment should be started immediately on positive Xpert or on high clinical suspicion.
🔑 Keyword: HIGH protein + LOW glucose + LYMPHOCYTES + WEEKS course = TB meningitis
★ Trap 07 — Pleural TB: Best Diagnostic Test
What Is the Best Test for Pleural TB Effusion?
What is the most informative diagnostic test for pleural TB (pleural effusion)?
⚠ Distractor: "Acid-fast smear of pleural fluid" or "Xpert MTB/RIF on pleural fluid." Both have very low sensitivity for pleural TB. Culture of pleural fluid is often falsely negative.
✅ Needle biopsy of the pleura (with culture and histology) — yields positive results in up to 80% of cases. ADA (Adenosine Deaminase) is also helpful. Xpert on BIOPSY yields ~75% sensitivity; Xpert on FLUID is NOT recommended (only ~50% sensitivity).
Pleural TB is a hypersensitivity reaction — organisms are sparse in the fluid. The pleural biopsy samples the tissue directly where granulomas form → much higher yield. ADA is a useful adjunct: elevated = consistent with TB; very low = can exclude TB.
🔑 Keyword: PLEURAL TB → biopsy (not fluid) → 80% yield
★ Trap 08 — IGRA in IVDU: Best Test and Threshold
IVDU Patient: What Should You Know About Testing?
A patient who is an intravenous drug user (IVDU) needs TB screening. What is the preferred approach?
⚠ Distractor: "PPD is positive at ≥10mm, so use 10mm cutoff" or "IGRA is the same as PPD in IVDU." While the PPD threshold is ≥10mm in IVDU, IGRA is preferred because it's not affected by BCG and is more specific.
✅ IGRA is more reliable in IVDU (not affected by BCG, more specific). If PPD is used: threshold is ≥10mm. IGRA does not require reading at 48–72 hours and is not affected by boosting phenomena or BCG vaccination.
IVDU have a relative risk of 10–30 for active TB. They often have BCG vaccination history (immigrants, certain populations). IGRA avoids BCG false-positives and has no boosting effect.
🔑 Keyword: IVDU → PPD ≥10mm; IGRA preferred (BCG-independent)
★ Trap 09 — HIV + Active TB Exposure: No TST Needed
HIV-Positive Patient Exposed to TB: TPT Without TST?
A 30-year-old anergic HIV-positive woman was unknowingly exposed to active TB. TST = 3mm (negative). What is the correct treatment approach?
⚠ Distractor: "TST is negative → no treatment needed." This is wrong. HIV patients are anergic and can have false-negative TST even when infected.
✅ HIV-infected patients should receive TPT regardless of TST/IGRA result if they have been exposed to an infectious TB patient. The negative TST in an HIV patient represents anergy, not absence of infection.
HIV suppresses cell-mediated immunity → TST (skin test) requires functional CD4+ T cells to mount a skin reaction. False-negative TST is common in HIV. The risk of progression from infection to disease in PLWH is very high (up to 13% per year). TPT with isoniazid reduces this risk significantly.
🔑 Keyword: HIV + TB exposure → treat REGARDLESS of TST result
★ Trap 10 — Standard Initial Regimen: 4 Drugs
80-Year-Old With No Suspected Drug Resistance: What Regimen?
An 80-year-old patient is newly diagnosed with drug-susceptible pulmonary TB with no prior exposure to anti-TB drugs. What is the correct treatment?
⚠ Distractor: "3 drugs (INH + Rifampin + PZA)" or "Add levofloxacin instead of ethambutol." Levofloxacin is NOT part of the standard first-line 4-drug regimen.
✅ Isoniazid + Rifampin + Pyrazinamide + Ethambutol (2HRZE/4HR) — the standard 6-month first-line regimen for drug-susceptible TB.
The intensive phase requires 4 drugs to prevent emergence of resistance. Ethambutol protects against resistance to the other 3 drugs while DST results are pending. Levofloxacin is used for MDR-TB or isoniazid-resistant TB, not standard drug-susceptible disease. Tobramycin has no role in TB treatment.
🔑 Keyword: Drug-susceptible TB → HRZE × 2 months + HR × 4 months
★ Trap 11 — Diagnosis: What to Order for Suspected Active TB
Young Woman with Hemoptysis, Upper Lobe CXR Infiltrate, ESR High → Best Next Step
37-year-old woman with hemoptysis, fever, upper-lobe CXR infiltrate. Hb 10, elevated ESR, CRP elevated. What is the most appropriate diagnostic test?
⚠ Distractor: "PCR test is the best" or "Acid-fast smear is definitive." PCR alone doesn't replace comprehensive workup; smear alone has only 40–60% sensitivity.
✅ Tuberculin Skin Test (TST/PPD) + sputum cultures/Xpert MTB/RIF to confirm active TB. For latent TB screening in this context, TST is appropriate. For active disease: Xpert MTB/RIF is first-line diagnostic test; culture is gold standard.
In this clinical scenario, the combination of upper-lobe infiltrate + hemoptysis + constitutional symptoms in a young woman = classic post-primary TB. TST (PPD) helps document infection. Xpert MTB/RIF on sputum confirms active disease rapidly. Sputum culture is the gold standard for definitive diagnosis and allows DST.
🔑 Keyword: Suspected active TB → Xpert MTB/RIF (rapid) + culture (gold standard)
★ Trap 12 — Pott's Disease: NO Steroids
Which Disease Cannot Be Treated with Steroids in TB?
A 30-year-old Eritrean woman presents with back pain and progressive paraparesis. CXR shows old TB lesions. MRI shows vertebral destruction at T10-T11. Diagnosis: Pott's disease. Steroids — yes or no?
⚠ Distractor: "Steroids are used adjunctively in TB meningitis and pericarditis, so they should be used here too." Students confuse the TB contexts where steroids ARE used with Pott's disease.
✅ Pott's disease (TB spondylitis) is NOT treated with steroids. Treatment: anti-TB chemotherapy. Paraparesis from large abscess → surgical drainage (emergency).
Steroids are used in: TB meningitis (reduces mortality in HIV-negative), pericardial TB (trend toward benefit), and IRIS. Pott's disease responds to chemotherapy alone. Steroids have NO proven benefit in skeletal TB and may worsen outcomes by reducing immune containment. The paraparesis is managed surgically (abscess drainage), not with steroids.
🔑 Keyword: Pott's disease → NO steroids; surgery for paraparesis
★ Trap 13 — Isoniazid-Resistant TB: Add Quinolone for 6 Months
Patient with Isoniazid-Resistant TB Already on Standard Regimen
28-year-old woman, regular close contact with TB index case, on isoniazid-based regimen. Resistance to isoniazid is confirmed. What is the best revised treatment approach?
⚠ Distractor: "Continue INH + add more drugs" or "Switch to 5-drug regimen with 18 months." Isoniazid-resistant TB (still rifampin-susceptible) has its own specific guideline.
✅ Rifampin + Ethambutol + Pyrazinamide + Levofloxacin (a fluoroquinolone) for 6 months. This is the recommended regimen for isoniazid-resistant, rifampin-susceptible TB.
When isoniazid resistance is confirmed but rifampin resistance is excluded, the regimen replaces isoniazid with a fluoroquinolone (levofloxacin). This 6-month fluoroquinolone-containing regimen is effective. Only use this after rifampin resistance has been definitively excluded by DST.
🔑 Keyword: INH-resistant TB → Rifampin + EMB + PZA + Levofloxacin × 6 months
★ Trap 14 — IGRA Tests Latent TB, NOT Active TB
What Does IGRA Actually Detect?
What does the Interferon-Gamma Release Assay (IGRA) diagnose?
⚠ Distractor: "Active tuberculosis" or "HIV-related immunodeficiency" or "Severity of TB disease." IGRA is frequently misunderstood as a test for active disease.
✅ IGRA detects Latent Tuberculosis Infection (LTBI). It measures the host's immune response (IFN-γ release by memory T cells) to M. tuberculosis antigens — it confirms the immune system has encountered M. tuberculosis, NOT that there is active disease.
IGRA and TST both measure immunological memory of M. tuberculosis exposure. They cannot distinguish active disease from latent infection, cannot predict progression, and have reduced sensitivity in immunocompromised patients. For ACTIVE disease, use sputum culture + Xpert MTB/RIF.
🔑 Keyword: IGRA → detects LATENT TB infection, not active disease
