01

Overview & Classification

What Is Bone Marrow Failure?

Bone marrow failure diseases cause low blood counts due to deficient hematopoiesis — the marrow cannot produce enough cells. This is distinct from peripheral destruction (hemolytic anemia, ITP, immune leukopenias) where the marrow is actually working harder.

  • Aplastic Anemia (AA): Pancytopenia + bone marrow hypocellularity (empty marrow)
  • Myelodysplastic Syndrome (MDS): Cytopenias + dysplastic (morphologically abnormal) marrow + high AML risk
  • Pure Red Cell Aplasia (PRCA): Selective erythroid failure — only red cell precursors are absent
  • Single-lineage cytopenias: Agranulocytosis, amegakaryocytic thrombocytopenia
Classification Principle
Marrow failure syndromes are classified by the dominant morphologic features of the bone marrow. While the distinction is usually clear, some processes — especially aplastic anemia vs. hypoplastic MDS — can overlap or evolve into one another. Many share an immune-mediated mechanism and an element of genomic instability that raises malignant transformation risk.

Key Clinical Point
The internist/GP must recognize marrow failure syndromes because prognosis without treatment is poor, yet effective therapies exist that require hematologist/oncologist input for proper delivery. Genomic testing has revolutionized diagnosis — but results require expert interpretation.

Aplastic Anemia — Definition & Epidemiology

Definition

Aplastic anemia = pancytopenia + bone marrow hypocellularity. The marrow is replaced by fat.

Acquired (Immune)

Most common form. T-cell mediated destruction of hematopoietic stem cells. The paradigmatic form in clinical boards questions.

Iatrogenic / Secondary

After intensive cytotoxic chemotherapy, transplant conditioning, or accidental radiation — this is an expected consequence, not immune in origin.

Constitutional (Inherited)

Germline genetic defects. Includes Fanconi anemia, dyskeratosis congenita, telomere biology disorders (TERC, TERT mutations), GATA2 deficiency, RUNX1 familial platelet disorder. Can present in adults without obvious physical anomalies.

Key Epidemiology Numbers

Incidence

Europe/Israel: 2 per million per year
Thailand/China: 5–7 per million per year
Equal M:F ratio
Biphasic age distribution: Major peak teens/20s + second peak in older adults

Severity Criteria (Classic)

Severe AA defined by ≥2 of 3:
• ANC <500/μL
• Platelets <20,000/μL
• Corrected retic <1% (or abs retic <60,000/μL)

Modern predictors: Abs retic >25,000 and lymphocytes >1,000/μL predict better IST response

Board Distinction — Inherited vs Acquired
Classical IBMFS (Fanconi anemia, dyskeratosis congenita) present in childhood with physical anomalies. However, telomere biology disorders (TERC/TERT mutations) and GATA2 / RUNX1 germline syndromes can present as marrow failure in completely normal-appearing adults. Always take a family history!

Radiation

  • Damages DNA; actively mitotic tissues (marrow) are most susceptible
  • Radiation dose estimated from rate/degree of blood count decline, or by dosimetry
  • MDS and leukemia (but NOT aplastic anemia) are late effects of radiation
  • Clinical relevance: estimate prognosis, protect medical staff from radioactive excreta

Chemicals — Benzene

  • The prototypical marrow toxin: epidemiologic, clinical, and laboratory evidence links it to aplastic anemia, acute leukemia, and marrow abnormalities
  • For leukemia: incidence correlates with cumulative exposure
  • Not all heavily exposed workers develop myelotoxicity — genetic susceptibility matters
  • Employment history critical (secondary solvent use in industry)
  • Benzene-related disease has declined with industrial regulation

Drugs

Dose-Dependent (All Recipients Affected)

Cytotoxic chemotherapy drugs: alkylating agents, antimetabolites, antimitotics, some antibiotics — these are expected toxicities

Frequently but Not Inevitably

Benzene — high probability but not universal

Idiosyncratic Reactions (Low Probability, Classic Board Drugs)

  • Chloramphenicol — the most classically tested antibiotic
  • NSAIDs — phenylbutazone, indomethacin, ibuprofen, sulindac, aspirin
  • Anticonvulsants — hydantoins (phenytoin), carbamazepine, phenacemide, felbamate
  • Heavy metals — gold, arsenic, bismuth, mercury
  • Sulfonamides — antibiotics, antithyroid (methimazole, PTU), antidiabetics (tolbutamide, chlorpropamide), carbonic anhydrase inhibitors (acetazolamide)
  • Antihistamines — cimetidine, chlorpheniramine
  • d-Penicillamine
  • Insecticides, antiprotozoals (quinacrine, chloroquine)
Causation ≠ Association
A drug may have been prescribed for the first symptoms of marrow failure (antibiotics for fever) or provoked the first symptom of a pre-existing disease (NSAIDs → petechiae in thrombocytopenic patient). Even 10–20× risk increase = only a handful of cases per year due to disease rarity.

Infections

  • Transient mild cytopenias are common with many viral and bacterial infections
  • Parvovirus B19 → pure red cell aplasia (rarely generalized marrow failure)
  • EBV (infectious mononucleosis) — rarely causes aplasia
  • Hepatitis (seronegative) — posthepatitis aplasia accounts for ~5% of cases; young men; very severe pancytopenia 1–2 months after liver inflammation; non-A, non-B, non-C; presumed immune-mediated
  • HIV-1 (AIDS)

Immunologic Diseases

  • Transfusion-associated GVHD — aplasia is inevitable cause of death; occurs after nonirradiated blood products in immunodeficient recipients
  • Eosinophilic fasciitis — rare collagen vascular disease; characterized by painful induration of subcutaneous tissues
  • Thymoma and hypoimmunoglobulinemia can co-occur with aplastic anemia
  • Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) — pancytopenia with marrow hypoplasia

Special Associations

PNH Association

Acquired PIG-A mutation in hematopoietic stem cell → clone deficient in GPI-linked surface proteins. PNH clones detectable by flow cytometry in ≥50% of AA patients at presentation. Patients with AA may later develop hemolytic PNH; PNH patients may later develop frank aplasia.

Pregnancy

Aplastic anemia can very rarely complicate pregnancy. May resolve with delivery or with spontaneous/induced abortion.

TERT/TERC mutations have variable penetrance — family members with the same mutation may have normal counts but subtle evidence of hematopoietic insufficiency. Aplastic Anemia — Pathophysiology

Stem Cell Compartment Destruction

  • CD34+ cells (marker of early hematopoietic cells) are greatly diminished
  • Committed and primitive progenitor cells are virtually absent in functional studies
  • Stem cell pool reduced to ≤1% of normal in severe disease at presentation
  • Bone marrow replaced by fat — visible on biopsy and MRI of the spine

Immune-Mediated Stem Cell Destruction (Acquired AA)

First evidence: recovery of marrow function after antilymphocyte globulin given as conditioning for BMT — suggesting immune pathophysiology.

  • T-cell driven: Blood and marrow cells from AA patients suppress normal hematopoietic progenitor growth in vitro; removing T cells from AA marrow improves hematopoiesis
  • Cytotoxic T-cell clones: Increased activated CD8+ T-cell clones (visualizable by single-cell RNA sequencing, mass cytometry); these decline with successful IST
  • Type 1 cytokines: IFN-γ induces Fas expression on CD34+ cells → apoptotic death of hematopoietic precursors
  • HLA loss: Immune selection can cause loss of HLA expression on HSCs; PNH clones are almost exclusively seen in immune marrow failure (GPI-anchor negative cells escape T-cell attack)
  • Genetic susceptibility: polymorphisms in histocompatibility antigens, cytokine genes, and T-cell polarization genes convert a normal immune response into a sustained autoimmune process

Chemical and Drug Injury Mechanism

  • Massive physical/chemical insults → direct marrow toxicity
  • Idiosyncratic reactions: altered drug metabolism → reactive electrophilic intermediates that bind to cellular macromolecules
  • Benzene → derivative hydroquinones and quinolones → tissue injury
  • Genetic susceptibility in metabolic pathways explains why only rare individuals develop aplasia despite common drug exposures
Why PNH Clones Expand in Immune AA
In immune aplastic anemia, the T-cell attack targets normal hematopoietic cells bearing HLA. GPI-negative cells (PNH clone) lack the surface proteins recognized by T-cells, so they escape immune destruction and are selectively expanded. PNH clones are therefore a marker of immune pathophysiology and predict responsiveness to immunosuppressive therapy.

Aplastic Anemia — Clinical Features & Diagnosis

History

  • Onset: Can be abrupt or insidious
  • Most common early symptom: Bleeding — easy bruising, gum oozing, nosebleeds, heavy menstrual flow, petechiae; days to weeks duration
  • With thrombocytopenia: massive hemorrhage unusual, but CNS bleeding (intracranial or retinal) can be catastrophic — small amounts matter
  • Anemia symptoms: lassitude, weakness, shortness of breath, pounding sensation in ears
  • Infection is an UNUSUAL first symptom in AA (contrast with agranulocytosis, where pharyngitis/sepsis occur early)
  • Patients often feel/look remarkably well despite drastically reduced blood counts
  • Red flags for other diagnoses: Systemic complaints, weight loss → not typical AA
  • Always ask: prior drug use, chemical exposure, preceding viral illness, family history of hematologic diseases
Clues to Inherited Syndromes in History
Pulmonary fibrosis or liver fibrosis → telomere biology disorder; family history of unusual infections and warts → GATA2 deficiency; early hair graying → telomere disorder; abnormal blood counts since childhood → constitutional

Physical Examination

  • Petechiae and ecchymoses — typical
  • Retinal hemorrhages — may be present
  • Pallor of skin and mucous membranes — if symptomatic for weeks
  • Lymphadenopathy and splenomegaly = HIGHLY ATYPICAL for aplastic anemia
  • Café au lait spots, short stature → Fanconi anemia
  • Peculiar nails, leukoplakia → dyskeratosis congenita
  • Early hair graying (patient may be using hair dye to hide it!) → telomere biology disorder

Laboratory Studies

Peripheral Blood Smear

• Large erythrocytes (macro/oval-macrocytes)
• Paucity of platelets and granulocytes
• Elevated MCV (very common)
• Reticulocytes: absent or very few
• Lymphocytes: normal or reduced
• Immature myeloid forms → suggest leukemia or MDS, NOT AA
• Nucleated RBCs → marrow fibrosis or tumor invasion
• Abnormal platelets → peripheral destruction or MDS

Bone Marrow (Critical for Diagnosis)

• Usually readily aspirated but dilute on smear
• Fatty biopsy specimen — grossly pale
• "Dry tap" → suggests fibrosis or myelophthisis (NOT AA)
• Severe aplasia: only RBCs, residual lymphocytes, and stromal cells in aspirate
• Biopsy (>1 cm): mainly fat; hematopoietic cells <25% of marrow space; sometimes virtually all fat
• Residual cells should have normal morphology (mild megaloblastic erythropoiesis acceptable)
• Megakaryocytes greatly reduced/absent
• Granulomas → infectious etiology

Ancillary Studies

  • Chromosome breakage study (diepoxybutane or mitomycin C on peripheral blood) → children and younger adults → diagnose Fanconi anemia
  • Telomere length (peripheral blood leukocytes) → very short = telomere biology disorder → pursue family studies + nucleotide sequencing
  • Bone marrow cytogenetics → should be negative in typical AA (abnormal in MDS)
  • Flow cytometry for PNH → sensitive test; PNH clone strongly suggests immune-mediated disease
  • Serologic studies → EBV, HIV; posthepatitis AA is seronegative
  • Next-generation sequencing (genomics) → Germline panels (>50 genes) for constitutional BMF in children and suspicious adults; somatic myeloid neoplasm panels (~100 genes) when MDS suspected
Key Genomic Distinction
Spliceosome gene mutations and cohesion gene family mutations are frequent in MDS and UNEXPECTED in aplastic anemia. Finding them should prompt reclassification toward MDS.

Diagnosis & Differential Diagnosis

The diagnosis of aplastic anemia is usually straightforward: pancytopenia + fatty bone marrow. Immune AA is a disease of the young — it should be the leading diagnosis in a pancytopenic adolescent or young adult.

  • Acquired vs Constitutional: Marrow morphology usually indistinguishable (exception: GATA2 deficiency has characteristic megakaryocyte atypia); distinguished by family history, flow cytometry (GATA2: absent B and NK cells), genomic testing
  • AA vs Hypocellular MDS: MDS favored by — morphologic abnormalities (especially megakaryocytes and myeloid precursors), cytogenetic abnormalities, somatic mutations in myeloid neoplasm genes; there is an unclear boundary, especially with 13q and 20q deletions
  • Secondary pancytopenia: usually obvious (massive spleen in cirrhosis, metastatic cancer history, miliary TB on CXR)

Prognosis

  • Natural history of severe AA: rapid deterioration and death without treatment
  • Few patients show spontaneous recovery
  • Major prognostic determinant = blood count
  • Additional prognostic factors: presence of PNH clone, short telomeres, somatically mutated white cells
  • ASXL1 mutations → worse prognosis, clonal evolution
  • Clonal evolution (~10–15% over a decade after ATG): isolated chromosome abnormalities, MDS development, or leukemia

Aplastic Anemia — Treatment

Critical First Point
Glucocorticoids are NOT of value as primary therapy for aplastic anemia and among their multiple toxicities is increased susceptibility to fungal infection. Also: awaiting spontaneous recovery is rarely advisable unless blood counts are only modestly depressed.

Hematopoietic Stem Cell Transplantation (HSCT)

  • First choice for younger patients with a fully histocompatible sibling donor
  • Long-term survival for children with sibling donors: ~90% or better
  • Adults: higher risk of chronic GVHD and infections → reduced but still good outcomes
  • In transplant candidates: avoid transfusions from family members to prevent sensitization to histocompatibility antigens
  • Unrelated matched donors: outcomes similar to sibling donors with high-resolution HLA matching, though GVHD/infection more frequent
  • Cord blood: source for children especially
  • HLA haploidentical family donor: increasingly popular (donor almost always available); post-transplant cyclophosphamide prevents GVHD
  • Transplant protocols now usually do NOT include radiation to avoid late cancer
  • Overall survival equivalent between transplant and IST — but transplant cures AA while IST leaves risk of relapse and malignant evolution

Immunosuppression: Triple Therapy (Current Standard of Care)

Standard Regimen (FDA-Approved 2018)
Horse ATG + Cyclosporine + Eltrombopag ("Triple Therapy")
Induces hematologic recovery in 70–80% of patients; complete response ~50%. FDA-approved standard of care since 2018.

1. Horse ATG (Antithymocyte Globulin)

  • Mechanism: Depletes T-lymphocytes that are destroying hematopoietic precursors
  • Administration: Intravenous infusions — requires hospitalization
  • Side effect — Serum Sickness: Flu-like illness with characteristic cutaneous eruption and arthralgias, ~10 days after initiating treatment
  • Methylprednisolone administered alongside ATG to ameliorate serum sickness (heterologous protein reaction) — but excessive/extended glucocorticoid use → avascular joint necrosis
  • Rabbit ATG is much less effective — possibly because it reduces T-regulatory cell numbers

2. Cyclosporine

  • Mechanism: Inhibits T-cell activation; helps maintain remissions achieved with ATG
  • Administration: Oral, high initial dose with adjustment to blood levels
  • Side effects: Nephrotoxicity, hypertension, seizures
  • Some centers replace with tacrolimus
  • Patients may become cyclosporine-dependent (relapse when tapered)

3. Eltrombopag (TPO Mimetic)

  • Mechanism: Thrombopoietin mimetic → stimulates hematopoietic stem cell (and iron chelation and increased regulatory T cells as possible additional benefits)
  • Serendipitous discovery: Initially tried in refractory AA; showed ~40% response rate; then added to first-line IST with dramatic improvement
  • Key trial: Eltrombopag added to first-line horse ATG → overall response ~80%, complete response ~50%
  • Administration: Once daily for 6 months
  • Side effect: Hepatotoxicity (most important)
  • Children have less additional benefit from eltrombopag than adults
  • Romiplostim (another TPO mimetic) also reported effective
Relapse After IST
Relapse (recurrent pancytopenia) is frequent, often when cyclosporine or eltrombopag is tapered. Most but not all patients respond to reinstitution. Bone marrow exams should be performed if unfavorable change in blood counts (may represent clonal evolution to MDS).

Androgens

  • Effectiveness NOT verified in controlled trials
  • Mechanism: sex hormones upregulate telomerase gene activity → improves marrow function
  • Particularly useful in patients with moderate disease and a telomere gene defect
  • Trial period: 3–4 months; may improve all blood counts

Supportive Care

Infection Management

Infection in severe neutropenia must be aggressively treated with prompt parenteral, broad-spectrum antibiotics — therapy is empirical, do NOT wait for culture results. Persistent/recrudescent fever → suspect fungal disease (Candida, Aspergillus — common after antibacterials). Improved antifungal drugs are a major reason for improved AA prognosis. Granulocyte transfusions if bacterial/fungal infection is progressive despite antibiotics. Non-absorbed antibiotics for gut decontamination: poorly tolerated and unproven. Reverse isolation: does NOT reduce infection mortality.

Transfusion Support

Platelets: Goal platelet count >10,000/μL. Use single donors, leukodepleted products. HLA-matched platelets for alloimmunized patients. Avoid ASA and NSAIDs. Suppress menstruation (oral estrogens or FSH/LH antagonists).

RBCs: Transfuse to maintain activity (usually Hb ~70 g/L, or 90 g/L if cardiac/pulmonary disease). ~2 units every 2 weeks if no marrow function.

Iron chelation: Add deferoxamine or deferasirox at ~50th transfusion to prevent secondary hemochromatosis.

HGFs (EPO, G-CSF): NOT effective in aplastic anemia.

Pure Red Cell Aplasia (PRCA)

Definition

PRCA = anemia + reticulocytopenia + absent or rare erythroid precursor cells in bone marrow. Only the erythroid lineage is selectively absent. Unlike aplastic anemia and MDS, the unaffected lineages (granulocytes, platelets) are quantitatively and qualitatively normal.

  • Adults: PRCA usually acquired
  • Congenital (Diamond-Blackfan anemia): diagnosed at birth or early childhood; responds to glucocorticoids; ribosomal protein gene mutations
  • In AA and MDS, affected and unaffected lineages coexist; in single-lineage failure, only one line is lost
  • Progression to pancytopenia or leukemia is unusual in single-lineage failure syndromes

Classification of PRCA

Self-Limited

Transient erythroblastopenia of childhood (TEC)
Transient aplastic crisis in hemolytic anemia (acute B19 parvovirus)
Fetal red blood cell aplasia (in utero B19 — nonimmune hydrops fetalis)

Congenital

Diamond-Blackfan anemia (ribosomopathy)

Acquired — Immune / Malignancy

• Primary immune (idiopathic)
• MDS — 5q– syndrome (ribosomal protein gene deletion mimics DBA)
• Thymoma (minority of cases)
• Lymphoid malignancies (LGL is most frequent) and CLL
• Paraneoplastic to solid tumors
• Connective tissue disorders: SLE, JRA, RA
• Multiple endocrine gland insufficiency

Acquired — Other Causes

• Parvovirus B19 (persistent infection in immunodeficient)
• Hepatitis, adult T-cell leukemia virus, EBV
• Pregnancy
• Drugs: especially phenytoin, azathioprine, chloramphenicol, procainamide, isoniazid
• Antibodies to erythropoietin (subcutaneous EPO administration)
• Hypogammaglobulinemia
• Allogeneic SCT complication (antibodies to blood group antigens)

Parvovirus B19 — High Yield Details

  • Common virus → causes fifth disease (benign exanthem of childhood) and polyarthralgia/arthritis in adults
  • In patients with underlying hemolysis (any condition with high RBC demand): transient aplastic crisis → acute, temporary worsening of anemia due to failed erythropoiesis
  • In healthy individuals: temporary cessation of RBC production is not clinically apparent (skin/joint symptoms = immune complex deposition)
  • In immunodeficient patients (congenital, acquired, or iatrogenic): persistent viral infection → chronic pure red cell aplasia
  • Bone marrow finding: giant pronormoblasts — pathognomonic cytopathic sign of B19 infection
  • Viral tropism: uses erythrocyte P antigen as cellular receptor
  • Diagnosis: Detection of viral DNA in blood — IgG and IgM antibodies are commonly absent in immunodeficient patients
  • Treatment: Intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) — almost all respond
Boards Trap — Parvovirus Diagnosis
In immunodeficient patients with parvovirus B19 PRCA, serology (IgG/IgM) may be NEGATIVE because the patient cannot mount an antibody response. Diagnosis requires PCR for viral DNA, not serology.

Treatment of Acquired PRCA

  • First: identify and address underlying disease or drug exposure
  • Search for thymoma by radiographic procedures (excision indicated, but anemia may not improve with surgery)
  • Parvovirus B19: IVIG
  • Idiopathic and immune PRCA: majority respond to immunosuppression
    • Glucocorticoids, cyclosporine, ATG, azathioprine, cyclophosphamide — all reported effective
    • Presence of erythroid colonies in vitro is predictive of response to IST
  • PRCA is compatible with long-term survival with supportive care alone (RBC transfusions + iron chelation)
  • T-cell inhibition is probably the prevalent immune mechanism in most PRCAs

Myelodysplastic Syndromes (MDS)

Definition

MDS = heterogeneous group of hematologic disorders characterized by: (1) cytopenias due to bone marrow failure (ineffective hematopoiesis), AND (2) high risk of AML development.

  • Anemia due to ineffective erythropoiesis (marrow makes cells but they die before release), often with thrombocytopenia and neutropenia
  • Dysmorphic (abnormal-appearing) bone marrow cells
  • Clonal hematopoietic stem cell disorder: disordered proliferation + impaired differentiation
  • Two current classifications: WHO 5th Edition (2022) and International Consensus Classification (ICC) 2022

Epidemiology

  • Disease of the elderly: Mean age at onset >70 years; slight male predominance
  • Incidence: 35–>100 per million general population; 120–>500 per million in older adults
  • US estimates: 30,000–40,000 new cases/year; prevalence 60,000–120,000
  • MDS is rare in children — when it occurs, usually has a constitutional genetic basis
  • Secondary/therapy-related MDS: Related to alkylating agents, other chemotherapy, or radiation; NOT age-related; latent period 5–7 years (radiomimetic agents) or 2 years (topoisomerase II inhibitors)

Etiology and Pathophysiology

  • Environmental exposures: radiation, benzene, smoking
  • Acquired AA, Fanconi anemia, and other constitutional marrow failure diseases can evolve into MDS
  • Clonal disease: Mutations accumulate in a single hematopoietic stem cell → clonal expansion with impaired differentiation
  • Both chromosomal and genetic instability → aging-related
  • Cytogenetic abnormalities in ~50% of patients (aneuploidy more common than translocations)
  • Non-random cytogenetic abnormalities: loss of 5, 7, and 20; trisomy 8; 11q23 after topoisomerase II inhibitors
  • Accelerated telomere attrition destabilizes genome → predisposes to chromosomal lesions

AML vs MDS Cutoff
WHO considers ≥20% blasts in bone marrow as the criterion separating AML from MDS. The ICC introduced an MDS/AML category (same outcomes with high blast counts). This distinction matters for treatment planning.

Clinical Features

  • Anemia dominates: Gradual onset of fatigue, weakness, dyspnea, pallor
  • At least 50% of patients are asymptomatic — MDS discovered incidentally on routine blood counts
  • Fever and weight loss → more features of myeloproliferative than myelodysplastic process
  • Physical exam: signs of anemia; ~20% of patients have splenomegaly
  • VEXAS syndrome: MDS + Sweet's syndrome (inflammatory disease) should prompt consideration; somatic UBA1 mutations; vacuolated myeloid/erythroid precursors on marrow

Laboratory Studies in MDS

Peripheral Blood

• Anemia (most cases) ± neutropenia ± thrombocytopenia
• Macrocytosis common
• Platelets: large, lack granules; functional abnormalities (bleeding despite adequate numbers)
• Neutrophils: hypogranulated, hyposegmented/ring nuclei, Döhle bodies, functionally deficient
• Circulating myeloblasts correlate with BM blast count (important for classification)
• Total WBC: normal or low (except CMML)
• PNH cells possible (clonal population)

Bone Marrow

• Usually normal or hypercellular (but 20% hypocellular — can be confused with AA)
• Dysplastic changes: dyserythropoiesis (nuclear abnormalities, ring sideroblasts), hypogranulation/hyposegmentation in granulocytes (increased myeloblasts), megakaryocytes with reduced/disorganized nuclei
• Megaloblastic nuclei + defective hemoglobinization in erythroid lineage
• Prognosis correlates with % BM blasts — must be enumerated manually AND by flow cytometry
• Flow cytometry: aberrant hematopoietic differentiation patterns

Prognosis — IPSS-R

  • Median survival: years (5q– or sideroblastic) to months (high-risk MDS with excess blasts)
  • IPSS-R (2012): Standard tool — includes marrow blasts (≤2, >2–<5, 5–10, >10–30%), 16 specific cytogenetic abnormalities in 5 risk subgroups, depth of cytopenias (Hb <80/80-100/≥100 g/L; plt <50/50-100/≥100×10³; ANC <800 vs ≥800), age/performance/ferritin/LDH/β2M
  • IPSS-M (2022): Incorporates molecular data; now emerging as current standard
  • Most die from complications of pancytopenia; ~⅓ succumb to concurrent disease unrelated to MDS
  • Therapy-related MDS: extremely poor; most progress within months to refractory AML

Treatment of MDS

Only hematopoietic stem cell transplantation offers cure of MDS.

HSCT in MDS

  • Survival: ~50% at 3 years in selected cohorts but improving
  • Results with unrelated matched donors similar to sibling donors
  • Older patients can be transplanted using reduced-intensity conditioning (RIC) — higher relapse risk
  • The transplant conundrum: high-risk patients have high transplant mortality; low-risk patients may do well for years without transplant
  • In practice, only a small proportion of MDS patients undergo transplantation

Hypomethylating Agents (HMA)

Azacitidine

Mechanism: DNA demethylating agent (epigenetic modulator) → restores normal gene regulation → allows differentiation from abnormal MDS stem cell

Administration: Subcutaneously, daily ×7 days, every 4 weeks; ≥4 cycles before assessing response

Outcomes: ~50% improved blood counts with decreased transfusion requirements; improves survival vs. supportive care

Limitation: Response dependent on continued administration; most eventually become refractory → AML

Toxicity: Myelosuppression (worsening blood counts)

Decitabine

Mechanism: Closely related to azacitidine; DNA demethylation

Administration: Continuous IV infusion, varying doses/durations 3–10 days in repeating cycles

Outcomes: 30–50% response in blood counts; ~1 year duration of response

Oral formulation: Decitabine-cedazuridine — FDA approved for high-risk MDS

Toxicity: Myelosuppression

HMAs frequently used in high-risk patients not candidates for transplant. In lower-risk, also effective but alternatives should be considered first. Likely HMA monotherapy will be replaced by HMA combination therapies.

Lenalidomide (5q– Syndrome)

  • Thalidomide derivative with favorable toxicity profile
  • Particularly effective in 5q– syndrome: High proportion become transfusion-independent with normal/near-normal Hb; cytogenetics also normalize
  • Administration: oral; most improve within 3 months
  • Toxicities: myelosuppression (worsening thrombocytopenia and neutropenia); increased risk of DVT and PE

Immunosuppression

  • May produce sustained transfusion independence and improve survival
  • ATG + cyclosporine + alemtuzumab (anti-CD52 monoclonal antibody) effective in younger MDS patients (<60 years) with more favorable IPSS
  • ~50% of patients with mainly refractory anemia responded to ATG/cyclosporine in retrospective reviews — especially those with hypocellular marrow

Targeted and Novel Agents

  • Ivosidenib (IDH1 inhibitor) — FDA approved for MDS
  • Luspatercept (TGF-β pathway modulator) — FDA approved for low-risk MDS anemia, particularly SF3B1 mutations
  • Venetoclax (BCL2 inhibitor) — FDA approved for AML; used with HMA in high-risk MDS but remains investigational in MDS
  • HGFs (EPO ± G-CSF): Can improve Hb in low serum EPO patients with low transfusion need; EPO improves survival via anemia amelioration; G-CSF alone did NOT improve survival in controlled trial
  • TPO mimetics: Improve platelet counts in some; may increase leukemic progression in high-risk MDS — no evidence of leukemic transformation in low-risk MDS
  • Promising in trials: TP53/splicing factor targeted therapies, inflammasome inhibitors, imetelstat (telomerase inhibitor)
Supportive Care in MDS
Same principles as AA. Many patients will be anemic for years. RBC transfusions + iron chelation with deferoxamine or deferasirox to prevent secondary hemochromatosis.

Myelophthisic Anemias

Definition and Pathophysiology

Myelophthisis (secondary myelofibrosis) = reactive fibrosis of the bone marrow. Distinguished from primary myelofibrosis/myeloid metaplasia (Chapter 108).

3 Pathophysiologic Features of Myelofibrosis
1. Proliferation of fibroblasts in the marrow space (myelofibrosis)
2. Extension of hematopoiesis into long bones and extramedullary sites — spleen, liver, lymph nodes (myeloid metaplasia)
3. Ineffective erythropoiesis
  • Etiology of fibrosis: dysregulated growth factors — platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF) and TGF-β implicated
  • Myelofibrosis is remarkable for pancytopenia despite very large numbers of circulating hematopoietic progenitor cells

Causes of Secondary Myelofibrosis

  • Metastatic cancer — epithelial cancer of breast, lung, prostate, or neuroblastoma (most common)
  • Infection: mycobacteria (TB, MAI), fungi, HIV, sarcoidosis
  • Gaucher disease — intracellular lipid deposition
  • Congenital osteopetrosis — absence of osteoclast remodeling → obliterates marrow space
  • Late consequence of radiation or radiomimetic drugs
  • Hematologic syndromes: CML, multiple myeloma, lymphomas, hairy cell leukemia

Clinical and Laboratory Features

  • Anemia dominant — usually normocytic, normochromic
  • Diagnosis suggested by leukoerythroblastic smear: characteristic blood smear picture
  • Erythrocyte morphology: circulating nucleated RBCs, teardrop cells (dacrocytes), shape distortions
  • Leukoerythroblastic reaction: circulating myelocytes, promyelocytes, myeloblasts; elevated WBC (mimics leukemoid reaction)
  • Platelets: may be abundant and giant
  • Characteristic "dry tap" on bone marrow aspiration → presumptive diagnosis before biopsy decalcified

Management

  • Course determined by etiology (usually metastatic tumor or advanced hematologic malignancy)
  • Treatable causes must be excluded — especially TB and fungal disease
  • Transfusion support can relieve symptoms

Exam Traps

Trap 01Glucocorticoids for Aplastic Anemia
A 22-year-old presents with severe aplastic anemia. The intern proposes starting high-dose methylprednisolone as initial immunosuppression. You should:
REFUSE — Glucocorticoids are NOT of value as primary therapy for aplastic anemia. They increase susceptibility to fungal infections. The correct first-line treatment is horse ATG + cyclosporine + eltrombopag (triple therapy) for those without a matched sibling donor, or HSCT for younger patients with a matched sibling.
Trap 02Rabbit ATG vs Horse ATG
A patient with aplastic anemia is started on rabbit ATG instead of horse ATG. What is the expected difference in outcome?
Rabbit ATG is much less effective than horse ATG in aplastic anemia — likely because it reduces T-regulatory cell numbers. Always use horse ATG. This is a classic boards distinction.
Trap 03Parvovirus Serology in Immunodeficient Patient
An immunocompromised patient presents with severe anemia, reticulocytopenia, and giant pronormoblasts on bone marrow biopsy. Parvovirus B19 IgG and IgM serology are both negative. The correct next step is:
Send PCR for parvovirus B19 DNA in blood. Immunodeficient patients cannot produce neutralizing antibodies, so serology is commonly NEGATIVE despite active infection. The presence of giant pronormoblasts on marrow is pathognomonic. Treatment is IVIG — almost all immunodeficient patients with B19 PRCA respond.
Trap 04Splenomegaly in Aplastic Anemia
A 25-year-old with pancytopenia and bone marrow hypocellularity is found to have moderate splenomegaly on exam. Does this support or argue against aplastic anemia?
Lymphadenopathy and splenomegaly are HIGHLY ATYPICAL of aplastic anemia. Their presence should prompt reconsideration of the diagnosis — think MDS, lymphoma, hairy cell leukemia, infiltrative disease, or another cause of pancytopenia. In classic aplastic anemia, the spleen is normal sized.
Trap 05Dry Tap on Bone Marrow Aspiration
You attempt a bone marrow aspiration in a patient with pancytopenia and get a "dry tap." What does this suggest?
A "dry tap" suggests fibrosis or myelophthisis — NOT aplastic anemia. In aplastic anemia, the marrow is readily aspirated but dilute and fatty. A dry tap should prompt consideration of myelofibrosis, myelophthisis (secondary to metastatic cancer, TB, or fungal infection), or hairy cell leukemia.
Trap 06G-CSF and EPO in Aplastic Anemia
A patient with aplastic anemia has a Hb of 6.5 g/dL. Should you start erythropoietin and G-CSF?
Hematopoietic growth factors (EPO and G-CSF) are NOT effective in aplastic anemia. They are ineffective because there are no hematopoietic progenitor cells to stimulate. In contrast, EPO improves survival in MDS by ameliorating anemia.
Trap 07AML vs MDS Threshold
A patient has bone marrow with 18% blasts, dysplastic morphology, and cytogenetic abnormalities. How is this classified?
MDS with increased blasts-2 (MDS-IB2). WHO uses ≥20% blasts as the threshold separating AML from MDS. 18% = still MDS. The ICC introduced an MDS/AML category, but the WHO cutoff of 20% remains the classic boards answer. Note: Auer rods at any blast percentage → MDS-IB2 per WHO.
Trap 08Lenalidomide — Which MDS Subtype
An elderly woman has MDS with anemia, low blasts, normal karyotype except for isolated 5q deletion, and becomes transfusion dependent. The most appropriate disease-modifying therapy is:
Lenalidomide — particularly effective in MDS with 5q– syndrome. Not only do patients become transfusion-independent with normal hemoglobin, but their cytogenetics also normalize. This is a highly specific drug-disease pairing on boards.
Trap 09Infection as First Symptom — AA vs Agranulocytosis
A 30-year-old presents with pharyngitis, fever, and anorectal abscess. Neutrophils are critically low. Blood counts otherwise normal except granulocytes. Most likely diagnosis?
Agranulocytosis, NOT aplastic anemia. Infection (pharyngitis, anorectal abscess, frank sepsis) is an early and common presenting symptom of agranulocytosis. In aplastic anemia, infection is an UNUSUAL first symptom — patients more typically present with bleeding. Agranulocytosis is also more prevalent in older adults and women, often drug-related.
Trap 10Telomere Disorder in Normal-Appearing Adult
A 45-year-old with no physical anomalies presents with moderate aplastic anemia. She has early gray hair and her brother has pulmonary fibrosis. No drugs, no chemical exposure. Most important diagnostic consideration?
Telomere biology disorder — measure telomere length and test for TERT/TERC mutations. Unlike classic dyskeratosis congenita (which presents in childhood with the clinical triad), adult-onset telomere disorders can appear in completely normal-looking adults. Clues: early graying, family history of pulmonary fibrosis or hepatic cirrhosis, variable penetrance. This affects treatment choice — androgens (upregulate telomerase) may be beneficial.
Trap 11Clonal Evolution After IST
A patient treated for aplastic anemia with horse ATG/cyclosporine/eltrombopag had an excellent initial response 3 years ago. Now presents with recurrent pancytopenia and new dysplastic changes on marrow. What happened?
Clonal evolution — MDS or rarely leukemia developing after IST for aplastic anemia. This occurs in approximately 10–15% of treated patients over a decade following ATG. May not invariably associate with pancytopenia return. Bone marrow examination should always be performed if unfavorable change in blood counts occurs in a patient with prior AA.
Trap 12Family Transfusions Before HSCT
A 19-year-old with severe aplastic anemia has a matched sibling donor identified. While awaiting transplant, her family wants to donate blood for her transfusions to "help the family." This is:
Contraindicated — transfusion from family members in a transplant candidate must be AVOIDED. Family member transfusions cause sensitization to histocompatibility antigens, which increases the risk of graft rejection. Only use non-family blood products, ideally leukodepleted, to minimize allosensitization before HSCT.
