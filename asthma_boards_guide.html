Overview — Definition, Manifestations & Epidemiology

Definition

Core Definition
Asthma is a disease characterized by episodic airway obstruction and airway hyperresponsiveness, usually accompanied by airway inflammation. In most cases, the obstruction is reversible; however, in a subset of asthmatics, a component may become irreversible.
  • In a large proportion of patients, the airway inflammation is eosinophilic
  • Some patients present with differing types of airway inflammation (neutrophilic, mixed)
  • In some cases, no obvious evidence of airway inflammation exists (pauci-granulocytic)

Manifestations

  • Episodic shortness of breath, wheezing, and cough — can occur in relation to triggers or spontaneously
  • Symptoms can occur in combination or separately
  • Additional symptoms: chest tightness and/or mucus production
  • Symptoms can resolve spontaneously or with therapy
  • In some patients, wheezing and/or dyspnea can be persistent
  • Exacerbations (episodes of acute bronchospasm) may be severe enough to require emergency care, hospitalization, and may result in death

Epidemiology

Global Burden
  • 262 million people affected globally
  • Most common chronic disease associated with significant morbidity and mortality
  • Prevalence: 7.9% in the US vs ~4.3% worldwide
  • Prevalence increasing (7.3% in 2001 → 7.9% now); associated with rural-to-urban transition
US Statistics
  • 2016: 1.8 million ER visits for asthma; 189,000 hospitalizations
  • Total economic cost in 2013: $82 billion
  • More prevalent in Blacks than Caucasians; Black race associated with greater morbidity
  • Highest US prevalence: Puerto Rican population
Age & Sex Distribution
  • More prevalent in children (8.4%) than adults (7.7%)
  • Children: boys > girls (2:1 male-to-female ratio)
  • By age 20: sex difference recedes; by age 40: women > men
  • Some resolve entering adulthood, only to "recur" later in life
Mortality Trends
  • Mortality increased worldwide in 1960s (related to overuse of inhaled β₂-agonists)
  • Reduction attributed to increased use of inhaled corticosteroids
  • Declined from 0.44 per 100,000 (1993) to 0.19 (2006)
  • No further reduction in mortality since 2006

Pathophysiology

Development Pathway (Fig. 298-1)
The development of asthma involves an interplay between genetic susceptibility (risk genes and atopy) and environmental exposures and endogenous developmental factors (aging, menopause). Continued exposures and triggers affect the progression of disease and degree of impairment.

Airway Hyperresponsiveness

Definition — Hallmark of Asthma
An acute narrowing response of the airways in reaction to agents that do not elicit airway responses in nonaffected individuals, or an excess narrowing response to inhaled agents compared to nonaffected individuals.

Three mechanisms produce AHR:

  • Direct smooth muscle hyperresponsiveness: to direct SM-acting agents (histamine, methacholine)
  • Indirect activation: stimulation of inflammatory cells (release bronchoconstrictors, mediators causing edema/mucus) and/or sensory nerves that act on smooth muscle
  • Structural etiology: airway wall thickness (from SM hypertrophy/hyperplasia, subepithelial collagen deposition, airway edema, mucosal inflammation) → airway narrows disproportionately to stimuli
Major Therapeutic Objective
A major goal of asthma treatment is to decrease the degree of airway hyperresponsiveness.

Pathologic Changes in Asthmatic Airways

These processes can occur individually or simultaneously, involve the entire airway (but not the parenchyma), and show significant spatial heterogeneity.

Airway Smooth Muscle — Three Contributions

  • Hyperresponsiveness to stimuli (direct contribution to AHR)
  • Hypertrophy and hyperplasia → airway wall thickening → structural AHR
  • Production of chemokines and cytokines → promotes airway inflammation + promotes survival of inflammatory cells, particularly mast cells

Airway Inflammation — Type 2 & Non-Type 2

Modern Nosology Shift
The old paradigm of atopic vs. nonatopic (intrinsic) asthma is being superseded by consideration of whether asthma is associated with Type 2 or Non-Type 2 inflammation. This framework is driven by understanding immune processes and by the development of therapeutics targeting Type 2 inflammation.

Type 2 Inflammation

Definition
An immune response involving the innate and adaptive arms of the immune system to promote barrier immunity on mucosal surfaces. Called "Type 2" because it is associated with the type 2 subset of CD4+ T-helper (TH2) cells.
IL-4
  • Induces B-cell isotype switching to IgE production
  • IgE binds basophils and mast cells → environmental sensitivity to allergens via IgE cross-linking
  • Products released: Type 2 cytokines + direct activators of smooth-muscle constriction and edema
IL-5
  • Critical role in regulating eosinophils
  • Controls formation, recruitment, and survival of eosinophils
  • Primary therapeutic target (mepolizumab, reslizumab, benralizumab)
IL-13
  • Induces airway hyperresponsiveness
  • Induces mucus hypersecretion
  • Induces goblet cell metaplasia
  • Stimulates nitric oxide production

Non-Allergic Pathway to Type 2 Inflammation — ILC2s

Key Concept — Non-Allergic Type 2
Non-allergic stimuli (irritants, pollutants, oxidative agents, fungi, viruses) can trigger production of Type 2 cytokines via Type 2 innate lymphoid cells (ILC2). Epithelial cells release alarmins (TSLP, IL-25, IL-33) → activate ILC2 → produce IL-5 and IL-13 → eosinophilia even without allergen sensitization.

Role of Eosinophils

Eosinophils in Asthma Pathobiology
Anti-IL-5 drugs that dramatically reduce eosinophils have confirmed that eosinophils play a major role in asthma pathobiology in many asthmatics.
  • Induce AHR via oxidative radicals and major basic protein → disrupt epithelium
  • Produce cysteinyl-leukotrienes → directly stimulate smooth muscle contraction → airway constriction
  • Mucus plugs with significant eosinophil aggregates may accumulate in airways → contribute to asthma severity (CT evidence)

Non-Type 2 Inflammation

  • Can exist in combination with Type 2 or without Type 2 inflammation
  • Neutrophilic inflammation: more common in severe asthma not responding to corticosteroids; associated with chronic infection (atypical pathogens like Mycoplasma → explains some response to macrolide antibiotics); also seen in RADS
  • Pauci-granulocytic asthma: structural changes (Fig. 298-2) without any evidence of tissue infiltration by inflammatory cells; etiology unclear
  • Mixed eosinophilic + neutrophilic: presents with the most severe phenotype
Clinical Implication
Patients with both eosinophilic and neutrophilic inflammation may present with the most severe phenotype. Mast cells are also more frequent in this group.

Fatty Acid Mediators

Cysteinyl Leukotrienes (LTC₄, LTD₄, LTE₄)

  • Produced by eosinophils and mast cells from arachidonic acid
  • Potent smooth-muscle constrictors
  • Stimulate mucus secretion
  • Recruit allergic inflammatory cells
  • Cause microvascular leakage
  • Modulate cytokine production; influence neural transmission
  • Leukotriene modifiers have shown clinical benefit in asthma

LTB₄ (Non-cysteinyl Leukotriene)

  • Produced primarily from neutrophils; also macrophages and epithelial cells
  • Potent neutrophil chemoattractant

Prostaglandins

  • Prostaglandins are for the most part proinflammatory
  • PGD₂: produced by mast cells; CRTH2 receptors present on TH2, ILC2, mast cells, eosinophils, macrophages, and epithelial cells → upregulates Type 2 inflammation
  • Studies with drugs blocking CRTH2 have been relatively disappointing thus far

Pro-Resolving Mediators

Resolution of Inflammation
Resolvins and lipoxins are responsible for the resolution of inflammation. Several studies suggest that deficiencies in these moieties may be responsible for ongoing inflammation in asthma, especially in severe asthma. Pro-resolving pathway therapies may be promising.

Other Mediators

Nitric Oxide (NO)
  • Potent vasodilator
  • In vitro: may increase mucus production and SM proliferation
  • Produced by epithelial cells (especially in response to IL-13), eosinophils, mast cells, neutrophils
  • Detectable in exhaled breath (FeNO)
  • Reduced by interventions interfering with IL-13 production or activity
  • Precise role in asthma unclear
Reactive Oxygen Species
  • Released when allergens, pollutants, bacteria, viruses activate inflammatory cells → respiratory bursts
  • Result in oxidative stress in surrounding tissue
  • Effects: SM contraction, ↑ mucus secretion, AHR, epithelial shedding
Chemokines
  • Secreted by epithelium and other inflammatory cells
  • Eotaxin: eosinophil chemoattractant
  • TARC and MDC: attract TH2 cells
  • RANTES: pluripotent pro-phlogistic effects

Risk Factors, Etiologic Mechanisms & Triggers

Heritable Predisposition

Heritability
Family and twin studies suggest a 25–80% degree of heritability. Complex polygenic inheritance complicated by interaction with environmental exposures and epigenetic modifications. Only 7.2% of asthma risk can be explained by the SNPs currently associated with asthma.

Key Asthma Genes

  • ORMDL3/GSDMB (17q21 chromosomal region) — most consistently identified; regulation associated with altered TH2 cytokine levels
  • ADAM33, DPP-10, TSLP, IL-12, IL-33, ST2 (IL-33 receptor), HLA-DQB1, HLA-DQB2, TLR1, IL-13, IL6R
  • PYHIN1 (interferon inducible protein X): only found in people of African descent — example of ancestry heterogeneity
  • Most currently identified traits act as "enhancers" of biologic processes (polymorphisms in noncoding regions)

Pharmacogenomics

  • β-receptor polymorphism (Arg16Gly in ADRB2) → altered response to β₂-agonists
  • Glucocorticoid-induced transcript 1 gene → altered corticosteroid response
  • Leukotriene synthesis and receptor pathway variants → altered leukotriene modifier response

Atopy

  • Atopy = genetic tendency toward specific IgE production in response to allergen exposure
  • Serum IgE levels correlate closely with development of asthma
  • NHANES III: half of asthma in patients aged 6–59 attributed to atopy with allergic sensitization
  • Allergens most associated with risk: house dust mites, indoor fungi, cockroaches, indoor animals

Special Etiologic Entities

RADS — Reactive Airway Dysfunction Syndrome

RADS Definition
A solitary exposure to a high concentration of irritant agents that rapidly (usually within hours) produces bronchospasm and bronchial hyperactivity.
  • Causative agents: oxidizing and reducing agents in aerosol or high levels of particulates
  • Acute pathology: epithelial injury with neutrophilia; little evidence of Type 2 inflammation
  • Key distinction from occupational asthma: NOT sensitized to the provocative agent → can return to work in that environment once recovered
  • Course variable: some series show documented abnormalities and persistent symptoms 10 years after exposure

ABPA — Allergic Bronchopulmonary Aspergillosis

ABPA Criteria
1–2% of asthmatics have IgE-mediated sensitization to airway colonization by fungi. Most common fungus: Aspergillus fumigatus. ABPA criteria:
  • Type 2 airway inflammatory response to Aspergillus
  • IgE >1000 IU/mL
  • Eosinophils >500/μL
  • Positive skin test to Aspergillus
  • Specific IgE and IgG antibodies to Aspergillus
  • Intermittent mucus plugging and central bronchiectasis
  • Up to two-thirds of patients will grow Aspergillus from sputum
  • Treatment: systemic antifungal (itraconazole or voriconazole) + oral corticosteroids; role for biologics also suggested

Exercise-Induced Symptoms in Elite Athletes

  • Exercise-induced airway narrowing during extreme exercise in strenuous conditions
  • These athletes may have little or no AHR or asthma risk factors
  • May involve direct epithelial injury
  • Also reported in swimmers — possibly related to pool chlorination

Triggers of Airway Narrowing

Table 298-2 — Major Triggers
Allergens · Irritants · Viral infections · Exercise and cold/dry air · Air pollution · Drugs · Occupational exposures · Hormonal changes · Pregnancy

Allergens

  • Allergen-specific IgE → mast cell/basophil activation → bronchoactive mediators
  • Immediate bronchospasm (early response) + late response (2–24h) with bronchial narrowing and inflammation
  • Accounts for: pollens, mold/dust, cockroaches, animals, occupational materials, seasonal worsening, "thunderstorm asthma"
  • Food allergies → bronchospasm via anaphylaxis; generally NOT etiologically linked to asthma

Viral Infections

  • Most asthmatics report URTI-triggered exacerbations
  • Inflammation: neutrophilic and eosinophilic
  • IgE generation may reduce interferon production → predisposition to viral effects
  • Increased AHR after viral infections persists 4–6 weeks; sometimes associated with permanent changes
  • ~50% reduction in exacerbations during COVID-19 pandemic attributed to decreased viral infections

Exercise and Cold/Dry Air

  • Hyperventilation dries airway lining → changes tonicity → bronchoconstrictive mediators released
  • More prominent with lower moisture and lower temperature of inspired air
  • Cold air → airway edema during airway wall rewarming
  • At routine exercise levels: effects short-lived

Drugs

  • Beta blockers: may trigger bronchospasm even in ophthalmic preparations; more selective agents safer for most asthmatics; may be cause of difficult-to-control asthma
  • Aspirin/NSAIDs: precipitate bronchospasm in aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease
  • ACE inhibitors (and to lesser extent ARBs): may cause cough attributed to poorly controlled asthma

Irritants

  • Strong odors, smoke, combustion products, cleaning fluids, perfumes
  • Effects generally short-lived; chronic/large-quantity exposure → long-lasting or permanent symptoms

Hormonal Factors

  • Small proportion of women: regular increase in perimenstrual symptoms
  • Symptoms may worsen during perimenopause — related to rapid estrogen fluctuations
  • Pregnancy precipitates worsening in approximately one-third of pregnant patients

Air Pollution & Stress

  • ↑ exacerbations with ↑ ambient ozone, SO₂, and NO₂
  • Stress: asthmatics report ↑ symptoms; mechanisms poorly understood
Important
Severe asthma exacerbations (those requiring systemic corticosteroids) may, in and of themselves, accelerate lung function decline. Chronic trigger exposure may lead to permanent deterioration in asthma control (except exercise or stress).

Comorbidities & Differential Diagnosis

Differential Diagnosis — Conditions Mimicking Asthma (Table 298-3)

  • Heart failure
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD)
  • α₁-Antitrypsin deficiency
  • Airway obstruction from mass or foreign body
  • Inducible laryngeal dysfunction (vocal cord dysfunction)
  • Bronchiolitis obliterans
  • Bronchiectasis
  • Tracheobronchomalacia

Key Comorbidities

Obesity
  • Obese adults with asthma have more severe symptoms than lean adults
  • 2–4x more likely to be hospitalized with asthma exacerbation
  • Adipokines and IL-6: pathobiologic role
  • Nonrandomized studies: improvement and significant reduction in exacerbations after bariatric surgery
GERD
  • Predicts poor quality of life; independent predictor of asthma exacerbations
  • Treatment of symptomatic GERD → modest improvements in airway function, symptoms, and exacerbation frequency
  • Treatment of asymptomatic patients → no benefit
Rhinosinusitis / Nasal Polyposis
  • Rhinosinusitis may be a manifestation of eosinophilic inflammation in the lower airway
  • Poorly controlled → aggravates asthma (inflammatory/irritant secretions, neural reflexes, inflammatory mediators)
  • Intranasal corticosteroids → decreased AHR and ER visits/hospitalizations
  • Surgical therapy evidence: inconclusive
  • Biologics targeting Type 2 inflammation particularly useful for asthma with rhinosinusitis/polyposis
  • Nasal polyposis rare in children; in adults → raises suspicion of aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease
Inducible Laryngeal Obstruction
  • Previously known as vocal cord dysfunction
  • Inappropriate narrowing of the larynx → resistance to airflow
  • Can complicate asthma and mimic it
  • More common in women and patients with anxiety and depression
  • Definitive diagnosis: laryngoscopy during symptomatic episodes or during induced obstruction
Anxiety/Depression
  • Increased rates of asthma exacerbations in asthmatics with anxiety, depression, or chronic stress
  • Some patients may be unable to distinguish anxiety attacks from asthma
Obstructive Sleep Apnea
  • Listed as a comorbidity that can make asthma difficult to control
  • Must be assessed in poorly controlled asthma

Diagnosis & Evaluation

Critical Fact
Studies have shown that more than one-third of patients with a physician diagnosis of asthma do not meet the criteria for the diagnosis. Always confirm with PFTs or AHR demonstration.

Approach

  • Presumptive diagnosis: compatible history of recurrent wheezing, SOB, chest tightness, or cough with common bronchoconstrictor precipitants after eliminating the differential
  • In some cases, a therapeutic trial of low-dose ICS may be considered
  • In all but the mildest cases, confirm with PFTs or demonstration of AHR
  • Caution: diagnosis may be difficult to confirm after initiating therapy (obstruction/AHR may be mitigated)
  • Cases requiring more than daily moderate-dose ICS/LABA → formal evaluation for comorbidities and reassessment of confounding diagnoses

History

  • Episodic wheeze, SOB, chest tightness, mucus production, or cough on trigger exposure (Table 298-2)
  • Symptoms may be worse on arising in the morning
  • Nocturnal symptoms alone: evaluate for post-nasal drip or GERD if sole presenting symptom
  • Rapid changes of temperature or humidity frequently reported
  • Exercise-induced symptoms: develop more slowly after initiation of exercise and resolve more slowly unless β₂-agonist administered (vs cardiac dyspnea)
  • Careful exposure history: home (pets, molds, dust, smoke), work (occupational sensitizers), recreational (hobbies, recreational inhalants)
  • Up to two-thirds of patients with asthma will be atopic; almost half have history of rhinitis
  • Adult-onset asthma: obtain careful occupational history; reactions to NSAIDs; use of beta blockers (including ophthalmic) or ACE inhibitors

Physical Examination

  • Between attacks: physical findings may be normal
  • Many patients: evidence of allergic rhinitis with pale nasal mucus membranes
  • ≥5% of patients may have nasal polyps (increased frequency in more severe asthma and aspirin-exacerbated disease)
  • Some patients: wheezing on expiration (less so on inspiration)
  • During acute attack: tachypnea, tachycardia, use of accessory muscles, prolonged expiratory phase, wheezing
  • As severity progresses, chest may become "silent" with loss of breath sounds — ominous sign

Pulmonary Function Tests

PFT Pattern in Asthma
Reduced PEFR, FEV₁, and FEV₁/FVC ratio (below LLN). Flow-volume loop shows characteristic scalloping. These findings may NOT be present during acute attacks or on therapy.

Reversibility Criteria

  • New definition: ≥10% absolute increase in FEV₁ percent of predicted (e.g., 60% → 70%) at least 15 min after β₂-agonist or after several weeks of corticosteroid therapy
  • Old definition (still widely used): 12% increase in baseline FEV₁ + minimum 200-mL change
  • Diurnal peak flow variability >20%: proposed as indicator of reversible airways disease; less reliable (quality control difficulties)

Lung Volumes & DLCO

  • Should be normal in uncomplicated asthma
  • In more severe asthma with severe airway obstruction: lung volumes may indicate air trapping
  • Total lung capacity may be under- or overestimated depending on whether body box or gas dilution methods are used (respectively)
Oscillometry
Oscillometry does not require forced expiratory maneuvers and is gaining increased use. Can be particularly useful for diagnosing large airway and variable tracheal obstruction.

Used when PFTs are nonconfirmatory and diagnosis remains in doubt.

Adjunctive Assessment Tools

Eosinophil Counts
  • Large proportion of untreated patients (not on oral or high-dose ICS) will have ≥300 cells/μL
  • Correlate with severity of disease in population studies
  • In severe asthma: indicates likelihood of response to Type 2 biologics
  • Extremely elevated levels: prompt consideration of EGPA or primary eosinophilic disorders
IgE / Skin Tests / RAST
  • Total IgE: useful for eligibility for anti-IgE therapy
  • IgE >1000 IU/mL: prompt consideration of ABPA
  • Skin tests or RAST: confirm atopy and allergic rhinitis; help identify environmental exposures aggravating asthma
Exhaled Nitric Oxide (FeNO)
  • Indicator of Type 2 (particularly IL-13-induced) inflammation
  • Easily suppressed by ICS → can assess adherence (if initially elevated)
  • >35–40 ppb in untreated patients: indicative of Type 2 inflammation
  • >20–25 ppb on moderate-to-high ICS: poor adherence OR persistent Type 2 inflammation despite therapy

Additional Evaluation in Severe / Poorly Responsive Asthma

  • Sinus radiographic studies (even in those without symptoms of sinus disease)
  • Esophageal studies in those with symptoms of reflux
  • Serum α₁-antitrypsin level in patients with nonreversible disease
  • Chest CT: assess for bronchiectasis and structural abnormalities; new image analysis tools assess airway wall thickness, diameter, mucus plugging, and air trapping
  • Induced sputum: characterize Type 2 (eosinophils) and non-Type 2 (neutrophils) inflammation; some patients have localized persistent eosinophilic inflammation despite lack of peripheral eosinophilia on blood analysis

Treatment

Goals of Asthma Therapy (Table 298-4)

  • Reduction in symptom frequency to ≤2 times/week
  • Reduction of nighttime awakenings to ≤2 times/month
  • Reduction of reliever use to ≤2 times/week (except before exercise)
  • No more than 1 exacerbation/year
  • Optimization of lung function
  • Maintenance of normal daily activities
  • Satisfaction with asthma care with minimal or no side effects of treatment

Reducing Triggers

Mitigation

  • Occupational exposures: removal from offending environment may result in complete resolution or significant improvement
  • Secondhand smoke exposure and cannabis combustion products are remediable
  • Removal of pets clearly associated with symptoms can reduce symptoms
  • Pest control (cockroach-sensitive patients with IgE RAST evidence)
  • Dust/mold control: moderate evidence that dust control (impermeable mattress and pillowcase covers) is effective only when part of a comprehensive allergen mitigation strategy

Allergen Immunotherapy

  • Clearly reduces symptoms of allergic rhinitis (helpful as comorbidity treatment)
  • Evidence for isolated asthma: variable
  • Guidelines: only in patients whose asthma is under control with mild to moderate asthma (anaphylaxis risk)
  • Evidence for sublingual immunotherapy in asthma: not substantial

Vaccination

  • Adult asthmatics: pneumococcal vaccines (regardless of age) and yearly influenza vaccines strongly advised
  • COVID-19 vaccination and RSV vaccines also advised

Bronchodilators

β₂-Agonists — Mechanism

Activate β₂-receptors (G protein-coupled) on airway smooth muscle → activate adenyl cyclase → ↑ cyclic AMP → smooth muscle relaxation. β₂-receptors also present on mast cells (contribute little to efficacy).

β₂-Agonist Safety — Key Adverse Effects
  • Tremor, tachycardia, palpitations, hypertension (β₂ specificity imperfect at higher doses)
  • Promote potassium reentry into cells → hypokalemia at high doses
  • Type B lactic acidosis (nonhypoxic): via ↑ glycogenolysis/glycolysis, ↑ lipolysis → ↑ fatty acids → inhibit conversion of pyruvate to acetyl-CoA
  • Increased asthma mortality with high-potency β₂-agonists (Australia/NZ)
  • Increased β₂-agonist use = clear marker of poor asthma control

Anticholinergics

  • Mechanism: antagonize cholinergic nerve-induced smooth-muscle constriction; pharmacologically designed to minimize systemic absorption
  • Long-acting agents = LAMAs (Long-Acting Muscarinic Antagonists)
  • Short-acting: can be used alone for acute bronchodilation; somewhat less effective than β₂-agonists; slower onset
  • Safety: dry mouth; at higher doses in elderly → acute glaucoma, urinary retention; numerical (not significant) mortality difference in African Americans with ICS/LAMA vs ICS/LABA

Theophylline

Now Rarely Used
Increases cyclic AMP levels by inhibiting phosphodiesterase. Now rarely used for asthma due to narrow therapeutic window, drug-drug interactions, and reduced bronchodilation compared to other agents.

Controller (Anti-Inflammatory/Antimediator) Therapies

Important Limitation
None of these therapies have yet been shown to prevent progression of airway remodeling or the more rapid decline in lung function that can occur in a subset of asthma patients.

Corticosteroids

Mechanism: Bind cytoplasmic glucocorticoid receptor → complex translocates to nucleus → binds positive and negative response elements → inhibition of T-cell activation; eosinophil function/migration/proliferation; proinflammatory cytokine elaboration; NF-κB activation. Also attaches to other transcription factors → deactivates other proinflammatory pathways.

Leukotriene Modifiers

  • Inhibit production of leukotrienes (5-LOX inhibition) or action (CysLT1 receptor blockade)
  • Moderately effective in asthma; also effective for allergic rhinitis
  • Effective for exercise-induced bronchoconstriction without tachyphylaxis (unlike regular LABA use)
  • Particularly effective in aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease (characterized by significant leukotriene overproduction)
  • Modest add-on effect in patients poorly controlled on high-dose ICS/LABA

General Principle
Biologics are incredibly effective in their specific endotypes (Type 2 with exacerbations and specific biomarkers), but their high cost currently relegates them to Step 5 therapy or beyond.

  • Omalizumab best responders: FeNO ≥20 ppb OR eosinophils ≥260/μL

Major Paradigm Change — AIR (Anti-Inflammatory Reliever)
Evidence has accumulated that as-needed ICS can replace regular ICS in milder asthma, triggered by patient perception of needing a reliever. GINA now recommends ICS/formoterol (AIR) as the reliever at ALL steps including Step 1. NAEPP recommends it for Steps 3 and 4. Only formoterol can be used this way (quick onset); other LABAs are slower.

Bronchial Thermoplasty & Alternative Therapies

  • Bronchial Thermoplasty: radiofrequency ablation of airway smooth muscle in major airways; 3 bronchoscopies; some evidence of reduced exacerbations in very select patients; significant morbidity; most guidelines: only in clinical trials/registries; manufacturer discontinued production
  • Macrolides (e.g., azithromycin): effective in a subset with poor response to other therapies; suggested as adjunctive therapy at Step 5
  • Acupuncture and yoga: not shown to improve asthma in controlled trials; studies demonstrate significant placebo response
  • Therapies in development: IL-33, OX-40, mucin production mediators; mast cell targeting via tyrosine kinase inhibition (proof-of-concept); pro-resolving pathway therapies

Special Situations

Asthma Attacks — Management Algorithm

Initial Treatment (Mild-Moderate)
  • β₂-agonist administered up to every 1 hour
  • Increasing ICS dose by 4–5 fold may help
  • If requires β₂-agonists hourly for several hours → refer for urgent care
Urgent Care Setting
  • Assess PEFR or FEV₁
  • Nebulized β₂-agonists up to every 20 minutes
  • PEFR >60% predicted: frequently respond to β₂-agonists alone
  • Fail to respond in 1–2 hours: intravenous corticosteroids
  • Supplemental oxygen for hypoxemia
  • Nebulized anticholinergics for additional bronchodilation
  • LTRA and magnesium sometimes given
  • Fail PEFR >60% or persistent severe tachypnea over 4–6 hours → consider hospital admission
Ventilatory Management — Critical Point
Most patients with asthma attacks present with hypocapnia (high respiratory rate). A normal or near-normal PCO₂ in a patient with asthma in respiratory distress should raise concerns of impending respiratory failure and need for mechanical ventilation.

Mechanical Ventilation in Status Asthmaticus

  • High positive pressures from high resistance; aim for low respiratory rates and/or low ventilation volumes to decrease peak airway pressures
  • Permissive hypercapnia: allow PCO₂ to rise; temporarily correct critical acidosis with bicarbonate to increase pH
  • Neuromuscular paralysis sometimes beneficial
  • Noninvasive positive-pressure ventilation to prevent need for intubation
  • Helium-oxygen (heliox) mixtures to decrease work of breathing
  • Antibiotics only if signs of infection
  • Bronchoscopy to clear mucus plugs has been described but may be dangerous with MV difficulties

Exercise-Induced Symptoms

  • May reflect poor asthma control → treat with step therapy first
  • Increase exercise capacity: starting at lower levels (warming up) and using a mask in colder weather to condition air
  • Pretreatment with a SABA increases the threshold of ventilation required to induce bronchoconstriction
  • LABAs may extend period of protection; not recommended for use alone in asthma
  • For occasional exercise: ICS/LABA can be used
  • If regular exercise: LTRAs can be used (prevent exercise-induced without tachyphylaxis)
  • Elite athletes: direct epithelial injury; conditioning incoming air of major assistance; ipratropium reported useful
  • SABA (or ICS/formoterol) should always be available for quick relief

Pregnancy

  • Asthma may improve, deteriorate, or remain unchanged during pregnancy
  • Poor asthma control especially exacerbations → poor fetal outcomes
  • General principles of asthma management and goals are unchanged
  • Avoid triggers especially smoking environments (smoking + child's asthma risk)
  • Safe medications in pregnancy: inhaled albuterol, beclomethasone, budesonide, fluticasone; reassuring: formoterol, salmeterol
  • Animal studies show no toxicity for: montelukast, zafirlukast, omalizumab, ipratropium
  • Antibodies cross the placenta → few human data on asthma biologics
  • Chronic OCS risks: neonatal adrenal insufficiency, preeclampsia, low birth weight, slight ↑ cleft palate — but poorly controlled asthma carries greater risk to fetus and mother
  • No hesitancy in administering routine pharmacotherapy for acute exacerbations
  • Do NOT initiate allergen immunotherapy during pregnancy
  • PGF₂-α should be avoided — associated with bronchoconstriction

Aspirin-Exacerbated Respiratory Disease (AERD)

Prevalence: 5–10% of Asthmatics
  • Adult onset; difficult-to-control asthma + Type 2 inflammation with eosinophilia, sinusitis, nasal polyposis
  • Severe asthma exacerbations precipitated by COX inhibitors (aspirin most prominent)
  • Mechanism: overproduce leukotrienes in response to COX-1 inhibition (secondary to PGE₂ inhibition)
  • Avoid: COX-1 inhibitors (aspirin and NSAIDs)
  • Can tolerate: COX-2 inhibitors and acetaminophen
  • Treat with: leukotriene modifiers
  • Aspirin desensitization: for those requiring chronic aspirin/NSAIDs for another indication; decreases upper respiratory symptoms
  • Dupilumab and IL-5-active biologics particularly helpful; approved to treat nasal polyposis; appearing to supersede aspirin desensitization in management

Severe Asthma

Definition
Asthma that, having undergone appropriate evaluation for comorbidities and mimics, education, and trigger mitigation, remains uncontrolled on Step 5 therapy or requires Step 5 therapy for its control.
  • Composes ~5–10% of all asthma
  • Accounts for almost 50% of the cost of asthma care in the United States
  • Significant proportion have trouble with adherence and/or inhaler technique — must investigate vigorously
  • More than half have evidence of persistent eosinophilic inflammation (peripheral blood eosinophils and/or induced sputum)
  • Recurrent exacerbations → substantially increased likelihood of responding to Type 2 targeted biologics
  • Tezepelumab shown effective even in those without elevated Type 2 biomarkers
  • Treatment for mixed inflammation, isolated neutrophilic, or pauci-granulocytic inflammation: remains to be determined
  • Some data suggest aberrations in pro-resolving pathways; macrolides useful in a subset

Elderly Patients with Asthma

  • Mortality in those >65 years old is 5x greater than younger cohorts (even adjusting for comorbidities)
  • Many had childhood asthma with quiescent periods; almost half of new-onset elderly asthmatics are/were smokers
  • One-quarter of adult-onset asthma attributed to occupational exposure
  • Eosinophilic inflammation → more severe asthma in elderly
  • May need adjustment to step therapy based on intolerance of β₂-agonist therapy due to arrhythmia or tremulousness
  • Coexistence of COPD must be carefully considered

Asthma-COPD Overlap

  • Most clinicians agree it is not a syndrome; useful to identify patients with simultaneous asthma and COPD
  • Smoking can blunt the response to ICS — important consideration
  • Dupilumab has been shown effective in COPD with recurrent exacerbations and elevated eosinophils — approved for this use
  • In patients with both diseases: earlier initiation of anticholinergics may be considered

Exam Traps — Board Exam Coach Edition

Trap 01The SABA Monotherapy Trap
A 28-year-old with mild persistent asthma currently uses only albuterol MDI as needed, approximately 3 times per week. Her symptoms are partially controlled. What is the most important change to make to her regimen?
Add an ICS — do not allow albuterol monotherapy. Frequent SABA use is a marker of poor control AND is associated with increased asthma mortality. GINA now recommends that ICS/formoterol (AIR) or ICS/SABA be used as the reliever at ALL severity levels instead of SABA alone. Using SABA only without inhaled corticosteroids is no longer acceptable as the sole treatment in persistent asthma.
Trap 02The Reversibility Definition Trap
A spirometry test shows FEV₁ of 65% predicted. After bronchodilator administration, FEV₁ rises to 73% predicted. Is this diagnostic of reversible airflow obstruction?
YES — by the new definition. The new GINA definition of reversibility is ≥10% absolute increase in FEV₁ percent of predicted. 65% → 73% = 8 percentage points... actually, this is 8%, NOT 10%, so it would be borderline. The old definition (also still used) requires a 12% increase OVER BASELINE FEV₁ PLUS ≥200 mL change. Know BOTH definitions. The new definition uses percentage POINTS (absolute), not relative change.
Trap 03The Normal CO₂ Trap
A patient with acute severe asthma is in respiratory distress. His arterial blood gas shows PCO₂ of 41 mmHg. The attending physician says this is "reassuring" because the PCO₂ is normal. Is the attending correct?
NO — a normal PCO₂ in an asthmatic in respiratory distress is an OMINOUS sign. Most patients with acute asthma HYPERVENTILATE and present with HYPOCAPNIA. A normal or near-normal PCO₂ in this context means the patient is tiring and can no longer maintain the compensatory hyperventilation — it signals IMPENDING RESPIRATORY FAILURE and should prompt urgent consideration of mechanical ventilation.
Trap 04The Tezepelumab "No Biomarker" Trap
A patient with severe asthma on high-dose ICS/LABA has two exacerbations per year requiring oral steroids. His FeNO is 15 ppb (normal) and blood eosinophils are 180 cells/μL (normal). Which biologic is most appropriate?
Tezepelumab (anti-TSLP). It is the only biologic with demonstrated effectiveness in patients WITHOUT elevated Type 2 biomarkers. Anti-IL-5 drugs require eosinophils ≥300/μL (without chronic OCS). Dupilumab works best with FeNO ≥20-25 ppb or elevated eosinophils. Only tezepelumab, by targeting TSLP (the most proximal alarmin), can help patients with non-Type 2 or low-biomarker asthma.
Trap 05The Dupilumab Eosinophilia Trap
A patient started on dupilumab for severe asthma returns for a 2-week follow-up. Blood eosinophil count is now 850 cells/μL (up from 420 at baseline). She feels the drug is making things worse and wants to stop. What do you advise?
Continue dupilumab — this is expected and paradoxical. Dupilumab paradoxically causes a temporary INITIAL INCREASE in circulating eosinophil counts. This is a known side effect, NOT a sign of treatment failure. The eosinophilia typically resolves over time. Most clinical effects are seen by 3–6 months. True serious systemic eosinophilia occurs rarely, most frequently when reducing oral corticosteroids.
Trap 06The RADS Return-to-Work Trap
A chemical plant worker was exposed to a high concentration of chlorine gas. Within 2 hours, he developed severe bronchospasm. He was diagnosed with RADS. Once he recovers, can he return to the same chemical plant?
YES — unlike occupational asthma, RADS patients CAN return to work. The key distinction is that RADS results from a single high-concentration irritant exposure, and the patient does NOT become sensitized to the provocative agent. In true occupational asthma (immunologic form), sensitization occurs, and re-exposure triggers symptoms. RADS patients who recover can return to the same environment — though some have persistent symptoms for years.
Trap 07The Montelukast Safety Trap
You are about to prescribe montelukast to an 8-year-old with mild persistent asthma. The parents ask about safety. What critical warning must you give?
Montelukast carries an FDA Black Box Warning for neuropsychiatric effects including suicidal ideation. This warning applies to all ages. The parents must be counseled about this risk and should watch for any behavioral changes, mood changes, or thoughts of self-harm. This warning has made montelukast a less appealing choice at Step 2, particularly when alternatives exist. (Note: zileuton also has hepatotoxicity risk and inhibits CYP1A2.)
Trap 08The FeNO on ICS Trap
A patient with severe asthma on high-dose ICS/LABA has a FeNO of 30 ppb (normal <25 ppb on therapy). The physician considers switching to a biologic. What should be done first?
Assess and address ADHERENCE first. FeNO is easily suppressed by ICS. Elevated FeNO (>20–25 ppb) in a patient on moderate-to-high ICS indicates EITHER poor adherence (most common) OR persistent Type 2 inflammation despite therapy. Since poor adherence has been identified as the cause of poor asthma control in up to 50% of referred patients, adherence must be confirmed before escalating therapy to biologics.
Trap 09The ABPA IgE Trap
A patient with asthma has central bronchiectasis on CT and an IgE of 1300 IU/mL. What should be suspected and what are the treatment pillars?
Suspect ABPA (Allergic Bronchopulmonary Aspergillosis). IgE >1000 IU/mL in an asthmatic with central bronchiectasis is classic. Confirm with: eosinophils >500/μL, positive Aspergillus skin test, specific Aspergillus IgE and IgG. Treatment: systemic antifungal (itraconazole or voriconazole) PLUS oral corticosteroids. Don't forget: up to 2/3 of ABPA patients grow Aspergillus from sputum. Biologics also have an emerging role.
Trap 10The Exercise-Induced vs Cardiac Dyspnea Trap
A 45-year-old describes breathing difficulty during exercise. She says symptoms come on "soon after starting exercise" and resolve immediately when she rests. Does this sound like exercise-induced asthma?
No — this pattern is more suggestive of CARDIAC dyspnea. Exercise-induced asthma (bronchoconstriction) typically develops MORE SLOWLY after initiation of exercise (not immediately), and symptoms RESOLVE MORE SLOWLY, unless a β₂-agonist is administered. Cardiac dyspnea develops quickly with exertion and typically improves with rest. This distinction on boards often hides in the description of onset timing.
Trap 11The Pregnancy Prostaglandin Trap
A pregnant asthmatic patient requires a prostaglandin for obstetric management of her third trimester. The obstetrician asks which prostaglandin is safe to use in her asthmatic patient. What is your recommendation?
Avoid PGF₂-α — it is associated with bronchoconstriction. PGF₂-α should NOT be used in asthmatic pregnant patients. Other prostaglandin preparations (e.g., PGE₂ variants used for cervical ripening or postpartum hemorrhage) do not carry the same risk of bronchoconstriction. This is a classic board question on the intersection of pregnancy management and asthma contraindications.
Trap 12The LABA Monotherapy Trap
A patient with COPD is doing well on salmeterol monotherapy. He is then also diagnosed with asthma (asthma-COPD overlap). His COPD pulmonologist wants to keep the same salmeterol-only regimen. What is your recommendation?
Do NOT use LABA monotherapy in asthma. In contrast to COPD (where LABA monotherapy is acceptable), LABAs are NOT recommended as monotherapy in asthma. Their use in asthma is generally restricted to use in combination with an ICS. In asthma-COPD overlap, ICS should be added, and earlier initiation of anticholinergics may also be considered.
