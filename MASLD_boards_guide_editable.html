01

Disease Nomenclature — MASLD / MASH

▼

The 2023 consensus changed disease terminology across the entire spectrum. Understanding old vs. new terms is itself a board-testable concept.

OLD — NAFLD / NASH
  • NAFLD — Nonalcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (umbrella)
  • NASH — Nonalcoholic Steatohepatitis
  • Criticized: stigmatizing ("nonalcoholic," "fatty"), defined by exclusion rather than positive features
NEW — MASLD / MASH
  • SLD — Steatotic Liver Disease (new umbrella term)
  • MASLD — Metabolic Dysfunction–Associated Steatotic Liver Disease
  • MASL — Simple steatosis without inflammation
  • MASH — Metabolic Dysfunction–Associated Steatohepatitis
  • MetALD — Metabolic dysfunction + social alcohol use (women 20–50 g/d; men 30–60 g/d)
  • ALD — Alcohol-Associated Liver Disease: ≥50 g/d women; ≥60 g/d men

MASLD Diagnostic Criteria (all three required)

  1. Hepatic steatosis — demonstrated on imaging or biopsy
  2. Low alcohol intake — <20 g/day women; <30 g/day men
  3. ≥1 cardiometabolic risk factor — overweight/obesity, hypertension, dyslipidemia, insulin resistance/T2DM
🔑 Board Fact
At least 98% overlap between the population historically defined as NAFLD/NASH and the population categorized as MASLD/MASH. Old NASH trial results are directly applicable to MASH — no clinical discontinuity.
02

Incidence, Prevalence & Natural History

▼

MASLD is the most common cause of chronic liver disease in the United States and worldwide, and now a leading indication for liver transplantation.

US Adults

25–30% estimated to have MASLD

MASH subset

3–6% of MASLD; 14% in asymptomatic patients ≥50 undergoing colon cancer screening

Global

30% overall; highest: Latin America 44%, Middle East/North Africa 36.5%

Ethnic Variation

Lowest: African Americans (~25%); Highest: Hispanic & Asian-Indians (~50%); Intermediate: Whites (~30%)

🔑 Fibrosis Progression Rates — Meta-Analysis
Steatosis alone (MASL): 1 fibrosis stage every 14 years
MASH: 1 fibrosis stage every 7 years — twice as fast
Once fibrosis reaches ≥F2 → "at-risk MASH" → intensive monitoring + pharmacotherapy consideration
Fibrosis severity is the primary predictor of liver-related morbidity and mortality in MASLD — not degree of steatosis or inflammation

Cirrhosis Outcomes

  • Decompensation rate in cirrhosis: 3–20% per year
  • Annual HCC incidence once cirrhosis develops: 1–2% per year → biannual HCC screening triggered
  • HCC risk even in noncirrhotic MASLD — a premalignant condition for both primary liver and extrahepatic malignancies
  • Cirrhosis can regress if underlying insult is removed (not always irreversible)
⚠ Pediatric MASLD
MASL, MASH, and MASH-related cirrhosis are documented in children and adolescents. Weight gain and insulin resistance are the main pediatric risk factors. Rising childhood obesity is a major future public health contributor.
03

Molecular & Cellular Pathogenesis

▼

MASLD is fundamentally a disease of energy imbalance: excessive energy intake + reduced expenditure + limited peripheral adipose expansion → visceral adiposity → insulin resistance → metabolic disease.

Step 1 — Steatosis: Sources of Hepatic Fat Accumulation

Source 1 — Dietary Fat

Uptake of dietary fatty acids delivered to liver via portal circulation

Source 2 — Adipose Lipolysis

Insulin resistance → increased lipolysis in adipocytes → excess free fatty acids released into circulation → hepatic TG production

Source 3 — De Novo Lipogenesis (DNL)

Hepatocytes synthesize fatty acids from carbohydrate substrates — especially fructose. DNL driven by excess dietary sugars.

  • Triglyceride (TG) accumulation is relatively safe — TGs are inert storage molecules
  • Steatosis alone does NOT damage the liver — but is a biomarker of metabolic dysfunction
  • TG also accumulates when fatty acid β-oxidation or TG export mechanisms are impaired

Step 2 — Steatohepatitis: Lipotoxicity → MASH

⚠ Key Distinction
Lipotoxicity — lipid-associated injury — distinguishes MASH from simple steatosis. Dysregulated processing of fatty acids → toxic intermediates and by-products accumulate → hepatocyte injury → lipotoxic stress → injured hepatocytes must be replaced → regenerative responses → if defective = fibrous scar (cirrhosis) or liver cancer.

Key Genetic Polymorphisms

PNPLA3 — I148M (Risk)

Impairs lipolysis of TG lipid droplets → increases hepatic fat accumulation, MASH severity, and liver fibrosis. Strongest genetic risk factor. Meta-analysis confirmed influence on fat, MASH, and fibrosis.

TM6SF2 (Risk)

Influences cholesterol metabolism. Variants increase heritability of MASLD and fibrosis risk.

MBOAT7 (Risk)

Influences phospholipid metabolism. Variants increase heritability of MASLD.

✓ HSD17B13 — Protective Gene
Loss-of-function variants in HSD17B13 (encodes an enzyme localizing to lipid droplets) appear protective against MASH, progressive fibrosis, and liver cancer. A therapeutic target under investigation.

Gut–Liver Axis & Epigenetics

  • Obesity-related gut microbiota alterations → enhanced energy harvest + increased intestinal permeability → hepatic exposure to gut-derived products → inflammatory mediators → worsen insulin resistance
  • Obese adipose depots produce adipokines → inhibit tissue insulin sensitivity → hyperglycemia → hyperinsulinemia → promotes lipid uptake, fat synthesis, fat storage
  • First-degree relatives of probands with MASH cirrhosis → 12-fold higher risk of advanced fibrosis
  • Twin studies: inheritance accounts for about half the risk for developing cirrhosis

⚠ T2DM — The Strongest Risk Factor
T2DM is the strongest risk factor for MASLD acquisition, fibrosis progression, and HCC. MASLD prevalence in T2DM: 30–75%. T2DM confers ~twofold increased risk for NASH with fibrosis. The relationship is bidirectional — MASLD increases risk for incident T2DM by 2–5-fold. Patients with MASLD must be screened for T2DM proactively.

Metabolic Syndrome Components

  • Android/central fat distribution (truncal subcutaneous + visceral) — increased MASLD/MASH risk vs. gynoid (hip/buttock) distribution
  • Hypertension — adds to MASH and fibrosis progression risk
  • Dyslipidemia — plasma lipid abnormalities 2× as prevalent in MASLD; serum lipid subfractions more atherogenic → ↑ cardiovascular risk

Endocrine Disorders Associated with MASLD

Hypothyroidism

Promotes obesity and metabolic syndrome; higher MASLD rates

Hypogonadism

Associated with higher MASLD rates

GH Deficiency

Higher MASLD rates

PCOS

Hyperinsulinemia + excess androgen → 2–4× increased prevalence of MASLD, MASH with fibrosis, and T2DM. Postmenopausal > premenopausal (role of ↑ free testosterone / ↓ estradiol)

💜 CVD & OSA Link
Both CVD and OSA are associated with MASLD and specifically with MASH and hepatic fibrosis. Patients with known CVD or OSA should be screened for MASLD. Overweight/obese patients with metabolic syndrome → high risk for OSA and CVD.

⚠ Micro vs. Macro Pattern
Microvesicular steatosis = mitochondrial dysfunction (numerous tiny droplets; nucleus central) — valproate, tetracyclines, NRTIs, Reye's/aspirin. Macrovesicular = large single lipid droplet displaces nucleus — amiodarone, glucocorticoids, tamoxifen, methotrexate.

Diagnosis & Clinical Features

▼

Most MASLD patients are asymptomatic. Diagnosis is often made incidentally.

How Patients Present

  • Incidentally detected steatosis on abdominal imaging (US, CT, MRI)
  • Incidentally elevated ALT on routine labs
  • Vague right upper quadrant pain, hepatomegaly, or abnormal liver at abdominal surgery
  • Obesity present in 50–90% of subjects
  • Subtle stigmata of CLD: spider angiomata, palmar erythema, splenomegaly
  • Advanced disease (minority): jaundice, portal hypertension (ascites, variceal hemorrhage)
⚠ Normal Liver Enzymes Do NOT Exclude MASLD
Normal ALT, ALP, bilirubin, albumin, and PT may be completely normal in MASLD. Lab-reported "normal" ALT ranges are inflated because they include values from people with undiagnosed liver disease.

True normal ALT: 29–33 U/L in men; 19–25 U/L in women

Workup Approach

  1. Suspect MASLD — steatosis + ≥1 metabolic syndrome criterion + little to no alcohol
  2. Screen for metabolic risk factors — BMI, insulin resistance/T2DM, HTN, dyslipidemia, hyperuricemia/gout, CVD in patient or first-degree family members
  3. Exclude other causes — viral hepatitis (HBV/HCV), autoimmune liver disease, iron or copper overload, α1-antitrypsin deficiency
  4. Detailed medication history — exclude drug-associated steatosis/steatohepatitis

Alternative Causes of Hepatic Steatosis (Key Examples)

Viral / Metabolic
  • HCV genotype 3 — causes steatosis directly
  • Wilson's disease, abetalipoproteinemia, glycogen storage disease, hereditary fructose intolerance, galactosemia, carnitine deficiency, tyrosinemia
Surgical / Procedural
  • Jejunoileal bypass, gastric bypass, extensive small-bowel resection, biliopancreatic diversion, parenteral nutrition
Special Conditions
  • Acute fatty liver of pregnancy, HELLP syndrome, Reye's syndrome, lipodystrophy, IBD, bacterial overgrowth, starvation
Lean MASLD
  • BMI <25 kg/m² (<23 kg/m² in Asians); prevalence 4% in US to 19% in Asia; increased visceral adiposity despite lean BMI

Staging & Risk Stratification — NITs

▼

Goal: distinguish steatosis from MASH, and identify which MASH patients have advanced fibrosis. Staging drives management intensity.

The FIB-4 Score — Step 1 (Primary Risk Assessment)

🔑 FIB-4 Formula
FIB-4 = Age × AST / (Platelets × √ALT)
Uses: Age, ALT, AST, Platelet count. Free calculator. Outperforms BARD, APRI, and NAFLD fibrosis score. Best negative predictive value for excluding advanced fibrosis.
FIB-4 ScoreRiskManagement
<1.3Low — advanced fibrosis unlikelyPrimary care follow-up; reassess every 2–3 years (1–2 yr if pre-DM or T2DM or ≥2 metabolic risk factors)
1.3–2.67IntermediateReassess periodically; secondary NIT if clinical concern
≥2.67High — may have advanced fibrosisSecondary NIT (ELF or VCTE) → refer to gastroenterology/hepatology
⚠ FIB-4 Age Limitations
Low accuracy in patients older than 65 years and younger than 35 years → use secondary NIT directly (VCTE or ELF) in these age groups, regardless of FIB-4 result.

Secondary NITs — Step 2

VCTE (Vibration-Controlled Transient Elastography)
LSM (kPa)Interpretation
<8.0Low — rule out advanced fibrosis
8.0–12.0Intermediate — may represent fibrotic MASH
>12.0High — high likelihood of advanced fibrosis
  • 20% increase in LSM → disease progression signal
  • False positives: obesity, nonfasting, severe hepatic inflammation, congestion, iron overload
  • May suggest portal hypertension
ELF Panel (Enhanced Liver Fibrosis)

Proprietary blood test; 3 matrix turnover biomarkers:

  • Hyaluronic acid
  • Tissue inhibitor of metalloproteinase-1 (TIMP-1)
  • N-terminal procollagen III peptide
ELF ScoreInterpretation
<7.7Low risk
7.7–9.8Intermediate risk

Advanced Imaging

MRE (Magnetic Resonance Elastography)

Most accurate NIT for fibrosis assessment. Expensive, not point-of-care. For indeterminate secondary NIT results or tertiary care. 20% increase in LSM → disease progression.

MRI-PDFF

Accurate, reproducible, precise liver fat quantification. Superior to CAP for fat quantification. ≥30% reduction in liver fat by MRI-PDFF → 5× improved likelihood of MASH resolution.

When to Perform Liver Biopsy

  • Indeterminate or discordant NITs
  • Competing or concomitant diagnoses (iron overload, autoimmune hepatitis, DILI)
  • Persistent elevation (>6 months) in liver chemistries without steatosis or metabolic risk factors
  • Diagnostic uncertainty or discordance with clinical suspicion

Treatment Response Biomarkers

  • ALT decrease of ≥17 IU/L → associated with higher odds of histologic response in clinical trials
  • Improvements in FIB-4, ELF, VCTE, MRE → correlated with reduction in liver injury and fibrosis
  • ≥30% reduction in liver fat by MRI-PDFF → 5× improved likelihood of MASH resolution

Lifestyle Modification

▼

Lifestyle modification — healthy diet and exercise — is foundational for all MASLD/MASH patients regardless of weight loss goals or pharmacotherapy.

Weight Loss Thresholds

3–5% Weight Loss

Improves hepatic steatosis alone

>10% Weight Loss

Improves MASH AND fibrosis

Sustained Weight Loss

Improves insulin sensitivity, cytokine response, hepatic stress, and gut microbiota

⚠ Weight Loss Reality
<10% of patients with insulin resistance achieve effective weight loss despite structured interventions at 1 year. Pharmacological and surgical interventions are often necessary.

Dietary Recommendations

  • Mediterranean diet — most recommended; long-term adherence; culturally adaptable; cardiovascular benefits
  • Avoid: excess saturated fats, refined carbohydrates, sugar-sweetened beverages (Western obesogenic diet)
  • Excess fructose increases risk for MASLD, MASH, and hepatic fibrosis — independent of total calorie intake
  • Various dietary approaches (low-fat, low-carb, intermittent fasting, Mediterranean green, calorie restriction) are relatively comparable
  • Coffee ≥3 cups/day — epidemiologic data show reduced risk for hepatic fibrosis and HCC

Exercise Prescriptions

  • Physical activity independent of weight loss complements dietary modification
  • Regular moderate exercise: at least 5× per week, 150 min/week total
  • More intensive exercise: at least 60 min/week
  • Dose-dependent response on MASLD/MASH improvement
  • In advanced fibrosis/cirrhosis: moderate-to-high-intensity exercise may decrease portal hypertension, sarcopenia, and HCC risk

Pharmacologic Therapies

▼

Pharmacotherapy is NOT recommended for hepatic steatosis without steatohepatitis or fibrosis. Liver-directed therapy is reserved for at-risk MASH (MASH + ≥F2 fibrosis).

★ RESMETIROM (Rezdiffra) — FDA Accelerated Approval 2024

Mechanism: Selective thyroid hormone receptor beta (THR-β) agonist — mimics thyroid hormone effects in liver → promotes lipid metabolism, reduces liver fat, improves liver function. Liver-targeted to minimize systemic thyroid effects.

Indication: At-risk MASH (F2–F3 fibrosis) WITHOUT cirrhosis

💜 Clinical Pearl
Only FDA-approved liver-directed therapy for MASH. MAESTRO-NASH study ongoing for long-term safety and full approval. Accelerated approval only — F2–F3, NO cirrhosis.
Pioglitazone — PPARγ Agonist (Thiazolidinedione)

Mechanism: PPARγ ligand → improves insulin resistance and serum lipids

PIVENS Trial (NIH, n=247 NASH without DM, 18 months): NASH resolution 47% vs. 21% placebo (p<0.001). Trend toward fibrosis improvement, confirmed in meta-analysis.

Weight gainBone loss (postmenopausal)Worsens heart failureDebated bladder cancer risk
⚠ Note
Largely supplanted in clinical practice by GLP-1RAs and SGLT-2is (better tolerated, improve glycemia, induce weight loss, reduce cardiovascular mortality).
GLP-1 Receptor Agonists (Incretin Mimetics)

Mechanism: Act on pancreas to optimize insulin/glucagon release; improve lipids, glucose, and weight; decrease cardiovascular risk.

  • Semaglutide (n=320 biopsy-proven NASH F1–F3, 72 weeks): NASH resolution without fibrosis worsening: 59% (0.4 mg group) vs. 17% placebo (p<0.001). No statistically significant fibrosis improvement — only protective against progression. Phase 3 fibrosis trial ongoing.
  • Liraglutide (small study): improved steatosis, resolved NASH, reduced fibrosis progression vs. placebo
  • Tirzepatide (GLP-1/GIP dual agonist): up to 20.9% weight loss vs. 3.1% placebo at 72 weeks; significant reductions in liver fat content
Vitamin E (Antioxidant)

PIVENS (RRR-α-tocopherol 800 IU/day × 96 weeks, NASH without DM): Improved histologic features of steatohepatitis: 43% vs. 19% placebo (p<0.04). No antifibrotic effect in any study.

↑ Perioperative bleeding riskHemorrhagic strokeIncreased cardiac mortalityDebated prostate cancer risk
⚠ Caveats
Safety concerns have dampened enthusiasm. No antifibrotic effect. NOT recommended for patients with T2DM or prediabetes in most guidelines.
Metformin — NOT Recommended for MASH
  • Adult NASH studies: some aminotransferase improvement, inconsistent histology
  • TONIC study (pediatric NASH): metformin did NOT improve liver histology
  • Current recommendation: NOT recommended as treatment for NASH/MASH
  • Exception: meta-analyses suggest metformin may decrease risk of HCC in patients with T2DM
Statins — Safe and Beneficial
  • Safe across the entire spectrum of MASLD, including well-compensated cirrhosis
  • Decrease cardiovascular morbidity and mortality — but underutilized despite extensive safety evidence
  • Decrease portal pressure → decrease risk for hepatic decompensation
  • Meta-analyses: long-term statin use may decrease risk for cirrhosis and HCC
  • Liver enzyme elevations in MASLD patients on statins: no different from healthy controls
  • Limited data in decompensated MASLD-related cirrhosis
⚠ Drugs Shown NOT to Benefit MASH
Do NOT use for MASH treatment — no meaningful histologic benefit:
1. Ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA) — bile acid conjugate
2. Betaine — metabolite of choline
3. DPP-4 inhibitors — insulin-sensitizing agent
4. Silymarin — putative antioxidant

Bariatric Surgery & Liver Transplantation

▼

Bariatric Surgery Indications

  • BMI ≥40 kg/m² (any comorbidities)
  • BMI ≥35 kg/m² with comorbidities (T2DM, HTN, OSA)

Efficacy in MASLD

  • Prospective study: resolved histologic features of MASH and improved hepatic fibrosis in 80% at 1 year
  • Maintained in patients who sustained weight loss at 5 years; failure to sustain → persistent MASH
  • Malabsorptive procedures more effective than restrictive procedures
  • Cures diabetes in majority; decreases liver-related and all-cause morbidity/mortality
⚠ Cirrhosis Risk Stratification
Compensated MASH cirrhosis: cannot be considered primary salvage therapy; may be accomplished at select tertiary care centers for selected patients

Decompensated cirrhosis / portal hypertension: associated with increased mortality — only at high-volume centers with combined liver transplant + bariatric surgery expertise

Liver Transplantation

  • MASLD: leading indication for liver transplantation in the United States
  • Outcomes generally good in well-selected patients
  • Comorbidities often limit candidacy: diabetes, obesity, CVD
  • MASLD may recur after liver transplantation

Risk Factors for Post-Transplant MASLD Recurrence

HypertriglyceridemiaObesityDiabetes mellitusImmunosuppressive therapyGlucocorticoids (especially)

Exam Traps

▼

Expert board exam coaching — Stage A Internal Medicine Boards. Answers are hidden by default — click "Reveal Answer" to test yourself.

Trap 01 Normal LFTs Do Not Exclude MASLD
A 44-year-old woman with BMI 31, T2DM, hypertriglyceridemia, and hypertension has ALT 28 U/L — reported as "within normal limits" by the lab. Abdominal ultrasound shows hepatic steatosis. The resident tells her liver tests are normal and MASLD can be ruled out. This is: Correct; OR incorrect because steatosis on US confirms MASLD regardless of labs; OR incorrect because the "true normal" ALT in women is 19–25 U/L and her ALT of 28 is actually elevated?
Correct Answer: Incorrect — normal liver enzymes do NOT exclude MASLD; AND the true normal ALT in women is 19–25 U/L (ALT of 28 is actually elevated)
MASLD patients may have completely normal ALT, ALP, bilirubin, albumin, and PT. Lab-reported normal ranges are inflated because they include values from people with undiagnosed liver disease. True normal ALT: 29–33 U/L in men and 19–25 U/L in women. This patient also has three metabolic risk factors confirming MASLD.
⚠ Trap: The lab printout says "within normal limits" — students accept this without questioning the reference range. Board keyword: true normal ALT for women (19–25) and men (29–33).
Trap 02 Fibrosis Is the Prognostic Driver — Not Steatosis
Patient A has severe steatosis (grade 3) with F1 fibrosis. Patient B has mild steatosis (grade 1) with F3 fibrosis. Which patient has a higher 10-year risk of liver-related morbidity and mortality, and should receive pharmacotherapy for MASH? Patient A because of more severe steatosis; Patient B because of advanced fibrosis; or both equally?
Correct Answer: Patient B (F3 fibrosis, mild steatosis)
Fibrosis severity is the primary predictor of liver-related morbidity and mortality in MASLD — not the degree of steatosis or inflammation. The 10-year probability of developing liver-related morbidity or mortality is negligible with steatosis alone (F0–F1). Patients with MASH and fibrosis ≥F2 are designated "at-risk MASH" and require intensive monitoring and pharmacotherapy.
⚠ Trap: Students focus on steatosis severity as the marker of disease severity. Board concept: steatosis alone (regardless of grade) has negligible long-term liver risk. Fibrosis stage is the prognosis determinant.
Trap 03 FIB-4 Age Limitations
A 70-year-old man with T2DM and obesity has a FIB-4 score of 1.2 (low risk). His primary care physician concludes he has a low probability of advanced hepatic fibrosis and plans to reassess in 2–3 years. This plan is: appropriate because FIB-4 <1.3 = low risk; or inappropriate because FIB-4 has low accuracy in patients older than 65 and a secondary NIT is required?
Correct Answer: Inappropriate — FIB-4 has low accuracy in patients older than 65 years
Due to low accuracy in patients older than 65 years (and younger than 35), a secondary assessment should be done — specifically elastography (VCTE or MRE) or the Enhanced Liver Fibrosis (ELF) panel. In elderly patients, FIB-4 may underestimate fibrosis risk.
⚠ Trap: Students apply the FIB-4 cutoff (<1.3 = low risk) without recognizing its age limitations. Board keyword: "older than 65 years" and "younger than 35" → always go straight to secondary NIT.
Trap 04 Metformin — NOT Recommended for MASH
A 52-year-old man with T2DM and biopsy-proven MASH (F2 fibrosis) is started on metformin to treat his insulin resistance, with the hope of also treating his MASH. The evidence for this decision is: Strong — metformin is recommended for MASH in T2DM; Weak — metformin is NOT currently recommended as MASH treatment; or No evidence at all?
Correct Answer: Metformin is NOT currently recommended as a treatment for NASH/MASH
While some adult NASH studies suggested aminotransferase improvements, the TONIC study (larger prospectively randomized trial in children with NASH) showed metformin did NOT improve liver histology. Preferred insulin-sensitizing agents for MASH: GLP-1RAs and pioglitazone. Exception: meta-analyses suggest metformin may decrease HCC risk in T2DM.
⚠ Trap: Metformin's mechanism (improves insulin sensitivity) makes it seem like a logical MASH treatment. The board keyword is the TONIC pediatric study showing no histologic improvement.
Trap 05 Resmetirom Indication — F2–F3 Only, No Cirrhosis
A 58-year-old woman with biopsy-proven MASH and compensated cirrhosis (F4) asks about resmetirom, the newly FDA-approved liver-directed therapy. Her hepatologist considers starting it. Is this appropriate? Yes — approved for at-risk MASH regardless of fibrosis stage; No — only approved for F2–F3 without cirrhosis; or Yes — cirrhosis is the strongest indication?
Correct Answer: No — Resmetirom is only FDA-approved for at-risk MASH with F2–F3 fibrosis WITHOUT cirrhosis
Resmetirom received accelerated FDA approval specifically for patients with at-risk MASH defined as F2–F3 fibrosis without cirrhosis. The MAESTRO-NASH trial enrolled patients with biopsy-proven NASH with fibrosis that had NOT yet advanced to cirrhosis.
⚠ Trap: Students assume the most severe patients should get the drug. The board trap is the specific exclusion of cirrhosis. Resmetirom = F2–F3 only, no cirrhosis.
Trap 06 Lean MASLD — Asian BMI Threshold
A 38-year-old Japanese man with BMI 22 kg/m² has elevated ALT, ultrasound shows hepatic steatosis, and has central obesity. A medical student states he cannot have MASLD because his BMI is normal. This is: Correct — MASLD only occurs in overweight/obese individuals; or incorrect because MASLD occurs in lean individuals and Asian BMI threshold is <23 kg/m²?
Correct Answer: Incorrect — MASLD can occur in lean individuals and is particularly common in Asians (BMI threshold <23 kg/m²)
MASLD can occur in BMI <25 kg/m² (or <23 kg/m² in Asian individuals). Lean MASLD is particularly common in Asian ethnicity. Prevalence: ~4% in US to ~19% in Asia. Even lean patients have increased visceral adiposity, insulin resistance, metabolic comorbidities, altered gut microbiota, and/or decreased muscle mass.
⚠ Trap: MASLD seems like a disease of obesity. The board concept is that visceral/truncal adiposity matters more than BMI. In Asians, use BMI <23 kg/m² as the threshold for "lean."
Trap 07 Semaglutide — NASH Resolution WITHOUT Fibrosis Improvement
In the large RCT of semaglutide for NASH (n=320, 72 weeks), which best describes the primary outcome: NASH resolution AND significant fibrosis improvement; NASH resolution without statistically significant fibrosis improvement; or only fibrosis improvement without NASH resolution?
Correct Answer: NASH resolution WITHOUT statistically significant fibrosis improvement
59% of the 0.4-mg semaglutide group achieved NASH resolution without worsening of fibrosis (primary endpoint) vs. 17% in placebo (p<0.001). However, there was NO statistically significant improvement in fibrosis — only a protective effect against fibrosis progression. This is a critical distinction: semaglutide resolves steatohepatitis but has not proven to regress existing fibrosis.
⚠ Trap: Impressive NASH resolution rate (59%) makes students assume fibrosis improved too. Board keyword: "no statistically significant fibrosis improvement." Contrast with resmetirom (MAESTRO-NASH) which achieved BOTH NASH resolution AND fibrosis improvement.
Trap 08 PNPLA3 vs. HSD17B13 — Risk vs. Protection
A patient with MASH and advanced fibrosis is found to carry the I148M polymorphism of PNPLA3. Which statement is most accurate: PNPLA3 I148M causes direct hepatocyte necrosis; PNPLA3 I148M impairs lipolysis of TG lipid droplets, increasing fat accumulation, MASH severity, and fibrosis; or PNPLA3 I148M is protective like HSD17B13?
Correct Answer: PNPLA3 I148M impairs lipolysis of triglyceride lipid droplets, increasing hepatic fat accumulation, MASH severity, and liver fibrosis
PNPLA3 (palatin-like phospholipase domain-containing protein 3) I148M variant impairs lipolysis of TG lipid droplets → fat cannot be efficiently mobilized from hepatocytes → accumulates. It is the strongest genetic risk factor for MASLD. In contrast, HSD17B13 loss-of-function variants are PROTECTIVE against MASH and fibrosis.
⚠ Trap: Students confuse PNPLA3 (RISK — I148M variant) with HSD17B13 (PROTECTIVE — loss-of-function). Board keywords: PNPLA3 I148M = risk; HSD17B13 loss-of-function = protection.
Trap 09 Statins in MASLD — The Underutilization Problem
A 61-year-old man with well-compensated MASLD-related cirrhosis and dyslipidemia has LDL 165 mg/dL and significant cardiovascular risk. His gastroenterologist hesitates to prescribe a statin due to concern about hepatotoxicity in cirrhosis. The evidence-based recommendation is: Avoid statins due to cirrhosis; prescribe statin — statins are safe across the spectrum of MASLD including well-compensated cirrhosis; or prescribe statin only if FIB-4 <2.67?
Correct Answer: Prescribe the statin — statins are safe across the entire spectrum of MASLD, including in patients with well-compensated cirrhosis
Statins are safe across the ENTIRE spectrum of MASLD. They decrease cardiovascular morbidity and mortality, decrease portal pressure, and reduce risk for hepatic decompensation. Meta-analyses suggest long-term statin use decreases risk for cirrhosis and HCC. Despite this evidence, statins remain underutilized due to unfounded hepatotoxicity concerns.
⚠ Trap: Reflexive avoidance of statins in liver disease is common but wrong for well-compensated MASLD/cirrhosis. Board keyword: "safe across the entire spectrum of MASLD including well-compensated cirrhosis." Limited data only for DECOMPENSATED cirrhosis — that is the actual caveat.
Trap 10 T2DM — The Bidirectional Risk Relationship
A 46-year-old woman with MASLD (no T2DM yet) asks about her long-term risks. Her physician replies that T2DM causes MASLD but not the reverse. Is this correct? Yes — only T2DM causes MASLD; No — the relationship is bidirectional; MASLD increases risk of incident T2DM by 2–5-fold?
Correct Answer: No — the relationship is bidirectional. MASLD increases risk of incident T2DM by 2–5-fold
T2DM is the strongest risk factor for MASLD acquisition, fibrosis progression, and HCC. However, the relationship is explicitly bidirectional — MASLD itself is associated with reduced insulin sensitivity, increasing risk for incident T2DM by 2–5-fold. As liver disease progresses, insulin resistance worsens, making control of T2DM more challenging. Patients with MASLD should be screened for T2DM proactively.
⚠ Trap: It seems intuitive that metabolic disease causes liver disease, not the reverse. Board concept: bidirectionality — MASLD → insulin resistance → incident T2DM (2–5× risk). Effective management of insulin resistance may decrease MASH risk.
