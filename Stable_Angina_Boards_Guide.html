01

Spectrum of IHD Presentations

Asymptomatic

Silent ischemia; postmortem MI scars; CAC score positive. Coronary atherosclerosis may begin before age 20.

Stable Angina

Predictable episodic transient ischemia. Relieved by rest or NTG in 1–5 minutes.

Ischemic CMP

Cardiomegaly + HF from ischemic LV damage; may appear without prior angina.

Acute Syndromes

UA, NSTEMI, STEMI — covered in Chapters 285–286. Plaque rupture/erosion → thrombus.

02

Clinical Presentation

Classic Patient

📋 Classic Patient
Typical patient: man >50 years or postmenopausal woman >60 years. Males constitute ~70% of all angina patients.

Symptom quality: crescendo-decrescendo chest heaviness, pressure, squeezing, smothering, or choking — rarely described as "pain." Lasts 2–5 minutes. Triggered by exertion or emotion. Relieved by rest and/or sublingual NTG within 1–5 min.

Radiation Pattern

  • Either shoulder and both arms — especially ulnar aspects of forearm and hand
  • Back, interscapular region, root of neck, jaw, teeth, epigastrium
  • Rarely below umbilicus or above mandible
  • DOES NOT radiate to the trapezius muscles → trapezius radiation = pericarditis, not angina

Angina Equivalents (especially elderly & diabetics)

DyspneaNauseaFatigueFaintness

Precipitants

Physical Triggers

  • Exercise, hurrying, sexual activity, defecation
  • Cold weather, heavy meal, or combinations

Emotional Triggers

  • Anger, fright, frustration, stress
  • Unfamiliar circumstances
🔑 Special Precipitants — Know These
Angina decubitus — occurs at rest while recumbent: ↑ venous return → ↑ LVEDV → ↑ wall tension → ↑ MVO₂

Nocturnal angina — episodic tachycardia + ↓ O₂ saturation + ↑ intrathoracic blood volume with recumbency → ↑ LVEDV → ↑ wall tension → ↑ MVO₂

Seasonal: worse in winter in temperate climates

Angina Threshold

Fixed Threshold
  • Predictable at certain activity level
  • Fixed stenosis + fixed vasomotor tone
  • Ischemia precipitated by ↑ MVO₂
Variable Threshold
  • Varies widely within and between days
  • Vasomotor tone changes
  • May have symptoms at minor exertion in AM but none by midday

High-Risk History Features (Unstable)

  • Angina with less exertion than before
  • Angina at rest
  • Angina awakening patient from sleep
  • Peripheral arterial disease symptoms (intermittent claudication)
  • Prior stroke or TIA
  • Family history of premature IHD (<55 yr male first-degree; <65 yr female first-degree)
03

Physical Examination

Often Normal Between Episodes — Always Search For

  • Atherosclerosis elsewhere: AAA, carotid bruits, diminished peripheral pulses (ankle-brachial index)
  • Xanthelasmas, xanthomas (hyperlipidemia)
  • Hypertensive retinopathy (↑ light reflex, AV nicking)
  • Nicotine staining on fingertips
  • Signs of anemia, thyroid disease
  • BP in both arms; arm-to-leg comparison
  • Protuberant abdomen → metabolic syndrome → ↑ atherosclerotic risk

During an Anginal Attack (examine if possible)

  • Transient S3 and/or S4
  • Dyskinetic cardiac apex (LV dyskinesia — best appreciated in left lateral decubitus)
  • Apical systolic murmur of MR (papillary muscle ischemia)
  • Pulmonary edema (transient LV failure with ischemia)

Exclude These — Cause Angina Without CAD

Aortic stenosis Aortic regurgitation Pulmonary hypertension Hypertrophic CMP
✓ Reassuring Against Ischemia
Chest wall tenderness on palpation, pain localizable to a single fingertip, or exact reproduction with palpation → myocardial ischemia unlikely.

Noninvasive Imaging Modalities

Nuclear Perfusion (SPECT/PET)
  • SPECT: thallium-201 or 99mTc sestamibi
  • PET: N-13 ammonia or Rb-82
  • Reversible defect = ischemia
  • Fixed defect = infarction
  • Best for pts who can exercise but have abnormal resting ECG
Stress Echocardiography
  • Can exercise → exercise echo
  • Cannot exercise → dobutamine echo
  • Positive = akinesis/dyskinesis during stress (exercise or dobutamine) but NOT present at rest
  • Assesses wall motion abnormality
  • More sensitive than exercise ECG alone
Cardiac MRI (CMR)
  • Dobutamine CMR → assesses wall motion abnormality during stress
  • Late gadolinium enhancement (LGE) → characterizes infarct size + transmural extent
  • Distinguishes viable from non-viable myocardium
  • Most complete ventricular evaluation

Coronary Arteriography

Outlines coronary lumina; detects or excludes significant obstruction. Limitation: provides no information about arterial wall. Severe atherosclerosis without luminal encroachment (remodeling) may be missed. Plaques grow outward initially — angiography underestimates plaque burden.

🩺 ISCHEMIA Trial — Key Boards Fact
Stable IHD + EF >35% + moderate-to-severe ischemia on noninvasive testing:
Early invasive strategy (coronary angiography → revascularization) was NOT associated with reduced death or MI vs. conservative medical therapy over 4 years.
However, the invasive strategy was superior for angina relief.
Bottom line: In stable IHD, start medical therapy. Revascularize for symptoms, not to reduce MI/death.

Indications for Coronary Arteriography (11 Points)

  1. Chronic stable angina — severely symptomatic despite medical therapy — considered for PCI or CABG
  2. Symptoms presenting diagnostic difficulty — need to confirm or rule out IHD
  3. Known/possible angina who survived cardiac arrest
  4. Angina or ischemia on noninvasive testing + clinical/lab evidence of ventricular dysfunction
  5. Negative/nondiagnostic stress test but angina symptoms requiring definitive diagnosis (management, psychological need, career, insurance)
  6. Repeated hospitalizations for suspected ACS without confirmed diagnosis
  7. Safety-critical occupations (pilots, firefighters, police) with questionable symptoms or positive noninvasive test
  8. Aortic stenosis or HCM + angina — chest pain may be due to IHD
  9. Men >45 yr / women >55 yr undergoing cardiac surgery (valve repair/replacement)
  10. Post-MI with high-risk features (recurrent angina, HF, frequent VPCs, ischemia on stress test)
  11. Suspected coronary spasm or nonatherosclerotic cause (coronary anomaly, Kawasaki disease)
05

Prognosis

Principal Prognostic Indicators

Age

Advanced age worsens prognosis at every level of disease severity.

LV Function

Most powerful indicator. EF <0.40 = high risk. ↑ LVEDP + ↑ LVEDV + ↓ EF = poor prognosis.

Coronary Anatomy

Location + severity of narrowing. Left main (>50%) and proximal LAD = highest risk (most myocardium at risk).

Ischemia Severity

Degree and extent of myocardial ischemia on noninvasive testing — worsens prognosis independently.

High-Risk Features → ↑ Adverse Coronary Events

Clinical / Symptoms:

  • Recent-onset, unstable, or early post-MI angina
  • Angina poorly responsive or unresponsive to medical therapy
  • Angina + symptoms of CHF
  • Physical signs of HF (pulmonary edema, transient S3, MR murmur)
  • Echocardiographic evidence of cardiac enlargement, EF <0.40
  • Frequent ST-segment deviation on ambulatory ECG (symptomatic AND asymptomatic)

Noninvasive Testing:

  • Inability to exercise to Stage II Bruce (<6 min)
  • ST depression ≥1 mm before Stage II completion
  • ST depression ≥2 mm at any stage
  • ST depression persisting >5 min post-exercise
  • SBP drop >10 mmHg during exercise
  • Ventricular tachyarrhythmia during exercise
  • Large or multiple perfusion defects; ↑ lung uptake on nuclear stress
  • ↓ LV EF during exercise on radionuclide ventriculography or stress echo

Risk Factors That Worsen Prognosis

Age >75HypertensionDyslipidemia Diabetes mellitusMorbid obesity Peripheral vascular diseaseCerebrovascular disease Prior MI
✓ Good Prognosis Markers
Completing Stage III Bruce + normal perfusion scan or negative stress echo = very low risk.
Chest discomfort with normal LV function + normal coronary arteries = excellent prognosis.
06

Treatment — Risk Factor Modification

📋 Six-Component Management Plan (Overview)
  1. Explanation + reassurance
  2. Identification + treatment of aggravating conditions
  3. Adaptation of activity
  4. Treatment of risk factors (this section)
  5. Drug therapy for angina
  6. Consideration of revascularization

Dyslipidemia

Central to long-term angina relief, ↓ revascularization need, and ↓ MI and death. Combine: low saturated/trans-fat diet + exercise + weight loss.

🔑 Stepwise Escalation — IHD + LDL ≥70 mg/dL
  1. Maximally tolerated statin (↓ LDL 25–50%, ↑ HDL 5–9%, ↓ TG 5–30%)
  2. Add ezetimibe
  3. Add PCSK9 inhibitor (very high-risk patients)
  4. Consider bempedoic acid if above insufficient or not tolerated
Icosapent ethyl (purified EPA): only TG-lowering agent shown to reduce cardiovascular risk in the statin era.

Hypertension

  • Coexists with other IHD risk factors; LVH from sustained HTN aggravates ischemia
  • Target: <130/80 mmHg (especially in diabetics)
  • Long-term effective treatment ↓ adverse coronary events

Diabetes Mellitus

  • Accelerates coronary, cerebrovascular, and peripheral atherosclerosis
  • Target LDL <70 mg/dL
  • In IHD + T2DM: SGLT-2 inhibitor OR GLP-1 receptor agonist recommended (↓ MACE per guidelines)
  • BP target: <130/80 mmHg

Smoking Cessation

  • Accelerates atherosclerosis in both sexes at all ages
  • ↑ MVO₂ and ↓ O₂ supply → aggravates angina
  • E-cigarettes may also increase frequency of anginal episodes
  • Message must be clear and strong; support programs for complete abstinence from all tobacco products

Women with IHD

  • Premenopausal: very low IHD incidence
  • Post-menopause: atherogenic risk factors ↑ (↑ LDL); event rate accelerates to level seen in men
  • Diabetes amplifies effects of HTN, hyperlipidemia, smoking disproportionately in women
  • Cardiac catheterization and revascularization are underused in women (performed at later, more severe stage)
  • When evidence-based therapies applied appropriately: women benefit equally to men

Drug Therapy for Angina

To achieve maximum benefit, frequently necessary to combine agents from different classes and titrate doses guided by individual risk factors, symptoms, hemodynamic responses, and side effects.

NitratesFirst-line antianginal · All classes
Mechanism
  • Systemic venodilation → ↓ LVEDV and LVEDP → ↓ wall tension → ↓ MVO₂
  • Dilation of epicardial coronary arteries
  • ↑ Blood flow through collateral vessels
  • Metabolized → releases NO → activates guanylyl cyclase → ↑ cGMP → vascular smooth muscle relaxation
  • Antithrombotic: NO-mediated ↓ intraplatelet Ca²⁺ → ↓ platelet aggregation
Key Preparation — Sublingual NTG

Dose: 0.4–0.6 mg SL (tablet or spray). Give both to relieve acute episodes AND 5 min before activities likely to trigger angina (prophylactic).

Calcium Channel BlockersAlternative to BB · Especially for vasospasm
Mechanism

Coronary vasodilators. Variable reductions in MVO₂, contractility, and arterial pressure. As effective as beta blockers for angina. Especially indicated when beta blockers are contraindicated or poorly tolerated.

Dihydropyridines (DHP)

Amlodipine · felodipine · nicardipine · nifedipine

  • Potent vasodilators; minimal negative inotropy
  • Treat angina + hypertension simultaneously
  • Safe to combine with beta blockers
  • Vasospastic angina responds especially well
  • Short-acting nifedipine: AVOID — risk of precipitating MI, especially without concurrent beta blocker
Non-DHPs

Verapamil · Diltiazem

  • Can cause conduction disturbances + bradyarrhythmias
  • Negative inotropic → can aggravate LV failure
  • Verapamil + beta blocker: AVOID (combined HR + contractility suppression)
  • Diltiazem + beta blocker: OK if normal LV function and no conduction disturbances
6 Indications to Use CCB Instead of or in Addition to Beta Blocker
  1. Inadequate response to beta blocker + nitrate combination
  2. Adverse reactions to beta blockers (depression, sexual disturbances, fatigue)
  3. Angina + asthma or COPD
  4. Sick-sinus syndrome or significant AV conduction disturbances
  5. Vasospastic (Prinzmetal) angina
  6. Symptomatic peripheral arterial disease

Coronary Revascularization

⚠ Guiding Principle
In stable IHD: initiate medical therapy first. Revascularization should be employed in conjunction with — not in place of — ongoing risk factor modification and drug therapy.

Percutaneous Coronary Intervention (PCI)

Indications & Patient Selection

  • Most common indication: symptom-limiting angina despite medical therapy + ischemia on stress test
  • PCI is more effective than medical therapy for angina relief
  • Improves outcomes in unstable angina and when used early in MI (with or without cardiogenic shock)
  • In stable exertional angina: PCI does NOT reduce death or MI vs. optimum medical therapy (ISCHEMIA trial)
  • Can treat stenoses in bypass grafts in patients with recurrent angina post-CABG
  • Primary success (angina relief): >95% of cases
  • Drug-eluting stents: restenosis rate <5% vs. bare-metal stents → now standard
  • Delayed endothelialization with DES → prolonged risk for subacute stent thrombosis

Post-PCI Antithrombotic Therapy (DAPT)

  • Aspirin indefinitely + P2Y₁₂ antagonist daily (DAPT) for ideally 1 year post drug-eluting stent
  • Continuing DAPT up to 30 months: further benefit but at cost of ↑ bleeding
  • Shorter DAPT (1–3 months): acceptable for high bleeding risk or those requiring oral anticoagulation

Evolving DAPT Strategies (Reducing Bleeding)

  1. Shorten DAPT: eliminate aspirin after 1–3 months → continue P2Y₁₂ antagonist alone
  2. De-escalate: switch from potent P2Y₁₂ inhibitor at 1 month → clopidogrel or reduced-dose prasugrel 5 mg/d
  3. Dual-pathway: switch from DAPT → low-dose direct oral anticoagulant + antiplatelet

Risks of PCI

  • Coronary dissection or thrombosis → vessel occlusion, uncontrolled ischemia, ventricular failure
  • Two or even three vessels can be treated in sequence when lesions are discrete and symmetric
  • Left main stenosis: generally refer for CABG. PCI of unprotected left main only by highly skilled operator if prohibitive surgical risk.

PCI vs. CABG Comparison

PCI Advantages
  • Less invasive and initially less expensive
  • Avoids stroke risk of CABG
  • Earlier return to work and active life
  • No general anesthesia required
  • Can be performed in high surgical risk patients
PCI Disadvantages
  • Early benefits reduced over time — ↑ follow-up and repeat procedures needed
  • Higher redevelopment of angina → more repeat revascularizations vs. CABG
  • In DM or 3-vessel/left main CAD: CABG superior at 12-month follow-up
🔑 Conceptual Difference — Why CABG Wins in Multivessel Disease
PCI targets the "culprit" lesion. CABG treats the epicardial vessel — protecting against both the culprit lesion AND future culprit lesions proximal to the graft anastomosis. This explains CABG superiority in multivessel disease at intermediate follow-up.
Stroke rates are lower with PCI.

Coronary Artery Bypass Grafting (CABG)

🔑 Preferred Conduits
Internal mammary artery (IMA) or radial artery anastomosed to coronary artery distal to obstruction = preferred. Long-term patency: IMA/radial >> saphenous vein. IMA bypass to LAD → better survival than saphenous vein graft.

CABG Indications — Clear Clinical Situations

  1. Left main coronary artery stenosis >50% — most critical anatomy; CABG improves survival
  2. 3-vessel disease — especially when EF <35% (greatest survival benefit)
  3. 2-vessel disease with proximal LAD involvement — especially with ↓ EF or diabetes
  4. T2DM + multivessel CAD — CABG + optimal medical therapy > PCI; CABG > PCI for death, MI, and repeat revascularization
  5. Hibernating myocardium with LV dysfunction — viable but hypocontractile myocardium; revascularization can restore function and improve survival
  6. Survivors of sudden cardiac death with significant CAD
  7. Prior CABG with multiple vein graft stenoses + high-risk noninvasive testing
  8. Recurrent stenosis post-PCI with high-risk anatomy not suitable for re-PCI
  9. Severely symptomatic angina unresponsive to maximum medical therapy (when anatomy suitable)
  10. Lesions anatomically unsuitable for PCI (small diffuse disease, complex bifurcation lesions)
Expected Outcomes
  • Operative mortality: <1% in appropriate candidates
  • Angina abolished/greatly reduced: ~90% of patients
  • Venous graft occlusion: 10–20% in first postoperative year; ~2%/yr at 5–7 yr; 4%/yr thereafter
  • Graft patency improved by treating dyslipidemia

📌 Multidisciplinary Decision
Given complexity of PCI vs. CABG decisions: cardiologist + cardiac surgeon + primary care physician + patient preferences should all contribute before committing to a revascularization strategy.

Exam Traps — Stage A Boards

Expert board exam coaching for Stage A Internal Medicine residents. Each trap shows exactly how the question is framed and the precise reasoning for the correct answer.

Trap 01Trapezius Radiation — Pericarditis vs. Angina
A 55-year-old man presents with chest discomfort radiating to his left trapezius ridge. The pain worsens when lying flat and improves when leaning forward. Which is the most likely diagnosis?
Correct Answer: Pericarditis, NOT ischemic heart disease.
Angina pectoris does NOT radiate to the trapezius muscles. Trapezius ridge radiation is a classic distinguishing feature of pericarditis. The positional component (worse supine, better leaning forward) confirms it.
⚠ Trap: Students see "chest pain radiating to the shoulder" and reflexively think angina. The key word is trapezius — angina radiates to ulnar aspects of the arm, jaw, teeth, and epigastrium, but never to the trapezius.
Trap 02False-Negative ETT — Circumflex Territory
A 62-year-old woman with typical angina undergoes an exercise stress test. She achieves 92% of target heart rate with no ST changes. Coronary angiography later reveals a 75% stenosis of the left circumflex artery. Why did the ETT miss this?
Correct Answer: The posterolateral wall (circumflex territory) is not well represented on the standard 12-lead ECG.
Isolated circumflex disease classically produces false-negative exercise ECG results. Imaging modality (nuclear SPECT, stress echo, or CMR) would have detected the ischemia.
⚠ Trap: A negative ETT is NOT a guarantee of no significant CAD. In a patient with intermediate/high pretest probability, a negative ETT should prompt functional imaging with an imaging modality, not reassurance.
Trap 03Verapamil + Beta Blocker = AVOID
A 58-year-old man with stable angina on metoprolol 50 mg BID continues to have symptoms. His BP is 155/90. You consider adding a calcium channel blocker. Which is safe?
Correct Answer: Amlodipine (dihydropyridine CCB) is safe to add. Verapamil must be AVOIDED.
Verapamil combined with a beta blocker causes additive suppression of HR, AV conduction, and myocardial contractility → severe bradycardia, AV block, or acute HF. Diltiazem + beta blocker can be used in patients with normal LV function and no conduction disturbances.
⚠ Trap: The question mentions BP needs control — don't let that distract you. DHP CCBs (amlodipine) are the right add-on when a beta blocker is already on board.
Trap 04ISCHEMIA Trial — Revascularize for Symptoms, Not Mortality
A 64-year-old man with stable angina (CCS Class II), EF 45%, and moderate ischemia on nuclear stress test is on optimal medical therapy. His cardiologist recommends coronary angiography. The patient asks: "Will a stent save my life?" What is the evidence-based answer?
Correct Answer: NO — early invasive strategy did NOT reduce death or MI vs. conservative medical therapy over 4 years (ISCHEMIA trial).
The invasive strategy was superior only for angina relief. Revascularization in stable IHD addresses symptoms, not mortality (in patients with EF >35%).
⚠ Trap: Students confuse stable angina (where PCI/CABG is for symptoms) with ACS (where PCI reduces mortality). The ISCHEMIA trial was a landmark study that confirmed: in stable IHD with moderate-severe ischemia and preserved EF, revascularize to improve quality of life, not to extend life.
Trap 05Diabetic + Multivessel CAD → CABG, Not PCI
A 60-year-old woman with T2DM and 3-vessel CAD including proximal LAD disease is referred for revascularization. Her EF is 55%. She prefers the "less invasive" option. What should she be counseled?
Correct Answer: CABG with left IMA conduit is the preferred strategy.
In T2DM + multivessel CAD: CABG + optimal medical therapy is superior to PCI for death, MI, and repeat revascularization. The survival and event-rate advantage of CABG is especially pronounced in diabetics with proximal LAD involvement.
⚠ Trap: The patient's preference for "less invasive" is understandable but the evidence clearly favors CABG. This is a classic boards setup — don't let patient preference override guideline-directed therapy when the question asks what is most appropriate.
Trap 06Nitrate Tolerance — The Free Interval
A 52-year-old man is started on a transdermal nitroglycerin patch (0.4 mg/h) worn continuously 24 hours/day. After 2 weeks his angina is no better. Why might this be failing, and what should be changed?
Correct Answer: Nitrate tolerance has developed due to continuous use. The patch must be removed for a 10–12 hour nitrate-free interval daily (e.g., remove at bedtime).
Continuous nitrate exposure depletes intracellular thiols, causing tolerance. Eccentric dosing schedules (e.g., patch on for 12–14 h, off overnight) prevent this.
⚠ Trap: Students know nitrates work but forget about tolerance. The clue is "continuous" use with loss of effect. The fix is NOT to increase the dose — it's to implement a nitrate-free interval.
Trap 07Short-Acting Nifedipine — AVOID in IHD
A 67-year-old man with angina and hypertension is started on immediate-release nifedipine 10 mg TID for angina management. Three days later he presents with an acute MI. What was the error?
Correct Answer: Immediate-release (short-acting) nifedipine should be AVOIDED in IHD.
Short-acting nifedipine causes reflex tachycardia and sympathetic activation due to rapid vasodilation → ↑ MVO₂ → can precipitate MI. This risk is especially high in the absence of a concurrent beta blocker. Use long-acting DHP formulations (e.g., amlodipine or slow-release nifedipine) instead.
⚠ Trap: "Nifedipine" alone sounds acceptable. The danger is specifically in the short-acting, immediate-release formulation. Long-acting DHP CCBs are safe and effective.
Trap 08Ivabradine — Contraindicated in Normal LV Function
A 61-year-old man with stable angina, resting HR 78, and EF 62% is already on maximum-dose metoprolol. He still has symptoms. A colleague suggests adding ivabradine to further lower his heart rate. What is the correct approach per current US guidelines?
Correct Answer: The most recent US CCD guidelines recommend AGAINST ivabradine in patients with normal LV function — it is associated with increased risk of death or MI.
Ivabradine may be considered in IHD patients with resting HR ≥70 AND LV systolic dysfunction. In patients with preserved EF, it is contraindicated per current US guidelines.
⚠ Trap: European guidelines still allow ivabradine in normal EF. Board questions testing US guidelines expect you to know the more restrictive US CCD recommendation: normal LV function = do NOT use ivabradine.
Trap 09Beta Blocker — Never Stop Abruptly
A 58-year-old man on metoprolol 100 mg BID for stable angina runs out of medication and stops taking it abruptly. 3 days later he presents with worsening chest pain at rest and is found to have an NSTEMI. What was the likely mechanism?
Correct Answer: Abrupt beta blocker withdrawal causes rebound adrenergic stimulation → ↑↑ HR, ↑ contractility, ↑ MVO₂ → intensified ischemia and potential MI.
Beta blockers must be tapered over at least 2 weeks when discontinuation is necessary. Abrupt cessation is particularly dangerous in patients with underlying IHD.
⚠ Trap: Students know beta blockers are safe long-term but forget the withdrawal syndrome. The question frames it as "ran out of medication" — this is a classic boards setup for abrupt discontinuation causing ischemic escalation.
Trap 10Ranolazine — CYP3A Interaction Trap
A 65-year-old man with stable angina on ranolazine 1000 mg BID is started on diltiazem for rate control of atrial fibrillation. What must be addressed?
Correct Answer: Diltiazem is a CYP3A inhibitor — it significantly increases ranolazine plasma levels, raising the risk of QTc prolongation. The ranolazine dose should be reduced (500 mg BID) or an alternative rate-control agent considered.
Ranolazine is a CYP3A substrate. CYP3A inhibitors (diltiazem, verapamil, ketoconazole, macrolides, HIV protease inhibitors) dramatically increase ranolazine levels → QTc prolongation → risk of arrhythmia.
⚠ Trap: Both drugs are commonly used in cardiology and students don't always think about drug-drug interactions within the same specialty. Ranolazine + diltiazem (or verapamil) is a high-yield CYP3A interaction pair.
Trap 11Reassuring Against Ischemia — The Single Fingertip Sign
A 48-year-old man presents with chest pain. During examination, he locates his pain precisely with a single fingertip over the left 4th rib and the pain is exactly reproduced with palpation of that area. ECG and troponin are normal. What is the most appropriate next step?
Correct Answer: This is musculoskeletal (costochondritis/chest wall tenderness) — NOT ischemic. The single-fingertip localization and exact palpation reproduction essentially rules out myocardial ischemia.
From the textbook: chest wall tenderness on palpation, localization to a single fingertip, or exact reproduction with palpation makes myocardial ischemia unlikely. No further cardiac workup is required in this clinical context.
⚠ Trap: 48-year-old male with chest pain can fool students into aggressive cardiac workup. The physical exam findings here are classic for chest wall origin. Knowing what reassures against ischemia is as high-yield as knowing the classic presentation.
Trap 12Dyslipidemia Escalation Sequence
A 58-year-old man with stable IHD is on atorvastatin 80 mg/d. His LDL is 88 mg/dL (target <70 mg/dL). What is the correct next step?
Correct Answer: Add ezetimibe.
The stepwise escalation in IHD patients with LDL ≥70 mg/dL on maximally tolerated statin is: (1) Max statin → (2) Add ezetimibe → (3) Add PCSK9 inhibitor → (4) Consider bempedoic acid. Ezetimibe comes before PCSK9 inhibitor.
⚠ Trap: Some students skip ezetimibe and jump to PCSK9 inhibitors. The guideline-directed sequence requires ezetimibe first (it's cheaper and available orally). PCSK9 inhibitors are reserved for very high-risk patients who still don't reach target on statin + ezetimibe.
