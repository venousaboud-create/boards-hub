01

Normal Acid-Base Homeostasis

The Core System

Systemic arterial pH is maintained between 7.35 and 7.45 by three integrated mechanisms working in concert:

Extracellular Buffering

Bicarbonate-carbonic acid system — the dominant buffer. Immediate response to acid/base loads.

Respiratory Control

CNS and respiratory system control arterial CO₂ tension (PaCO₂). Minutes to hours response time.

Renal Regulation

Kidneys control plasma [HCO₃⁻] by excreting or retaining acid or alkali. Hours to days.

Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation

The fundamental equation describing pH when CO₂ solubility is considered (dissolved CO₂ = 0.03 × PaCO₂):

pH = pK' + log10 ([HCO₃⁻] / αCO₂ × PCO₂)
pK' = 6.1 · αCO₂ = 0.03 mmol/L/mmHg
🔑 Key Relationship
pH is determined by the ratio of [HCO₃⁻] to PaCO₂ — not by either value alone. Compensation works by adjusting the other variable to restore this ratio toward normal.

CO₂ Regulation

  • Usual steady-state PaCO₂ maintained at ~40 mmHg — production matched to excretion
  • Hypercapnia = CO₂ underexcretion → usually due to hypoventilation, not increased production
  • Hypocapnia = CO₂ overexcretion → due to hyperventilation
  • PaCO₂ is regulated primarily by neural respiratory factors, not by the rate of CO₂ production

⚠ Rule
Compensatory responses always move PaCO₂ and [HCO₃⁻] in the same direction as the primary change. If they move in opposite directions → mixed disorder.
02

Stepwise Diagnosis of Acid-Base Disorders

The 8-Step Approach

  • Obtain ABG and venous electrolytes simultaneously — before therapy begins
  • Verify HCO₃⁻ — calculated HCO₃⁻ on ABG and measured on electrolyte panel should agree within ±2 mmol/L. If not → suspect lab error or non-simultaneous draws
  • Assess anion gap — correct to albumin 4.5 g/dL; high AG if >10 mEq/L
  • Identify high-AG causes — ketoacidosis, lactic acidosis, advanced kidney disease, toxic alcohol ingestion
  • Identify non-gap causes — GI bicarbonate loss, renal tubular acidosis
  • Estimate predicted compensatory response — use formulas in Table 58-1
  • Compare delta values — ΔAG vs ΔHCO₃⁻ (see below)
  • Compare change in [Cl⁻] with change in [Na⁺] on the electrolyte panel

The Delta-Delta Ratio: Unmasking Hidden Disorders

In high-AG acidosis, compare:

ΔAG = Patient's AG − 10 (normal AG)
ΔHCO₃⁻ = 25 − Patient's [HCO₃⁻]
ΔAG vs ΔHCO₃⁻Interpretation
ΔAG ≈ ΔHCO₃⁻Pure high-AG metabolic acidosis
ΔAG >> ΔHCO₃⁻High-AG acidosis + concurrent Metabolic Alkalosis (HCO₃⁻ inappropriately high)
ΔAG << ΔHCO₃⁻High-AG acidosis + concurrent Normal-AG (hyperchloremic) acidosis
🚨 Classic Trap
Normal pH/HCO₃⁻/PaCO₂ does NOT exclude an acid-base disorder. Always check the AG. An elevated AG = metabolic acidosis regardless of normal pH (e.g., alcoholic with vomiting + ketoacidosis: pH 7.40, HCO₃⁻ 25, but AG = 30 → mixed met alkalosis + met acidosis).
03

The Anion Gap

Calculation & Normal Values

AG = [Na⁺] − ([Cl⁻] + [HCO₃⁻])
Note: K⁺ is typically omitted in US. Normal AG = 6–12 mmol/L; average ~10 mmol/L

Normal unmeasured anions: anionic proteins (albumin), phosphate, sulfate, and organic anions.

Why the AG Changes

AG Increases When
  • ↑ Unmeasured anions (ketoacids, lactate, phosphate)
  • ↓ Unmeasured cations (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, K⁺)
  • ↑ Anionic albumin (severe dehydration)
AG Decreases When
  • ↑ Unmeasured cations
  • Abnormal cations added: lithium, cationic immunoglobulins
  • ↓ Albumin (nephrotic syndrome, liver disease, malabsorption)
  • Hyperviscosity / severe hyperlipidemia (underestimates Na⁺, Cl⁻)

Albumin Correction — Critical Step

Corrected AG = Reported AG + 2.5 × (4.5 − Serum albumin g/dL)
Example: Albumin 2.5 g/dL, AG = 15 → Corrected AG = 15 + (2.5 × 2) = 20 mmol/L
🚨 Critical Point
Clinical labs do NOT automatically correct the AG for hypoalbuminemia. You must do this manually. In critically ill patients (low albumin), a "normal" AG of 12 may actually represent a high AG acidosis.
04

Mixed Acid-Base Disorders

Mixed disorders are independently coexisting disorders, not compensatory responses. Common in critically ill patients and can cause dangerous extremes of pH.

Recognizing a Mixed Disorder

  • PaCO₂ or [HCO₃⁻] moves in the opposite direction to what's expected = mixed disorder
  • Compensation that overshoots or undershoots predicted values = mixed disorder
  • ΔAG incongruent with ΔHCO₃⁻ = mixed disorder

05

Metabolic Acidosis — Overview & Treatment

Mechanisms of Metabolic Acidosis

Endogenous Acid Production ↑

Lactate, ketoacids accumulate and overwhelm buffering capacity

Bicarbonate Loss

GI loss (diarrhea) or renal loss (RTA) depletes HCO₃⁻ buffer

Impaired Acid Excretion

Kidney failure → insufficient net acid excretion → acid accumulates

Systemic Effects of Metabolic Acidosis

  • Respiratory: Characteristic ↑ ventilation (Kussmaul breathing) — driven by ↓ pH stimulating medullary chemoreceptors
  • Cardiac: Intrinsic contractility depressed; BUT inotropic function can be normal due to catecholamine release. Peripheral arterial vasodilation + central venoconstriction → ↓ pulmonary compliance → risk of pulmonary edema with even minimal volume overload
  • CNS: Headache, lethargy, stupor, coma
  • Metabolic: Glucose intolerance

Two Major Categories

High-AG Metabolic Acidosis
  • Lactic acidosis
  • Ketoacidosis (DKA, AKA, starvation)
  • Kidney failure
  • Toxins: ethylene glycol, methanol, salicylates, propylene glycol, pyroglutamic acid
Normal-AG (Hyperchloremic) Acidosis
  • GI: diarrhea, fistulas, bowel diversions
  • Renal: Proximal RTA (Type 2), Distal RTA (Type 1), Type 4 RTA
  • Drugs: acetazolamide, amphotericin B, topiramate
  • Expansion acidosis (rapid saline)

Treatment of Metabolic Acidosis

⚠ General Principle
Alkali therapy should be reserved for severe acidemia EXCEPT when the patient has no "potential HCO₃⁻" in plasma (i.e., nonmetabolizable anions).
  • Potential HCO₃⁻ = ΔAG (patient's AG − 10) — applies ONLY if the anion is metabolizable (β-hydroxybutyrate, acetoacetate, lactate). When the underlying cause resolves, these acids are converted back to HCO₃⁻
  • Nonmetabolizable anions (advanced CKD, toxin ingestion) → no potential HCO₃⁻ → alkali therapy is needed
  • For uremic or hyperchloremic acidosis: NaHCO₃ tablets or Shohl's solution PO, or IV NaHCO₃ — titrate to target [HCO₃⁻] ≥ 22 mmol/L
  • Avoid overcorrection — can paradoxically worsen outcome
🚨 DKA-Specific Rule
Bicarbonate therapy in DKA is reserved for: pH < 7.00 AND/OR shock. If given: 50 mEq NaHCO₃ in 300 mL saline over 30–45 min. Goal: raise [HCO₃⁻] to 10–12 mmol/L and pH to ~7.20 — NOT to normal. Risk of cerebral edema. Regular insulin: 0.1 U/kg bolus → 0.1 U/kg/h until AG normalizes.
06

High-Anion Gap Acidoses

The Four Principal Causes

1. Lactic Acidosis 2. Ketoacidosis 3. Ingested Toxins 4. Kidney Failure

Lactic Acidosis

Type A — Poor Tissue Perfusion
  • Shock (all types), cardiac failure
  • Severe anemia
  • Mitochondrial enzyme defects
  • CO, cyanide poisoning
  • Unrecognized bowel ischemia (elderly + vasopressors)
Type B — Aerobic Disorders
  • Malignancies, seizures
  • Nucleoside analogue RTIs (HIV)
  • Diabetes mellitus, kidney/hepatic failure
  • Thiamine deficiency
  • Biguanides, ethanol, toxic alcohols
  • Severe infections (cholera, malaria)
  • Pyroglutamic acidemia (acetaminophen)
Treatment: L-Lactic Acid Acidosis
  • Primary goal: correct the underlying cause — restore tissue perfusion
  • Avoid vasoconstrictors if possible (may worsen perfusion)
  • Alkali therapy for acute severe acidemia (pH < 7.00) to improve cardiovascular function
  • NaHCO₃ paradox: may depress cardiac performance and exacerbate acidosis by stimulating phosphofructokinase → ↑ lactate production
  • Never attempt to normalize pH with NaHCO₃ — deleterious. Target: pH ≤ 7.2, [HCO₃⁻] ≤ 12 mmol/L
  • If cause resolves: lactate → HCO₃⁻ → risk of rebound alkalosis if NaHCO₃ was given excessively

Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)

  • Cause: ↑ fatty acid metabolism → accumulation of acetoacetate + β-hydroxybutyrate
  • Triggers: insulin cessation, infection, gastroenteritis, pancreatitis, MI
  • Classic triad: hyperglycemia + ketonemia + high-AG acidosis
  • Euglycemic DKA (eDKA): SGLT2 inhibitors → glycosuria → osmotic diuresis → ↓ plasma glucose → normal glucose + ketosis + high-AG. The accumulation of ketoacids in plasma accounts for the increment in AG in both classical and euglycemic DKA.
  • Dipstick limitation: nitroprusside reaction detects acetoacetate, NOT β-hydroxybutyrate → underestimates ketosis
  • Ketoacid excretion obligates Na⁺ + K⁺ loss → volume depletion + Cl⁻ retention → possible mixed high-AG/non-AG acidosis
🚨 Volume Overexpansion Warning
Aggressive saline administration during DKA treatment may cause overt volume overload and/or hyperchloremic acidosis. Volume overexpansion with isotonic saline should be avoided.

Alcoholic Ketoacidosis (AKA)

  • Setting: chronic alcoholism, binge drinking, vomiting, poor nutrition, volume depletion
  • Glucose: variable (may be normal, low, or high) — absence of hyperglycemia + high-AG = AKA clue
  • Predominantly β-hydroxybutyrate (not acetoacetate) → dipstick may be falsely low; measure plasma β-hydroxybutyrate specifically
  • As circulation restored → β-hydroxybutyrate shifts to acetoacetate → nitroprusside becomes positive (not worsening!)
  • Normal kidney function → excretes ketoacids + retains Cl⁻ → mixed high-AG/non-AG acidosis (ΔHCO₃⁻ exceeds ΔAG)
Treatment: AKA
  • IV saline + glucose (5% dextrose in 0.9% NaCl) — replete ECFV
  • Monitor and correct: hypophosphatemia (emerges 12–24h; may cause hemolysis, rhabdomyolysis, respiratory arrest), hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia
  • Watch for: upper GI hemorrhage, pancreatitis, pneumonia

In ethylene glycol/methanol intoxication: osmolar gap is elevated EARLY (parent compound present) but declines as the parent compound is metabolized to acids. As osmolar gap declines, the AG increases. Do NOT wait for lab confirmation before treating when clinical suspicion is high.
🔑 Fomepizole Mechanism
Fomepizole (4-methylpyrazole) and ethanol both compete with ethylene glycol/methanol for metabolism by alcohol dehydrogenase. Fomepizole is preferred: predictable decline in levels, no excessive obtundation. Continue until blood pH normal or osmolar gap <10 mOsm/kg H₂O.

Chronic Kidney Disease

  • Moderate CKD (stage 3B): hyperchloremic (normal-AG) acidosis
  • Advanced CKD (stages 4–5): converts to high-AG acidosis — poor filtration + reabsorption of organic anions
  • Characterized by ↓ NH₄⁺ production and excretion
  • Despite retaining up to 20 mmol/d of acid, serum [HCO₃⁻] doesn't typically decrease further → bone buffers take up the excess (bone calcium carbonate) → significant bone loss
  • Recent evidence: high-AG acidosis of CKD itself contributes to progressive loss of kidney function
Treatment: CKD Metabolic Acidosis
  • Target serum [HCO₃⁻] > 22–24 mmol/L
  • NaHCO₃ tablets (650-mg tablet = 7.8 mEq) or Shohl's solution
  • Relatively modest doses: 1.0–1.5 mmol/kg body weight/day
  • Fruits and vegetables (citrate) safely ↑ plasma [HCO₃⁻] and slow CKD progression
  • Benefits: slows CKD progression, reduces muscle catabolism, improves bone disease

Non–Anion Gap Metabolic Acidoses & RTAs

In non-AG acidosis, reciprocal changes in [Cl⁻] and [HCO₃⁻] maintain a normal AG. The increase in [Cl⁻] approximates the decrease in [HCO₃⁻].

Causes — The Comprehensive List

I. Gastrointestinal Bicarbonate Loss

  • Diarrhea — most common; stool [HCO₃⁻] higher than plasma → metabolic acidosis develops
  • External pancreatic or small-bowel drainage/fistula
  • Urinary diversion: ureterosigmoidostomy, jejunal loop, ileal loop
  • Drugs: calcium chloride (acidifying agent), magnesium sulfate (diarrhea), cholestyramine
🔑 Diarrhea Urine pH Clue
Despite systemic acidosis, urine pH is usually >6 in diarrhea because acidosis + hypokalemia ↑ renal NH₄⁺ synthesis → urine buffer ↑ → alkaline urine. This distinguishes diarrhea from RTA.

II. Renal Tubular Acidoses

  • Type 1 (Distal RTA): hypokalemia, urine pH >5.5, ↓ NH₄⁺, nephrolithiasis/nephrocalcinosis
  • Type 2 (Proximal RTA): Fanconi syndrome, urine pH variable
  • Type 4 RTA: hyperkalemia, hyporeninemic hypoaldosteronism

III–IV. Drug-Induced & Other

  • K⁺-sparing diuretics, trimethoprim, pentamidine, ACE-I/ARBs, NSAIDs, calcineurin inhibitors, heparin
  • Expansion acidosis (rapid saline), ammonium chloride, loss of potential bicarbonate (ketone excretion)

Urine Anion Gap — Estimating NH₄⁺ Excretion

UAG = [Na⁺]u + [K⁺]u − [Cl⁻]u

Hyporeninemic Hypoaldosteronism

  • Classic patient: older adult + diabetes mellitus + CKD stage 3–4
  • Presents with: non-AG metabolic acidosis, hyperkalemia ([K⁺] 5.2–6.0 mmol/L), hypertension, CHF
  • Both acidosis and hyperkalemia are OUT OF PROPORTION to reduction in GFR
  • NSAIDs, trimethoprim, pentamidine, ACE-I/ARBs, calcineurin inhibitors, heparin can all precipitate/worsen

Treatment of Non-AG Acidoses

  • GI losses: NaHCO₃ IV or PO based on severity; calcium citrate preferred over carbonate when intestinal disease present (better absorbed; ↑ urinary citrate → ↓ oxalate nephrolithiasis risk)
  • Type 1 RTA: sodium citrate or NaHCO₃ tablets; correct hypokalemia first; long-term benefits = fewer kidney stones, better bone density, normal growth in children
  • Type 2 RTA: potassium citrate essential (Polycitra K / Cytra-K); most challenging to treat
  • Type 4 RTA: correct acidosis + K⁺; patiromer (8.4 g packets PO BID, max 25.2 g/d) preferred over sodium polystyrene sulfonate; low-K⁺ diet; fludrocortisone cautiously (risk of volume/HTN aggravation)

Metabolic Alkalosis

Definition & Characteristics

  • Established by: elevated arterial pH + ↑ serum [HCO₃⁻] + ↑ PaCO₂ (compensatory alveolar hypoventilation)
  • Often accompanied by hypochloremia and hypokalemia
  • Elevated pH distinguishes it from respiratory acidosis (which also has elevated PaCO₂ but low pH)
🔑 Generation vs. Maintenance
Generation stage: initial loss of acid (e.g., vomiting → HCl loss → HCO₃⁻ retained). Maintenance stage: kidneys FAIL to excrete the excess HCO₃⁻. Without maintenance factors, kidneys would normalize HCO₃⁻ within hours.

Maintenance Factors — Why Kidneys Cannot Correct It

Saline-Responsive (Urine [Cl⁻] < 20 mEq/L)

Volume + chloride + K⁺ deficiency + ↓ GFR

  • Vomiting/gastric suction
  • Diuretic use (thiazides, loops)
  • Posthypercapnic state
  • Recovery from lactic/ketoacidosis
  • Mg²⁺ deficiency

Corrected by: IV NaCl + KCl

Saline-Unresponsive (Urine [Cl⁻] > 20 mEq/L)

Hypokalemia from autonomous hyperaldosteronism

  • Primary aldosteronism (adenoma, hyperplasia)
  • Renal artery stenosis, renin-secreting tumor
  • Cushing's syndrome
  • Bartter's/Gitelman's syndrome
  • Liddle's syndrome (ENaC gain-of-function)
  • Licorice, carbenoxolone, chewer's tobacco

Requires: pharmacologic or surgical correction

Gastrointestinal Origin — Mechanism

  • Vomiting/NG suction → loss of HCl from stomach → HCO₃⁻ added to ECF (can't be neutralized without gastric HCl)
  • During active vomiting: filtered bicarbonate exceeds proximal tubule reabsorptive capacity → alkaline urine + K⁺ secretion stimulated
  • After vomiting stops: volume + K⁺ + Cl⁻ depletion → ↑ HCO₃⁻ reabsorption → maintenance of alkalosis
  • Fix: correct ECFV with NaCl + repair K⁺ deficit with KCl

Diuretic-Induced Alkalosis

  • Thiazides and loop diuretics ↑ salt excretion → ↓ ECFV without altering total body bicarbonate → [HCO₃⁻] "concentrates" = contraction alkalosis
  • Maintained by: contraction of ECFV, secondary hyperaldosteronism, K⁺ deficiency, direct diuretic effect
  • Chronic administration: ↑ distal Na⁺ delivery → ↑ H⁺ + K⁺ secretion

Posthypercapnia Alkalosis

  • Chronic CO₂ retention → renal ↑ HCO₃⁻ absorption + new HCO₃⁻ generation (↑ net acid excretion)
  • When PaCO₂ is rapidly corrected → HCO₃⁻ absorption persists due to residual stimulation → metabolic alkalosis

Rebound Alkalosis After Acidosis Treatment

  • When lactic acid/ketoacid stimulus is corrected (shock resolved, insulin given for DKA) → lactate/ketones metabolized → converted to HCO₃⁻ equivalent
  • If exogenous NaHCO₃ was given → additive → rebound alkalosis

Mineralocorticoid Excess Alkalosis

  • Aldosterone upregulates ENaC in collecting tubule → Na⁺ retention + kaliuresis
  • ↑ transepithelial voltage → ↑ H⁺ + K⁺ secretion → metabolic alkalosis + hypokalemia
  • Liddle's syndrome: gain-of-function mutation of ENaC genes → volume expansion → hypertension + hypokalemia + alkalosis; treat with amiloride or triamterene (NOT aldosterone antagonists)

Symptoms of Metabolic Alkalosis

  • CNS changes similar to hypocalcemia: mental confusion, obtundation
  • Predisposition to seizures, paresthesias, muscular cramping, tetany
  • Aggravation of cardiac arrhythmias
  • Hypoxemia in COPD
  • Related electrolyte abnormalities: hypokalemia, hypophosphatemia

Treatment of Metabolic Alkalosis

  • Goal 1: Correct underlying stimulus for HCO₃⁻ generation (stop vomiting, treat aldosteronism)
  • Goal 2: Eliminate maintenance factors — K⁺ deficits, ECFV contraction
  • Saline-responsive: Isotonic saline + KCl
  • Cannot give saline (CHF): Acetazolamide 125–250 mg IV — carbonic anhydrase inhibitor → forces HCO₃⁻ excretion. Monitor closely: triggers urinary K⁺ losses → hypokalemia
  • Extreme cases: Dilute HCl IV (0.1 N HCl) — causes hemolysis, must be central vein, slow; not advised due to harm risk
  • Primary aldosteronism/Cushing's: Treat the underlying cause
  • Liddle's: Amiloride or triamterene (ENaC blockers) — NOT spironolactone
  • All cases: Always repair K⁺ deficits

Respiratory Acid-Base Disorders

Respiratory Acidosis

  • Elevated PaCO₂ + reduced pH from: severe pulmonary disease, respiratory muscle fatigue, or abnormal ventilatory control
  • Acute: HCO₃⁻ ↑ 1 mmol/L per 10 mmHg ↑ PaCO₂ (cellular buffering)
  • Chronic (>24h): HCO₃⁻ ↑ 4 mmol/L per 10 mmHg ↑ PaCO₂ (renal adaptation)
  • Serum HCO₃⁻ does NOT exceed 38 mmol/L in pure respiratory acidosis
Acute Hypercapnia Symptoms
  • Anxiety, dyspnea, confusion, psychosis, hallucinations → coma
Chronic Hypercapnia Symptoms
  • Sleep disorders, daytime somnolence, memory loss, personality changes, tremor, myoclonic jerks, asterixis, papilledema

Causes of Respiratory Acidosis

  • Central: Drugs (anesthetics, morphine, sedatives), stroke, infection
  • Airway: Obstruction, asthma
  • Parenchyma: Emphysema, pneumoconiosis, bronchitis, ARDS, barotrauma
  • Neuromuscular: Poliomyelitis, kyphoscoliosis, myasthenia, muscular dystrophies
  • Miscellaneous: Obesity, hypoventilation, permissive hypercapnia
Treatment: Respiratory Acidosis
  • Acute: life-threatening → reverse underlying cause simultaneously with restoring alveolar ventilation (may need intubation + mechanical ventilation)
  • Oxygen cautiously in COPD — injudicious O₂ → worsening respiratory acidosis → severe acidemia
  • Avoid aggressive/rapid hypercapnia correction → same complications as acute respiratory alkalosis (arrhythmias, ↓ cerebral perfusion, seizures)
  • In chronic respiratory acidosis: lower PaCO₂ gradually to baseline; provide Cl⁻ and K⁺ to enhance renal HCO₃⁻ excretion
  • Permissive hypercapnia in ARDS: NaHCO₃ to raise arterial pH to ~7.20 only; do not normalize

Respiratory Alkalosis

  • Alveolar hyperventilation → ↓ PaCO₂ → ↑ HCO₃⁻/PaCO₂ ratio → ↑ pH
  • Hypocapnia develops when ventilatory stimulus causes CO₂ output to exceed metabolic production
  • Acute: HCO₃⁻ ↓ 0.2 mmol/L per mmHg ↓ PaCO₂
  • Chronic: HCO₃⁻ ↓ 0.4–0.5 mmol/L per mmHg ↓ PaCO₂; full renal adaptation takes days
  • Plasma HCO₃⁻ rarely below 12 mmol/L from pure respiratory alkalosis
  • Chronic respiratory alkalosis is the ONLY disorder where compensation can return pH to normal

Causes of Respiratory Alkalosis

  • CNS stimulation: pain, anxiety, fever, CVA, meningitis/encephalitis, tumor, trauma
  • Hypoxemia/tissue hypoxia: high altitude, pneumonia, pulmonary edema, aspiration, severe anemia
  • Drugs/hormones: salicylates (direct medullary chemoreceptor stimulation), pregnancy/progesterone (↓ PaCO₂ by 5–10 mmHg), theophylline, aminophylline
  • Chest receptors: hemothorax, flail chest, cardiac failure, pulmonary embolism
  • Miscellaneous: septicemia (early — before fever/hypoxemia/hypotension), hepatic failure (severity correlates with hepatic insufficiency), mechanical hyperventilation, heat exposure, recovery from metabolic acidosis

Effects of Respiratory Alkalosis

  • ↓ Cerebral blood flow → dizziness, mental confusion, seizures (even without hypoxemia)
  • Minimal cardiovascular effects in conscious humans; BUT in anesthetized/ventilated patients: ↓ cardiac output + ↓ blood pressure
  • Cardiac arrhythmias in heart disease
  • Left shift of Hgb-O₂ dissociation curve (Bohr effect)
  • Acute: intracellular shifts of Na⁺, K⁺, PO₄²⁻; ↓ free [Ca²⁺] by ↑ protein-bound fraction
  • Chronic respiratory alkalosis: most common acid-base disturbance in critically ill patients; when severe, portends poor prognosis
Treatment: Respiratory Alkalosis
  • Direct treatment toward alleviation of the underlying disorder
  • Ventilator-related: adjust dead space + tidal volume to minimize hypocapnia
  • Hyperventilation syndrome: reassurance, rebreathing from paper bag, address psychological stress. NO antidepressants or sedatives. β-blockers may help peripheral hyperadrenergic manifestations
  • Diagnosis of hyperventilation syndrome is by exclusion — rule out PE, CAD, hyperthyroidism

Exam Traps

Expert Internal Medicine Boards coaching — Stage A. Each trap shows exactly how the question is framed, the seductive wrong answer, and the correct reasoning.

Trap 01Normal pH Does Not Mean No Acid-Base Disorder
A 48-year-old alcoholic with chronic vomiting is admitted. Labs: Na 135, Cl 80, K 2.8, HCO₃⁻ 25, pH 7.40, PaCO₂ 40. He then develops alcoholic ketoacidosis with β-hydroxybutyrate of 15 mmol/L. Repeat ABG: pH 7.40, HCO₃⁻ 25, PaCO₂ 40. Which acid-base disorder is present?
Correct Answer: Mixed metabolic alkalosis + high-AG metabolic acidosis.
The AG is 30 (135 − 80 − 25), well above normal of 10. ΔAG = 20, but ΔHCO₃⁻ = 0 (25 − 25). The 20 mmol/L unaccounted by ΔHCo₃⁻ reveals the hidden concurrent metabolic alkalosis (from prior vomiting).
⚠ Trap: Most students see normal pH/HCO₃⁻/PaCO₂ and say "no disorder." The key is to always check the anion gap — it reveals acid-base disorders invisible to pH/HCO₃⁻. Board keyword: "normal pH + elevated AG"
Trap 02Winter's Equation: Is the Compensation Appropriate?
A 22-year-old with type 1 DM presents with nausea/vomiting. ABG: pH 7.25, HCO₃⁻ 12, PaCO₂ 42. Which mixed disorder is present?
Correct Answer: Metabolic acidosis + respiratory acidosis (mixed).
Winter's equation: expected PaCO₂ = (1.5 × 12) + 8 ± 2 = 26 ± 2 = 24–28 mmHg. Actual PaCO₂ is 42 — far above the expected upper limit of 28. This means respiratory compensation is inadequate → a superimposed respiratory acidosis (e.g., pneumonia, aspiration).
⚠ Trap: Students apply Winter's and stop there. The critical step is comparing the actual PaCO₂ to the predicted range. PaCO₂ >28 with HCO₃⁻ 12 = mixed disorder. Board keyword: "PaCO₂ above predicted upper boundary"
Trap 03Albumin Correction — Hidden High-AG Acidosis in ICU
A septic 65-year-old in the ICU has: Na 140, Cl 105, HCO₃⁻ 21, albumin 2.0 g/dL, AG reported as 14. What is the correct interpretation?
Correct Answer: High-AG metabolic acidosis is present.
Corrected AG = 14 + 2.5 × (4.5 − 2.0) = 14 + 6.25 = 20.25 mmol/L. This is clearly elevated above the normal of 10, revealing a high-AG acidosis masked by the low albumin.
⚠ Trap: Labs report the uncorrected AG. Students see AG = 14 and call it normal. In any critically ill or malnourished patient, always correct the AG for albumin. Board keyword: "albumin correction in hypoalbuminemia"
Trap 04Osmolar Gap — Act Before the Lab Comes Back
A 35-year-old is found confused with antifreeze containers nearby. ABG: pH 7.15, AG 28. Measured osmolality = 340, calculated = 308 (osmolar gap = 32). Ethylene glycol level is pending. What is the most important next step?
Correct Answer: Initiate fomepizole immediately — do NOT wait for ethylene glycol level.
The combination of high AG + elevated osmolar gap in a patient with suspected EG ingestion is sufficient evidence for EG toxicity. Early treatment with fomepizole prevents metabolism of EG to the toxic metabolites glycolate and oxalate.
⚠ Trap: Waiting for EG levels costs precious time. Osmolar gap is elevated early when EG is still in the bloodstream — as it is metabolized, the osmolar gap falls and the AG rises. Delay = irreversible renal/CNS/cardiac damage. Board keyword: "high AG + high osmolar gap = treat empirically"
Trap 05Isopropyl Alcohol: Osmolar Gap WITHOUT High-AG Acidosis
A 28-year-old is brought to the ED after ingesting rubbing alcohol. Labs: pH 7.38, AG 10, osmolar gap = 45, blood glucose 60 mg/dL, ketones positive in serum. What is the most likely ingestion?
Correct Answer: Isopropyl alcohol (NOT ethylene glycol or methanol).
Isopropyl alcohol is metabolized to acetone — which does NOT contribute to high-AG acidosis (acetone is rapidly excreted). The osmolar gap is elevated (from both isopropyl + acetone), but AG is normal. Hypoglycemia is common. The presence of ketones without acidosis is the classic pattern.
⚠ Trap: Students see osmolar gap + ketones and immediately think ethylene glycol or methanol. The key distinguisher is the absence of high-AG acidosis in isopropanol. Board keyword: "elevated osmolar gap + normal AG + ketones = isopropanol"
Trap 06NaHCO₃ in Lactic Acidosis — When It Makes Things Worse
A 55-year-old with septic shock has pH 6.95, HCO₃⁻ 8, lactic acid 12 mmol/L. A colleague proposes aggressive NaHCO₃ to normalize the pH and HCO₃⁻. Why is this inappropriate?
Correct Answer: Attempts to normalize pH with NaHCO₃ are deleterious in lactic acidosis.
NaHCO₃ can: (1) paradoxically depress cardiac performance, (2) stimulate phosphofructokinase → ↑ lactate production (HCO₃⁻ is a substrate), (3) cause fluid overload, hypercapnia, and hypertension. Reasonable target is pH ≤ 7.20 or HCO₃⁻ ≤ 12, not normalization.
⚠ Trap: Instinct is to normalize values. In lactic acidosis, normalization is harmful. If the underlying cause resolves, blood lactate converts to HCO₃⁻ → rebound alkalosis if excess NaHCO₃ was given. Board keyword: "do not normalize pH in lactic acidosis"
Trap 07Urine pH in Diarrhea vs. Distal RTA — The Opposite of What You Expect
A 30-year-old with chronic diarrhea has pH 7.26, HCO₃⁻ 15, AG 10, urine pH 7.2. Another patient with distal RTA (type 1) has identical blood values and urine pH 7.4. How do you differentiate these two patients?
Correct Answer: Calculate the urine anion gap (UAG).
In diarrhea: high urine NH₄⁺ excretion → UAG is NEGATIVE (Cl⁻u > Na⁺u + K⁺u) → kidneys responding appropriately. In distal RTA: kidney cannot excrete NH₄⁺ → UAG is POSITIVE. Both can have urine pH >6 due to NH₄⁺ buffering in diarrhea.
⚠ Trap: Students see alkaline urine in acidosis and immediately say "RTA." But in diarrhea, the urine is also alkaline because NH₄⁺ buffers the urine. The UAG is the discriminator. Board keyword: "negative UAG = extrarenal acidosis"
Trap 08Euglycemic DKA — SGLT2 Inhibitors
A 45-year-old type 2 diabetic on empagliflozin presents with nausea and a high-AG acidosis. Glucose is 145 mg/dL. Blood ketones are elevated. What is the diagnosis and why is glucose misleading?
Correct Answer: Euglycemic DKA (eDKA) due to SGLT2 inhibitor use.
SGLT2 inhibitors cause continuous glycosuria → osmotic diuresis → volume depletion → ↓ plasma glucose despite active ketogenesis. The accumulation of ketoacids (acetoacetate + β-hydroxybutyrate) accounts for the elevated AG, but glucose is in the normal range — hence "euglycemic." Normal glucose does NOT rule out DKA in SGLT2 inhibitor users.
⚠ Trap: Boards will show a "DKA" patient with a blood glucose of 140–160 mg/dL. Students rule out DKA because glucose isn't high. The key is the medication history and recognizing the mechanism. Board keyword: "SGLT2 inhibitor + high-AG acidosis = euglycemic DKA"
Trap 09Liddle's Syndrome — Spironolactone Does NOT Work
A 25-year-old presents with hypertension, hypokalemia, and metabolic alkalosis. Plasma renin is very low, aldosterone is low. Spironolactone fails to correct the hypertension or hypokalemia. What is the diagnosis and correct treatment?
Correct Answer: Liddle's syndrome. Treat with amiloride or triamterene.
Liddle's is caused by a gain-of-function mutation of the collecting duct Na⁺ channel (ENaC), leading to ECFV expansion → secondarily suppressed renin AND aldosterone. Since ENaC is constitutively active, blocking aldosterone (spironolactone) has no effect. Only direct ENaC blockers (amiloride, triamterene) are effective.
⚠ Trap: Low renin + low aldosterone → students may not think of hyperaldosteronism. The triad of hypertension + hypokalemia + alkalosis + low aldosterone + failure of spironolactone = Liddle's. Board keyword: "spironolactone failure in alkalosis = Liddle's = use amiloride"
Trap 10Respiratory Alkalosis in Salicylate Toxicity — The Mixed Picture
A 17-year-old is found obtunded with empty aspirin bottles. ABG: pH 7.52, PaCO₂ 20, HCO₃⁻ 16. What is the acid-base disorder and what is the correct treatment sequence?
Correct Answer: Mixed respiratory alkalosis + high-AG metabolic acidosis.
Salicylates directly stimulate medullary chemoreceptors → respiratory alkalosis (↓ PaCO₂). Simultaneously, they cause high-AG metabolic acidosis (salicylate accumulation + ↑ lactic acid). Treatment: gastric lavage + activated charcoal (1 g/kg up to 50 g); IV NaHCO₃ to alkalinize urine to pH >7.5 to trap ionized salicylate and increase clearance 5-fold; careful monitoring of K⁺ (hypokalemia risk); hemodialysis if AKI.
⚠ Trap: Students see alkaline pH and think metabolic alkalosis from the vomiting. In adults, salicylates classically produce respiratory alkalosis OR mixed resp alkalosis + metabolic acidosis. Urine alkalinization (not blood alkalinization) is the goal. Board keyword: "salicylates = respiratory alkalosis + metabolic acidosis"
