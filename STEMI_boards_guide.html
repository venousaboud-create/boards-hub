Epidemiologic Shift

There has been a shift in the pattern of AMI over recent decades, with more patients now presenting with NSTEMI than STEMI. This reflects improved primary prevention (aspirin, statins, smoking cessation) reducing the incidence of complete occlusive thrombosis, alongside the rising burden of metabolic disease.

🔑 Chain of Survival Concept
Management of STEMI involves a highly integrated system — starting with prehospital care and extending to early hospital management — with the goal of expeditious reperfusion. The two major classes of complications driving mortality are: (1) electrical complications (arrhythmias) and (2) mechanical complications ("pump failure").
02

Pathophysiology

Core Mechanism

STEMI usually occurs when coronary blood flow decreases abruptly after a thrombotic occlusion of an atherosclerotic coronary artery. Slowly developing high-grade stenoses do NOT typically precipitate STEMI because of the development of a rich collateral network over time. Instead, STEMI requires a rapidly forming thrombus at a site of vascular injury.

Triggers facilitating vascular injury: cigarette smoking, hypertension, lipid accumulation, and inflammation.

Plaque Disruption → Thrombus Cascade

  • Plaque erosion or rupture — exposes lipid core contents to flowing blood; rupture-prone plaques have a rich lipid core and thin fibrous cap. Less than 5% of thin-capped fibroatheromas actually cause AMI during long-term follow-up.
  • Other rupture-prone morphology — expansive remodeling, neovascularization, plaque hemorrhage, adventitial inflammation, "spotty" calcification pattern
  • Platelet monolayer forms — initial layer at disruption site
  • Platelet activation — agonists (collagen, ADP, epinephrine, serotonin) → thromboxane A₂ release (potent vasoconstrictor) → further platelet activation → potential fibrinolysis resistance
  • GPIIb/IIIa conformational change — platelet receptor gains high affinity for fibrinogen → fibrinogen cross-links two platelets simultaneously → aggregation
  • Coagulation cascade — tissue factor exposure in damaged endothelium → factors VII and X activated → prothrombin → thrombin → fibrinogen → fibrin strands
  • Complete occlusion — thrombus containing platelet aggregates and fibrin strands occludes the epicardial coronary artery → STEMI
    • 💜 Thrombin Autoamplification
    Fluid-phase and clot-bound thrombin participate in an autoamplification reaction — leading to further activation of the coagulation cascade. This is why anticoagulation is so critical in STEMI management.

    Rare Causes of STEMI (Non-Atherothrombotic)

    Coronary emboliCongenital coronary abnormalities Coronary spasmSpontaneous coronary artery dissection (SCAD) HypercoagulabilityCollagen vascular disease Cocaine abuseIntracardiac thrombi/masses

    Determinants of Infarct Size

    The amount of myocardial damage depends on seven factors:

  • Territory supplied by the affected vessel
  • Total vs partial occlusion of the vessel
  • Duration of coronary occlusion
  • Collateral vessels supplying affected tissue
  • O₂ demand of the suddenly ischemic myocardium
  • Spontaneous fibrinolysis capacity (endogenous)
  • Adequacy of reperfusion in the infarct zone when flow is restored
    • 🔑 Ischemic Penumbra
    While the central zone of the infarct contains irretrievably lost necrotic tissue, the surrounding ischemic penumbra can be salvaged by: timely reperfusion, reduced O₂ demands, preventing noxious metabolite accumulation, and blunting reperfusion injury mediators (calcium overload, free radicals). Up to one-third of STEMI patients achieve spontaneous reperfusion within 24h.

    Temporal Stages of STEMI Healing

    Acute

    First few hours – 7 days. PMN leukocyte recruitment → early damage clearance

    Healing

    7–28 days. Pro-inflammatory monocytes (CCR2) → reparative monocytes (F4/80) → angiogenesis, extracellular matrix

    Healed

    ≥29 days. Monocyte-derived fibroblasts → interstitial collagen → myocardial scar formation and ventricular remodeling

    Avoid in STEMI — Infarct Healing Disruptors

    🚨 Avoid Glucocorticoids and NSAIDs (except aspirin)
    These agents impair infarct healing, increase risk of myocardial rupture, may result in a larger infarct scar, and can increase coronary vascular resistance (reducing flow to ischemic myocardium). They are contraindicated in STEMI patients.

    Clinical Presentation

    Precipitating Factors & Timing

    • Precipitating factor in up to 50% of cases: vigorous exercise, emotional stress, medical or surgical illness
    • Can occur any time; circadian pattern: clusters in the morning within a few hours of awakening

    Classic Pain — The Defining Feature

    Pain is the most common presenting complaint. Characteristics:

    • Quality: Deep and visceral — heavy, squeezing, crushing (occasionally stabbing or burning)
    • Location: Central chest and/or epigastrium
    • Radiation: Arms (common); abdomen, back, lower jaw, neck (less common); up to occipital area
    • NOT below the umbilicus — important boundary
    • Accompaniments: Sweating, nausea, vomiting, anxiety, sense of impending doom
    • Persistence: Does NOT subside with cessation of activity (unlike angina pectoris)
    ⚠ Epigastric Location = Frequent Misdiagnosis
    The frequent location of pain beneath the xiphoid and epigastrium + patient denial = commonly mistaken for indigestion. This is a classic boards scenario for missed STEMI.

    Atypical Presentations — High-Risk Groups

    Pain is not uniformly present in STEMI patients. The proportion of painless STEMIs is greater in:

    Painless STEMI More Common In:
    • Patients with diabetes mellitus
    • Elderly patients (increases with age)
    Other Atypical Presentations:
    • Sudden-onset breathlessness
    • Sudden loss of consciousness
    • Confusional state
    • Sensation of profound weakness
    • Appearance of an arrhythmia
    • Evidence of peripheral embolism
    • Unexplained drop in arterial pressure

    Differential Diagnosis — Must Exclude

    Aortic Dissection

    Tearing/ripping pain to back; pulse differential; widened mediastinum

    Pulmonary Embolism

    Pleuritic pain, dyspnea, hypoxia, tachycardia; right heart strain on ECG

    Pericarditis

    Pleuritic, positional; friction rub; trapezius radiation = key differentiator from STEMI; diffuse ST elevation, PR depression

    🔑 Trapezius Sign — Board Favorite
    Radiation of discomfort to the trapezius muscle is NOT seen in STEMI patients — it is a distinguishing feature that suggests pericarditis. If trapezius pain → think pericarditis, not STEMI.

    Physical Examination Findings

    Anterior Infarction
    • Sympathetic hyperactivity
    • Tachycardia
    • Hypertension
    Inferior Infarction
    • Parasympathetic hyperactivity
    • Bradycardia
    • Hypotension
    🔑 High-Yield Exam Finding
    Substernal chest pain lasting >30 minutes + diaphoresis strongly suggests STEMI. Additional signs of LV dysfunction: S3/S4 gallop, decreased S1, paradoxical S2 splitting, transient mitral regurgitation murmur, basilar rales. Pericardial friction rub may appear in transmural STEMI. Temperature up to 38°C in first week is normal.

    Diagnosis, ECG & Laboratory Findings

    ECG — ST Elevation Criteria (Memorize These)

    New ST-elevation at the J-point in 2 contiguous leads:

    LeadsPopulationRequired Elevation
    All leads except V₂–V₃All patients≥0.1 mV (1 mm)
    V₂–V₃Men ≥40 years≥0.2 mV (2 mm)
    V₂–V₃Men <40 years≥0.25 mV (2.5 mm)
    V₂–V₃Women (any age)≥0.15 mV (1.5 mm) — lower threshold!
    🚨 Women Have a Lower ST Elevation Threshold in V₂–V₃
    Women require only ≥1.5 mm of ST elevation in V₂–V₃ for diagnosis — lower than the male threshold of 2 mm. Missing this leads to under-diagnosis of STEMI in women.

    Most patients with ST elevation ultimately evolve Q waves. Q wave development is more dependent on the volume of infarcted tissue than on transmurality. In absence of ST elevation, fibrinolysis is not helpful and may be harmful.

    Cardiac Biomarkers

    Cardiac Troponins (Preferred)
    • cTnT and cTnI are preferred biomarkers — cardiac-specific amino acid sequences
    • Criteria for AMI: rise and/or fall with ≥1 value above 99th percentile
    • In STEMI: rise to 20–50 times the upper reference limit
    • Remain elevated for 7–10 days after STEMI
    • High-sensitivity assays are of less immediate value in STEMI (reperfusion decision made on clinical + ECG, before results available)
    • Earlier troponin peak → suggests successful reperfusion (rapid washout from interstitium)
    CK-MB (Historical Use)
    • No longer preferred; troponin is superior
    • CK-MB/CK ratio ≥2.5 suggests myocardial (not skeletal muscle) source
    • Useful for early reinfarction detection — declines faster than troponin after STEMI, so a new rise is identifiable
    • Not cost-effective to measure both CK-MB and troponin routinely

    MI Classification — The Five Types (Table 286-1)

    TypeDefinitionKey Feature
    Type 1Spontaneous MI — atherothromboticPlaque rupture or erosion with thrombosis
    Type 2Supply/demand imbalance (non-thrombotic)Spasm, anemia, tachycardia, hypertension — no plaque disruption
    Type 3Cardiac death before biomarker results availableSymptoms + presumed new ischemic ECG changes → death
    Type 4aPCI-related MIcTn >5× 99th percentile within 48h of PCI
    Type 4b/4cStent thrombosis / restenosisMeet Type 1 criteria
    Type 5CABG-related MIcTn >10× 99th percentile within 48h of CABG

    Cardiac Imaging

    • 2D Echocardiography: Wall motion abnormalities almost universally present — useful screening tool; cannot distinguish acute from chronic infarct
    • Echo identifies: RV infarction, ventricular aneurysm, pericardial effusion, LV thrombus
    • Doppler: VSD (color flow), mitral regurgitation
    • Cardiac MRI: High-resolution infarct imaging with gadolinium — bright signal in infarct zone (gadolinium percolates into intercellular region) vs dark normal myocardium
    • Radionuclide: rarely used; cannot distinguish acute from chronic

    Nonspecific Inflammatory Markers

    • WBC: Rises within hours of pain onset → peaks at 12,000–15,000/μL, persists 3–7 days
    • ESR: Rises more slowly, peaks during first week, may remain elevated 1–2 weeks

    Reperfusion Strategy

    The Overarching Goal

    The overarching goal is to minimize time from first medical contact (FMC) to initiation of reperfusion. The primary tool for triage is the initial 12-lead ECG — ST elevation meeting criteria = candidate for reperfusion.

    🚨 Fibrinolysis WITHOUT ST Elevation = Harmful
    In the absence of ST-segment elevation, fibrinolysis is NOT helpful, and evidence exists suggesting it may be harmful. Never give fibrinolytics for NSTE-ACS.

    Primary PCI — Preferred Strategy

    PCI (usually stenting without preceding fibrinolysis) is the preferred reperfusion strategy when it can be performed in a timely manner. More effective than fibrinolysis at opening occluded arteries; better short-term and long-term outcomes when performed by experienced teams at dedicated centers.

    🔑 When to Prefer Primary PCI Over Fibrinolysis
    Primary PCI is preferred unless FMC-device time anticipated >120 min. Also preferred when: diagnosis is in doubt, cardiogenic shock is present, high bleeding risk, or symptoms have been present >2–3 hours (clot less easily lysed by fibrinolytics).

    Multivessel Disease at Primary PCI

    STEMI WITHOUT Cardiogenic Shock

    PCI of nonculprit vessels — either during index procedure OR within 45 days — results in lower CV events and lower need for ischemia-driven revascularization. (PRAMI, CvLPRIT, COMPLETE trials)

    STEMI WITH Cardiogenic Shock

    Routine PCI of nonculprit arteries during initial primary PCI is CONTRAINDICATED — associated with increased rate of death or severe renal failure compared with culprit-only PCI.

    Fibrinolytic Therapy — When PCI is Not Available

    All four approved agents promote plasminogen → plasmin → lyses fibrin thrombi. TIMI grade 3 coronary flow is the goal (full perfusion with normal flow).

    AgentDose / RegimenNotes
    tPA (alteplase)15 mg bolus + 50 mg over 30 min + 35 mg over 60 minFibrin-specific; slightly higher intracranial hemorrhage than streptokinase
    TNK (tenecteplase)0.53 mg/kg single IV bolus over 10 secondsBolus fibrinolytic; weight-based; easy to administer
    rPA (reteplase)10 MU bolus × 2, given 30 min apartDouble-bolus; no prolonged infusion needed
    Streptokinase1.5 million units IV over 1 hourNon-fibrin-specific; cheaper; 2% allergic reactions; don't repeat within 5 days–2 years
    🚨 Absolute Contraindications to Fibrinolysis — Must Know All
    • Prior cerebrovascular hemorrhage at any time
    • Nonhemorrhagic stroke or CVE within the past year
    • Marked hypertension: SBP >180 mmHg and/or DBP >110 mmHg at any time during presentation
    • Suspicion of aortic dissection
    • Active internal bleeding (excluding menses)
    Relative Contraindications to Fibrinolysis
    • Current use of anticoagulants (INR ≥2)
    • Recent invasive procedure or trauma (<2 weeks)
    • Prolonged (>10 min) CPR
    • Known bleeding diathesis
    • Pregnancy
    • Hemorrhagic ophthalmic condition (e.g., hemorrhagic diabetic retinopathy)
    • Active peptic ulcer disease
    • History of severe hypertension now adequately controlled
    ⚠ Age and Fibrinolysis
    Advanced age increases hemorrhagic complications but the benefit of fibrinolysis in elderly still justifies use IF: no other contraindications, substantial myocardium in jeopardy, AND timely primary PCI is not available.
    🔑 Post-Fibrinolysis Management
    • Failure of reperfusion (persistent pain + ST elevation >90 min) → rescue PCI
    • Reocclusion (re-elevation of ST + recurrent pain) → urgent transfer for PCI
    • Successful fibrinolysis → transfer to PCI center, angiography 3–24h later
    • Angiography NOT in first 2–3h after fibrinolytic therapy

    TIMI Flow Grading System

    Grade 0

    Complete occlusion of infarct-related artery — no flow

    Grade 1

    Some contrast penetrates beyond obstruction — but no perfusion of distal bed

    Grade 2

    Full vessel into distal bed — but flow is delayed vs normal artery

    Grade 3

    Full perfusion with NORMAL flow — the goal of reperfusion therapy

    Emergency Department — Control of Discomfort

    Aspirin — First and Essential

    Rapid inhibition of COX-1 → reduced thromboxane A₂. 160–325 mg chewed in the ED (buccal absorption). Followed by daily oral 75–162 mg. Essential across the entire spectrum of ACS.

    Sublingual Nitroglycerin
    • 0.4 mg SL, up to 3 doses, 5 min apart for persistent ischemic symptoms
    • Mechanism: venodilation (↓preload = ↓O₂ demand) + coronary/collateral dilation (↑supply)
    • If good initial response followed by return of pain + ongoing ischemia → consider IV nitroglycerin
    🚨 Nitrate Contraindications in STEMI
    • SBP <90 mmHg
    • Suspected RV infarction: inferior ECG + elevated JVP + clear lungs + hypotension
    • PDE-5 inhibitor within 24h (sildenafil, vardenafil) — profound hypotension
    Idiosyncratic sudden marked hypotension from nitrates → treat with rapid IV atropine.
    Morphine — Analgesic
    • 2–4 mg IV, every 5 minutes as needed
    • May reduce sympathetically mediated vasoconstriction → venous pooling → ↓CO and BP → elevate legs; if severe, volume expansion with IV saline
    • Vagotonic effect: bradycardia or heart block especially with inferior infarction → treat with atropine 0.5 mg IV
    • Slows GI absorption of oral antiplatelet therapy (but no proven increase in adverse outcomes)
    Beta Blockers — IV then Oral
    • IV metoprolol: 5 mg q2–5 min × 3 doses — IF: HR >60, SBP >100, PR <0.24s, no acute HF signs
    • IV beta blockers reduce: pain, infarct size, VF risk, reinfarction risk
    • IV no longer routinely recommended (risk of precipitating HF)
    • Oral in first 24h: start if no acute HF, no low-output state, no cardiogenic shock risk, no contraindications
    • Oral regimen: 50 mg q6h × 48h → then 100 mg q12h
    • Contraindicated in STEMI if: acute HF, low-output, cardiogenic shock risk, bradycardia, PR >0.24s, 2nd/3rd AV block, active asthma, reactive airway disease
    ⚠ Calcium Antagonists in Acute STEMI
    Calcium antagonists are of little value in the acute STEMI setting. Short-acting dihydropyridines (e.g., nifedipine) may be associated with increased mortality risk. Routine use is not recommended.

    Hospital Pharmacotherapy

    Antithrombotic Agents

    Primary goal: maintain patency of infarct-related artery in conjunction with reperfusion. Secondary goal: reduce mural thrombus and deep venous thrombosis.

    Aspirin — Mortality Reduction

    The Antiplatelet Trialists' Collaboration (nearly 20,000 MI patients, 15 trials): antiplatelet therapy (mainly aspirin) showed a 27% relative mortality reduction — from 14.2% (control) to 10.4% (treated).

    P2Y₁₂ Inhibitors
    • Clopidogrel: reduces death/reinfarction/stroke; in fibrinolysis patients, prevents reocclusion of reperfused artery
    • Prasugrel and Ticagrelor: more effective than clopidogrel in preventing ischemic complications in STEMI patients undergoing PCI; both increase bleeding
    • Glycoprotein IIb/IIIa inhibitors: reserved for thrombotic complications during PCI
    Anticoagulants — Parenteral Options
    AgentDoseKey Points
    UFH (standard)60 U/kg bolus (max 4,000 U) + 12 U/kg/h (max 1,000 U/h); aPTT 1.5–2× controlRecommended for primary PCI; combined with aspirin + streptokinase saves ~5 lives per 1,000
    Enoxaparin (LMWH)SC; dose per weightReduces death/reinfarction vs UFH in fibrinolysis patients; higher serious bleeding but net benefit favors enoxaparin
    Fondaparinux2.5 mg SC dailySuperior to placebo if no reperfusion; uncertain vs UFH; requires supplemental UFH for PCI (catheter thrombosis risk)
    BivalirudinDirect thrombin inhibitorAlternative to UFH for primary PCI; less bleeding (access site hematomas, transfusions)
    🔑 Mural Thrombus Anticoagulation
    Patients at high risk for systemic/pulmonary thromboembolism (anterior infarction, severe LV dysfunction, HF, history of embolism, echo-confirmed mural thrombus):
    • Full therapeutic anticoagulation (LMWH or UFH) while hospitalized
    • Followed by at least 3 months of oral anticoagulant therapy

    Beta-Adrenoceptor Blockers — Long-Term Benefits

    • Oral beta blocker therapy after STEMI is useful for most patients
    • Continue for at least 1 year (well-conducted, placebo-controlled trials)
    • Contraindicated in: HF or severely compromised LV function, heart block, orthostatic hypotension, history of asthma
    • Possibly not needed in excellent prognosis (<1% annual mortality, <55 years, no prior MI, normal LV function, no complex VEA, no angina)

    ACE Inhibitors — RAAS Inhibition

    ACE Inhibitors
    • Reduce mortality after STEMI; additive to aspirin + beta blockers
    • Maximum benefit: high-risk patients (anterior infarction, prior MI, globally depressed LV)
    • Short-term benefit even unselectively in hemodynamically stable patients (SBP >100 mmHg)
    • Mechanism: reduction in ventricular remodeling after infarction
    • Continue indefinitely: clinically evident HF, reduced global LV function, large regional wall motion abnormality, hypertension
    • May also reduce rate of recurrent infarction
    ARBs, Mineralocorticoid Antagonists, ARNI
    • ARBs: For ACE inhibitor-intolerant patients with clinical or radiologic signs of HF
    • Mineralocorticoid antagonists (spironolactone, eplerenone): STEMI + already on ACE inhibitor + LVEF ≤40% + symptomatic HF OR diabetes — do NOT use if creatinine ≥2.5 (men) or ≥2.0 (women) mg/dL, or K⁺ ≥5.0 mEq/L
    • Sacubitril/valsartan (ARNI): NOT more effective than ACE inhibitor in preventing incident HF post-MI (despite superiority in established HFrEF)

    Other Agents

    • IV nitroglycerin: Large RCT showed no benefit for major outcomes in STEMI. May be considered in: persistent hypertension, HF, or ongoing ischemia
    • Calcium antagonists: Multiple trials failed to establish a role in STEMI. Routine use NOT recommended
    • Blood glucose control in diabetic STEMI patients: reduces mortality rate
    • Serum magnesium: Measure on admission; correct deficits to minimize arrhythmia risk; Mg goal ~2.0 mmol/L; K⁺ goal ~4.5 mmol/L
    • Colchicine (0.5 mg once daily): consider for secondary prevention, especially if recurrent atherosclerotic event despite guideline-directed medical therapy

    Complications & Their Management

    Killip Classification — Hemodynamic Staging

    ClassDefinitionHistorical MortalityKey Feature
    Class INo signs of pulmonary or venous congestion0–5%Normal
    Class IIModerate HF — rales at lung bases, S₃, tachypnea, right HF signs10–20%Diuresis often effective
    Class IIISevere HF — pulmonary edema35–45%Aggressive diuresis + vasodilators
    Class IVCardiogenic shock — SBP <90, peripheral vasoconstriction, cyanosis, confusion, oliguria85–95%Mortality plateaued for 25 years
    🔑 Key Hemodynamic Thresholds
    • LV dysfunction apparent when 20–25% of LV contraction impaired
    • Cardiogenic shock when infarction ≥40% of LV
    • Cardiogenic shock incidence reduced from 20% to ~7% with modern reperfusion
    • Of those with cardiogenic shock: only 10% present with it; 90% develop it during hospitalization
    • High LV filling (>22 mmHg) + normal CO → diuresis
    • Low LV filling (<15 mmHg) + low CO → volume expansion

    Mechanical Complications — The "Surgical Three"

    ComplicationIncidenceClassic MurmurKey Differentiator
    Ventricular Septal Rupture (VSR)0.2–3% (without reperfusion)Harsh holosystolic murmur + thrill; S₃; accentuated S₂Right heart cath: O₂ step-up RA→RV; large v waves; pulmonary edema + RV+LV failure
    Free Wall Rupture~0.3–1%No specific murmur (often electromechanical dissociation)JVD (29%), pulsus paradoxus (47%), pericardial effusion on echo; signs of tamponade; sudden death
    Papillary Muscle Rupture~0.1–1%Soft murmur, NO thrillNo O₂ step-up RA→RV; very high PCWP; severe pulmonary edema; posteromedial > anterolateral papillary muscle
    🚨 All Three Have Bimodal Timing
    Peak incidence within 24 hours AND 3–5 days after STEMI; range 1–14 days. This timing pattern is classic for board questions.

    Right Ventricular Infarction

    • ~1/3 of inferior infarction patients have at least minor RV involvement
    • Classic triad: JVD + clear lungs + hypotension (without pulmonary congestion)
    • Kussmaul's sign and hepatomegaly may be present
    • ECG: ST elevation in right-sided leads, especially V₄R — present in first 24h
    • Treatment: Volume expansion to maintain RV preload; improve LV performance
    🚨 RV Infarction + Nitrates = Dangerous
    Nitrates should be AVOIDED in RV infarction — these patients depend on adequate preload to maintain cardiac output. Nitrates cause venodilation → ↓preload → precipitous cardiovascular collapse. Same applies to diuretics.
    ⚠ RV Infarction + Ventricular Pacing
    RV infarction patients often respond poorly to ventricular pacing alone because of loss of atrial contribution to ventricular filling. These patients may require dual-chamber AV sequential pacing.

    Ventricular Remodeling

    After STEMI, the LV undergoes changes in shape, size, and thickness — referred to as ventricular remodeling — that generally precede clinically evident HF in the months to years after infarction. Greater dilation follows infarction of the anterior wall and apex → more marked hemodynamic impairment, more frequent HF, poorer prognosis. ACE inhibitors/ARBs ameliorate progressive dilation.

    🔑 ACE Inhibitor for LVEF <40%
    In patients with LVEF <40%, regardless of whether HF is present, prescribe ACE inhibitors (or ARBs) AND eventually beta blockers to prevent progressive remodeling and improve outcomes.

    Other Complications

    Pericarditis

    Friction rub and/or pericardial pain frequent in transmural STEMI. Treat with aspirin 650 mg four times daily.

    🚨 Avoid Anticoagulants in Pericarditis
    Anticoagulants can cause tamponade in the presence of pericarditis — avoid unless compelling indication. The trapezius radiation helps distinguish from recurrent ischemia.
    Thromboembolism
    • Clinically apparent in ~10% of cases; 20% at autopsy
    • Contributing cause of death in 25% of post-admission STEMI deaths
    • LV thrombus: ~1/3 of anterior wall MIs (rare in inferior/posterior)
    • Anticoagulation for confirmed thrombus → 3–6 months oral anticoagulation
    LV Aneurysm
    • True aneurysm = dyskinesis/paradoxical wall motion; composed of scar tissue → does NOT predispose to cardiac rupture
    • Complications: HF, arterial embolism, ventricular arrhythmias
    • Apical aneurysms most common; detected by echo
    • Physical exam: double, diffuse, or displaced apical impulse
    • Pseudoaneurysm: myocardial rupture contained by pericardium + thrombus; maintains communication with LV through narrow neck; often ruptures spontaneously → surgically repair if recognized

    Arrhythmias After STEMI

    Overview

    The incidence of arrhythmias after STEMI is highest early in the illness. Mechanisms include autonomic imbalance, electrolyte disturbances, ischemia, and slowed conduction in ischemic zones. Most VF deaths occur in the first 24h, with over half in the first hour.

    🔑 Electrolyte Targets in STEMI
    Potassium goal: ~4.5 mmol/L — both hypokalemia AND hypomagnesemia are risk factors for VF.
    Magnesium goal: ~2.0 mmol/L — measure on admission, correct all deficits.

    Ventricular Arrhythmias

    Ventricular Premature Beats (VPBs)
    • Infrequent, sporadic VPBs occur in almost all STEMI patients — do not require treatment
    • Prophylactic antiarrhythmic therapy (lidocaine IV or oral agents) for VPBs is CONTRAINDICATED — may increase mortality rate
    • Beta blockers should be used routinely (without contraindications) — effective against ectopic activity and VF prevention
    Sustained VT (Hemodynamically Stable)
    • Amiodarone IV: 150 mg bolus over 10 min → 1.0 mg/min × 6h → 0.5 mg/min
    • Alternative — Procainamide: 15 mg/kg over 20–30 min; infusion 1–4 mg/min
    • If VT does not stop promptly → electrical cardioversion
    VF or VT with Hemodynamic Deterioration
    • Unsynchronized 200 J biphasic defibrillation immediately
    • VT/VF refractory to electroshock → amiodarone 150 mg bolus, then repeat shock
    AIVR — Accelerated Idioventricular Rhythm
    • Rate 60–100 bpm; often occurs transiently during fibrinolysis at time of reperfusion — a reperfusion rhythm
    • Benign — does not presage development of classic VT
    • Most episodes require no treatment — careful monitoring is sufficient
    🔑 Primary vs Secondary VF — Critical Distinction
    • Primary VF (first 48h, no predisposing factors like HF/shock/bundle branch block/aneurysm): In-hospital mortality increased, but long-term prognosis excellent after hospital discharge
    • Secondary VF (due to severe pump failure): Poor prognosis
    • Late VF (after first 48h): Increased both in-hospital AND long-term mortality → consider ICD implantation

    ICD Implantation — Post-STEMI Algorithm

    ⚠ Timing Is Critical — Wait Before Reassessing LVEF
    ICD implantation should generally wait — reassess LVEF at >40 days after MI and/or >90 days after revascularization before implanting for primary prevention. The available evidence does not suggest a survival advantage to early ICD placement post-MI.
    Clinical ScenarioICD Recommendation
    NYHA Class I + LVEF ≤30%ICD — Class I
    NYHA Class II or III + LVEF ≤35%ICD — Class I
    LVEF ≤40% + NSVT + inducible sustained VT on EP studyICD — Class IIa
    NYHA Class IV candidate for advanced HF therapyICD — Class IIa
    MI <40 days or revascularization <90 daysGDMT first; WCD option (Class IIb); reassess LVEF at appropriate time

    💜 Wearable Cardioverter-Defibrillator (WCD)
    An option while waiting for LVEF reassessment. However, WCD is associated with frequent alarms, skin irritation, and emotional distress — resulting in reduced wear time in many patients.

    Bradyarrhythmias and Conduction Disturbances

    Inferior MI — AV Block
    • Commonly due to increased vagal tone and/or adenosine release
    • Therefore transient and usually reversible
    • Better prognosis
    • Treat with atropine if hemodynamic compromise
    Anterior MI — AV Block
    • Due to ischemic malfunction of conduction system
    • Associated with extensive myocardial necrosis
    • Worse prognosis — pacing may have limited impact
    • In-hospital AND post-discharge mortality markedly higher
    🔑 Sinus Bradycardia Treatment
    • Atropine 1.0 mg IV initially (up to total 3.0 mg)
    • Persistent bradycardia <40 bpm despite atropine → electrical pacing
    • External noninvasive pacing in "demand" mode for: sinus brady <50 unresponsive to drugs, Mobitz II, 3rd degree AV block, bilateral BBB

    Supraventricular Arrhythmias

    • Sinus tachycardia: Most common supraventricular arrhythmia. Treat underlying cause first (anemia, fever, HF, metabolic). If due to sympathetic overstimulation → beta blocker
    • AF/flutter: Often secondary to LV failure. Rate control options:
      • HFrEF present → digoxin
      • No LV systolic dysfunction → beta blockers, verapamil, or diltiazem
      • HR >120 with HF/shock/ischemia → synchronized cardioversion
    09

    Secondary Prevention

    Long-Term Drug Therapy

    Antiplatelet Therapy
    • Long-term aspirin: 25% risk reduction in recurrent MI/stroke/CV death (36 fewer events per 1,000 patients)
    • DAPT (aspirin + P2Y₁₂: clopidogrel, prasugrel, or ticagrelor): 12 months after STEMI — absence of high bleeding risk or bleeding complications
    ACE Inhibitor / ARB + Aldosterone Antagonist
    • Indefinitely in: clinically evident HF, reduced LVEF, large regional wall motion abnormality
    • Prevents late ventricular remodeling and recurrent ischemic events
    • Aldosterone antagonist: LVEF ≤40% + symptomatic HF or DM (with adequate renal function and K⁺)
    Beta Blockers
    • Chronic routine use for at least 1 year after STEMI supported by well-conducted placebo-controlled trials
    Anti-Inflammatory (Colchicine)
    • Low-dose colchicine 0.5 mg once daily
    • May be considered as secondary prevention — especially if recurrent atherosclerotic vascular event despite guideline-directed medical therapy

    Post-Infarction Risk Stratification

    Important risk factors for increased cardiovascular risk after STEMI recovery:

    High-Risk Features (Most Important)
    • Persistent ischemia (spontaneous or provoked)
    • Depressed LVEF (<40%)
    • Rales above lung bases / congestion on imaging
    • Symptomatic ventricular arrhythmias
    Additional Risk Features
    • Prior MI, age >75, diabetes mellitus
    • Prolonged sinus tachycardia, hypotension
    • Silent ischemia on ECG at rest
    • Abnormal signal-averaged ECG
    • Non-patency of infarct-related artery
    • Persistent advanced heart block or new intraventricular conduction abnormality

    Activity & Rehabilitation

    • Uncomplicated STEMI hospitalization: 3–5 days
    • Bed rest first 6–12h; then dangling feet and sitting in chair within 24h
    • First 1–2 weeks at home: walking and light activity; normal sexual activity may resume
    • After 2 weeks: regulate activity based on exercise tolerance
    • Most patients return to work within 2–4 weeks
    • Cardiac rehabilitation program with progressive exercise — initiated in hospital, continued post-discharge; includes educational component and individualized exercise prescription

    Exam Traps

    Expert board exam coaching — Stage A Internal Medicine. Each trap shows the clinical vignette, the seductive wrong answer, and the board-correct reasoning.

    Trap 01Women and the Lower ST Elevation Threshold
    A 58-year-old woman presents with 45 minutes of crushing substernal chest pressure and diaphoresis. The ECG shows 1.3 mm ST elevation in V₂ and 1.4 mm in V₃. The emergency physician says the ECG does not meet STEMI criteria. Is he correct?
    Correct Answer: No — this IS a STEMI in a woman
    Women require only ≥1.5 mm of ST elevation in V₂–V₃ for STEMI criteria, while men ≥40 need ≥2 mm. This patient has 1.3–1.4 mm elevation in V₂–V₃, which does not meet the threshold of 1.5 mm, but the clinical context demands serial ECGs and cardiology consultation immediately. Had she had ≥1.5 mm, it would formally meet criteria. The key is knowing the sex-specific thresholds.
    ⚠ Trap: Applying the male 2 mm threshold to women. Board keyword: women = ≥0.15 mV (1.5 mm) in V₂–V₃; men ≥40 = ≥0.2 mV (2 mm); men <40 = ≥0.25 mV (2.5 mm). Women are systematically underdiagnosed by applying the male threshold.
    Trap 02Trapezius Radiation — STEMI or Pericarditis?
    A 35-year-old man presents with chest pain that radiates to both trapezius muscles. It is pleuritic in nature and improves when he leans forward. ECG shows diffuse ST elevation with PR depression. What is the most likely diagnosis?
    Correct Answer: Pericarditis — NOT STEMI
    Radiation to the trapezius is NOT seen in STEMI patients — it is a distinguishing feature of pericarditis. The diffuse ST elevation (rather than regional), PR depression, pleuritic quality, and positional relief all point to pericarditis. STEMI causes regional ST elevation corresponding to the territory of the occluded coronary artery.
    ⚠ Trap: Seeing "ST elevation" and automatically thinking STEMI. Board keyword: trapezius radiation = pericarditis, not STEMI. If anticoagulants are given for presumed STEMI in pericarditis → tamponade risk. Diffuse vs regional ST elevation is the key ECG differentiator.
    Trap 03Nitrates + RV Infarction = Cardiovascular Collapse
    A 67-year-old man presents with inferior STEMI (ST elevation in II, III, aVF). He is hypotensive (BP 82/50) with elevated JVP and clear lung fields. The nurse is about to administer sublingual nitroglycerin for his chest pain. Should this be given?
    Correct Answer: Absolutely NOT — this is RV infarction, a contraindication to nitrates
    The triad of inferior ST elevation + elevated JVP + clear lungs + hypotension = RV infarction. These patients depend entirely on adequate preload to maintain cardiac output. Nitrates cause venodilation → ↓preload → precipitous, potentially irreversible cardiovascular collapse. Treatment is volume expansion (IV fluids) to maintain RV preload.
    ⚠ Trap: Reflex nitroglycerin for any chest pain. Board keyword: inferior MI + JVD + clear lungs + hypotension = RV infarct = nitroglycerin contraindicated. Also avoid diuretics in RV infarction for the same reason.
    Trap 04Prophylactic Lidocaine — Do NOT Give
    A 70-year-old man with STEMI is in the cardiac unit. He has frequent VPBs on the monitor. A resident suggests starting prophylactic IV lidocaine to prevent ventricular fibrillation. Is this appropriate?
    Correct Answer: No — prophylactic antiarrhythmic therapy for VPBs is CONTRAINDICATED and may increase mortality
    Infrequent VPBs occur in almost all STEMI patients and do not require therapy. Prophylactic antiarrhythmic drugs (either IV lidocaine early or oral agents later) for VPBs in the absence of clinically important ventricular tachyarrhythmias may INCREASE the mortality rate. Beta blockers are the appropriate agents for VF prevention.
    ⚠ Trap: "Warning arrhythmias" (frequent/multifocal VPBs) historically prompted prophylactic treatment. This is now known to increase mortality. Board keyword: VPBs alone in STEMI → no antiarrhythmic therapy; prophylactic lidocaine = contraindicated.
    Trap 05Nonculprit PCI in Cardiogenic Shock — CONTRAINDICATED
    A 65-year-old man presents with anterior STEMI complicated by cardiogenic shock. Coronary angiography reveals an LAD culprit lesion and a high-grade stenosis in the RCA. After successful PCI of the LAD, the interventional cardiologist proposes to also stent the RCA during the same procedure. Is this appropriate?
    Correct Answer: No — routine nonculprit PCI is CONTRAINDICATED in cardiogenic shock
    While nonculprit PCI during the index procedure or within 45 days is beneficial in STEMI WITHOUT cardiogenic shock (PRAMI, CvLPRIT, COMPLETE trials), it is contraindicated in cardiogenic shock — associated with increased rate of death or severe renal failure compared with culprit-only PCI.
    ⚠ Trap: Applying the "complete revascularization" strategy universally. Board keyword: STEMI + cardiogenic shock + nonculprit PCI = contraindicated (culprit-only). Without shock, complete revascularization is beneficial. This is a nuanced distinction boards love to test.
    Trap 06Primary VF vs Secondary VF — Opposite Prognosis
    A 55-year-old man develops VF 6 hours after STEMI admission. He has no HF, no cardiogenic shock, and no bundle branch block. He is successfully defibrillated. What is the expected long-term prognosis?
    Correct Answer: Excellent long-term prognosis — this is PRIMARY ventricular fibrillation
    Primary VF occurs in the first 48h as a direct response to acute ischemia, without predisposing factors (HF, shock, bundle branch block, ventricular aneurysm). Despite increased in-hospital mortality, long-term survival after hospital discharge is excellent.
    ⚠ Trap: Assuming all VF after STEMI carries a poor prognosis. Board keyword: Primary VF (first 48h, no predisposing factors) = excellent long-term prognosis. Secondary VF (due to pump failure) = poor prognosis. Late VF (after 48h) = ↑mortality + consider ICD.
    Trap 07VSR vs Papillary Muscle Rupture — Murmur Distinction
    A 72-year-old woman develops sudden respiratory distress 4 days after inferior STEMI. She has a loud holosystolic murmur with a palpable thrill at the left sternal border, S₃ gallop, and signs of biventricular failure. The question asks: What does right heart catheterization most likely show?
    Correct Answer: Oxygen step-up from right atrium to right ventricle — consistent with ventricular septal rupture
    VSR presents with a harsh holosystolic murmur WITH a palpable thrill and biventricular failure. Right heart catheterization shows a step-up in oxygen saturation from RA to RV (left-to-right shunt) and large v waves. Papillary muscle rupture also causes a holosystolic murmur but is SOFT with NO thrill, and shows no O₂ step-up (but very high PCWP).
    ⚠ Trap: Both VSR and papillary muscle rupture cause post-MI holosystolic murmurs. The distinguishing features: VSR = loud murmur + thrill + O₂ step-up RA→RV; PMR = soft murmur + no thrill + no step-up + high PCWP.
    Trap 08ICD Timing — Don't Implant Too Early Post-MI
    A 60-year-old man had a large anterior STEMI 20 days ago. Post-PCI LVEF is 30%. He is NYHA Class II. The cardiologist wants to implant an ICD now. Is this appropriate?
    Correct Answer: Not yet — reassess LVEF after >40 days from MI
    The available evidence does not suggest a survival advantage to early ICD placement after MI. LVEF should be reassessed at >40 days post-MI and/or >90 days post-revascularization before deciding on ICD implantation. LVEF may improve significantly with medical therapy and revascularization. While waiting, the wearable cardioverter-defibrillator (WCD) is a Class IIb option.
    ⚠ Trap: Acting on current LVEF immediately post-MI without waiting for remodeling to occur. Board keyword: reassess LVEF >40 days after MI, >90 days after revascularization. Even if LVEF remains ≤30% at that point, THEN ICD is Class I (NYHA I) or Class I (NYHA II–III with LVEF ≤35%).
    Trap 09NSAIDs/Glucocorticoids in STEMI — Dangerous
    A 68-year-old man with STEMI also has significant pericarditic chest pain on day 3. A nurse practitioner suggests adding ibuprofen for the pericarditis. Is this safe?
    Correct Answer: No — NSAIDs (and glucocorticoids) are contraindicated in STEMI
    NSAIDs impair infarct healing, increase risk of myocardial rupture, may result in a larger infarct scar, and can increase coronary vascular resistance (reducing flow to ischemic myocardium). The correct treatment for post-MI pericarditis is aspirin 650 mg four times daily — the only exception to anti-inflammatory contraindication.
    ⚠ Trap: Using NSAIDs for valid indications (pericarditis, pain) in a patient who just had an MI. Board keyword: NSAIDs and glucocorticoids (except aspirin) = contraindicated in STEMI — impair infarct healing and increase rupture risk.
    Trap 10AIVR — Benign Reperfusion Rhythm, Not VT
    A 59-year-old man receives tenecteplase for STEMI at a non-PCI hospital. Shortly after, the monitor shows a wide complex rhythm at 75 bpm. The rhythm is regular and the patient is hemodynamically stable and asymptomatic. What is the diagnosis and what should be done?
    Correct Answer: Accelerated idioventricular rhythm (AIVR) — a benign reperfusion rhythm; no treatment needed
    AIVR (rate 60–100 bpm) frequently occurs transiently during fibrinolytic therapy at the time of reperfusion. It is benign, does not presage development of classic VT, and should be treated only with careful monitoring. Overly aggressive treatment can cause complete heart block.
    ⚠ Trap: Treating AIVR as ventricular tachycardia and administering amiodarone or antiarrhythmics. Board keyword: AIVR (60–100 bpm) after fibrinolysis = reperfusion arrhythmia = benign = monitor only. VT requires rate >100 bpm with hemodynamic compromise to warrant intervention.
