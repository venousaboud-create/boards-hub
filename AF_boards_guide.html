01

Pathophysiology

The Core Mechanism

AF is a cardiac arrhythmia characterized by seemingly disorganized, rapid, and irregular atrial electrical activation, resulting in loss of organized atrial mechanical contraction. These chaotic signals bombard the AV node, which then determines ventricular rate — resulting in an irregular, usually rapid ventricular response.

Key Concept — Why Is the Ventricular Rate Irregular?
The AV node acts as a "gatekeeper" — it receives hundreds of fibrillatory impulses per minute but only conducts some of them, unpredictably, producing the classically irregularly irregular ventricular rhythm with no discernible P waves on ECG.

The Multifactorial Pathway to AF

The pathophysiology is incompletely understood but follows a "final common pathway" model:

Risk Factors (HTN, obesity, DM, sleep apnea, alcohol, age)
↓
Electrophysiologic remodeling of atrial tissue
↓
Abnormal membrane channel regulation → Enhanced automaticity
↓
Ectopic beats (PACs) — especially from pulmonary vein musculature
↓
Bouts of rapid atrial ectopy → initiate atrial tachycardia or frank AF
↓
Cellular + tissue remodeling → Shortening of atrial refractory periods
↓
Sustained AF via rapid automaticity "drivers" + functional reentry
↓
Further remodeling: fibrosis + left atrial enlargement

Key Trigger Regions

  • Pulmonary veins — Primary source; musculature exhibits enhanced automaticity
  • Posterior wall of the left atrium — Secondary trigger zone
  • Superior vena cava — Muscular tissue sleeves can fire ectopically
  • Coronary sinus — Another ectopic source
  • Vein of Marshall (remnant) — Can harbor triggered activity
Why This Matters for Treatment
Because pulmonary veins are the dominant trigger, pulmonary vein isolation (PVI) is the cornerstone of ablation therapy. This is not arbitrary — it directly targets the anatomic source.

AF as a Progressive Disease

  • Typically begins with sporadic PACs and short atrial tachycardia runs from pulmonary veins
  • Progresses to paroxysmal AF as ectopy becomes more frequent and substrate develops
  • Untreated, episodes lengthen → persistent AF (does not self-terminate)
  • Eventually: long-standing persistent AF with severe remodeling, fibrosis, LA enlargement
  • The more remodeled the atria, the less effective therapeutic interventions become
Important Caveat
There is currently no definitive "cure" that completely eliminates AF durably in a predictable fashion.
02

Epidemiology & Risk Factors

Prevalence & Demographics

Most Common

AF is the most common sustained cardiac arrhythmia — a major public health issue worldwide

Age

>95% of AF patients are >60 years old. Prevalence in those >80 years: ~20%

Lifetime Risk

Men aged 40 have a ~25% lifetime risk of developing AF

  • Slightly more common in men than women
  • More common in whites than blacks

Risk Factors

Modifiable

  • Hypertension — most common modifiable RF
  • Obesity
  • Alcohol use — can trigger acute AF ("holiday heart")
  • Sleep-disordered breathing (sleep apnea)
  • Sedentary lifestyle
  • Smoking

Non-Modifiable / Medical

  • Age — strongest independent risk factor
  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Cardiac disease (cardiomyopathy, HF, valvular disease, CAD)
  • Family history of AF
  • Thyroid disease (especially hyperthyroidism)
  • Male sex

Precipitating Factors (Secondary AF)

  • Hyperthyroidism — Always check TSH in new AF
  • Acute alcohol intoxication
  • Myocardial infarction
  • Pulmonary embolism
  • Pericarditis
  • Cardiac surgery — AF occurs in up to 50% of patients postoperatively

Consequences & Mortality

AF is NOT Benign
  • 1.5–1.9× increased mortality risk after controlling for underlying cardiac disease
  • Causes ~25% of all strokes
  • AF detected in 8.9% of patients within 6 months of cryptogenic stroke (using insertable cardiac monitors)
  • Increased risk of dementia and MRI-detected asymptomatic embolic infarcts
  • Risk of tachycardia-induced cardiomyopathy with prolonged uncontrolled rate

Caffeine — The Counterintuitive Finding

Board Pearl
Despite common belief, large cohort studies show a modest DECREASE in AF risk with modest caffeine intake. Caffeine is NOT a confirmed AF trigger. This is a classic board trap.

Exercise and AF — A Complex Relationship

  • Males: U-shaped curve — AF risk high in sedentary individuals AND in those with extensive endurance athletics (long-distance running, cycling). Moderate exercise is protective.
  • Females: Linear relationship — AF risk decreases continuously with increasing exercise activity

Newer 4-Stage Classification (2023 ACC/AHA)

  • Stage 1 — At Risk: Risk factors present but no AF yet; focus on prevention and lifestyle modification
  • Stage 2 — Pre-AF: Structural/electrical changes (e.g., frequent PACs, short atrial tachycardia runs) increasing risk; screening warranted
  • Stage 3 — AF: Established AF (paroxysmal, persistent, long-standing persistent)
  • Stage 4 — Permanent AF: Decision made to not pursue rhythm control
Why Two Classifications?
The temporal classification guides acute therapeutic decisions. The 4-stage classification recognizes AF as a disease continuum requiring prevention strategies at earlier stages, not just arrhythmia management.

Subclinical AF

  • Short-lasting and asymptomatic — detectable only via long-term monitoring (implantable loop recorders, pacemakers, defibrillators)
  • Associated with 2.5× increased stroke risk
  • Subclinical AF lasting 6 min to 24 h: apixaban decreases stroke/systemic embolism risk but increases major bleeding risk
  • Role of continuous monitoring to guide anticoagulation in borderline-risk patients remains unclear
04

Clinical Presentation & Manifestations

Four Domains of Clinical Consequence

1 · Symptoms from Rate

Palpitations, awareness of heartbeat, exercise intolerance, fatigue, weakness, presyncope, dyspnea from irregular/rapid ventricular rate. Many patients are unaware of the irregular rhythm for unknown reasons.

2 · Hemodynamic Consequences

Loss of atrial systole → reduced cardiac output. Variable stroke volume with irregular ventricular rate. Exacerbates underlying HFpEF, HFrEF, HCM, CAD, valvular disease, amyloidosis.

3 · Cardioembolic Phenomena

Blood stasis → thrombus (primarily in left atrial appendage) → embolization → ischemic stroke. Recurrent subclinical emboli → cognitive impairment, dementia.

4 · Long-term Cardiovascular Impact

Tachycardia-induced cardiomyopathy (generally reversible with rate control). "Stiff left atrial syndrome" in long-standing persistent AF: fibrotic, non-compliant LA → elevated filling pressures, volume overload, CHF.

ECG Diagnosis

  • No organized P waves — replaced by fibrillatory baseline (best seen in V1)
  • Irregularly irregular ventricular response — the hallmark
  • Ventricular rate typically 110–160 bpm; can exceed 200 bpm or be slow in patients with AV nodal disease or high vagal tone
  • Diagnosed via 12-lead ECG, ambulatory monitoring, or implantable loop recorders

Special Presentations

  • Pauses at conversion to sinus rhythm — manifestation of underlying sinus node dysfunction (very common with AF)
  • Bradycardia during AF — suggests concomitant AV nodal conduction disease → may cause presyncope/syncope
  • Tachycardia-induced cardiomyopathy — depressed LV function from prolonged rapid ventricular rates; generally reversible once rate controlled
05

Treatment Overview — The Three Pillars

The Three Goals of AF Management
  1. Control patient symptoms — via rate control and/or rhythm control strategy
  2. Mitigate thromboembolism risk — anticoagulation based on stroke risk score
  3. Address modifiable risk factors — lifestyle and comorbidity management

Acute Hemodynamic Instability → Emergency Cardioversion

If significant hemodynamic compromise, pulmonary edema, or evidence of coronary ischemia is present, emergent cardioversion is recommended.

  • Electrical cardioversion — QRS-synchronous DC shock (preferably with sedation)
  • Pharmacologic cardioversion — IV ibutilide (class III agent) most typical
Ibutilide Warning
Avoid ibutilide in patients with baseline prolonged QT interval or severe LV dysfunction — risk of torsades de pointes.
06

Rate Control

Goal of Rate Control

Allow more diastolic filling time → improve cardiac output → reduce symptoms. Longer term: minimize risk of CHF and tachycardia-induced cardiomyopathy.

Acute Rate Control

  • Beta-blockers — first-line; IV or oral depending on urgency
  • Non-dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers — verapamil, diltiazem; IV or oral
  • Digoxin — useful in patients with labile BP or cardiomyopathy (no negative inotropy); acts synergistically with beta-blockers/CCBs. However, recent evidence suggests increased mortality with chronic use → utilization has declined

Chronic Rate Control Targets

Rate Control Thresholds (Memorize These Numbers)
  • Strict rate control: Resting HR <80 bpm; increases to <100 bpm with light exertion (walking)
  • Lenient rate control: Resting HR <110 bpm — acceptable if asymptomatic and ventricular function is normal
  • In permanent AF: lenient strategy (<110 bpm at rest) is as effective as strict strategy for major outcomes (cardiovascular death, HF hospitalization, stroke, embolism, bleeding, life-threatening arrhythmias) — and easier to achieve
Monitoring Requirement
If allowing resting rate up to 110 bpm: periodic assessment of ventricular function is warranted because some patients develop tachycardia-induced cardiomyopathy even at this rate.

Exertion-Related Symptoms

Often indicate inadequate rate control. Assess heart rate response with exertion and adjust medications accordingly.

When Rate Control Fails: Ablate and Pace

  • Catheter ablation of AV junction → creates permanent AV block → requires permanent pacemaker implantation
  • Patients remain in AF but become pacemaker-dependent
  • Standard RV apical pacing → dyssynchronous ventricular activation → may depress LV function in some patients
  • Alternatives: biventricular pacing or left bundle branch area pacing to minimize dyssynchrony
Clinical Trial Pearl
AV nodal ablation + cardiac resynchronization therapy (CRT) is superior to pharmacologic therapy in improving quality of life and reducing development of heart failure, HF hospitalizations, and all-cause mortality in patients with permanent AF and narrow QRS — irrespective of baseline LVEF.
07

Cardioversion & Anticoagulation

Why Cardioversion Carries Thromboembolic Risk

  • Thrombus forms primarily in left atrial appendage (stagnant flow) during AF
  • After conversion to sinus rhythm, atrial mechanical function is delayed for weeks ("atrial stunning") — thrombi can form even after restoration of sinus rhythm
  • Risk highest when AF has been present for >48 hours

The 48-Hour Rule (Board Favorite)

*Low risk = no prior embolic events, no rheumatic mitral stenosis, no hypertrophic cardiomyopathy with marked LA enlargement

Special Considerations

  • Atrial stunning: After cardioversion, even if TEE showed no thrombus, continue anticoagulation for minimum 4 weeks to allow recovery of atrial mechanical function
  • Watchman device (LAA closure): Cardioversion is feasible without oral anticoagulation if pre-procedural TEE shows good device position, no device-related thrombus, and peri-device leak ≤5 mm
  • Cardioversion associated with substantial risk of AF recurrence, which may be asymptomatic
Data Caveat
Recommendations for short-term anticoagulation and thrombus exclusion at time of cardioversion lack contemporary robust data to support these strategies — noted by current guidelines.

Anticoagulation Threshold
Anticoagulation recommended in US and Europe for patients with score ≥1, unless the lone risk factor is female sex alone (score of 1 for female only → do NOT anticoagulate). Annual stroke risk can approach nearly 20% without anticoagulation at high scores.
AF Burden Does NOT Predict Stroke Risk
The perceived burden of AF has NOT been shown to predict stroke risk. Paroxysmal AF is treated the same as persistent AF for anticoagulation purposes. Absence of AF during periodic monitoring is NOT sufficient to indicate low risk.

DOACs vs Warfarin (Pooled Data)
DOACs are noninferior to warfarin in individual trials; intent-to-treat pooled analysis suggests superiority by small absolute margins (0.4–0.7%) in reduction of mortality, stroke, major bleeding, and intracranial hemorrhage.
  • DOACs have lower risk of intracranial bleeding compared to warfarin
  • Dabigatran, rivaroxaban, apixaban have renal excretion — dose adjust for CKD (especially important in elderly)
  • Apixaban and rivaroxaban show safety/efficacy in patients on chronic hemodialysis

Reversal Agents

  • Warfarin: Fresh frozen plasma, prothrombin complex concentrate, vitamin K
  • Dabigatran: Idarucizumab (IV)
  • Xa inhibitors: Andexanet alfa (IV)
  • These reversal agents may be prothrombotic — use judiciously

When Warfarin is REQUIRED

Warfarin-Only Situations
  • Rheumatic mitral stenosis — DOACs have failed to demonstrate noninferiority; warfarin led to lower composite cardiovascular events/death vs rivaroxaban
  • Mechanical heart valves — DOACs are less effective for preventing valve thrombosis/thromboembolism

Antiplatelet Agents — Insufficient for AF Stroke Prevention

  • Warfarin reduces annual stroke risk by 64% vs placebo and 37% vs antiplatelet therapy
  • Aspirin alone or aspirin + clopidogrel are inferior to warfarin and do NOT have less bleeding risk

Anticoagulation in Special Scenarios

After PCI (Percutaneous Coronary Intervention)

  • Oral anticoagulation + P2Y12 inhibitor (preferably clopidogrel) is recommended
  • Triple therapy (OAC + aspirin + P2Y12 inhibitor): preferably include a DOAC; consider for high ischemic risk (ACS) for up to 30 days

Left Atrial Appendage Closure (Watchman Device)

  • Noninferior to warfarin in reducing stroke risk
  • Provides significant additional reductions in major bleeding, particularly hemorrhagic stroke, and all-cause mortality
  • Appears noninferior to DOACs in preventing major AF-related cardiovascular, neurologic, and bleeding events
  • Considered in patients with high thromboembolism risk but serious bleeding risk from chronic anticoagulation
09

Rhythm Control

When to Choose Rhythm Control

  • Symptomatic paroxysmal AF
  • Recurrent episodes of symptomatic persistent AF
  • AF with difficult rate control
  • AF causing depressed ventricular function or aggravating heart failure
  • Younger patients (more likely to benefit vs sedentary/elderly where rate control suffices)

Rate vs Rhythm Control — Evidence

Older Trials vs. Modern Evidence
  • Older randomized trials: AAD-based rhythm control did NOT improve survival or symptoms vs rate control; drug group had more hospitalizations
  • Flaws: disappointing drug efficacy, toxicities, inappropriate anticoagulation discontinuation, patient selection bias
  • Recent trial (early rhythm control within 1 year): Demonstrated reduction in cardiovascular events including death and stroke compared to rate control — likely due to use of catheter ablation and high anticoagulation adherence
  • HF with depressed LVEF: Catheter ablation-based rhythm control provides all-cause mortality benefit vs medical rhythm control

Critical Rule: Anticoagulation Continues Even in Sinus Rhythm

Do NOT Stop Anticoagulation After Rhythm Control
Even if sinus rhythm is apparently maintained, anticoagulation is recommended based on CHA₂DS₂-VASc score, because asymptomatic AF episodes are common and perceived AF burden does not predict stroke risk.

All AADs have modest efficacy in paroxysmal AF — only ~30–50% of patients will benefit. Amiodarone is the exception with ~66% efficacy.

Catheter Ablation

First-Line Status (2023 ACC/AHA Guidelines)

Catheter Ablation is Now First-Line
Based on recent randomized trial data showing superiority of ablation over medications for maintenance of sinus rhythm and benefits of early rhythm control, catheter ablation is considered first-line therapy, especially for paroxysmal AF.

Indications (Table 258-3 Recommendations)

ClassEvidenceRecommendation
1ASymptomatic AF where AADs are ineffective, contraindicated, not tolerated, or not preferred — ablation is useful to improve symptoms
1ASelected patients (generally younger, few comorbidities) with symptomatic paroxysmal AF — ablation is useful as first-line therapy to improve symptoms and reduce progression to persistent AF
1ASymptomatic or clinically significant atrial flutter — ablation is useful for improving symptoms
2aBDuring AF ablation — ablation of additional clinically significant SVT arrhythmias can be useful
2aBOther symptomatic paroxysmal or persistent AF patients on rhythm control — first-line ablation can be useful

Technique — Pulmonary Vein Isolation (PVI)

  • Percutaneous venous access (femoral veins) → trans-septal puncture
  • Radiofrequency ablation or cryoablation to electrically isolate left atrial regions around pulmonary vein antra
  • Abolishes triggering foci; also impacts substrate for reentry in left atrium
  • Gaps in healed ablation areas or new trigger sites outside PVs → repeat procedure needed in 10–30% of patients

Pulsed-Field Ablation (PFA) — Emerging Technology

  • Uses short pulses of high-energy electric fields to cause electroporation (cell death) — no heat generation
  • Myocardial cells are uniquely sensitive: electroporation threshold 268–375 V/cm
  • Other tissues (nerves, endothelium, smooth muscle, blood cells) have thresholds >1600 V/cm → theoretically cardiac-selective
  • Noninferior to conventional thermal ablation (RF + cryo) at 1 year for paroxysmal AF
  • Significantly reduced risk of pulmonary vein stenosis, phrenic nerve injury, and left atrial-esophageal fistula
  • Current limitation: unlike RF ablation, PFA lacks the ability to titrate and tailor energy delivery during ablation

Ablation Outcomes by AF Type

  • Paroxysmal AF: Sinus rhythm maintained for >1 year after single procedure in ~80%; >90% after multiple procedures
  • Persistent / Long-standing persistent: More difficult — more extensive atrial abnormalities, greater comorbidities, more remodeling. Often requires additional ablation beyond PVI

Complications of Catheter Ablation

Major Complication Rate: 2–7%
  • Stroke: 0.5–1%
  • Cardiac tamponade: ~1%
  • Phrenic nerve paralysis
  • Bleeding from femoral access sites
  • Fluid overload with HF: can emerge 1–3 days post-procedure
  • Pulmonary vein stenosis: presents weeks to months later with dyspnea or hemoptysis
  • Atrioesophageal fistula (rare — <0.1%): presents 10 days to 3 weeks post-procedure as endocarditis + stroke. Diagnosis by chest CT with oral + IV contrast. Endoscopy is CONTRAINDICATED (risk of air/fluid embolus). Requires emergent surgical repair. Delayed diagnosis → likely death.

Complication rates are lowest with high-volume operators and centers.

Surgical Ablation

  • Most often performed concomitant with valve or CAD surgery; rarely stand-alone
  • No significant difference in freedom from AF between PVI alone and biatrial maze procedure
  • For persistent AF: surgical/hybrid procedures have comparable efficacy to catheter ablation
  • Risks: sinus node injury requiring pacemaker, higher morbidity
  • Surgical LAA removal may reduce stroke risk, but not unequivocally proven — thrombus can form in remnant or if not completely ligated
11

Risk Factor Modification

Strong evidence links AF to modifiable lifestyle factors. Aggressive treatment of these can substantially reduce AF episodes in some patients — and provides additional cardiovascular benefits beyond AF.

Sedentary Lifestyle

Increased AF risk. Moderate exercise is protective in both sexes (with sex-specific nuance).

Obesity

Strong association with AF development. Weight loss reduces AF burden.

Hypertension

Most common modifiable risk factor. Aggressive BP control reduces AF progression.

Smoking

Associated with increased AF risk. Cessation is warranted.

Alcohol Use

Can trigger acute AF. Reduction in alcohol intake reduces AF recurrence.

Sleep Apnea

Strong independent risk factor. CPAP therapy can reduce AF burden.

Integrated Management Model

Emerging emphasis is on coordinated management addressing: risk factor modification + stroke prevention + rate control + rhythm control + management of associated comorbidities — simultaneously, not sequentially.

Exam Traps

Trap 01The caffeine–AF connection
A patient with paroxysmal AF asks whether he should stop drinking coffee. He drinks 2 cups/day. You advise him...
CORRECT: Modest caffeine intake does NOT increase AF risk — large cohort studies actually show a modest DECREASE in AF risk. Tell the patient moderate caffeine is not a confirmed trigger. This is the opposite of the common assumption boards like to test.
Trap 02Paroxysmal AF and anticoagulation
A 72-year-old woman with hypertension, diabetes, and paroxysmal AF that self-terminates in under 12 hours comes in. Her physician says since episodes are short and she's always in sinus rhythm at clinic visits, she doesn't need anticoagulation. Is this correct?
CORRECT ANSWER: NO. The perceived burden of AF has NOT been shown to predict stroke risk. Paroxysmal AF is managed the same as persistent AF for anticoagulation purposes. She has a CHA₂DS₂-VASc score ≥2 and should be anticoagulated. Absence of AF during monitoring does not indicate low risk.
Trap 03Rate control target in permanent AF
Which heart rate target is recommended in a patient with permanent AF who is otherwise asymptomatic with normal ventricular function?
CORRECT: Lenient rate control (resting HR <110 bpm) is as effective as strict rate control (<80 bpm at rest) for permanent AF in terms of major cardiovascular outcomes — and is easier to achieve. Do not over-aggressively target <80 bpm if the patient is asymptomatic and has normal LVEF.
Trap 04DOACs in mechanical valves and rheumatic MS
A patient with AF and a mechanical mitral valve is started on rivaroxaban. Is this appropriate?
CORRECT ANSWER: NO. DOACs are contraindicated in mechanical heart valves and rheumatic mitral stenosis. Warfarin is required in these situations. DOACs have failed to demonstrate noninferiority and are less effective for preventing valve thrombosis and thromboembolism in these patients.
Trap 05Female sex and anticoagulation
A 62-year-old woman has AF. Her only "risk factor" on CHA₂DS₂-VASc is female sex (score = 1). Should you anticoagulate?
CORRECT ANSWER: NO. Female sex as the SOLE risk factor (score of 1 only because of sex) is NOT an indication for anticoagulation. Anticoagulation is recommended for score ≥1 UNLESS the lone risk factor is female gender. She needs score ≥2 with at least one non-sex risk factor.
Trap 06Dronedarone in heart failure
You have a patient with AF and heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF). Which antiarrhythmic drug should be avoided?
CORRECT: Dronedarone is CONTRAINDICATED in HF and long-standing persistent AF — it INCREASES mortality in these patients. Amiodarone is the preferred AAD in HF (can also be used with CAD), despite its significant toxicity profile.
Trap 07Atrioesophageal fistula — how to diagnose
Two weeks after AF ablation, a patient develops fever, neurologic deficits, and signs of sepsis. Atrioesophageal fistula is suspected. What is the next step?
CORRECT: Chest CT with water-soluble oral AND IV contrast. DO NOT perform endoscopy — it is contraindicated due to risk of air/esophageal fluid embolus. Definitive treatment is emergent surgical repair. Delayed diagnosis leads to likely death.
Trap 08Sinus rhythm maintained → stop anticoagulation?
After successful ablation, a patient is in sinus rhythm for 6 months with no documented AF recurrence. His CHA₂DS₂-VASc score is 3. Can you stop anticoagulation?
CORRECT ANSWER: NO. Anticoagulation should continue based on CHA₂DS₂-VASc score even after apparent rhythm control. Asymptomatic AF episodes are common and go undetected. The perceived absence of AF does not mean the stroke risk is gone.
Trap 0909The 48-hour cardioversion rule — even low-risk patients
A patient with AF of unknown duration has a CHA₂DS₂-VASc score of 0. Do you still need to worry about thromboembolism before cardioversion?
CORRECT: YES. If AF duration exceeds 48h or is unknown, there is greater concern for thromboembolism EVEN in patients with CHA₂DS₂-VASc of 0 or 1. Either anticoagulate for 3 weeks before cardioversion, or perform TEE/cardiac CT to exclude LAA thrombus first.
Trap 10Dofetilide initiation
You decide to start dofetilide for AF. Can you initiate it outpatient?
CORRECT ANSWER: NO. Dofetilide MUST be initiated in a hospital with continuous ECG monitoring due to ~3% risk of inducing excessive QT prolongation and torsades de pointes. Many cardiologists also take this same approach with sotalol.
Trap 11Exercise and AF — sex differences
Which statement is TRUE about exercise and AF risk? A) In males, AF risk decreases linearly with more exercise. B) In females, moderate exercise increases AF risk. C) Extensive endurance exercise in males carries high AF risk similar to sedentary males.
CORRECT: C is true — males show a U-shaped curve where BOTH sedentary lifestyle AND extensive endurance athletics carry high AF risk. Moderate exercise is protective in men. In females, the relationship is linear — more exercise continuously lowers AF risk.
Trap 12Post-op AF prevalence
How common is AF after cardiac surgery?
CORRECT: AF occurs in up to 50% of patients postoperatively after cardiac surgery. This is a specific high-yield number boards love to test. It is classified as secondary AF with an identifiable precipitating cause.
