01

Epidemiology

Core Definition
Sarcoidosis is a systemic inflammatory disease of unknown cause characterized by formation of nonnecrotizing granulomatous inflammation. Affects the lung in >90% of cases but can afflict almost any organ system. First described >150 years ago; the term "sarkoid" coined by Caeser Boeck (Norwegian dermatologist, 1899) because lesions resembled sarcoma, yet were largely benign and granulomatous.

Incidence & Prevalence

Vary by race, gender, geography, ethnicity, and season. Increased familial risk noted.

Highest Incidence Groups
  • African Americans: 35.5 per 100,000
  • Northern Europeans (US/Sweden): 24 per 100,000
  • U.S. Black Women's Health Study: 71 per 100,000 annual incidence; prevalence 2%
Key Demographics
  • Gender: Higher incidence in women (ratio ≤2:1); consistent across studies
  • Peak age: 35–45 years; significant cases after age 55
  • Men manifest disease earlier than women
  • Seasonal: Peak diagnoses in springtime
  • Incidence may be increasing in US and Japan
Risk Factors
Increase risk: Certain occupations (agriculture, teaching, metalworking, radiation, cotton ginning, automobiles), elevated BMI, musty/humid environments, mold, insecticides, air conditioning, raising birds, wood stoves/fireplaces.
Decreases risk: Smoking — consistently a negative predictor of sarcoidosis development (possibly via immunomodulatory effects of nicotine).
  • Subclinical sarcoidosis may be underestimated — only identified in screening studies or autopsy
  • WTC example: U.S. firefighter baseline 13–15/100,000; after WTC collapse rates rose to 86/100,000 in year 1 and 22/100,000 over next 4 years — cases included multisystem disease

Mortality

  • US: Average age- and gender-adjusted mortality rate 4.32 per 1,000,000; higher in women and African Americans
  • Leading cause of death in US/Europe: Pulmonary fibrosis (respiratory mortality)
  • Japan: Lower rate (~0.1 per million); deaths primarily from cardiac involvement
02

Etiology

Core Concept
The cause is unknown. Current evidence strongly indicates a heightened host immune response to an undetermined environmental exposure in a genetically susceptible individual. The immunologic response appears physiologic, but either exaggerated or deficient in regulatory mechanisms.

Evidence for an Antigen

  • Kveim-Siltzbach reagent: Homogenate of human splenic tissue from sarcoidosis patients — when injected into skin, induces systemic granulomatous response in patients with early sarcoidosis
  • Transplant recipients can develop sarcoid-like inflammatory responses after receiving organ tissue from a person with sarcoidosis
  • Case-cluster events: Spatial clustering of non-related sarcoidosis cases suggests shared environmental antigen exposure

Candidate Antigens

Mycobacterial Proteins
  • mKatG, ESAT-6, mycobacterial superoxide dismutase A, antigen 85A, heat shock proteins — found disproportionately in sarcoidosis granulomas
  • Heightened macrophage immune responses against these peptides in blood and BAL of sarcoidosis patients
Other Agents
  • Cutibacterium acnes (formerly Propionibacterium acnes) — implicated in some cohorts
  • Beryllium, fungi (histologic similarities with sarcoidosis)
  • Bioaerosols and inorganic agents (explains epidemiologic variability)
  • May not be a single antigen — differing antigens per individual or population

Genetic Predisposition

  • Antigen recognition and presentation molecule polymorphisms associated with disease development and phenotype
  • Gene-environment interactions identified — complex interplay of susceptible host in predisposing environment
  • HLA haplotype variants, BTNL2, TNF-α, ANXA11 genes — associated with specific phenotypes and worse prognosis

Key Immune Mechanisms

  • TH1 immune polarization is the immunopathogenic hallmark — highly polarized CD4+ TH1 lymphocytes; CD8+ lymphocytes less so
  • Inflammatory cells accumulate via both in situ proliferation and redistribution of peripheral blood to the lung
  • Process driven by amplification of oligoclonal T cells of undefined antigenic specificity
  • Pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1, IL-2, IL-15, IL-18, CXCR3) consistently upregulated at inflammation sites — activate macrophages, propagate response, form granulomas
  • IFN-γ: TH1-derived; key cytokine maintaining granulomatous inflammation; drives unregulated 1,25-OH VitD3 production by macrophages → hypercalcemia
  • Altered T-regulatory (Treg) and TH17 cells — thought to be involved in failure of resolution or progression to fibrosis
  • The critical step as to why some patients transition to fibrosis is not understood
04

Clinical Presentation

Spectrum
Varies considerably from asymptomatic to progressive organ dysfunction. In many cases, findings discovered incidentally on imaging. Patients present to diverse specialties — cardiopulmonary symptoms can mimic more common diseases, causing diagnostic delays.

Acute Presentations — Excellent Prognosis

Löfgren Syndrome — up to 10% of cases
  • Fever
  • Erythema nodosum (tender red nodules on anterior shins)
  • Ankle arthralgias
  • Bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy
  • Prognosis: Full spontaneous resolution; immunosuppression rarely necessary
  • Occurs in 20–30% of Caucasians (especially Northern Europeans); <5% of Asians and African Americans
  • Erythema nodosum/fever remit within 6 weeks; lymphadenopathy may persist >1 year
Heerfordt Syndrome (Uveoparotid Fever)
  • Facial nerve palsy
  • Fever
  • Anterior uveitis
  • Bilateral parotid gland enlargement
  • Prognosis: Excellent with full spontaneous resolution
  • Facial nerve palsy associated with good prognosis vs other neurologic findings

Subacute / Chronic Presentation

  • More insidious onset: chronic cough, dyspnea, atypical chest pain, intermittent joint or muscle pain
  • Nonspecific: fevers, malaise, night sweats, weight loss
Life-Threatening / Urgent Presentations
Acute hypercalcemia, sudden cardiac death, blindness, arrhythmias, heart block, neurologic demise — require urgent hospitalization. In rare cases, sarcoidosis can be fatal — usually from heart failure, sudden cardiac death, or neurologic involvement.
05

Establishing a Diagnosis

Three Diagnostic Criteria (ALL must be met)
  • 1. Compatible clinical presentation
  • 2. Presence of nonnecrotizing granulomatous inflammation in ≥1 tissue
  • 3. Exclusion of alternative causes of granulomatous inflammation

Workup must also assess: extent/severity of organ involvement, prognostic factors, and whether therapy is beneficial. No well-established clinically useful serum biomarker can secure the diagnosis alone.

Biopsy Approach

  • If compatible clinical picture or radiographic finding: biopsy is the next step
  • Noninvasive sites: Skin, conjunctiva, superficial lymph nodes
  • When lung involvement + mediastinal/hilar lymphadenopathy prominent → bronchoscopy preferred
  • EBUS-guided lymph node sampling — high diagnostic yield, low procedural risk (preferred)
  • BAL: Elevated lymphocyte %; CD4:CD8 ratio >3.5 supports diagnosis (normal ratio does NOT exclude sarcoidosis); BAL important to exclude infection
  • "Cobblestone" appearance on bronchoscopy — granulomas lining all airway wall segments
  • Transbronchial biopsies — if lung infiltrates present
  • Endoscopic ultrasound (via esophagus) — alternative biopsy approach based on location
  • Surgical lung biopsy / mediastinoscopy — only when less invasive procedures nondiagnostic and no other accessible biopsy sites
  • 18F-FDG PET-CT — identifies active tissue inflammation and biopsy site in difficult cases
When Biopsy Can Be Deferred
Classic Löfgren syndrome, Heerfordt syndrome, or lupus pernio — compelling enough to defer biopsy. Also consider deferring in asymptomatic patients with symmetrical bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy (patient-oriented discussion). Close clinical follow-up always required — the diagnosis of sarcoidosis is never fully secure.

Differential Diagnosis

Infections
  • Mycobacteria: TB, atypical
  • Fungi: Histoplasma, Coccidioides, Blastomyces, Aspergillus, Cryptococcus
  • Parasites: Toxoplasma, Schistosoma
  • Bacterial/viral: Herpes zoster, Tularemia, Q fever, Bartonella
Other Inflammatory
  • Hypersensitivity pneumonitis, aspiration pneumonitis
  • Chronic beryllium disease, silicosis, talc
  • Drug reactions: immune checkpoint inhibitors, antiretrovirals, interferon, TNF-α antagonists
  • ANCA vasculitis, IgG4-RD, RA nodules, IBD, CVID-associated lung disease
Malignancy-Related
  • Local granulomatous reaction around tumor
  • Systemic sarcoid-like reaction to malignancy
  • Chemotherapy or immunotherapy-induced sarcoid-like reaction

Pathology

The Sarcoidosis Granuloma — Anatomy

Structure
An organized collection of:
  • Center: Macrophages → epithelioid cells → multinucleated giant cells (formed by fusion of epithelioid cells) + CD4+ T lymphocytes interspersed
  • Periphery: Well-circumscribed rim of lymphocytes and fibroblasts + CD8+ T lymphocytes + B lymphocytes
  • Key hallmark: NONNECROTIZING (noncaseating) — punctuate necrosis may rarely be present; abundant necrosis strongly indicates infection
  • Inclusions (supportive but not diagnostic): asteroid bodies, birefringent calcium carbonate/oxalate crystals, Schaumann bodies, Hamazaki-Wesenberg bodies (especially in lymph nodes)
  • Most granulomas resolve; hyalinization and fibrosis can occur

Granuloma Comparison — Boards High-Yield

Sarcoidosis
  • Tightly formed
  • Nonnecrotizing (noncaseating)
  • CD4+ dominated, TH1 skewed
Infection (TB / Fungal)
  • Tightly formed
  • Necrotizing center (caseating in TB)
  • Requires special stains + cultures
Hypersensitivity Pneumonitis
  • Loosely formed
  • Without necrosis
  • Peribronchiolar distribution

Critical Boards Point
Scadding stages do NOT predict disease activity, progression of lung disease, or extrapulmonary involvement — they only reveal overall radiographic resolution rates. Do not use staging to predict outcome for individual patients.

Imaging Findings

  • Distribution: Upper-lobe predominant; lymphangitic distribution (along pleural/subpleural areas, interlobular septa, bronchovascular bundles)
  • Classic CT: Widespread small nodules (perilymphatic and subpleural distribution), bronchovascular bundle thickening, thickened interlobular septa, mediastinal/hilar lymphadenopathy (may calcify), conglomerate masses with architectural distortion
  • Advanced disease CT: Honeycombing, cyst/cavity formation, bronchiectasis
  • Nodular sarcoidosis or necrotizing sarcoid granulomatosis — nodular masses
  • Pleural effusions — rarely direct sarcoidosis (more often infections, heart failure); can cause lymphocytic exudative effusions

Pulmonary Function

  • Many patients have normal PFTs despite abnormal imaging (especially early)
  • Restrictive pattern in more advanced disease
  • Airflow obstruction in up to one-third of patients — central airway scarring and distortion

Upper Respiratory Tract & Fibrosis Complications

  • Upper respiratory tract (larynx, pharynx, sinuses) — associated with more chronic severe disease; sinus involvement is adverse prognostic factor
  • Progressive pulmonary fibrosis — significant cause of complications and death
  • Fibrosis complications: cavitary lung disease, chronic pulmonary aspergillosis / aspergillomas, pulmonary hypertension
  • Fibrosis can occur even in the setting of anti-inflammatory therapy
08

Cardiac Sarcoidosis & Pulmonary Hypertension

High-Stakes Organ
Cardiac sarcoidosis carries the risk of sudden death — which can be the initial presentation of disease. Severity of pulmonary involvement does not predict cardiac involvement — every patient must be screened with an ECG.

Epidemiology

  • Clinical evidence in <5% of U.S. patients and 23% of Japanese patients; autopsy suggests higher subclinical rate
  • In broader population, cardiac sarcoidosis causes significant proportion of new-onset AV block, cardiomyopathy, and ventricular dysrhythmias — especially in younger patients
  • Isolated cardiac sarcoidosis can rarely occur

Cardiac Manifestations

Arrhythmias & Conduction
  • Sudden death — most common cause of death in cardiac sarcoidosis; can be initial presentation
  • Ventricular tachycardia — most frequent tachyarrhythmia
  • Atrial tachycardias — related to ventricular dysfunction, dilated atria, severe pulmonary disease
  • AV block — conduction system infiltration
Structural & Mechanical
  • Heart failure — most often from myocardial infiltration; also valve/papillary muscle infiltration
  • Pericardial effusion, cardiomyopathy
  • Predictors of sudden death: Severity of heart failure, left ventricular end-diastolic diameter, sustained VT

Cardiac Diagnosis

  • ECG — required on initial evaluation of EVERY sarcoidosis patient: Conduction block, ST-T abnormalities, Q waves, frequent PVCs, resting tachycardia
  • Gold-standard biopsy lacks sensitivity — patchy granulomas in cardiac tissue, predominant base-of-heart location, lack of RV involvement
  • If ECG abnormal: Holter monitor, signal-averaged ECG, further cardiac imaging
  • Cardiac MRI (gadolinium-enhanced) — NOW PREFERRED: Superior resolution, detects early myocardial involvement with high sensitivity and specificity
  • Cardiac PET/CT — alternative when MRI unavailable (incompatible devices, claustrophobia, inconclusive)
  • Historically: Thallium-201 scintigraphy / Tc-sestamibi SPECT — identify segmental defects
  • Echocardiography — ventricular/valve dysfunction, septal wall thickness, wall motion abnormalities
Reverse Redistribution — Boards Pearl
In cardiac sarcoidosis, nuclear imaging shows perfusion defects that reverse (improve) with exercise or dipyridamole — the opposite of coronary artery disease (where defects worsen with stress). This "reverse redistribution" pattern is a key distinguishing clue.

Cardiac Treatment

  • Traditional treatments for cardiac arrhythmias + heart failure based on presentation
  • Plus immunosuppressive medications; optimal duration unclear; cardiac sarcoidosis typically more chronic and protracted
  • Electrophysiologist referral for rhythm disturbances — consider ICD/pacemaker implantation
  • Heart transplantation — for refractory severe cardiac sarcoidosis; better short- and intermediate-term survival

Pulmonary Hypertension in Sarcoidosis

Classification
WHO Group 5 cause of pulmonary hypertension (multifactorial and/or unclear mechanisms)
  • Significantly contributes to morbidity and mortality
  • Mechanisms: Granulomatous vasculitis; pulmonary arterial occlusion by lymphadenopathy; thrombosis; pulmonary venous occlusion; destruction of vascular bed; left heart dysfunction from myocardial involvement; portopulmonary hypertension (associated liver cirrhosis); hypoxic vasoconstriction from parenchymal disease; rarely resembles idiopathic PAH
  • When to evaluate: Symptoms disproportionate to parenchymal disease, extensive parenchymal abnormalities, desaturation on 6-min walk test, low diffusing capacity
  • Diagnosis: Echocardiography (screening) → Right heart catheterization (gold standard)
  • Treatment: Corticosteroids may improve pressures in some; pulmonary vasodilator therapy on case-by-case basis

Nervous System & Cutaneous Involvement

Neurosarcoidosis

  • Highly associated with systemic disease but can occur without significant pulmonary or systemic disease
  • Predilection for the base of the brain
  • Most common manifestation: Cranial nerve involvement (facial + optic nerves most common) — result of granulomatous basal meningitis OR direct nerve involvement
  • Facial nerve palsy — good prognosis compared to other neurologic findings
  • Hypothalamus/pituitary involvement → neuroendocrine abnormalities (can persist even after immunosuppressive treatment)
  • Other findings: space-occupying masses, acute/chronic meningitis, peripheral neuropathy, neuromuscular or spinal cord involvement
  • Rare: seizures, neuropsychiatric symptoms
  • Small-fiber neuropathy — increasingly recognized as cause of pain and autonomic dysfunction
  • Pain syndromes often do not respond well to immunosuppressives regardless of underlying cause

Neurosarcoidosis Workup

  • Gadolinium-enhanced MRI — preferred; evaluates brain parenchyma, meninges, dura, spinal cord; leptomeningeal/parenchymal enhancement or multiple periventricular white matter lesions are sensitive but nonspecific (can mimic malignancy/infection)
  • CSF analysis: Mononuclear cell pleocytosis, elevated proteins, elevated ACE, soluble IL-2 receptor, lysozyme, decreased glucose, oligoclonal bands — supportive but not diagnostic; important to exclude infection
  • Chest imaging often helpful — many have pulmonary abnormality that can be biopsied
  • Histologic confirmation — when significant diagnostic uncertainty or not responding to therapy
  • Large-fiber neuropathy: nerve biopsy or nerve conduction studies

Cutaneous Sarcoidosis

  • Most common: maculopapular, nodular eruptions, and plaques (granulomatous involvement)
  • Other: infiltration of old scars/tattoos, hypo/hyperpigmented areas, scales, annular lesions, ulcerations, subcutaneous nodules
  • Most lesions are not painful or pruritic
Erythema Nodosum
  • Nonspecific, nongranulomatous (reactive, not direct granulomatous infiltration)
  • Raised, tender, erythematous bumps/nodules on anterior legs
  • Common in Europeans and women; prominent feature of Löfgren syndrome
  • Remits spontaneously within 6 weeks
  • Uncommon in Japanese and Black populations
Lupus Pernio — Boards High-Yield
  • Distinctive chronic lesion: indurated plaques + violaceous discoloration of nose, cheeks, lips, ears; frequently involves nasal mucosa
  • Most common in African-American women
  • Adverse prognostic factor — associated with bone cysts, pulmonary fibrosis, prolonged clinical course
  • Can allow clinical diagnosis without biopsy in classic cases
  • Treatment: Topical or injected corticosteroids; disfiguring/symptomatic lesions may need systemic therapy

Other Organ Involvement

Eye (Ocular Sarcoidosis)

  • Can affect any part of eye or orbit; ranges from asymptomatic to permanent vision loss
  • Ophthalmologic exam recommended on initial evaluation AND with any change in visual symptoms
  • Uveitis (anterior, posterior, or panuveitis) — most common manifestation, often bilateral; chronic uveitis complications: synechiae, glaucoma, cataracts, cystoid macular edema, blindness
  • Optic neuropathy → sudden permanent vision loss → treat as emergency with immediate immunosuppression
  • Other: conjunctival/eyelid granulomas, lacrimal gland enlargement, keratoconjunctivitis sicca, dacryocystitis, retinal vasculitis, periorbital swelling
  • Conjunctival nodule biopsy — relatively noninvasive diagnostic approach

Liver

  • Hepatic granulomas in up to 80% of pulmonary sarcoidosis patients on autopsy; clinically significant in up to 15%
  • Hepatic sarcoidosis can occur without lung involvement
  • LFTs abnormal in up to one-third; elevated alkaline phosphatase most common
  • Elevated transaminases also frequent; elevated bilirubin → more significant progressive disease
  • Hepatomegaly common; imaging shows small innumerable low-attenuation nodules
  • Symptoms: abdominal pain, pruritus, fevers, weight loss, jaundice
  • Rare progression: chronic cholestasis, hepatocellular disease, portal hypertension, Budd-Chiari syndrome, cirrhosis
  • Treatment: immunosuppressives (effective in some); liver transplantation for hepatic failure

Musculoskeletal

  • Acute arthritis (as in Löfgren syndrome) — common early; pain is an indication to treat
  • Chronic arthritis/synovitis — rarer than acute presentations
  • Most common joints: Ankles, knees, elbows, wrists, small joints of hands and feet
  • Bone lesions: Lacy, reticular osteolytic lesions on X-ray — often asymptomatic; occasionally painful; cystic bone lesions = adverse prognostic factor
  • Bone marrow involvement → anemia and lymphopenia
  • Muscle involvement: Pain, tenderness, weakness (myositis, chronic myopathy, muscle nodules); myopathy more common in women; can be sole presentation of disease

Hypercalcemia — Mechanism is Critical

Pathophysiology — Must Know
Activated macrophages and granulomas produce 1,25-hydroxyvitamin D3 (calcitriol) via 1-alpha-hydroxylase (CYP27B1), driven by IFN-γ. This conversion occurs unregulated (unlike in the kidney, which is controlled by PTH), causing:
  • Increased intestinal calcium absorption
  • Increased bone resorption
  • Elevated serum calcium despite low PTH (suppressed by hypercalcemia)
  • If untreated → nephrocalcinosis → renal dysfunction
  • Hypercalcemia: only in 10–20% of sarcoidosis patients
  • Hypercalciuria: much more frequent — up to 50% of patients
  • Management: Monitor vitamin D intake, avoid excessive sunlight, maintain hydration
  • Severe acute symptomatic hypercalcemia → hospital admission + urgent corticosteroid therapy

Kidney

  • Renal dysfunction mostly from hypercalcemia and nephrocalcinosis — not direct granulomatous infiltration
  • Sarcoidosis can cause interstitial nephritis (with or without granulomas) — generally responsive to treatment
  • Urinalysis: sterile pyuria, bland sediment, or mild proteinuria

Lymphoid System, Parotid Glands & Other

Lymphoid System
  • Peripheral lymphadenopathy — presenting sign in minority of cases; cervical, axillary, epitrochlear, inguinal most common extrathoracic glands
  • Enlarged glands: discrete, movable, nontender — no ulceration or drainage
  • Splenomegaly — common but usually clinically silent; hypodense nodules on imaging
  • Rarely: splenic involvement causes pressure, pain, constitutional symptoms, hematologic abnormalities → may require splenectomy
Parotid & Other
  • Parotid/salivary involvement: 4% of patients; associated with Heerfordt syndrome
  • Can present asymptomatically or with painful swollen glands / xerostomia; self-limiting; can resemble Sjögren's
  • GI tract (rare): Most commonly stomach; symptoms: dysphagia, bleeding, nausea/vomiting, pain, weight loss
  • Reproductive: Female uterus or male epididymis; any new nodules require malignancy exclusion

Clinical Course & Prognosis

Overall Course

  • >50% of patients resolve spontaneously — full remission without treatment
  • 30–40% develop chronic disease threatening vital organs or causing debilitating symptoms → require treatment
  • Chronic symptoms can be progressive or waxing and waning; predicting course in any individual is difficult
  • Most cases show improvement in CXR or spirometry after 2 years of follow-up, whether treated or untreated
  • Serious extrapulmonary involvement (cardiac, CNS, hepatic) occurs in 4–7% at presentation and can develop as disease evolves
  • Relapses common — associated with shorter treatment duration; occur in >1/3 of patients

Mortality

  • Death in 1–5% of patients — causes:
Pulmonary Deaths (US/Europe)
  • Progressive respiratory insufficiency
  • Complications of pulmonary scarring: hemoptysis, infection, aspergilloma
  • Leading cause in Western populations
Other Causes
  • Cardiac: heart failure, cardiac block, sudden cardiac death, arrhythmias (predominant in Japan)
  • CNS involvement
  • Liver failure
  • Complications of immunosuppressive therapies

Clinical Prognostic Factors

Good Prognosis
  • Löfgren syndrome — spontaneous remission up to 80%; occurs in 20–30% of Caucasians (esp. Northern European), <5% of Asians/African Americans
  • Heerfordt syndrome — spontaneous remission up to 80%
  • Erythema nodosum + fever → remit within 6 weeks; lymphadenopathy resolution delayed >1 year
  • Facial nerve palsy
  • Higher BAL CD4:CD8 ratio (relatively better prognosis)
  • Robust initial immune response vs. subacute/chronic presentation
Adverse Prognostic Factors
  • Lupus pernio
  • Chronic uveitis
  • Older age at onset
  • Chronic hypercalcemia + nephrocalcinosis
  • African origins
  • Pulmonary hypertension
  • Fibrotic pulmonary sarcoidosis
  • Sinus involvement
  • Cystic bone lesions
  • Neurosarcoidosis
  • Cardiac involvement
Key Concept — Scadding Stages ≠ Prognosis
Parenchymal infiltrates on the Scadding classification do NOT predict activity, progression of lung disease, or extrapulmonary involvement. They only reveal overall resolution rates. Do NOT use radiographic stage to guide decisions about extrapulmonary screening.

Follow-Up Schedule

  • First 2 years after presentation: Most intensive monitoring period — decreasing frequency as time passes
  • After starting therapy: Follow-up in 1–3 months, then every 3–6 months once stable
  • After full remission: Follow for a few years to assess for relapses; lengthier observation needed for severe disease or residual organ dysfunction
  • Follow-up testing: symptoms, complete physical exam, imaging of affected organs, periodic PFTs, markers of specific organ dysfunction
  • Any new symptoms → full evaluation of that organ system
12

Treatment

Goals & Philosophy

No Cure — Goals of Therapy
  • Suppress granulomatous inflammation
  • Prevent irreversible organ fibrosis
  • Alleviate debilitating symptoms

Treatment decisions guided by expert opinion, uncontrolled trials, and only a few larger RCTs. Every decision requires weighing risk of immunosuppression vs. benefit of treatment.

When NOT to Treat:

  • Asymptomatic Stage I disease — high inherent rate of spontaneous resolution
  • Mild symptoms — monitor closely; topical therapy preferred for skin if possible

Indications for Oral Immunosuppressive Therapy:

  • Significant symptoms or disfigurement
  • Dangerous impending organ damage
  • Pulmonary: worsening cough/dyspnea, moderate-to-severe parenchymal involvement, progressive decline in PFTs
  • Life- or organ-threatening situations: CNS involvement, ocular involvement with vision loss, extensive myocardial inflammation

First-Line: Corticosteroids

Prednisone Dosing Protocol
  • Induction: Prednisone 0.5 mg/kg/day or ~20–40 mg/day × 4 weeks
  • Maintenance: Taper to control symptoms/progression — ideally <10 mg/day
  • Higher starting doses NOT more effective + associated with increased morbidity
  • Consider taper early based on clinical improvement to minimize toxicity
  • Greatest pulmonary improvement in first weeks to months of therapy
  • Limited data beyond 2 years — unclear if oral steroids modify long-term progression or mortality
  • Inhaled CS: May be considered for very mild pulmonary cough symptom control — NOT shown to benefit the larger sarcoidosis population
  • Monitoring: bone health (bisphosphonates for osteoporosis prevention), weight gain, diabetes risk, eye exams (glaucoma and cataracts)
  • More aggressive doses justified in: CNS involvement, vision-threatening ocular disease, extensive myocardial inflammation

Second-Line: Steroid-Sparing Agents

Indicated for: intolerance or toxicity to CS, inability to taper CS, or refractory disease. Choice guided by alcohol use, pregnancy plans, comorbidities (liver/renal dysfunction), pharmacogenetic profiles.

Third-Line: TNF Inhibitors (Refractory Disease)

Why TNF Inhibitors Work
TNF-α is produced by activated macrophages of the granuloma and drives propagation of inflammation. Targeting it interrupts the granulomatous cascade.
Infliximab (Monoclonal Ab) — Most Evidence
  • Dose: 3–5 mg/kg IV at weeks 0 & 2, then every 4–8 weeks
  • Has RCT data + larger case series showing positive effects on PFTs, imaging, and inflammatory cytokines
  • Particularly effective in more severe disease
  • Pre-treatment: Screen for tuberculosis (TB reactivation risk)
  • Contraindicated in heart failure
  • Allergic/infusion reactions possible
  • Long-term risks: demyelination syndrome, malignancy
  • Can paradoxically induce sarcoid-like reactions
Adalimumab vs. Etanercept
  • Adalimumab: 40 mg SQ every 1–2 weeks (exact dose unknown); monoclonal antibody; similar precautions to infliximab; efficacy esp. in ocular sarcoidosis
  • Etanercept: TNF receptor antagonist (not monoclonal Ab) → INEFFECTIVE for sarcoidosis — do NOT use
  • Other refractory options: Anti-B-cell therapy; antifibrotic medications

Treatment Duration

  • ~1 year for most patients
  • Shorter courses → higher relapse rates (though can be considered for very responsive cases)
  • Longer courses necessary for life-threatening or refractory disease
  • Relapses: Treat with the last known effective dosing regimen
  • Greatest pulmonary improvement in first weeks to months → taper early to minimize toxicity

Approach to the Whole Patient

  • Fatigue: >80% of sarcoidosis patients experience multifactorial fatigue; can persist even after treatment and remission; due to medications, sleep disorders, pain, and inflammation itself; associated with extrapulmonary involvement
  • Depression: Highly prevalent; associated with female sex, dyspnea, and poor access to care
  • Cognitive impairment: Well-noted, poorly understood; contributes to decreased QOL
  • Pain syndromes (neuropathic, fibromyalgia, autonomic dysfunction): often do NOT respond well to immunosuppressives → symptomatic care needed
  • Physical training: Formalized programs improve exercise capacity, strength, and fatigue
  • Pregnancy risk: All second-line agents have adverse reproductive effects — counsel patients
  • Organ transplantation: Occasionally necessary for progressive end-organ damage (lungs, liver, heart, kidney); sarcoidosis can recur in transplanted organs but rarely causes post-transplant organ failure

Exam Traps

Trap 01Smoking is PROTECTIVE in Sarcoidosis
You see sarcoidosis — should smoking history push the diagnosis toward or away from it?
Away from it. Smoking is a negative predictor of incident sarcoidosis — it is consistently associated with reduced odds of developing the disease. The mechanism is thought to involve immunomodulatory effects of nicotine and smoke. This counterintuitive fact makes it a favorite board question: do NOT assume that a smoker cannot have sarcoidosis, but recognize that smoking actually makes sarcoidosis less likely, not more.
Trap 02Löfgren Syndrome: Watchful Waiting, NOT Immunosuppression
A patient has fever, erythema nodosum, bilateral ankle arthralgias, and bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy. What is your treatment?
Watchful waiting. This is classic Löfgren syndrome, which has spontaneous remission in up to 80% of cases. Immunosuppressive therapy is rarely necessary. Erythema nodosum and fever typically remit within 6 weeks on their own. Jumping to systemic steroids is a trap — reserve treatment for those who do not resolve spontaneously or who have significant symptoms requiring control.
Trap 03Scadding Stages Do NOT Predict Activity or Extrapulmonary Involvement
A patient is found to have Stage 3 sarcoidosis on CXR. Can you use this to predict disease activity, likelihood of cardiac involvement, or need for treatment?
No. Scadding radiographic stages only reveal overall resolution rates — they do NOT predict disease activity, progression of lung disease, or the presence of extrapulmonary involvement. A patient with Stage 1 can have life-threatening cardiac sarcoidosis. Every sarcoidosis patient requires a baseline ECG and full organ assessment regardless of their radiographic stage.
Trap 04Hypercalcemia in Sarcoidosis: Low PTH, Not High
A sarcoidosis patient has hypercalcemia. Is PTH elevated, low, or normal? How does this differ from primary hyperparathyroidism?
PTH is LOW (suppressed). Mechanism: activated macrophages use CYP27B1 (1-alpha-hydroxylase) to convert vitamin D to calcitriol unregulated — bypassing the normal PTH-controlled kidney pathway. Elevated calcitriol raises calcium, which then suppresses PTH via normal feedback. Contrast with primary hyperparathyroidism where PTH is HIGH. In sarcoidosis: Ca ↑, PTH ↓, Calcitriol ↑. If you see Ca ↑ + PTH ↑ — think primary hyperPTH, not sarcoidosis.
Trap 05Etanercept is INEFFECTIVE — Use Infliximab or Adalimumab
Which TNF-targeting agent is contraindicated in sarcoidosis?
Etanercept. Despite targeting the TNF pathway, etanercept (a TNF receptor antagonist) has been shown to be ineffective for sarcoidosis and should not be used. The reason is not fully understood but may relate to the difference between blocking TNF vs. its receptor. Use infliximab (most evidence; RCT data) or adalimumab (monoclonal antibodies) instead. Boards will test this distinction — "TNF antagonist" does not mean "useful in sarcoidosis."
Trap 06Cardiac Nuclear Imaging: Reverse Redistribution = Opposite of CAD
Thallium scintigraphy shows perfusion defects at rest that improve with exercise. What does this suggest?
Cardiac sarcoidosis. This pattern is called reverse redistribution — defects appear at rest (granulomatous inflammation or fibrosis) and improve with exercise or dipyridamole. This is the opposite of coronary artery disease, where defects appear or worsen with stress (ischemia). In CAD: rest normal → stress defect. In cardiac sarcoidosis: rest defect → exercise improvement. This pearl is high-yield for distinguishing the two conditions.
Trap 07Lupus Pernio = Adverse Prognosis, NOT Just a Cosmetic Finding
A patient has violaceous plaques on the nose, cheeks, lips, and ears. Is this purely cosmetic?
Absolutely not. Lupus pernio is a chronic, disfiguring lesion that is a major adverse prognostic factor — associated with bone cysts, pulmonary fibrosis, and a prolonged clinical course. It is most common in African-American women. Do NOT treat it as cosmetic; it signals systemic severe sarcoidosis requiring aggressive workup. It can also allow clinical diagnosis without biopsy in classic cases (along with Löfgren/Heerfordt syndromes).
Trap 08CD4:CD8 Ratio >3.5: Supports Diagnosis Only — Does Not Predict Progression
A patient has BAL showing CD4:CD8 ratio of 5.0. Does this confirm sarcoidosis? Does a normal ratio exclude it? Does this ratio predict who will progress?
No to all three. A CD4:CD8 ratio >3.5 supports the diagnosis when combined with consistent clinical and radiographic findings — it does NOT confirm it. A normal ratio does NOT exclude sarcoidosis. The ratio does NOT correlate with disease progression or need for treatment. However, higher values tend to implicate a better overall prognosis. In contrast, elevated BAL neutrophils or eosinophils are associated with worse prognosis.
Trap 09Hydroxychloroquine: Skin and Hypercalcemia ONLY — NOT Pulmonary
Can you use hydroxychloroquine to treat pulmonary sarcoidosis?
No. Hydroxychloroquine is effective for skin disease and hypercalcemia but is not recommended for pulmonary sarcoidosis. Do not confuse its utility in rheumatoid arthritis and lupus with broad applicability in sarcoidosis. If you see a question asking which agent to use for refractory lung disease — hydroxychloroquine is not the answer. For pulmonary disease, use methotrexate, azathioprine, or leflunomide as second-line agents.
Trap 10Optic Neuropathy = Emergency → Immediate Immunosuppression
A sarcoidosis patient presents with acute vision loss. How urgent is this?
This is an emergency. Optic neuropathy in sarcoidosis can lead to sudden permanent vision loss and must be treated with immediate initiation of immunosuppressive therapy. Do not delay for additional workup. The clock is ticking — delay means permanent blindness. Contrast this with uveitis, which is more chronic and allows a more measured approach (though still warrants prompt attention).
Trap 11Serum ACE: Monitoring Tool Only — NOT Diagnostic
A patient has elevated serum ACE. Does this confirm sarcoidosis?
No. Serum ACE has low sensitivity and is not a clinically useful diagnostic tool — it is also elevated in tuberculosis, histoplasmosis, diabetes, and other conditions. Its value lies in monitoring systemic disease activity over time: it correlates roughly with granulomatous burden and can track response to therapy. A normal ACE does not exclude sarcoidosis; an elevated ACE does not confirm it. Never use it as a standalone diagnostic test.
Trap 12Cardiac Screening Required for EVERY Sarcoidosis Patient — Regardless of Pulmonary Severity
Your sarcoidosis patient has minimal pulmonary disease. Do they need cardiac screening?
Yes — always. Severity of pulmonary involvement does NOT predict the presence of cardiac sarcoidosis. Isolated cardiac sarcoidosis can occur. An ECG must be obtained on initial evaluation of every sarcoidosis patient, regardless of pulmonary severity. Cardiac sarcoidosis causes AV block, ventricular tachycardia, heart failure, and sudden death — and it can be the initial presentation of sarcoidosis. Pulmonary Stage 1 does not give you permission to skip cardiac screening.
