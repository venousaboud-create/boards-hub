Section 1

Definition & Staging

AKI is defined as impairment of kidney filtration and excretory function over days to weeks (generally known/expected to have occurred within 7 days), resulting in retention of nitrogenous and other waste products normally cleared by the kidneys.

📋 Mechanism KDIGO Definition — Diagnostic Criteria
▼
  • Rise in SCr ≥ 0.3 mg/dL within 48 hours
  • Rise in SCr ≥ 1.5× baseline within 7 days
  • Urine output <0.5 mL/kg/h for >6 hours

Any ONE criterion is sufficient. AKI is a clinical diagnosis, not structural — a patient may have AKI with or without parenchymal injury.

⚠️
Critical Concept: AKI is a clinical diagnosis, NOT a structural one. A patient may have AKI with or without actual injury to the kidney parenchyma — making the term technically a misnomer. Prerenal azotemia has no parenchymal damage.

Clinical Pearl: AKI can range from asymptomatic lab changes to overwhelming, rapidly fatal derangements in volume, waste excretion, electrolyte balance, and acid-base homeostasis.
Section 2

Epidemiology

Hospital-Acquired AKI
  • Complicates 5–7% of acute-care admissions
  • Complicates up to 30% of ICU admissions
  • ICU morbidity exceeds 50% in many studies
  • Incidence grown >4-fold since 1988 in USA
  • Yearly incidence: 500/100,000 — higher than stroke
Community-Acquired AKI
  • Volume depletion
  • Heart failure
  • Adverse medication effects
  • Urinary tract obstruction
  • Malignancy

Most Common Hospital Settings for AKI

  • Sepsis (most common)
  • Major surgical procedures
  • Critical illness involving heart or liver failure
  • Nephrotoxic medication administration

Long-Term Implications

Mortality Risk

Significantly increased in-hospital and long-term mortality; longer length of stay; increased costs

CKD Risk

AKI increases risk of future CKD. Survivors of dialysis-requiring AKI: up to 10% develop ESKD

Cardiovascular Risk

AKI associated with increased risk of future cardiovascular disease events (mechanisms not fully understood)

AKI in the Developing World

Epidemiology differs due to demographics, economics, environmental factors, and comorbid disease burden. Region-specific etiologies include:

  • Envenomations: snakes, spiders, caterpillars, bees
  • Infectious causes: malaria, leptospirosis
  • Crush injuries/rhabdomyolysis from earthquakes
  • Resources to diagnose and manage AKI are often limited
Section 3

Etiology Overview

The causes of AKI are traditionally divided into three broad categories:

1 · Prerenal Azotemia

Most common. Results from inadequate renal plasma flow → reduced intraglomerular hydrostatic pressure → ↓ GFR

  • Hypovolemia
  • ↓ Cardiac output
  • Impaired autoregulation (NSAIDs, ACE-I/ARB)
  • Hepatorenal syndrome
2 · Intrinsic AKI

Direct injury to renal parenchyma

  • Sepsis-associated
  • Ischemia (ATN)
  • Nephrotoxins (exo/endogenous)
  • Glomerulonephritis
  • Tubulointerstitial nephritis
  • Vascular (TTP-HUS, vasculitis)
3 · Postrenal AKI

Obstruction of urine flow → ↑ retrograde hydrostatic pressure → ↓ GFR

  • Bladder outlet obstruction (BPH)
  • Bilateral ureteral obstruction
  • Unilateral (if solitary kidney)
Section 4

Prerenal Azotemia

Mechanisms of Normal GFR Autoregulation

Normal GFR is maintained by renal blood flow and the relative resistances of afferent/efferent arterioles. Under reduced perfusion, compensatory responses include:

  • Afferent vasodilation: myogenic reflex + vasodilatory prostaglandins (prostacyclin, PGE₂) + NO via tubuloglomerular feedback from macula densa
  • Efferent vasoconstriction: angiotensin II → maintains GFR despite ↓ renal blood flow (raises filtration fraction)
  • Neurohormonal response: angiotensin II, norepinephrine, vasopressin → enhanced Na⁺/water reabsorption
⚠️ Caution Autoregulation Limit
▼

Even in healthy adults, renal autoregulation fails once systolic BP falls below 80 mmHg. Beyond this threshold, GFR drops proportionally with perfusion pressure regardless of compensatory mechanisms.

How Drugs Disrupt Autoregulation

NSAIDs
  • Inhibit prostaglandin synthesis
  • Block afferent vasodilation
  • ↑ Afferent resistance → ↓ GFR
  • Risk especially when renal perfusion ↓
vs.
ACE-I / ARB
  • Block angiotensin II action
  • Dilate efferent arteriole
  • ↓ Filtration fraction → ↓ GFR
  • Especially dangerous in bilateral RAS or solitary kidney
🚨 Critical Danger NSAIDs + ACE-I/ARB — Highest Risk Combination
▼

Using NSAIDs together with ACE-I or ARBs blocks both autoregulatory mechanisms simultaneously:

  • NSAIDs → eliminate prostaglandin-mediated afferent vasodilation
  • ACE-I/ARB → eliminate angiotensin II–mediated efferent vasoconstriction

Result: catastrophic fall in GFR, especially dangerous in bilateral RAS or solitary functioning kidney.

Factors That Impair Autoregulatory Response

  • Atherosclerosis, chronic hypertension, older age: hyalinosis + myointimal hyperplasia → structural narrowing → impaired afferent vasodilation
  • CKD: afferent vasodilation may already be at maximal capacity
  • Coexisting insults: sepsis, vasoactive/nephrotoxic drugs, rhabdomyolysis, burns, pancreatitis

SGLT-2 Inhibitors — Important Exception

💎 Pearl SGLT-2 Inhibitors — Protective, Not Harmful
▼

SGLT-2 inhibitors lower intraglomerular pressure and induce natriuresis, yet do NOT increase AKI risk — in fact, recent studies suggest a protective effect against AKI. This distinguishes them from ACE-I/ARBs despite a superficially similar hemodynamic effect.

Hepatorenal Syndrome

  • AKI complicating advanced liver disease — diagnosis of exclusion
  • Hemodynamics resemble prerenal azotemia but in the setting of total-body volume overload
  • Mechanism: primary arterial vasodilation in splanchnic circulation → systemic hypotension → vasoconstrictor activation
  • Triggered by: volume depletion, spontaneous bacterial peritonitis
Type 1 HRS — Severe
  • AKI persists despite volume administration and diuretic withdrawal
  • Particularly poor prognosis
Type 2 HRS — Less Severe
  • Less severe form
  • Characterized mainly by refractory ascites
Section 5

Intrinsic AKI

Most common causes: sepsis, ischemia, and nephrotoxins (both endogenous and exogenous). Intrinsic AKI can be classified anatomically by the major site of damage.

Sepsis-Associated AKI

  • >1 million sepsis cases/year in USA; AKI complicates >50% of severe sepsis cases
  • AKI greatly increases the risk of death in sepsis
  • AKI can occur even without overt hypotension (decreased GFR without frank shock)
  • Reduced urine output is common

Pathophysiology of Sepsis-AKI

  • Generalized arterial vasodilation mediated by cytokines → upregulation of inducible NO synthase → can cause afferent vasodilation (early) or renal vasoconstriction (from SNS, RAAS, vasopressin, endothelin)
  • Endothelial damage: ↑ microvascular leukocyte adhesion and migration, thrombosis, ↑ permeability, ↑ interstitial pressure, ↓ local tubular blood flow, reactive oxygen species → tubular cell injury
  • Postmortem studies: Frank tubular necrosis often absent — inflammation, mitochondrial dysfunction, and interstitial edema may be the dominant contributors
  • AKI also predisposes to sepsis (bidirectional relationship)
🦠
Viral AKI: Hantavirus, dengue, SARS-CoV-2 can cause AKI. SARS-CoV-2 mechanisms: cytokine storm → diffuse intrarenal vasoconstriction + generalized hypercoagulable state → impaired intrarenal blood flow.

Ischemia-Associated AKI

Kidney's Unique Vulnerability
  • Receives 20% of cardiac output
  • Accounts for 10% of resting O₂ consumption
  • Only 0.5% of body mass
  • Renal medulla = one of most hypoxic regions in the body
Outer Medulla Vulnerability
  • Vascular architecture makes outer medulla most vulnerable
  • ↑ Leukocyte-endothelial interactions → ↓ local blood flow
  • S3 segment of proximal tubule most at risk
  • Depends on oxidative metabolism; mitochondrial dysfunction → impaired ATP generation

Mechanisms Reducing GFR in Ischemia

  • Preglomerular vasoconstriction: tubuloglomerular feedback activation from enhanced solute delivery to macula densa (after proximal tubule injury), increased basal vascular tone, ↓ vasodilator responsiveness
  • Backleak of filtrate across damaged tubular epithelium
  • Tubular obstruction from necrotic debris
  • Mitochondrial dysfunction → reactive oxygen species
⚠️
Transient ischemia alone in a normal kidney is usually NOT sufficient to cause severe AKI — even after total interruption of renal blood flow (e.g., suprarenal aortic clamping). Prerenal azotemia and ischemia-associated AKI represent a continuum.

Postoperative AKI

Procedures most commonly associated with AKI:

  • Cardiac surgery with cardiopulmonary bypass (especially combined valve + bypass) — severe AKI requiring dialysis in ~1%
  • Vascular procedures with aortic cross-clamping
  • Major intraperitoneal procedures

CPB-Specific Mechanisms of AKI

  • Ischemia from sustained hypoperfusion
  • Extracorporeal circuit activation of leukocytes and inflammation
  • Hemolysis → pigment nephropathy
  • Aortic injury → atheroemboli
  • Longer CPB duration = higher AKI risk

Burns & Acute Pancreatitis

  • Burns: AKI affects 25% of those with >10% total body surface area involvement
  • Mechanisms: severe hypovolemia, dysregulated inflammation, ↑ sepsis risk, ↑ acute lung injury risk
  • Massive fluid resuscitation → abdominal compartment syndrome (intra-abdominal pressure >20 mmHg → renal vein compression → ↓ GFR)

Glomerulonephritis

  • Less common (~5%) cause of AKI
  • Early recognition is critical — can respond to timely immunosuppression or plasma exchange, potentially reversing AKI
  • Diseases involving glomerular podocytes, mesangial, and/or endothelial cells → compromise filtration barrier and renal blood flow

Anticoagulant-Related Nephropathy

Excessive anticoagulation (warfarin or others) → glomerular hemorrhage → obstructing RBC casts in tubules → tubular injury.

Section 6

Nephrotoxin-Associated AKI

The kidney's high susceptibility to nephrotoxins is due to extremely high blood perfusion and concentration of filtered substances along the nephron. All structures are vulnerable. Risk factors: older age, CKD, prerenal azotemia, hypoalbuminemia.

Contrast Agents (Iodinated)

CI-AKIContrast-Associated AKI
Terminology: "Contrast nephropathy" → now "contrast-associated AKI" (CA-AKI) or "contrast-induced AKI" (CI-AKI — smaller subgroup)
Risk: Negligible with normal renal function; ↑ in CKD, especially diabetic kidney disease + congestive heart failure
Typical course: SCr rises 24–48h post-exposure → peaks 3–5 days → resolves within 1 week
High-risk: Multiple myeloma + renal disease
Gadolinium: High-dose → nephrogenic systemic fibrosis (NSF) in advanced CKD/AKI. Group I gadolinium agents (rare now); Group II agents have very low AKI risk
💎
Dialysis-requiring AKI from contrast is uncommon EXCEPT when significant preexisting CKD coexists with CHF or other ischemic insults.

Aminoglycosides

GentamicinTobramycinAmikacin
Mechanism: Freely filtered → accumulate in renal cortex (concentrations >> plasma) → tubular necrosis
Incidence: Nonoliguric AKI in 10–30% of courses, even with therapeutic plasma levels
Timing: AKI typically manifests after 5–7 days of therapy; can appear even after drug discontinuation
Labs: Hypomagnesemia (common finding)

Amphotericin B

Mechanisms: (1) Renal vasoconstriction via ↑ tubuloglomerular feedback; (2) Direct tubular toxicity via reactive oxygen species; (3) Binds tubular membrane cholesterol → pores
Dose-dependent: Nephrotoxicity is dose AND duration dependent
Clinical features: Polyuria, hypomagnesemia, hypocalcemia, nongap metabolic acidosis

Vancomycin

Risk factors: High trough levels + combined with other nephrotoxic antibiotics
Mechanism: Tubular injury OR crystallization in tubules → intratubular obstruction

Acyclovir & Antiviral Agents

AcyclovirFoscarnetPentamidineTenofovirCidofovir
Acyclovir: Precipitates in tubules → AKI by tubular obstruction, especially with IV bolus at high doses (500 mg/m²) or hypovolemia
Others: Associated with AKI via tubular toxicity

Chemotherapeutic Agents

CisplatinCarboplatinIfosfamideBevacizumabMitomycin CGemcitabine
Cisplatin/Carboplatin: Accumulated by proximal tubular cells → necrosis + apoptosis; intensive hydration reduces risk but remains dose-limiting toxicity
Ifosfamide: Hemorrhagic cystitis + tubular toxicity → Type II RTA (Fanconi syndrome), polyuria, hypokalemia, modest ↓ GFR
Bevacizumab (anti-VEGF): Proteinuria + hypertension via glomerular microvasculature injury (thrombotic microangiopathy)
Checkpoint inhibitors (ipilimumab, nivolumab, pembrolizumab, tremelimumab): Immune-related adverse events → acute interstitial nephritis. Risk factors: low GFR, PPI use, extrarenal immune events

Toxic Ingestions

  • Ethylene glycol (antifreeze) → metabolized to oxalic acid, glycolaldehyde, glyoxylate → direct tubular injury + tubular obstruction
  • Diethylene glycol → metabolite HEAA → tubular injury; has caused outbreaks from adulterated pharmaceuticals
  • Melamine (food contamination) → nephrolithiasis + AKI via intratubular obstruction or direct toxicity
  • Aristolochic acid → "Chinese herb nephropathy" + "Balkan nephropathy"

Endogenous Toxins

Rhabdomyolysis
  • Myoglobin released from injured muscle cells
  • Causes: traumatic crush injuries, muscle ischemia (vascular/orthopedic surgery), compression in coma, seizures, excessive exercise, heat stroke, malignant hyperthermia, infections, metabolic disorders (hypophosphatemia, hypothyroidism), myopathies
  • Pathogenesis: intrarenal vasoconstriction + direct proximal tubular toxicity + mechanical obstruction when myoglobin precipitates with Tamm-Horsfall protein (uromodulin) — favored by acidic urine
Hemolysis
  • Hemoglobin released during massive hemolysis → pigment nephropathy
  • Similar mechanism to myoglobin nephropathy
  • Labs: red/brown urine that persists in supernatant after centrifugation
Tumor Lysis Syndrome
  • Follows cytotoxic therapy in high-grade lymphomas or ALL
  • Massive uric acid release (serum levels often >15 mg/dL) → precipitation in renal tubules
  • Features: hyperkalemia, hyperphosphatemia, hypocalcemia
  • Can occur spontaneously or with solid tumors/myeloma
Myeloma Light Chains
  • Glomerular damage AND/OR direct tubular toxicity
  • Bind Tamm-Horsfall protein → obstructing intratubular casts
  • Hypercalcemia (seen in myeloma) → intense renal vasoconstriction + inhibition of Na⁺/water reabsorption → volume depletion

Acute Tubulointerstitial Nephritis (AIN)

  • Drugs responsible for ~75% of biopsy-proven acute interstitial nephritis
  • Common culprits: PPIs, NSAIDs, antibiotics (penicillins, cephalosporins, quinolones, sulfonamides, rifampin), immune checkpoint inhibitors
  • Non-drug causes: TINU syndrome, lupus, viral infection (COVID, HIV, hantavirus), Legionella
  • Clinical: eosinophilia, sterile pyuria, often nonoliguric; fever, arthralgias, pruritic rash (classic triad)
  • Absence of systemic hypersensitivity does NOT exclude AIN — kidney biopsy may be needed
Section 7

Postrenal AKI

Occurs when normally unidirectional urine flow is acutely blocked (partially or totally) → ↑ retrograde hydrostatic pressure → impaired glomerular filtration.

⚠️
Key Concept: Normal urinary flow rate does NOT rule out partial obstruction — GFR is normally two orders of magnitude higher than urinary flow rate. Preserved urine output can mask significant postrenal obstruction.

Requirements for AKI

  • For moderate-to-severe AKI: obstruction must affect both kidneys (unless asymmetric baseline function)
  • Unilateral obstruction may cause AKI in: significant underlying CKD with ↓ renal reserve, or (rarely) reflex vasospasm of contralateral kidney

Causes by Level of Obstruction

LocationCauses
Bladder neck / urethraBPH, prostate cancer, neurogenic bladder, anticholinergic drugs, strictures, obstructed Foley catheter, blood clots, calculi
Ureter (bilateral or solitary kidney)Intraluminal: calculi, blood clots, sloughed renal papillae
Wall infiltration: neoplasia
External compression: retroperitoneal fibrosis, neoplasia, abscess, inadvertent surgical damage

Pathophysiology of Postrenal AKI

Acute ureteral/bladder obstruction
↓
↑ Intratubular pressure → initial afferent vasodilation → brief hyperemia
↓
Intrarenal vasoconstriction: ↑ angiotensin II, thromboxane A₂, vasopressin; ↓ NO production
↓
↓ Glomerular perfusion + possible ↓ in ultrafiltration coefficient → ↓ GFR
💎
Clinical Pearl: Prompt recognition and relief of urinary tract obstruction can forestall permanent structural damage. Obstruction can be present WITHOUT radiologic abnormalities (volume depletion, retroperitoneal fibrosis, tumor encasement, early in course) — if high suspicion, perform antegrade or retrograde pyelography.
Section 8

Diagnostic Evaluation

AKI vs. CKD — Key Distinction

AKI
  • Normal-sized kidneys on US
  • Recent rise in SCr
  • No normocytic anemia (unless other cause)
  • Serial SCr showing continued substantial rise
vs.
CKD
  • eGFR <60 OR ACR >30 mg/g for ≥3 months
  • Small, shrunken kidneys with cortical thinning
  • Normocytic anemia (in absence of blood loss)
  • Secondary hyperparathyroidism, hyperphosphatemia, hypocalcemia
⚠️ Caution AKI on CKD — Cannot Be Ruled Out
▼

No set of tests can definitively rule out AKI superimposed on CKD — AKI is a frequent complication in CKD patients.

"Acute kidney disease" = renal dysfunction persisting >1 week but <3 months.

History & Physical — Clues to Etiology

Prerenal Clues
  • Vomiting, diarrhea, poor fluid intake
  • Glycosuria → polyuria (osmotic diuresis)
  • NSAIDs, ACE-I/ARB, diuretics (medications)
  • Signs: orthostatic hypotension, tachycardia, ↓ JVP, dry mucous membranes, ↓ skin turgor
  • Heart failure, liver disease, nephrotic syndrome → ↓ effective circulating volume
Postrenal Clues
  • Prostatic disease history
  • Nephrolithiasis history
  • Pelvic/para-aortic malignancy
  • Colicky flank pain → groin = ureteric obstruction
  • Nocturia, urinary frequency/hesitancy = prostatic disease
  • Abdominal fullness, suprapubic pain = bladder enlargement
Intrinsic / Glomerular Clues
  • Palpable purpura + pulmonary hemorrhage/sinusitis → systemic vasculitis with GN
  • SLE history → lupus nephritis
  • Pregnancy → preeclampsia
  • Tense abdomen → abdominal compartment syndrome
  • Limb ischemia → rhabdomyolysis
Allergic Interstitial Nephritis Clues
  • Fever, arthralgias, pruritic erythematous rash
  • Recent new medication (PPIs, NSAIDs, antibiotics)
  • Absence of systemic features does NOT exclude AIN
  • Biopsy for definitive diagnosis

Urine Output Patterns

  • Complete anuria (early in AKI): uncommon — suggests complete urinary tract obstruction, renal artery occlusion, overwhelming septic shock, severe ischemia with cortical necrosis, or severe proliferative GN/vasculitis
  • Oliguria (<400 mL/24h): usually denotes more severe AKI (lower GFR); associated with worse clinical outcomes
  • Nonoliguric AKI: seen in nephrogenic DI (long-standing obstruction), tubulointerstitial disease, cisplatin/aminoglycoside nephrotoxicity

Proteinuria in AKI

  • Mild (<1 g/day): ischemic or nephrotoxin-induced AKI (without preexisting CKD)
  • Greater proteinuria: glomerular ultrafiltration barrier damage OR light chains (myeloma — NOT detected by dipstick; requires sulfosalicylic acid test or immunoelectrophoresis)
  • Heavy/nephrotic range (>3.5 g/day): GN, vasculitis, NSAIDs (affecting glomerulus + tubulointerstitium)

🔵 Mechanism FeNa — Formula & Interpretation
▼

FeNa (%) = (Urine Na × Plasma Cr) / (Plasma Na × Urine Cr) × 100

  • FeNa <1% → suggestive of prerenal (avid tubular Na⁺ reabsorption)
  • FeNa >1% → suggests intrinsic AKI (impaired tubular reabsorption)
  • Most useful in oliguric patients NOT on diuretics without CKD
  • Low FeNa ≠ proof of prerenal — can be <1% in early sepsis, rhabdomyolysis, contrast nephropathy
  • CKD baseline FeNa may be >1% despite superimposed prerenal state

Blood Laboratory Findings

  • Hyperkalemia, hyperphosphatemia, hypocalcemia: common in AKI
  • Marked hyperphosphatemia + hypocalcemia: suggest rhabdomyolysis or tumor lysis syndrome
  • ↑ Anion gap: uremia (retention of phosphate, hippurate, sulfate, urate)
  • ↑ Anion gap + osmolal gap: suggest ethylene glycol poisoning
  • Low anion gap: suggests multiple myeloma (unmeasured cationic proteins)
  • Schistocytes + ↑ LDH + anemia + thrombocytopenia: TTP/HUS
  • TTP/HUS workup: ADAMTS13 levels, Shiga toxin E. coli testing, genetic evaluation of complement regulatory proteins (atypical HUS — 60–70% have mutations in alternative complement pathway genes)
  • GN/vasculitis: ↓ complement, ↑ ANA, ↑ ANCA, anti-GBM antibodies, cryoglobulins

Novel Biomarkers

  • KIM-1 (Kidney Injury Molecule-1): type 1 transmembrane protein; abundantly expressed in proximal tubular cells injured by ischemia or nephrotoxins (cisplatin); NOT expressed without tubular injury; detectable in urine and plasma
  • NGAL (Neutrophil Gelatinase-Associated Lipocalin): also known as lipocalin-2 or siderocalin; first found in human neutrophil granules; binds iron siderophore complexes; tissue-protective effects in proximal tubule; detected in plasma/urine within 2 hours of CPB-associated AKI; FDA-approved (2023) for early identification of AKI in pediatric patients
  • suPAR (Soluble Urokinase Plasminogen Activator Receptor): plasma levels predict subsequent development of AKI
  • IGFBP7 + TIMP-2 (cell-cycle arrest biomarkers): FDA-approved (2014) combination urinary test for predicting moderate-to-severe AKI risk in critically ill patients
  • CXCL9: recently reported to help distinguish tubulointerstitial nephritis (dominant interstitial inflammation) from other causes
  • Furosemide stress test: In oliguric AKI, IV furosemide 1.0–1.5 mg/kg; urine output <200 mL over 2h → higher risk of progression + need for RRT

Radiologic Evaluation

  • Always consider postrenal AKI — treat early for best results
  • Simple bladder catheterization → rules out urethral obstruction
  • Renal ultrasound or CT: hydroureteronephrosis = obstruction
  • If high suspicion persists with normal imaging → antegrade or retrograde pyelography
  • AKI: normal-sized kidneys expected; CKD: usually smaller (except diabetic nephropathy, HIV-associated, infiltrative diseases)
  • Enlarged kidneys in AKI → consider acute interstitial nephritis or infiltrative diseases

Kidney Biopsy

Indicated when prerenal AKI, postrenal AKI, and ischemic/nephrotoxic AKI are deemed unlikely AND other diagnoses are being considered: GN, vasculitis, interstitial nephritis, myeloma kidney, HUS/TTP, allograft dysfunction. Risk: bleeding (severe and life-threatening in thrombocytopenia/coagulopathy).

Section 10

Complications of AKI

Hypervolemia
  • Major complication of oliguric/anuric AKI
  • Impaired salt/water excretion → weight gain, edema, ↑ JVP, pulmonary edema
  • AKI may induce/exacerbate acute lung injury (↑ vascular permeability, inflammatory infiltration)
  • Recovery: polyuric phase (osmotic diuresis from retained urea + delayed tubular recovery)
Hyperkalemia
  • Especially severe in: rhabdomyolysis, hemolysis, tumor lysis (release of intracellular K⁺)
  • Muscle weakness
  • Most dangerous: cardiac conduction effects → potentially fatal arrhythmias
Metabolic Acidosis
  • Usually elevated anion gap (retained phosphate, hippurate, sulfate, urate)
  • Can worsen with coexisting sepsis, DKA, or respiratory acidosis
Hyperphosphatemia + Hypocalcemia
  • Especially in catabolic patients or rhabdomyolysis/hemolysis/TLS
  • Metastatic calcium phosphate deposition → hypocalcemia
  • Also from vitamin D–PTH–FGF-23 axis disruption
  • Hypocalcemia symptoms: perioral paresthesias, muscle cramps, seizures, carpopedal spasms, ↑ QT interval
Uremia
  • Elevated BUN = hallmark (direct/indirect toxic effects)
  • Mental status changes, bleeding complications
  • ↑ PTH, advanced glycosylation end products, "middle molecules"
  • Correlation of BUN with uremic symptoms is extremely variable
Bleeding & Anemia
  • AKI uremia → ↓ erythropoiesis + platelet dysfunction
  • Anemia usually multifactorial
  • Exacerbated by coexisting sepsis, liver disease, DIC
Cardiac Complications
  • Arrhythmias, pericarditis, pericardial effusion
  • Volume overload + uremia → cardiac injury, impaired function
  • Animal studies: cellular apoptosis, capillary vascular congestion, mitochondrial dysfunction in heart after renal ischemia-reperfusion
Malnutrition & Infections
  • AKI = severely hypercatabolic state → malnutrition is major complication
  • Infections: common precipitant AND dreaded complication of AKI
  • Impaired host immunity described in ESKD, may be operative in severe AKI
Section 11

Prevention & Treatment

General Principles (All Types)

  • Optimization of hemodynamics
  • Correction of fluid and electrolyte imbalances
  • Discontinuation of nephrotoxic medications
  • Dose adjustment of administered medications
  • Maintain mean arterial pressure >65 mmHg
  • Nutrition: 20–30 kcal/kg/day (KDIGO); enteral route preferred

Fluid Management — Prerenal

  • Oliguria alone is NOT an indication for fluid — intravascular hypovolemia should be the only indication
  • Target replacement fluid composition to type of fluid lost
  • Severe acute blood loss → packed RBCs
  • Crystalloids preferred over colloids
  • Hydroxyethyl starch: no longer available for hospitalized patients (increased AKI risk)
  • 0.9% saline (most commonly used) → can cause/exacerbate hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis
  • Buffered crystalloids (Ringer's lactate, Hartmann's, Plasma-Lyte): slightly hypotonic, preferred in hypernatremia
  • Bicarbonate solutions useful in metabolic acidosis

Cirrhosis & Hepatorenal Syndrome

  • Definitive treatment: orthotopic liver transplantation
  • Bridge therapies: norepinephrine, terlipressin (vasopressin analogue), or octreotide + midodrine + IV albumin (25–50 g, max 100 g/day)
  • Albumin may prevent AKI in those treated with antibiotics for spontaneous bacterial peritonitis
  • Rule out peritonitis with ascitic fluid cell count and culture before volume administration

Specific Treatment — Intrinsic AKI

GN / Vasculitis

Immunosuppressive agents, anticomplement therapies, and/or plasmapheresis — early recognition is essential

Allergic Interstitial Nephritis

Discontinue offending agent. Glucocorticoids used (not tested in RCTs) when AKI persists/worsens despite drug cessation

Scleroderma Renal Crisis

ACE inhibitors

Idiopathic TTP

Medical emergency → prompt plasma exchange

Atypical HUS

Pharmacologic blockade of complement activation (e.g., eculizumab)

✅ Rhabdomyolysis Treatment

Early aggressive volume repletion (may initially require 10 L/day). Alkaline fluids (75 mmol/L NaHCO₃ in 0.45% saline) may prevent cast formation — watch for hypocalcemia. Target urine flow 200–300 mL/h with diuretics if needed.

Volume overloadSalt/water restriction; diuretics; if severe → IV furosemide bolus 200 mg followed by drip 10–40 mg/h ± thiazide; ultrafiltration
HyponatremiaRestrict free water; minimize hypotonic IVF including dextrose-containing

Nutrition in AKI

  • Energy: 20–30 kcal/kg/day
  • Protein:
    • Noncatabolic AKI without dialysis: 0.8–1.0 g/kg/day
    • Patients on dialysis: 1.0–1.5 g/kg/day
    • Hypercatabolic + continuous RRT: up to 1.7 g/kg/day
  • Trace elements + water-soluble vitamins should be supplemented in patients receiving dialysis or CRRT

Dialysis — Indications & Modalities

🚨 Critical Dialysis Indications — AEIOU Mnemonic
▼
  • Acidosis — refractory metabolic acidosis
  • Electrolytes — refractory hyperkalemia
  • Ingestions — certain toxic ingestions
  • Overload — volume overload refractory to medical management
  • Uremia — asterixis, pericardial rub/effusion, encephalopathy, uremic bleeding
  • BUN >100 mg/dL empirically (without clinical signs of renal recovery)
⚠️ Caution Timing of Dialysis — Balance Is Key
▼
  • RCTs have NOT shown early vs. late initiation improves survival; early initiation may increase adverse events
  • Do NOT await a life-threatening complication to start dialysis
  • Do NOT initiate unnecessarily early — risks include infection, bleeding, procedural complications, hypotension

★ Section 13 · Exam Traps

Board Exam Traps

★ Trap 01 · Number Trap
A 74-year-old man with BPH and hypertension develops AKI. His serum creatinine rises from 1.0 to 2.1 mg/dL over 3 days. His urine output is 450 mL/24h. Which KDIGO AKI stage is he in?
Tempting Wrong AnswerHe has >400 mL/day, so "preserved urine output" — might seem like Stage 1 or nonoliguric. Creatinine doubling might seem like Stage 2 or 3.
Correct AnswerStage 2 AKI. SCr 2.1 vs. baseline 1.0 = 2.1× baseline, which falls in the Stage 2 range (2.0–2.9× baseline). Urine output of 450 mL/day exceeds the Stage 1 UO criterion of <400 mL/day, so UO does not contribute to staging here. Either SCr OR UO criterion — take the higher stage.
MechanismKDIGO staging takes the HIGHER of the two criteria (SCr or UO). The absolute SCr rise of 1.1 mg/dL alone would be Stage 1 (≥0.3 criterion), but the ratio 2.1× baseline pushes it to Stage 2.
Board Keyword: "Take the HIGHER stage from either criterion"
★ Trap 02 · Drug Interaction Trap
A 68-year-old man with bilateral renal artery stenosis, heart failure, and osteoarthritis is started on lisinopril for heart failure. He takes ibuprofen daily for joint pain. Two weeks later his creatinine doubles. What is the mechanism?
Tempting Wrong AnswerBlame only the ACE-I (heart failure is its indication, so residents hesitate to implicate it alone).
Correct AnswerCombined blockade of both autoregulatory mechanisms. NSAIDs eliminate afferent vasodilation (by blocking prostaglandin-mediated dilation). ACE-I eliminates efferent vasoconstriction (by blocking angiotensin II). Together, both compensatory mechanisms are ablated, causing catastrophic fall in GFR — especially devastating in bilateral RAS where efferent constriction is already critical to maintaining GFR.
MechanismNormal GFR in ↓ perfusion requires: afferent dilation (prostaglandins, NO) AND efferent constriction (angiotensin II). NSAIDs + ACE-I/ARB eliminate both simultaneously.
Board Keyword: "NSAIDs + ACE-I/ARB = highest risk combination"
★ Trap 03 · Look-Alike Trap
A 58-year-old woman develops AKI after starting omeprazole for GERD 3 weeks ago. She has sterile pyuria, mild eosinophilia, and a nonoliguric AKI. No rash or fever. Renal biopsy shows tubulointerstitial inflammation. What is the diagnosis and next step?
Tempting Wrong AnswerAbsence of rash and fever (classic triad) makes AIN "less likely" — some residents exclude the diagnosis without these features.
Correct AnswerAcute Interstitial Nephritis (AIN) due to PPI. Next step: discontinue omeprazole. Consider glucocorticoids if AKI persists/worsens despite drug cessation. PPIs are one of the most common drug causes of AIN.
MechanismThe classic triad (fever, rash, eosinophilia) is present in a minority of AIN cases. Absence of systemic hypersensitivity does NOT exclude AIN — biopsy is needed for definitive diagnosis when cause is unclear.
Board Keyword: "Absence of rash/fever does NOT exclude AIN"
★ Trap 04 · Timing Trap
A 62-year-old man receives IV contrast for a CT abdomen. His baseline creatinine is 1.2 mg/dL. 12 hours later it is 1.3 mg/dL and he is asymptomatic. When should you expect the SCr to peak in contrast-induced AKI?
Tempting Wrong AnswerWithin 24 hours of contrast exposure (confusing "onset" with "peak"). Some may try to treat aggressively because creatinine is rising.
Correct AnswerContrast-induced AKI peaks at 3–5 days after exposure and typically resolves within 1 week. SCr begins rising at 24–48h but does NOT peak until 3–5 days.
MechanismClassic CI-AKI course: SCr rise begins 24–48h → peaks 3–5 days → resolves by 7 days. Dialysis-requiring AKI uncommon unless significant pre-existing CKD + CHF coexist.
Board Keyword: "Contrast AKI: onset 24–48h, peak 3–5 days, resolves by 1 week"
★ Trap 05 · Mechanism Trap
A 72-year-old with cirrhosis and ascites develops AKI with SCr rising from 0.8 to 2.4 mg/dL over 5 days. His FeNa is 0.6%, suggesting avid sodium retention. He is given 3L of IV saline with no improvement in creatinine. What is the most likely diagnosis?
Tempting Wrong AnswerPrerenal azotemia — FeNa <1% and liver disease context.
Correct AnswerHepatorenal Syndrome (most likely Type 1). Key distinguishing feature: AKI persists despite volume resuscitation and withholding of diuretics. HRS is a diagnosis of exclusion that mimics prerenal azotemia hemodynamically but occurs in the setting of total-body volume overload (not depletion). FeNa can be <1% in HRS.
MechanismPrimary splanchnic arterial vasodilation (mediated by vasodilators like NO) → ↓ effective arterial blood volume → activation of RAAS + vasopressin → renal vasoconstriction despite total body Na⁺ and water excess.
Board Keyword: "AKI persists despite volume challenge + diuretic withdrawal = hepatorenal syndrome"
★ Trap 06 · Trial/Evidence Trap
An ICU patient develops oliguric AKI with Stage 3 creatinine. Which of the following interventions has been shown to improve survival — early initiation of dialysis vs. standard (late) initiation?
Tempting Wrong AnswerEarly dialysis improves outcomes by preventing complications.
Correct AnswerNeither has been shown to be superior in randomized controlled trials. Earlier versus later initiation of dialysis has NOT demonstrated improved survival and may increase the risk of adverse events. The correct clinical approach: avoid waiting for a life-threatening complication, but also avoid unnecessarily early initiation.
MechanismEarly dialysis risks: IV line placement, procedural complications, bleeding, infection, hypotension (which can perpetuate AKI). Late dialysis risks: avoidable volume, electrolyte, and metabolic complications. Balance is key.
Board Keyword: "RCTs show NO survival benefit of early over late dialysis initiation"
★ Trap 07 · Population Trap (SGLT-2 Inhibitors)
A 55-year-old man with type 2 diabetes and stage 3 CKD is started on empagliflozin. His nephrologist notes that empagliflozin lowers intraglomerular pressure. Should this agent be avoided due to AKI risk?
Tempting Wrong AnswerYes — lower intraglomerular pressure from SGLT-2 inhibitors is analogous to ACE-I/ARB mechanism and should increase AKI risk.
Correct AnswerNo — SGLT-2 inhibitors do NOT appear to increase AKI risk. In fact, recent studies suggest a protective effect against AKI, despite their effects on lowering intraglomerular pressure and inducing natriuresis. This is mechanistically distinct from ACE-I/ARB effects on the efferent arteriole.
MechanismThe mechanism of AKI protection with SGLT-2 inhibitors is not yet fully clarified. Unlike ACE-I/ARBs (which dilate the efferent arteriole), SGLT-2 inhibitors primarily reduce tubuloglomerular feedback pressure.
Board Keyword: "SGLT-2 inhibitors = AKI protective, NOT harmful"
★ Trap 08 · Number Trap (FeNa Limitations)
A 44-year-old woman with severe sepsis develops oliguria and AKI. Her FeNa is 0.7%. The team diagnoses prerenal azotemia and withholds additional evaluation. Is this correct?
Tempting Wrong AnswerYes — FeNa <1% confirms prerenal state; treat with fluids.
Correct AnswerNo — FeNa <1% in sepsis does NOT indicate prerenal azotemia. Several causes of ischemia-associated and nephrotoxin-associated AKI can present with FeNa <1%: early sepsis, rhabdomyolysis, and contrast nephropathy. A low FeNa is suggestive of, but NOT synonymous with, effective intravascular volume depletion.
MechanismIn early sepsis, tubular sodium reabsorption may remain relatively intact (intact tubular function initially) → FeNa <1% despite intrinsic AKI developing. FeNa is most useful in oliguric patients NOT on diuretics and without CKD.
Board Keyword: "FeNa <1% in sepsis ≠ prerenal — early sepsis can have FeNa <1%"
★ Trap 09 · Look-Alike Trap (Rhabdomyolysis vs. Hemolysis)
A 28-year-old marathon runner collapses at the finish line. Labs show urine that is dark red-brown and tests heme-positive on dipstick. Microscopy shows very few RBCs. Serum creatine kinase is 42,000 U/L. What is the diagnosis and key pathophysiologic mechanism?
Tempting Wrong AnswerGross hematuria and heme-positive dipstick = bleeding → evaluate for renal trauma or bladder injury.
Correct AnswerRhabdomyolysis with myoglobinuria. Heme-positive dipstick with few or no RBCs on microscopy = pigment nephropathy (myoglobin or hemoglobin). The massively elevated CK confirms rhabdomyolysis. Key mechanisms: intrarenal vasoconstriction + direct proximal tubular toxicity + mechanical obstruction (myoglobin precipitates with Tamm-Horsfall protein in acidic urine).
MechanismMyoglobin (MW ~17,000 Da) is freely filtered → accumulates in tubules → precipitates with uromodulin (Tamm-Horsfall protein) in acidic pH → obstructing casts → AKI. Treatment: aggressive fluid resuscitation; consider alkalinization of urine.
Board Keyword: "Heme-positive + few RBCs = pigment nephropathy, NOT hematuria"
★ Trap 10 · Mechanism Trap (Postrenal)
A 76-year-old man with BPH undergoes evaluation for AKI. His urine output is 800 mL/day, and the team initially rules out postrenal obstruction because urine output is "preserved." Is this reasoning correct?
Tempting Wrong AnswerYes — preserved urine output excludes significant obstruction.
Correct AnswerNo — normal or even elevated urine output does NOT rule out partial obstruction. GFR is normally two orders of magnitude higher than urinary flow rate. A patient with 30% residual filtration can still produce "adequate" urine while having significant AKI from partial obstruction. Always image the urinary tract when the clinical scenario suggests obstruction.
MechanismIn partial obstruction, glomerular filtration is partially maintained → urine still formed → output preserved but significantly reduced from baseline. The ratio GFR:urine flow rate is ~100:1 normally, meaning small amounts of filtration through an obstructed system can maintain "preserved" urine output.
Board Keyword: "Preserved urine output does NOT exclude postrenal obstruction"
